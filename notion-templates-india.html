Tax & Compliance for Indian Freelancers

Freelance income in India is treated as "Income from Business and Profession" under the Income Tax Act. This means you have different obligations than a salaried employee — and different opportunities to reduce your taxable income through legitimate deductions. Here is a plain-language overview of the four key compliance areas every Indian freelancer should understand.

01

GST Registration

You are required to register for GST if your annual freelance income exceeds ₹20 lakhs (₹10 lakhs in special category states including Uttarakhand, Himachal Pradesh, and the North-Eastern states). Below this threshold, registration is optional — though some corporate clients will request your GSTIN for their own input tax credit, so registering voluntarily can help you win larger contracts.

If you provide services to clients outside India (export of services), those services are zero-rated under GST — meaning no GST is collected and you can claim a refund of any GST you paid on your business inputs. This makes the export market particularly attractive from a tax perspective.

Once registered, you must file GSTR-1 (outward supplies) monthly or quarterly and GSTR-3B (summary return) monthly. Notion's GST Invoice Tracker in FreelanceOS keeps all the data you need for these filings organised automatically.

02

Advance Tax

If your total tax liability for the year exceeds ₹10,000, you are required to pay advance tax in four instalments rather than settling the full amount at the end of the year. The four due dates are:

  • 15th June — 15% of estimated annual tax
  • 15th September — 45% cumulative (30% this quarter)
  • 15th December — 75% cumulative (30% this quarter)
  • 15th March — 100% cumulative (25% this quarter)

Missing these deadlines triggers interest under Section 234B and 234C. Freelancers who earn in USD have the additional complexity of estimating their INR income before the year ends — which is why the USD/INR Project Tracker in FreelanceOS includes an advance tax calculator based on year-to-date earnings.

03

TDS on Freelance Payments

When Indian companies or individuals pay you for professional services, they are typically required to deduct TDS (Tax Deducted at Source) before paying you. Under Section 194J of the Income Tax Act, TDS on professional services (which includes most freelance work — design, development, writing, consulting) is 10%. Under Section 194C, TDS on contract work is 1% (individual/HUF) or 2% (others).

Your clients should provide you with a Form 16A TDS certificate each quarter. Keep these certificates — they represent tax already paid on your behalf and reduce your final tax liability. Download your annual TDS credit summary from the TRACES portal and reconcile it with your own income records. A mismatch can delay your refund or trigger a notice.

For foreign clients paying you from abroad, there is typically no TDS — the income is received gross, which is why advance tax planning for foreign-income freelancers is particularly important.

Tax & Compliance for Indian Freelancers

Navigating India's tax system as a freelancer is more complex than it is for salaried employees — but it's entirely manageable with the right systems in place. Here's what every Indian freelancer needs to understand about GST, advance tax, TDS, and how Notion can help you stay on top of all of it without paying an accountant to handle things you can track yourself year-round.

GST Registration

GST registration is mandatory for freelancers whose annual turnover exceeds Rs 20 lakhs (reduced to Rs 10 lakhs for special category states including Manipur, Mizoram, Nagaland, Tripura, Meghalaya, Sikkim, Arunachal Pradesh, and Uttarakhand). Below these thresholds, registration is optional — though many clients, especially corporate ones, will request your GSTIN for their own input tax credit claims. Some corporate clients will refuse to engage unregistered vendors entirely, so registration can be a commercial necessity well before the legal threshold.

If you cross the threshold, registration must happen within 30 days. Services exported to foreign clients (outside India) are treated as zero-rated supplies, meaning you may be eligible for GST refunds even if you're registered — a significant benefit for freelancers billing international clients in USD or GBP. Once registered, you'll need to file GSTR-1 (outward supplies) and GSTR-3B (summary return) monthly or quarterly depending on your turnover, plus an annual GSTR-9. A well-maintained Notion invoice tracker dramatically reduces the time these filings take.

Advance Tax Obligations

Freelancers whose total tax liability exceeds Rs 10,000 in a financial year are required to pay advance tax in four installments. The due dates are fixed: June 15 (15% of estimated liability), September 15 (45%), December 15 (75%), and March 15 (100%). Missing these dates attracts interest under Section 234B and 234C — a preventable cost if you track your income projections systematically through the year rather than estimating everything in March.

For freelancers earning in USD from foreign clients, estimating advance tax is complicated by exchange rate fluctuation. The key is to log each receipt in both currencies at the time of conversion, so your INR income figure is accurate throughout the year rather than reconstructed at filing time from bank statements you've largely forgotten about.

TDS: What You Need to Know

When Indian clients (companies or individuals subject to tax audit) pay you for professional or technical services, they are required to deduct TDS under Section 194J at 10% (or 2% for technical services in certain cases). If you're engaged as a contractor for a specific project rather than for ongoing professional services, Section 194C may apply at 1 to 2% instead. This TDS is deducted at source and deposited with the government — reducing your immediate cash flow but offsetting against your final tax liability when you file your return.

Critically, you need to collect Form 16A from every Indian client who deducts TDS, typically on a quarterly basis. Without these certificates, you cannot claim the TDS credit when filing your ITR and may end up paying tax twice on the same income. Tracking which clients owe you Form 16A — and following up proactively when they're late — is a workflow Notion handles well with a simple TDS certificate tracking database.

Your CA or a simple online tax filing tool handles the calculations — but only if you've maintained clean, consistent records throughout the year.
