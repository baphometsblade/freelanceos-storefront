Questions From Tax Preparers and Enrolled Agents

How does the filing deadline tracker work for different return types?

The filing deadline tracker is a unified database covering all key IRS filing deadlines — January 31 (W-2 and 1099 distribution), March 15 (S-Corps and partnerships), April 15 (individual 1040, C-Corps, FBAR), October 15 (individual extensions), September 15 (S-Corp and partnership extensions) — combined with per-client tracking: return type, original deadline, extension filed (yes/no), extended deadline, return filed date, and confirmation received. You can filter by deadline date to see everything due this week, this month, or in the next 30 days across all clients simultaneously. For tax practices managing 50 to 300+ returns across multiple entity types, this tracker is the most operationally critical feature in the template.

How does the document checklist system work?

The document checklist tracks per-client, per-return document collection: W-2s from each employer, 1099-NEC, 1099-INT, 1099-DIV, 1099-B, K-1s, prior year return, mortgage interest 1098, student loan 1098-E, property tax statements, quarterly estimates made, business income and expense summary, home office details, vehicle mileage log, health insurance premiums for self-employed, retirement contributions, charitable donation receipts, and others you add for specific client situations. Each document has a received status (yes/no) and follow-up sent (yes/no) so you can filter to see exactly what is outstanding per client rather than relying on memory or searching email threads to figure out what you are still waiting on.

How does the communication log protect my practice?

The client communication log tracks every substantive contact: date, type (document request/tax return review/signature request/tax planning call/notice response/general question), client question or issue, response sent (yes/no), and issue resolved (yes/no). The IRS notice management section tracks notice type (CP2000, audit notification, payment plan, balance due, refund inquiry, identity verification), date received, response deadline, action taken, and status. This log is your audit trail of client communications — essential for practice protection if a client later disputes advice given, claims they were not informed of a deadline, or if an IRS matter requires demonstrating your responsiveness and diligence. Many tax professionals find that maintaining this log also helps them recognize patterns in client questions that can be addressed proactively in future engagement letters.
