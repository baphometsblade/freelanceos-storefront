The brief is the foundation of every project. When it's vague, incomplete, or buried in a six-email thread, the work suffers and scope creep kicks in. The Creative Brief Database gives you a structured intake system so every project starts with clarity.

No more trying to reconstruct what the client wanted from a three-week-old Slack thread.

Feature 04

Brand Asset Library

Working with multiple clients means managing multiple brand identities — and the assets that go with them. Every client has their own logo versions, approved colour palettes, font stacks, and usage rules. Managing all of that across separate folders, emails, and Google Drive links is a recipe for using the wrong asset on the wrong deliverable.

The Brand Asset Library is a per-client brand vault inside Notion. Each record covers one client brand and stores linked logo files (SVG, PNG transparent, reversed versions), the full colour palette with HEX, CMYK, and Pantone references, the complete typography stack (primary typeface, secondary, body, weights), usage guidelines (what you can and can't do with the mark), and a last-updated date so you always know you're working from the current version.

When a client sends you updated brand guidelines — a new primary colour, a redrawn sub-mark, a revised font choice — you update one record and every project card pulling from that client's brand library stays current. Particularly valuable when managing retainer clients whose brand evolves over time, or when working with in-house teams alongside external agencies who need consistent asset access.

  • One brand record per client — all logo variants, colours, typography and usage notes in a single place
  • Colour palette fields store HEX, CMYK and Pantone values side by side — switch between print and digital specs without hunting
  • Last-updated date prevents you from delivering work with superseded colours or deprecated logo versions
  • Link brand records to any project card so the correct assets are one click away during production

Feature 05

Revision & Feedback Tracker

Revision rounds are where design projects live or die. A client who gives clear, consolidated feedback in two rounds is a joy to work with. A client who sends piecemeal notes across five rounds, changes their mind between rounds, and re-opens closed feedback is a business liability — but only if you can't see the pattern.

The Revision and Feedback Tracker logs every round for every project. Each entry records the round number, the exact feedback notes (so you're not relying on memory or a buried email), the specific changes made in response, who approved the round and on what date. This creates a timestamped revision history that protects you if a client disputes whether a change was agreed.

The revision count visible on the project card connects directly to this tracker. When a client has used two of their two included rounds and requests another round, you have the documentation to have the scope conversation professionally. Over time the tracker reveals which clients consistently exceed scope — informing how you write contracts and price revisions in future projects with them. It's not just a record, it's pricing intelligence.

  • Round-by-round log with timestamped feedback notes and your documented response — full revision paper trail
  • Approved-by field creates client sign-off records at each stage, preventing "but I never approved that" disputes
  • Revision count rolls up to the project card so you can see at a glance which clients are on borrowed rounds
  • Patterns across multiple projects reveal serial scope-creepers — useful data for repricing or rescoping contracts

Feature 06

Freelance Finance Dashboard

Most freelance designers have no clear picture of what they actually earned last month. Revenue comes in as project payments, expenses leak out as software subscriptions and font licenses, and the difference — actual net profit — is a number you only discover when your accountant tells you. The Finance Dashboard fixes that.

Every completed project feeds its rate into the dashboard. The income view shows earnings by month, by client, and by project type so you can see which work is most profitable and which clients represent the bulk of your income. Outstanding invoices are flagged separately so you can chase payment without losing track of what's owed.

The expense tracker captures the full cost of running a design practice: Figma and Adobe Creative Cloud subscriptions, font licenses (Google Fonts doesn't cover every project), stock photography and illustration assets, hardware costs, and professional development. Subtracting confirmed expenses from confirmed income gives you the net profit figure you actually need — not gross revenue that looks impressive on paper but includes money you haven't received yet.

  • Monthly income summary by client and project type — identify your most profitable work and highest-value client relationships
  • Outstanding invoices view flags unpaid projects so chasing payment is systematic, not reactive
