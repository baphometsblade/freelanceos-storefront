Feature 01

Product Profitability Tracker

Most Shopify sellers look at revenue and feel good. The Profitability Tracker forces a different question: what does this product actually net after every real cost is deducted?

For every SKU in your catalog, you log the full cost stack: supplier cost, Shopify transaction fee (2.9% + $0.30 on Basic), payment processing, outbound shipping, packaging materials, and average ad spend per unit sold. The database calculates net margin automatically — no formulas to copy, no spreadsheet maintenance.

The eye-opener for most sellers is the first time they see a $35 product actually netting $4.20. That's a 12% margin. Not bad until you realize a 10% uptick in ad costs wipes it out entirely. With every SKU visible side-by-side, you can immediately see which products carry the store and which ones are subsidized by your winners.

The tracker also surfaces a per-unit contribution margin so you can model "what if I raised the price by $3?" or "what if I switched to a cheaper shipper?" directly inside Notion. Decisions that used to take an hour of spreadsheet work take two minutes.

Feature 02

Supplier Hub

Every supplier relationship you have lives in one structured database. No more digging through email threads to find a contact, estimate a lead time, or remember which vendor has the faster shipping option.

Each supplier card captures everything that matters operationally: vendor name, primary contact and backup contact, current lead time in days, minimum order quantity, your personal reliability rating from 1 to 5 stars based on experience, the reorder trigger point for each linked product, and payment terms including net-30 or upfront requirements.

The reorder trigger is especially powerful. When you link a product to its supplier and set a trigger point — say, "reorder when stock drops below 50 units" — the dashboard flags it before you hit zero. Combined with the supplier's lead time, you always know how many days of runway you have before a stockout.

You can sort suppliers by reliability rating to see who your most dependable partners are, or filter by lead time when planning a new product launch around a specific go-live date. All product cards link back to their supplier record, so context is always one click away.

Feature 03

Product Launch Pipeline

Every successful product launch follows a repeatable sequence. The problem is remembering every step when you're juggling multiple products at different stages. The Launch Pipeline is a Kanban board that forces each product through six defined stages from first idea to live listing.

The stages are: Idea (validating demand before spending), Research (competitor analysis, pricing research, keyword targeting), Design (creative briefs and supplier specs), Photography (shot list, lifestyle and white-background assets), Listing (copywriting, SEO optimization, upload), and Launch (go-live checklist, initial ad budget, first-week monitoring).

Each stage has a built-in checklist of tasks that must be completed before moving to the next column. This prevents the common mistake of publishing a listing with missing photos, unoptimized titles, or a pricing error because you rushed the last step.

Products link directly to their Profitability Tracker record and Supplier Hub entry. When something is in the Research stage, you can immediately see the supplier lead time to set a realistic launch date. Nothing exists in isolation — the whole system is connected.

Feature 05

Customer Feedback Database

Most Shopify sellers read their reviews in isolation — one at a time, on the product page, with no system for spotting patterns. The Customer Feedback Database aggregates every review and support ticket by product, then tags each entry by issue category, so you can see at a glance what your customers are actually complaining about.

Issue categories include: Shipping (late, damaged, lost), Quality (defect, material, sizing), Listing Accuracy (photos don't match, description misleading), Packaging (arrived damaged, inadequate protection), and Positive (explicit praise you should amplify in your marketing).

Each feedback entry links to the affected product in the Profitability Tracker and creates a linked action item. If five customers in a month report that a mug arrives chipped, that's a packaging problem with a trackable fix — a new box spec, a different shipper, or a bubble-wrap insert. The action item stays open until it's resolved and tested.

The aggregate view by product gives you a signal-to-noise ratio: a product with 12 reviews and 8 negative shipping mentions has a systemic problem. A product with 40 reviews and 2 scattered complaints is performing normally. You stop over-rotating on single reviews and start fixing the actual patterns.

Feature 06

Revenue & P&L Dashboard

The P&L Dashboard is the executive view of your entire store — monthly revenue, gross margin percentage, total COGS, net profit, and year-over-year comparison. All data flows automatically from your other databases, so there's nothing extra to enter.

The monthly view shows: gross revenue by month, COGS total (pulled from Profitability Tracker), gross profit, operating costs (Shopify subscription, apps, software), marketing spend, and net profit. At the bottom of each month column you see net profit margin as a percentage — the one number that tells you if the store is actually a business or an expensive hobby.

The YoY comparison widget shows this month versus the same month last year. It's the most motivating view in the template — seeing $12,400 net profit in June this year vs. $7,800 in June last year is better validation than any revenue milestone.

You also get a COGS trend chart that flags when your supplier costs are creeping up — often a sign that a supplier has quietly raised prices or that your product mix has shifted toward lower-margin SKUs. Most sellers don't notice this for months. You'll notice it in a week.
