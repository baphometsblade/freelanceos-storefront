Frequently asked questions

Everything freelancers ask about setting rates

Why is there a 30% buffer above minimum?
The minimum rate is your break-even point — if you charged exactly that, one slow week would put you behind. The 30% buffer covers late-paying clients, dry spells between projects, unpaid admin time, and the reality that you'll rarely hit your billable hours target every single week.
What counts as billable hours?
Only time you can actually invoice clients — project work, consultations, deliverable reviews. Not proposal writing, bookkeeping, email, social media, or learning. Most freelancers overestimate their billable hours by 30–40%, so be conservative here.
Should I charge hourly or per project?
Project pricing almost always earns more — but you need your hourly rate to price projects correctly. Calculate your hourly rate here, estimate the hours a project will take, add a 20% buffer, and that's your project quote. Your hourly rate is your internal benchmark regardless of how you bill.
How do market rates factor in?
Your minimum rate is math — your premium rate is market positioning. If your calculated rate is below market average, you have room to charge more without losing clients. If it's above, you need to clearly communicate your value, specialization, or niche expertise to justify the premium.
What expenses should I include?
Software subscriptions, equipment, home office costs (a % of rent/utilities), professional development, accounting fees, health insurance premiums, professional memberships, and a reserve for equipment replacement. Most freelancers undercount this by $2,000–$5,000 per year.
How often should I recalculate my rate?
At minimum: once a year, and whenever you gain a major new skill, niche down further, or move up-market. Most freelancers raise rates too rarely — if you haven't raised your rate in 18 months and you're fully booked, that's a signal you're undercharging.
