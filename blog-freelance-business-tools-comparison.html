Trello: is it the right project management tool for freelancers?

Trello Free tier / $5/mo Standard

Trello is the classic Kanban board: cards move left to right through columns. For simple task lists and team collaboration, it works. For freelancers, the core problem is that it's a project management tool that doesn't know anything about your clients, invoices, or business. Your Trello board has no idea which project has an invoice outstanding, which client is overdue on feedback, or which retainer ends next month.

The free tier limits you to 10 boards, which sounds like plenty until you realize you need one board per active client, one for your business operations, one for personal projects, and one for your content. You hit that limit faster than you'd expect.

Good for: simple Kanban workflows Doesn't connect to client or financial data

What Notion does instead: In a Notion-based project system, each project record has a relation to the client it belongs to — which means Notion already knows that "Acme Corp — Q2 Content" belongs to Sarah at Acme Corp, is tied to Invoice #047 for $3,500, and is due on May 30. Kanban view is one option, but you can also see your projects as a timeline, a calendar, or a table sorted by deadline. All without leaving the same workspace where everything else lives.

Harvest: time tracking and invoicing done right, at a price

Harvest Free (1 seat, 2 projects) / $12/mo

Harvest is genuinely excellent for time tracking. The timer integrations, the automatic invoice generation from tracked hours, and the project budget tracking are all well-executed. If time tracking is central to how you bill — hourly freelancers especially — Harvest earns its $12/month.

The problem for most freelancers is that Harvest's core value is time tracking, and a significant portion of freelancers don't bill hourly. If you work on retainers, project-based fees, or value pricing, you're paying $12/month for time tracking features you never use, plus the invoice generation (which is the only other thing you'd want — and which Notion handles with a tracker and a template).

Best-in-class time tracking Overkill if you don't bill hourly

When to keep Harvest: if you bill by the hour and time tracking is a core part of your workflow, Harvest is worth the $12/month. If you do project-based or retainer billing, Notion's invoice tracker in FreelanceOS is a direct replacement — with the added bonus that it connects directly to your client records and shows outstanding balances inline.

FreshBooks: the full accounting suite for freelancers

FreshBooks $19/mo (Lite) – $55/mo (Premium)

FreshBooks is the go-to for freelancers who want proper accounting: double-entry bookkeeping, expense tracking, tax categories, profit/loss reports, and accountant access. If you're making serious revenue and need to hand reports to an accountant at year-end, FreshBooks earns its place.

For freelancers with simpler finances — a few recurring clients, straightforward project billing, no employees — FreshBooks is often more accounting software than the business actually needs. The $19/month Lite plan limits you to five billable clients. The next tier up is $33/month. Most early-stage freelancers don't need double-entry bookkeeping; they need to know which invoices are paid and what's outstanding.

Full accounting features for complex finances Expensive if you only need invoice tracking

The Notion alternative: For invoice tracking (who owes what, what's been paid, what's overdue), Notion handles this perfectly. FreelanceOS includes an invoice tracker with status tracking, outstanding balance views, and a per-client revenue rollup. What it doesn't replace is real accounting software when you genuinely need it — expense categorization, P&L statements, and tax prep. If you need those, keep FreshBooks or switch to Wave (free). If you just need invoice tracking, Notion has you covered.

Calendly: scheduling that earns its keep

Calendly Free (1 event type) / $10/mo Standard

Calendly is one of those tools that earns its keep the moment you stop trading emails to schedule a call. The free tier — one event type, unlimited meetings — is genuinely useful. The paid tier adds multiple event types (useful for different call lengths or consultation types), routing forms, and integrations.

This is one area where Notion genuinely cannot replace a purpose-built scheduling tool. There's no clean way to let clients self-book time from a Notion page that actually syncs with your Google Calendar and sends automated reminders. Calendly solves a specific real-time coordination problem that static databases can't.

Genuinely saves time on scheduling Free tier covers most freelancer needs

Our recommendation: Calendly's free tier is usually enough. One event type (a 30-minute discovery call, for example) covers the majority of use cases. If you need multiple booking types, the $10/month Standard plan is worth it. This is one of the tools worth keeping — but the free tier, not the paid one.

Dubsado: powerful CRM, but is it too much?

Dubsado $20/mo (monthly) / $16/mo (annual)

Dubsado is one of the most comprehensive freelance business management platforms out there. It handles client onboarding, proposals, contracts, invoices, questionnaires, automations, and CRM in one place. If you're a wedding photographer, interior designer, or any creative with a complex, document-heavy client workflow, Dubsado is genuinely powerful.

For many freelancers, though, Dubsado is a significant learning curve for features they'll rarely use. The automation system is powerful but complex. The setup time is substantial — easily 20–30 hours before you're fully operational. And at $20/month, it's one of the more expensive items in the stack.

Best for complex client workflows with lots of documents Overkill for digital/consulting freelancers
