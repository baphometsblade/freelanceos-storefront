Why Couples Struggle

Wedding Planning Is Supposed to Be Joyful.
So Why Does It Feel Like a Second Job?

You got engaged. You opened a spreadsheet. Now you have seventeen of them — and none of them talk to each other. Sound familiar?

The average couple uses 6–8 different tools to plan their wedding: a Google Sheet for the budget, a Notes app for vendor phone numbers, a shared Google Doc for the guest list, a calendar for deadlines, Zola or The Knot for inspiration, a group chat with family, and a planner PDF they downloaded once and never touched again. The problem isn't that you're disorganized. The problem is that the tools don't work together.

📊

Too Many Apps, Zero Clarity

You have a budget in one tab, vendors in another, and your checklist buried in a notes app. Every time you sit down to plan, you spend 20 minutes just finding things. The planning itself never gets done.

Month-by-Month Guide

Your 12-Month Wedding Planning Timeline

The master checklist in this template follows this exact framework. Here's what to tackle and when — so nothing catches you off guard.

12 Months Out

Set the Foundation

The engagement high is real — channel it into these high-stakes decisions before the best venues and photographers book up. The biggest mistake couples make is waiting six months and discovering their top venue is gone.

Set total budget Create guest list draft Pick a wedding style Research + book venue Set the date Hire wedding planner (if using)
10–11 Months

Book Your Key Vendors Early

Photographers, videographers, and popular bands/DJs book 12–18 months out in many markets. If you've set your date and venue, now is the time to secure these before they're gone. Getting quotes from three vendors per category is a good rule of thumb.

Interview + book photographer Book videographer Start caterer research Book band or DJ Begin dress shopping Announce engagement formally
8–9 Months

Build Your Team

This is when the planning machine really starts turning. You're filling out your vendor roster, getting your bridal party sorted, and handling the logistics that take the longest to finalize — like dress alterations and wedding invitations, which require long lead times.

Choose + ask bridal party Order wedding dress Book florist Book officiant Book hair + makeup Start invitation design Book block of hotel rooms
6–7 Months

Nail the Details

The big vendors are booked. Now you shift to the details that make a wedding feel personal. Engagement photos, registry setup, and save-the-dates go out — giving guests enough lead time to make travel arrangements for destination or out-of-town weddings.

Send save-the-dates Book engagement session Create wedding registry Plan honeymoon destination Book honeymoon travel Choose wedding cake/desserts Book transportation
4–5 Months

Get the Paperwork Right

Invitations mail out, final menus get selected, and the administrative side of the wedding takes center stage. Marriage license requirements vary by county — research yours now so you know the application window (typically 30–90 days before the wedding).

Mail invitations Finalize catering menu Research marriage license Order bridesmaid dresses Schedule dress fittings Create wedding website Plan rehearsal dinner
2–3 Months

Confirm Everything Twice

RSVPs are coming in, final headcounts are taking shape, and it's time to confirm every vendor has the right date, time, and address. Surprises at this stage are preventable — this is the most important coordination window before the wedding.

Track RSVP deadline Confirm all vendors Finalize seating chart Create ceremony program Apply for marriage license Break in wedding shoes Final dress fitting
1 Month Out

Final Headcounts and Logistics

You have your numbers. Now it's about logistics — delivering final guest counts to the caterer, assembling welcome bags if you're doing them, and making sure every member of your wedding party knows exactly where to be and when. Write out your day-of timeline in detail.

Submit final guest count Build day-of timeline Prepare vendor payments Assemble welcome bags Distribute timeline to wedding party Pick up wedding dress
Wedding Week

Breathe. You've Got This.

The planning is done. This week is for final confirmations, rehearsal dinner, packing for the honeymoon, and actually enjoying the anticipation. Hand your detailed day-of timeline to a designated point-of-contact — a coordinator, wedding planner, or trusted friend — so you don't have to answer vendor questions on your wedding day.

Attend rehearsal + dinner Final vendor confirmations Pack honeymoon bags Delegate day-of contact Prepare wedding party payments Get good sleep

The average US wedding costs $29,000–$35,000 — but the average isn't your wedding. Here's how most couples allocate their budget, and how the Notion budget tracker keeps each category visible in real time.

30%

12%

Intimate Celebrations

Planning an Elopement or Micro-Wedding?
This Template Scales Down With You

Not every celebration needs 150 guests and a $40,000 budget. The template includes a dedicated module for intimate weddings — streamlined, personal, and just as organized.

The rise of micro-weddings (typically 20 guests or fewer) and elopements has changed what wedding planning looks like for a lot of couples. You still need to plan carefully — you just need a different framework. The standard 200-person wedding checklist has tasks that simply don't apply: seating charts for 18 people are overkill, and a catering coordinator is redundant when you're booking a private chef for dinner at a mountaintop lodge.

Elopement (2–10 People)

An elopement is defined by its intimacy. It might be just the two of you with a photographer, or a small group of immediate family in a meaningful location. The planning focus shifts almost entirely to logistics: permits for public lands, officiant licensing in the specific state/country, travel arrangements, and a single meaningful photographer who can double as a witness.

Budget for an elopement typically runs $1,500–$8,000 depending heavily on location and whether you include travel.

Micro-Wedding (10–20 People)

A micro-wedding keeps the structure of a traditional wedding — ceremony, dinner, toasts, first dance — but scales it to your most important people. You can book a private room at a restaurant, a vacation rental, or a small venue that a traditional 150-person wedding couldn't accommodate. The result is often more personal and meaningful than a large event.

Budget for a micro-wedding typically runs $5,000–$20,000, depending on venue and food/beverage choices.

Elopement Checklist (Simplified)

  • Choose location and check permit requirements
  • Hire a photographer who specializes in elopements
  • Book officiant (or confirm self-solemnization laws)
  • Apply for marriage license in the correct jurisdiction
  • Plan your outfits — practical for the environment
  • Book travel and accommodation
  • Decide on witnesses (required in most states)
  • Plan a celebration dinner after the ceremony
  • Notify immediate family with your plan
  • Prepare announcement for after the wedding

Micro-Wedding Additions

  • Select venue for intimate group (private dining, estate, etc.)
  • Build a guest list of your most important people
  • Send invitations (digital is totally acceptable)
  • Arrange seating for dinner — usually one table
  • Plan a simple ceremony with personal vows
  • Hire a photographer + consider small florals
  • Choose a caterer or restaurant experience
  • Create a simple 3–4 hour reception flow
  • Plan a wedding cake or dessert moment
  • Coordinate overnight accommodations for guests
