Roadmap Frameworks Explained

The best product teams don't use just one prioritization method — they choose the framework that fits the decision at hand. This template supports four proven frameworks. Here's when to use each one.

Now / Next / Later

Best for: flexible horizon planning

Instead of committing to specific dates that will inevitably change, Now/Next/Later organizes your roadmap into three fluid horizons. "Now" is your current quarter or sprint cycle. "Next" is roughly the following quarter. "Later" is everything important but not yet scheduled.

Use this when: your team works in a fast-moving environment where quarterly plans frequently shift, or when you want to share a roadmap with customers without locking yourself into release dates you can't guarantee.

RICE Scoring

Best for: data-driven prioritization

RICE stands for Reach, Impact, Confidence, and Effort. The formula produces a numeric score for each feature so you can rank your backlog objectively. Reach is how many users are affected. Impact is how much it moves the needle. Confidence is how certain you are about those estimates. Effort is engineer-weeks required.

Use this when: you have a contested backlog and need a defensible, quantitative reason to prioritize one feature over another. Especially useful when aligning stakeholders with competing priorities.

MoSCoW Method

Best for: scope and release planning

MoSCoW divides features into Must Have, Should Have, Could Have, and Won't Have (this time). It was developed for rapid application development and remains one of the clearest ways to define MVP scope and manage scope creep during active development cycles.

Use this when: planning a specific release, defining an MVP, or when your team needs a simple shared vocabulary for scope decisions without the overhead of numerical scoring.

Theme-Based Roadmapping

Best for: strategic narrative

Instead of listing features, you organize your roadmap around strategic themes — "Make onboarding 10x easier," "Win the enterprise segment," "Reduce churn." Each theme contains the features that advance that goal. This communicates strategy, not just a task list.

Use this when: presenting to boards, investors, or all-hands meetings where the strategic intent matters more than the specific features, or when you want to maintain a coherent product narrative across multiple quarters.

🔧 The Integration Philosophy

The most durable integrations between Notion and dev tools are simple, not clever. Over-engineering the sync creates fragile automations that break and lose data. The guide in the template recommends a "hub and spoke" approach: Notion is the hub (roadmap, strategy, specs), and dev tools are the spokes (execution, tracking). Information flows one direction — from Notion to dev tools — rather than attempting full bidirectional sync.

  • Notion owns: roadmap decisions, feature specs, prioritization reasoning, stakeholder communication
  • Dev tools own: implementation tasks, code review, CI/CD, release management
  • The handoff: when a feature moves to "In Sprint" in Notion, the team creates the corresponding tickets in their dev tool and links back
  • Status updates: engineers update Notion status (Shipped/Blocked) as part of their weekly sync, keeping the roadmap current without automation overhead
