Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you want to know about the Notion engineering template

Does this Notion template work for software engineers only, or all engineering disciplines? ▼
This template is designed for any engineering professional — software engineers, civil engineers, mechanical engineers, electrical engineers, product engineers, and DevOps engineers. The career ladder, learning log, project impact database, and promotion prep modules are discipline-agnostic. You simply customize the skill categories and competency levels to match your company's engineering ladder. Software engineers get the most out-of-the-box coverage since the default templates use SWE terminology, but every section can be renamed and repurposed in minutes. We've had civil PEs use it to track continuing education credits, mechanical engineers log product development projects, and embedded engineers map firmware deliverables to their tech ladder.
Can I actually use this for promotion prep? Does it really work? ▼
Absolutely — promotion prep is one of the primary use cases the template was built around. The Promotion Prep Doc Builder module structures your evidence log around the exact competencies your company evaluates: technical impact, leadership, collaboration, mentorship, and scope. By logging projects and learnings throughout the year using the other modules, the system effectively writes your promo packet for you when review time arrives. Engineers who have used this report arriving at their performance review with a fully documented, competency-mapped evidence packet — which dramatically increases both the clarity and strength of their promotion case. The key insight is that promo prep shouldn't happen in the weeks before your review. It should happen throughout the year, five minutes at a time. This template makes that effortless.
Does this Notion template for engineers work for engineering managers? ▼
Yes, and it's one of our most popular use cases among EMs. The 1:1 Meeting Tracker, Weekly Engineering Log, and Career Ladder Tracker are all directly useful for engineering managers growing toward senior EM, Director, or VP Engineering roles. The competency categories just shift — technical leadership, people management, organizational impact, business alignment — but the system works the same way. Several engineering managers also use the template to coach their direct reports — they duplicate the Career Ladder Tracker for each IC on their team, use the 1:1 Tracker to surface recurring patterns, and use the Promotion Prep module to build strong cases for their ICs' promotion cycles. If you manage a team of 5–15 engineers, this template can become the operating system for both your own growth and your team's development.
How does the learning system work, and is it actually useful? ▼
The Learning System is a Notion database where you log every course, book, research paper, tutorial, conference talk, podcast episode, and blog post you want to engage with. Each entry has fields for format (book, course, video, article, paper, talk), topic area, priority, status (to-read, in-progress, completed), time investment estimate, and a rich-text notes section for key takeaways. Filtering views let you see your active learning queue sorted by priority, completed materials by year, everything tagged to a specific skill area like "distributed systems" or "leadership," and a monthly digest of your takeaways. That monthly digest view is particularly powerful — it aggregates what you've learned each month so you can reference it during performance reviews, 1:1s, and when mentoring colleagues. Most engineers find they actually complete more learning goals after six weeks of using the system because visibility and priority create accountability.
Can I track projects across multiple companies or roles throughout my career? ▼
Yes — and this is one of the most underrated features of the template. The Project Impact Database has a Company/Role field so you can maintain a lifelong record of every significant project you have ever worked on. This becomes invaluable when switching jobs: your impact narrative is fully documented and you can quickly pull specific examples for job interviews, reference conversations, and technical writing. When an interviewer asks "tell me about a time you improved system performance," you don't have to reconstruct the story from memory — you open your database and read the exact metrics, context, and decisions you documented at the time. Many engineers find this becomes their most valuable professional asset over a 10-year career, giving them a searchable archive of every technical decision, business outcome, and lesson learned across every company they've worked at.
Is this Notion template good for early-career engineers, or is it more for senior people? ▼
It is actually ideal for early-career engineers — arguably more valuable than for seniors. Most junior engineers spend their first two to three years doing great work that goes completely undocumented. Then they hit their first promotion cycle and struggle to articulate their impact because they never tracked it. Starting this system at the IC1 or IC2 level means you build a documented track record from day one. The Career Ladder Tracker shows you exactly what "Senior Engineer" looks like at your company so you can work toward it intentionally rather than discovering the gap at review time. Engineers who start early and stick with the weekly log consistently report that their promotion conversations are dramatically smoother — because they can demonstrate growth, not just claim it. If you're fresh out of college or in your first two years, this system will compound far more than it does for someone starting at the Staff level.
