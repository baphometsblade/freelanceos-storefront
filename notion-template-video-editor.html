The Problem

You Create the Work. But Managing the Business of Editing Is a Full-Time Job Itself.

Video editing careers are built on craft — colour grading, pacing, storytelling, sound design, the invisible decisions that make footage feel cinematic. What no one teaches you is that the business side — tracking revision rounds, managing asset libraries, communicating delivery specs, chasing invoices — is a constant drain that pulls your attention away from the creative work that actually pays and that your clients actually hired you to do.

🗂️

Revision rounds tracked in your head

Client feedback arriving simultaneously via Slack message, email thread, voice note, and Frame.io comment with no consolidated log of what version you are on, what was changed in round two versus round three, or whether the current round exceeds what was included in scope. By round four you are working from memory and hoping the client is too.

📁

Asset chaos across drives and folders

Footage, music, SFX, motion graphics templates, and project files named inconsistently across multiple drives with no master record of where anything lives. When a client comes back six months later asking for a re-edit or a new cut from the same raw footage, you spend forty minutes just locating the project before you can start the work.

📅

Deadlines managed by calendar notifications alone

No single view of all active projects, their current edit stage, and their delivery deadlines in one place. When you have five projects on the go simultaneously, calendar reminders are not enough — you need to see the entire pipeline at a glance to know what needs attention today and what has breathing room until next week.

💸

Invoices sent late because billing is an afterthought

You finish the project, deliver the final files, and then spend an hour reconstructing what was in scope, whether any revision rounds exceeded the agreed number, what the delivery add-ons were, and what your original quote said before you can issue an invoice. Late invoicing compounds into late payment and unpredictable cash flow.

Project Pipeline — Brief → Edit → Review → Revisions → Delivery → Invoiced

A master project database that tracks every engagement from the moment a brief lands to the moment an invoice is paid, with clearly defined pipeline stages you move each project through: Brief Received, Pre-Production, First Edit, In Review, Revisions, Final Delivery, and Invoiced. Each project record holds the client name, project type (commercial, social media, documentary, wedding, corporate, music video), agreed fee, delivery deadline, current stage, and a running log of notes. Filter by stage to see everything currently in revision, sort by deadline to identify what ships this week, and link directly to your revision tracker, deliverable checklist, and asset inventory from the same record so every piece of project information is one click away. Revision Round Tracker — Client Feedback Log, Version Notes & Round Counter

A structured revision log for every project that records each round of client feedback with the round number, the date feedback was received, the channel it arrived through, a detailed summary of requested changes, the version file name you worked from, the date the revised cut was delivered back to the client, and a field for any notes on scope that may affect billing. The round counter makes it immediately visible whether a project is within the revision rounds included in your scope or has exceeded them — a common source of unpaid extra work for freelance editors who do not track this systematically. When a client says "I thought we were only on round two" and you have a time-stamped record showing round five was delivered last Tuesday with a full log of what changed in each round, the conversation resolves quickly and in your favour. Linking the revision tracker to your project pipeline record means the full revision history for any project is accessible in seconds.

Asset & Footage Inventory — Organised by Project, Client, Resolution & Status

A master asset database that tracks every file associated with every project — raw footage, exported cuts, graphics, motion templates, sound effects, licensed music, client-supplied brand assets, colour-graded exports, and project files — with the asset name, file type, resolution or format (4K ProRes, H.264 1080p, vertical 9:16), the project it belongs to, its storage location (drive name and folder path), file size, version or date created, and status (Active, Archived, Delivered, or Licensed). Unlike a folder structure that only makes sense to the person who created it, a Notion asset inventory is searchable, filterable, and linkable — you can filter all assets for a specific client, find every delivered 4K master across all projects, or check the status of a licensed music file without opening a single folder. When a client returns months later asking for a new cut from old footage, you locate everything in under a minute rather than spending the first hour of the job just finding the files.

Stock Music & Sound FX Library Database — Source, License Type, Genre & Project Use

A curated database of every music track and sound effect you have licensed or downloaded for professional use, organised by title, artist or source platform (Epidemic Sound, Artlist, Musicbed, Pond5, Freesound), genre, mood, BPM, license type (single-project, unlimited, subscription), whether the license covers commercial broadcast use, and which projects the track has been used in. Music licensing compliance is a real professional and legal risk for editors working on commercial projects — having a clear record of where every track came from, what the license covers, and which clients have used it protects you and your clients from copyright disputes. The database also functions as a creative reference library: when you need upbeat corporate background music for a new project, you can search your own curated collection first rather than spending an hour browsing subscription platforms from scratch every time.

Client Deliverable Checklist — Formats, Specs, Delivery Method & Sign-Off Log

A structured checklist for every deliverable agreed in a project's scope, with a separate record for each format: the H.264 master for the client, the ProRes version for broadcast, the vertical 9:16 cut for social media, the 15-second version for paid advertising, the caption file, the thumbnail, the project file archive. Each deliverable record captures the exact technical specifications (resolution, frame rate, codec, audio standard, colour space), the delivery method (WeTransfer link, Dropbox upload, Frame.io, physical drive), the agreed delivery date, the date it was actually delivered, and a sign-off field where you log the client's approval confirmation. Completing the deliverable checklist before invoicing means you can confirm with certainty that everything in scope was delivered, every format is accounted for, and every sign-off is on record — so the invoice goes out immediately after delivery rather than a week later when you finally find time to figure out what to charge.

Rate Card & Package Pricing Template — Hourly, Day Rate, Package & Rush Rates

A complete pricing reference that documents every rate and package in your service offering — your day rate for on-location work, your per-minute editing rate, your fixed-price packages for common project types (social media reels package, corporate video package, wedding highlight film, music video), your rush rate multiplier for same-week turnaround, and any additional line items you charge for such as colour grading, sound design, motion graphics, or subtitle creation. Each entry includes the rate, what is included in the price, what is explicitly out of scope, the number of revision rounds included, and your standard payment terms. Having your rate card documented in Notion means you never have to calculate a quote from scratch — you reference your existing packages, adjust for project scope, and send a quote that is consistent with what you have charged before. It also protects you from the common freelancer mistake of underpricing a complex project because you forgot to include all the line items that you are actually going to deliver.

Client Portal Workspace — Share Links, Approval Notes & Feedback History by Project

A structured client-facing workspace template for each active project that you can share with clients via Notion's public or permission-based sharing — showing the current project status, the current review link (Frame.io, Vimeo review, YouTube unlisted), the revision round they are currently on, upcoming delivery milestones, and a structured feedback form embedded in the page for organised responses rather than unstructured Slack messages. The backend of each client portal record stores the full approval log — every version shared, every piece of feedback received in writing, every approval confirmation — giving you a complete paper trail of the decision-making history for every project. Clients who can see their project's status without sending you a check-in message become more confident and easier to work with, and you arrive at every client call knowing exactly where the project stands rather than scrambling to locate the last email chain before the meeting starts.

Software & Plugin Subscription Tracker — Tools, Renewal Dates & Cost per Month

A complete log of every software subscription and plugin in your creative stack — Adobe Creative Cloud, DaVinci Resolve Studio, Final Cut Pro, After Effects plugins (Motion Bro, Video Copilot Element 3D, Film Impact, BRAW Toolbox), LUT packs, stock footage platforms (Artgrid, Storyblocks), stock music subscriptions (Epidemic Sound, Artlist, Musicbed), cloud storage (Backblaze, Google One, Dropbox), Frame.io, collaboration and review tools, and any other recurring professional expense. Each record captures the tool name, category, monthly and annual cost, billing cycle, renewal date, the card it is billed to, and a status field indicating whether the subscription is actively essential to your current projects or could be paused or cancelled without impact to your workflow. A freelance video editor's subscription stack grows incrementally and silently — a quarterly review of this tracker regularly surfaces four-figure annual savings in tools that seemed essential when purchased but have since been replaced, superseded, or simply forgotten about while still charging your card every month.

Frequently Asked Questions

Questions From Video Editors & Post-Production Professionals

How do video editors use Notion to manage projects?

Video editors use Notion to centralise the entire project lifecycle in one place — from the initial client brief through pre-production, editing, revision rounds, final delivery, and invoicing. Instead of tracking active projects across spreadsheets, calendar reminders, and memory, a Notion project pipeline gives you a single database where every project has a status, a deadline, a current stage, and all relevant notes in one record. You can filter by client, sort by delivery date, and see at a glance exactly what needs attention today versus what is on track. The relational structure means your projects link directly to your client records, your asset inventory, your deliverable checklists, and your revision logs so that every piece of information about a project is connected and findable in seconds without switching between tools.

Can Notion help freelance video editors track revision rounds?

Yes — and revision tracking is one of the most practically valuable things a freelance video editor can implement in Notion. The revision round tracker logs every round of client feedback with the round number, the date feedback was received, the channel it arrived through (email, Slack, Frame.io, voice note), a summary of the changes requested, which version you worked from, and the date the revised cut was delivered back to the client. Having this log on record means you know instantly what round you are on, what changed in each round, and whether any revision request exceeds the number of rounds included in your original scope agreement. It also protects you in scope disputes — you can show precisely what was requested and what was delivered at every stage of the project, with timestamps, which makes conversations about additional fees significantly more straightforward.

How do I organise footage and assets in Notion?

The asset and footage inventory in Notion works as a master database for everything associated with a project — raw footage files, exported cuts, graphics, motion templates, SFX, licensed music, client-supplied brand assets, and project files. Each record captures the asset name, file type, resolution or format, the project it belongs to, the storage location (drive name and folder path), version or date, and status (in use, archived, delivered, or licensed). The key advantage over a simple folder structure is that Notion lets you filter across all assets by project, by client, by format, or by status — so when a client comes back eighteen months later asking for a re-edit, you can locate every asset associated with their project in seconds rather than digging through drives hoping the folder names still make sense to you.

What is the best way to manage client deliverables in Notion?

The client deliverable checklist approach in Notion works by creating a structured record for every deliverable agreed in the project scope — the format (H.264 MP4, ProRes, vertical cut for social, caption file, thumbnail), the exact technical specifications (resolution, frame rate, codec, audio standard), the delivery method (WeTransfer, Dropbox, Frame.io, hard drive), the delivery deadline, and a sign-off field where you log the date the client confirmed receipt and approval. This prevents the common problem of finishing a project and then realising you owe the client three social media cuts and a podcast audiogram that were mentioned in the original brief but never formally tracked anywhere. Checking off deliverables against a structured list also makes invoicing significantly easier — you can confirm everything in scope was delivered before you issue the invoice rather than trying to reconstruct the scope from memory or email threads.

Can I use Notion as a client portal for video editing clients?

Yes. Notion's sharing functionality allows you to create a read-only or comment-enabled page for each client that shows the current project status, the revision round history, upcoming delivery dates, and any shared review links. The client portal workspace in FreelanceOS Pro gives you a structured template for each client relationship — a project overview, share links to review cuts (Frame.io, Vimeo review links), a log of approval notes and feedback received, and the history of all rounds submitted and approved. It positions you as an organised professional who communicates proactively rather than requiring clients to chase you for updates, and it significantly reduces the volume of "just checking in — where are we up to?" messages you receive mid-project, which interrupt editing sessions and pull you out of deep creative work.

How do I track my software subscriptions as a video editor?

The software and plugin subscription tracker logs every tool in your creative stack — Adobe Creative Cloud, DaVinci Resolve Studio, Final Cut Pro, After Effects plugins (Motion Bro, Video Copilot, Film Impact), LUTs and preset packs, stock footage subscriptions (Artgrid, Storyblocks), stock music platforms (Epidemic Sound, Artlist), cloud storage, collaboration tools, and any other recurring professional expense. Each record captures the tool name, category, monthly or annual cost, renewal date, and whether the subscription is actively essential to your current workflow. Reviewing this database quarterly lets you identify subscriptions you have outgrown, tools you are duplicating, and costs you can renegotiate or cancel — a discipline that becomes more financially significant the longer your subscription stack grows and the more silently those charges accumulate on your card.

What Notion views work best for video editing project management?

For video editing project management, the most useful Notion views are Board view (to see all projects by stage — Brief, Pre-Production, Editing, Revision, Delivery, Invoiced), Timeline or Calendar view (to visualise delivery deadlines across your active roster and spot capacity conflicts before they become emergencies), and Table view with filters (to quickly see all projects for a specific client, all projects currently at the Revision stage, or all projects with deliverables due this week). The Board view is especially effective during busy periods — moving a project card from Editing to Revision to Delivered gives you a tactile, at-a-glance picture of where everything stands without needing to open individual records or check a calendar. Timeline view becomes critical when you are managing four or five concurrent projects and need to verify that you have not accidentally committed to three final deliveries in the same two-day window.

How do I invoice clients faster as a freelance video editor using Notion?

Faster invoicing as a video editor starts with capturing everything at the start of a project rather than reconstructing it at the end. The FreelanceOS Pro project pipeline links your project record to your rate card (so your agreed fee is always visible), your deliverable checklist (so you can confirm everything in scope was delivered before invoicing), and your revision round tracker (so you can identify if any revision rounds exceeded your included scope and need to be billed as extras). When the project reaches the Delivered stage, you open the project record, confirm all deliverables are checked off, check the revision round count against your original agreement, and your complete invoice data is right there — client name, project name, agreed fee, add-on revision fees, and payment terms. No hour spent reconstructing scope from Slack history and email threads before you can send the invoice; no delay between delivery and billing that stretches your cash flow unnecessarily.
