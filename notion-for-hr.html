Overview

Why HR Teams Choose Notion in 2026

Dedicated HRIS platforms — BambooHR, Workday, HiBob — are purpose-built for HR and genuinely excellent at what they do. They are also expensive and often overkill for small teams. At $6–20 per employee per month, a 20-person company is paying $1,440–$4,800 per year for HR software before adding payroll, benefits, or ATS costs. Notion has emerged as the practical alternative for People Ops at startups and small businesses (10–200 employees) who need structured HR management without the full enterprise software price tag.

Notion's key strengths for HR: an employee directory that gets built and actually used (because adding a new page is zero friction), onboarding flows that new hires complete inside the platform from day one, performance review templates that live alongside the goals they are evaluating, and a job pipeline the entire recruiting team accesses without needing a separate ATS subscription.

Important caveat: Notion is NOT a replacement for a payroll system (use Gusto, Rippling, or ADP), benefits administration platform, or HRIS for legal compliance at scale. It is not appropriate for storing SSNs, medical information, I-9 documents, immigration records, or detailed legal/disciplinary documentation. Notion excels as the knowledge and process layer — sitting alongside the dedicated compliance tools your company needs.

Setup Guide

Building Your Notion HR OS: 8 Core Databases

Follow this sequence to build a complete HR system in Notion. Start with the Employee Directory — it takes one hour to populate and delivers value immediately.

1

Employee Directory

Properties: Name, role, department, manager (relation to same database — enables org chart view), start date, location/remote status, LinkedIn, Slack handle, birthday, work anniversary, direct reports count, emergency contact flag (flag only — keep actual contact info elsewhere). Filtered views: by department, by manager, by remote vs. office. Gallery view creates a visual org chart. This is your living team directory — HR and all managers should have edit access. Keep sensitive personal data (home address, emergency contact details, salary) out of Notion.

2

Onboarding Tracker

Properties: New hire name (linked to Employee Directory), start date, buddy assigned, equipment ordered (yes/no), system access checklist (each access item as a checkbox property — email, Slack, GitHub, Notion, Jira, etc.), day 1 agenda linked, week 1 schedule linked, 30-day check-in scheduled (yes/no with date), 60-day check-in, 90-day review. Each onboarding entry links to a role-specific onboarding page with the full checklist. Create a filtered view for HR showing all active onboarding in progress. Duplicate the onboarding template for each new hire and assign their buddy on Day 1.

3

Job Requisition Tracker

Properties: Role title, department, hiring manager (linked to Employee Directory), headcount budget approved (yes/no), job description written (yes/no), JD posted where (multi-select: LinkedIn / Indeed / Greenhouse / AngelList / direct), applications received (number), interview stage (select: JD drafting / sourcing / phone screen / technical / panel / offer / closed), final candidates count, decision date, offer extended (yes/no), accepted or declined, start date planned. The master recruiting dashboard — HR and hiring managers see every open role simultaneously with current stage and velocity.

4

Candidate Pipeline (Per Requisition)

Properties: Candidate name, role (linked to Req Tracker), source (LinkedIn / referral / AngelList / direct apply / recruiter / agency), resume link, phone screen done (yes/no + notes), technical/skills assessment (yes/no + result), hiring manager interview (yes/no + notes), panel interview (yes/no + scorecard link), references checked (yes/no + notes), offer stage (select: not offered / verbal offer / written offer / negotiating / accepted / declined), decision and date. Kanban view by interview stage gives a visual pipeline view. For companies doing under 20 hires per year, this replaces a full ATS at a fraction of the cost.

5

Performance Review System

Properties: Employee (linked to Directory), review period (H1 2026 / H2 2026 / Annual 2026), self-assessment status (not started / in progress / submitted), manager assessment status, calibration status (draft / calibrated / approved), overall rating (select: exceeds expectations / meets expectations / needs improvement / does not meet), promotion consideration (yes/no + details), compensation change recommendation, review meeting scheduled (date), review signed off (yes/no). Filtered view for managers shows only their direct reports' reviews. HR sees all reviews in one calibration view sorted by department and rating for compensation planning.

6

Goals & OKR Tracker

Properties: Employee (linked to Directory), goal title, category (individual / team / company), owner, quarter target (Q1/Q2/Q3/Q4 + year), key results (nested or sub-properties), current progress (0–100%), status (on track / at risk / achieved / missed / deferred), linked to company OKR (relation to a company OKR database if applicable), notes. Views: per employee (used in 1:1 meetings), per department (team OKR dashboard), all at-risk goals (for manager and HR check-ins). This database connects directly to the Performance Review System — reviews reference the goal data rather than requiring employees to recreate it from memory.

7

PTO & Leave Log

Properties: Employee (linked to Directory), leave type (select: PTO / sick leave / parental leave / unpaid / bereavement / jury duty / military leave), start date, end date, total days (formula), approval status (pending / approved / denied), manager, notes. Create per-employee views that managers can filter to see only their team. Annual summary view shows total days taken per employee per leave type — useful for identifying patterns and ensuring policy compliance. Note: once your HRIS handles PTO tracking automatically, this Notion log becomes redundant and can be retired. It solves the problem of the first 1–3 years before an HRIS is warranted.

8

Team Policies & FAQ Wiki

Properties: Policy name, category (select: compensation / benefits / remote work / performance / conduct / safety / data & security / hiring), version number, effective date, approved by, last reviewed date, next review date, link to full policy document. The searchable policy library that replaces the "what's our PTO policy again?" Slack message. Each policy page includes the full policy text (or a link to it in Google Drive if it is a PDF), a summary section, an FAQ, and a changelog. New employees are linked to this wiki in their onboarding page. Managers have edit access; all employees have read access.

By Company Size

Notion for HR by Company Size

The right Notion HR setup scales with your company — from founder-managed people ops to a full HR team.

🚀 Early Stage Startup (5–25 employees)

Minimal HRIS budget, founder or first People Ops hire wearing all hats. Notion as the entire HR system: employee directory, onboarding checklists, job pipeline, and team wiki. Pair with Gusto for payroll (Gusto is designed specifically for small businesses at ~$40/month base + per-employee).

At this stage, Notion saves $200–500/month vs. BambooHR while providing 80% of the functionality. The trade-off: manual processes where BambooHR would automate (e-signatures, automated PTO balances, offer letter generation). The calculation is straightforward: at 15 employees, BambooHR costs ~$1,800–2,700/year. Notion is $0 extra if you already have a team plan.

🏧 Growth Stage Startup (25–100 employees)

Notion for team wikis, onboarding content, performance reviews, and documentation. Add BambooHR or Rippling for HRIS/payroll/benefits as headcount grows and compliance complexity increases — typically around the 30–50 employee threshold when electronic I-9s, ACA compliance tracking, and state-by-state labor law management become genuinely complex.

Notion remains the "people knowledge base" alongside the HRIS: new manager resources, team wikis, HR policy library, and onboarding content. The two systems are complementary rather than competing — HRIS for compliance and data of record, Notion for knowledge and process.

🏢 Mid-Size Company (100–500 employees)

Notion supplements the HRIS for the content it does not handle well: new manager training resources, department wikis, HR policy library and handbook, onboarding page content that new hires read (not the checklists a dedicated HRIS manages). The compliance and payroll functions belong in the HRIS — Notion is the knowledge layer on top.

HR teams at this size often use Notion for: documenting HR processes and SOPs, building manager training content, maintaining the internal HR FAQ, and collaborating on policy drafts before they move to the official HRIS policy library or a legal review process. It is the workspace for HR's own work, not the system of record for employee data.

Tool Comparison

Notion for HR vs Alternatives

Notion vs BambooHR

BambooHR is purpose-built HRIS with electronic signatures, benefits administration, ATS, payroll (US), reporting dashboards, and compliance features. At roughly $7–12/employee/month, it becomes cost-competitive with Notion at larger team sizes — and provides significantly more automation. For companies with 25+ employees, BambooHR or an equivalent HRIS is the right call for compliance-sensitive HR functions. Notion complements BambooHR as the wiki, onboarding content, and policy library layer rather than replacing it.

Notion vs Rippling

Rippling is a comprehensive HR, IT, and payroll platform for hypergrowth companies — handling device management, app provisioning, global payroll, and benefits alongside HR. At $8+/employee/month minimum with modules stacking up quickly, it is expensive but genuinely powerful. Notion is not a substitute — it is a knowledge layer that could sit on top of Rippling. Companies that can afford Rippling generally should use it for its automation value; Notion fills the documentation and process gaps.

Notion vs Lattice (Performance Management)

Lattice specializes in OKRs, continuous feedback loops, performance reviews, and manager/employee-facing review UX with strong integration to HRIS platforms. At $11+/person/month for the performance management module, it is purpose-built for companies that take structured performance management seriously. Notion's performance review system works well for companies doing semi-annual or annual reviews up to roughly 50 employees. Beyond that scale, or for companies wanting continuous feedback cycles and detailed performance analytics, Lattice is the more appropriate tool.

Notion vs Lever/Greenhouse (ATS)

Lever and Greenhouse are enterprise ATS platforms with sourcing integrations, compliance tracking, offer letter management, structured interview kits, and deep recruiting workflow automation. They are appropriate for companies hiring at volume — dozens to hundreds of roles per year. Notion's candidate pipeline works for startups hiring a handful of roles at a time — 2–15 hires per year — where the overhead of an enterprise ATS is not justified. The break-even point is roughly 20+ hires per year, at which point ATS automation pays for itself in recruiter time saved.

Notion vs Confluence (Team Wiki)

HR teams at larger companies sometimes use Confluence for HR knowledge bases and policy libraries, particularly if they are in an Atlassian-heavy engineering environment. Notion is more modern, easier to use for non-technical HR professionals, has better database functionality, and is significantly cheaper for small HR teams. For a People Ops team of 1–5 people who need an internal wiki, Notion is almost always the better choice over Confluence.

Notion vs Google Docs + Sheets (The Typical Small Business HR Stack)

Most small businesses manage HR in a Google Drive folder full of documents and spreadsheets — a "new hire checklist v3 FINAL.docx," a "team directory 2026.xlsx," an "open roles Feb 2026.gsheet," and a policy document folder organized by whoever set it up five years ago. Notion consolidates this into a connected, searchable, permission-controlled system where the employee directory links to onboarding checklists, which link to the job description that was used to hire the person, which links to their first performance review. Every piece of information is one click from every related piece of information.

Data Sensitivity

What About Sensitive Employee Data?

Notion is NOT appropriate for storing:

  • Social Security Numbers or national ID numbers
  • Salary data beyond general compensation bands
  • Medical information, health records, or accommodation details
  • I-9 employment eligibility verification documents
  • Immigration records or visa documentation
  • Detailed performance documentation in legal or disciplinary contexts
  • Background check results or criminal record information

Use Notion for: Team directory (non-sensitive professional info), onboarding checklists and schedules, job requisition pipeline and candidate notes (non-offer stage), performance goals and development discussions (general), team policies, handbook content, and manager training resources. For sensitive employment records, use your HRIS system with appropriate access controls, data processing agreements, and compliance features. Notion's permission settings can restrict database views by team or individual, but it is not designed as a system of record for protected employment data at scale.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Notion be used as an HRIS?
Notion functions as a lightweight HRIS for small companies (up to 25–50 employees) handling employee directory, onboarding tracking, job pipeline, performance goals, and HR policy library. It is NOT appropriate as a system of record for payroll, benefits administration, legal compliance documentation, I-9s, SSNs, or sensitive medical information. For those functions, use a purpose-built HRIS. Notion works best as the knowledge and process layer alongside a dedicated payroll and benefits platform like Gusto or Rippling.
Can Notion replace BambooHR for small companies?
For early-stage startups with under 25 employees and a limited HR budget, Notion handles most of what BambooHR provides: employee directory, onboarding checklists, PTO tracking, job pipeline, and HR policy library. At $7–12/employee/month, BambooHR's cost is significant for a 10-person company. Many startups use Notion for HR in their first 2–3 years, then migrate to BambooHR or Rippling as they scale past 25–50 employees and compliance complexity increases. For payroll, Gusto complements Notion well at this size.
How do HR teams use Notion?
HR teams use Notion for: employee directory with org chart, onboarding checklists and 30-60-90 day plans, job requisition and candidate pipeline tracking, performance review templates and goal tracking, PTO and leave request logs, HR policy library and employee handbook, and manager training and development resources. Notion excels as the knowledge and process layer — where documents, checklists, and databases live together and link to each other without the friction of switching between multiple tools.
What HR databases should I build in Notion?
The 8 core HR databases: (1) Employee Directory, (2) Onboarding Tracker, (3) Job Requisition Tracker, (4) Candidate Pipeline, (5) Performance Review System, (6) Goals and OKR Tracker, (7) PTO and Leave Log, (8) Team Policies and FAQ Wiki. Then build the Onboarding Tracker, which delivers value as soon as your next hire starts.
Is Notion suitable for storing employee data?
Notion is suitable for non-sensitive employee data: professional directory information, onboarding checklists, job pipeline notes, performance goals, and HR policies. It is NOT appropriate for: Social Security Numbers, salary data beyond general bands, medical information, I-9 documents, immigration records, or detailed legal/disciplinary documentation. For sensitive employment records, use your HRIS with appropriate access controls. Notion's permission settings can restrict database views, but it is not designed as a compliance-grade system of record for protected employment data.

In Week 3, set up the Job Requisition Tracker if you have active hiring. Add the Performance Review System before your next review cycle. Build the PTO Log and Policies Wiki as bandwidth allows. Start with the databases that solve your current pain point, not everything at once.
