The Problem

Commercial Drone Operations Require Compliance Documentation, Equipment Management, and Client Project Tracking Simultaneously

FAA Part 107 pilots manage regulatory compliance, equipment maintenance, airspace authorizations, client deliverables, and business financials across every shoot. Here is what the unmanaged version looks like.

📋

Flight records scattered or missing

You flew fourteen commercial shoots last month across real estate, construction, and events. Your flight records are a mix of handwritten notes, app-generated logs from your DJI fly app, and a few emails with location details. If the FAA asked to review your flight records or an incident required documentation, reconstructing a coherent record would take hours — and might still have gaps.

🔋

Battery cycles tracked nowhere

You have eight LiPo batteries across two aircraft. You know roughly which ones are newer but you have no documented cycle count for any of them. A battery approaching 300 cycles and starting to show degraded performance is a field safety risk — but without documented tracking, you discover it when it delivers unexpected short flight times mid-shoot rather than in a review that would have caught it weeks earlier.

🏛️

FAA compliance deadlines missed

Part 107 recurrent knowledge test: required every 24 calendar months. If you earned your certificate in April 2024, your recurrent test deadline is April 2026. If you miss it, every commercial flight you take after that date is operating without a valid certificate — creating both FAA enforcement exposure and insurance coverage questions. This deadline should be in your calendar with a 90-day alert. Is it?

📦

Post-production and delivery untracked

A real estate client wants to know when their edited aerial footage will be delivered. You shot it Tuesday, started editing Wednesday, and think you sent the Drive link Thursday — but you're not sure if they confirmed receipt or downloaded it before the link expired. Without a delivery log, client delivery status lives in your memory and your email inbox, neither of which is reliable under a full shoot schedule.

Frequently Asked Questions

Questions From Commercial Drone Pilots & UAV Operators

How does the flight log support FAA Part 107 compliance requirements?

FAA Part 107 does not mandate a specific flight log format but the regulations create practical documentation requirements that commercial operators should maintain: flight records that support airspace authorization documentation, incident reporting, and certificate audit defense. The flight log records date, location (GPS coordinates and street address), aircraft used and its FAA registration number, flight purpose, altitude flown in feet AGL, weather conditions at time of flight (wind speed, visibility, cloud cover), whether airspace authorization was required, LAANC authorization reference if obtained, any waivers active for the flight, flight duration in minutes, batteries used and their serial numbers, and any incidents or anomalies. This record serves multiple compliance functions: it documents that you operated within authorized altitudes and airspace, it creates the factual basis for any incident report the FAA might request, and it builds the flight history that demonstrates professional operation if your certificate is ever reviewed. The airspace authorization fields are particularly important for operators who regularly fly near airports — maintaining a clean LAANC authorization record linked to individual flight log entries is the documentation standard that commercial operators should maintain regardless of formal requirement.

How do I track battery health and know when to retire a battery?

The battery tracker records each battery's serial number, cycle count (updated after each flight with the number of batteries used), storage charge status (batteries stored at partial charge last longer than batteries stored fully charged or fully depleted), date of purchase, and a retirement flag triggered when the battery reaches the cycle threshold you set — typically 300 cycles for most DJI batteries, though some operators use lower thresholds based on manufacturer guidance and observed performance. The cycle count is the primary retirement indicator: a battery approaching or past its cycle threshold may charge to reported full capacity but deliver reduced flight time or behave unpredictably under load, which is a safety risk in commercial operation. The swelling detection note field captures physical inspection findings — a battery showing any swelling should be retired immediately regardless of cycle count. For operators running a fleet of six to twelve batteries across two or three aircraft, the tracker gives you a dashboard view of your full battery fleet's health rather than tracking cycle counts per battery in your head or in a separate spreadsheet.

How does the FAA Part 107 compliance tracker help me stay current with certificate requirements?

The Part 107 compliance tracker records your certificate number, certificate issue date, and critically the recurrent knowledge test deadline — Part 107 certificates require a recurrent aeronautical knowledge and safety test every 24 calendar months, and the penalty for operating on an expired certificate is operating without a valid remote pilot certificate, which creates both regulatory and insurance exposure. The tracker sets the renewal deadline prominently so it surfaces in your monthly review rather than surprising you when a client asks to verify your certificate status before a commercial shoot. Beyond the certificate, the tracker logs active COAs, LAANC authorization history organized by location, waivers held with their scope and expiry (night operations, beyond visual line of sight, operations over people — each with its own renewal requirements), insurance policy details (carrier, coverage amount, policy number, expiry date), and incident report filings.

How does the client project database handle different service types like real estate versus construction monitoring?

The client project database is structured around deliverable type rather than a one-size approach, because a real estate aerial photography engagement and a construction progress monitoring contract have fundamentally different operational and delivery requirements. The project record captures the service type, the specific deliverables contracted (edited video, raw footage, still photos, orthomosaic map, thermal imagery — these vary significantly by service type), resolution and codec specifications, file delivery method, and whether this is a retainer or one-off engagement. For construction monitoring contracts, the recurring nature means each monthly flight generates a new entry linked to the same client record, building a sequential progress photography archive that the general contractor can reference against project timeline. The retainer flag separates these workflows visually, so your monthly retainer clients appear as a distinct category — clients where the monthly invoice should generate automatically rather than requiring a new scope conversation each time.

How does the post-production delivery log handle large raw footage volumes?

Commercial drone footage generates substantial raw data volumes — a single real estate shoot can generate 30 to 80 GB of raw footage, a film production day substantially more. The delivery log records per project: total raw footage size in GB, delivery files created (edited video, color-graded final, still selections), editing software and preset applied, output resolution and codec, delivery platform (Google Drive folder link, Dropbox, WeTransfer download link, or physical hard drive), delivery date, client approval date, and archiving status after the delivery period closes. The archiving status field addresses one of the practical storage management challenges for high-volume drone operators: raw footage from completed projects should not live on primary storage indefinitely, but archiving too aggressively creates problems if a client returns requesting additional edits. Tracking archiving status — Raw Retained, Archived to Cold Storage, Deleted After Client Approval — gives you a documented policy on raw footage retention that you can communicate to clients at project inception and enforce consistently.
