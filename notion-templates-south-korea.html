Freelancing in South Korea: The 2026 Landscape for 한국 프리랜서

South Korea is one of the world's most connected and technologically advanced economies, with near-universal internet penetration, world-leading 5G infrastructure, and a highly educated, digitally fluent population. Seoul in particular is a global technology hub: home to Samsung, LG, Hyundai, Kakao, Naver, and Krafton (PUBG), as well as a thriving startup ecosystem in Gangnam, Pangyo Techno Valley (often called Korea's Silicon Valley), and the Mapo-gu creative district.

Korea's Freelance Platform Ecosystem

Korean freelancers operate in a unique market where domestic platforms provide structured marketplace infrastructure for services ranging from web development to translation to graphic design, while internationally skilled Korean professionals increasingly work directly with US, European, and Southeast Asian clients through global platforms:

  • Kmong (크몽) — Korea's leading freelance marketplace, covering design, development, marketing, writing, and consulting; similar to Fiverr in structure
  • Wishket (위시켓) — IT and software development project marketplace; higher-value technical projects
  • Rallit — tech talent platform for engineers and developers targeting quality tech projects
  • Freemoa — design and creative services marketplace
  • Saramin — primarily recruitment but has freelance project listings for professionals
  • Upwork & Toptal — used by Korea-based freelancers targeting international clients for higher USD-denominated rates

Key Cities and Hubs for Korean Freelancers

Seoul dominates the Korean freelance market — the majority of high-paying clients, agencies, and tech companies are headquartered in Gangnam, Mapo, Jongno, and Pangyo. Pangyo Techno Valley (판교 테크노밸리) in Seongnam-si is home to NCSoft, Kakao, Krafton, and hundreds of tech startups — a key source of IT freelance projects. Busan has a growing creative and digital content ecosystem. Jeju Island has attracted digital nomads and remote workers due to its nature, lower costs, and improving infrastructure.

The 3.3% Withholding Tax System

One of the most distinctive features of Korean freelance taxation is the 3.3% withholding system. When a Korean company (법인 or 개인사업자) pays a freelancer for business income, it is required by law to withhold 3.3% of the gross payment before remitting the balance. This consists of 3.0% income tax (소득세) and 0.3% local income tax (지방소득세, essentially a resident surtax). The company then issues a withholding tax receipt (원천징수영수증) to the freelancer.

At year end, the freelancer files a comprehensive income tax return (종합소득세 신고) in May, covering all income sources. The actual tax rate depends on total taxable income — Korea uses a progressive income tax rate system from 6% to 45%. Business expenses (필요경비) are deductible from income. If actual tax liability is lower than total withheld amounts, the NTS refunds the excess. This makes the May filing a potential source of a meaningful refund for many freelancers.

(If you are freelancing in South Korea, 3.3% will be withheld from payments by Korean clients. File a comprehensive income tax return every May to reconcile your actual tax liability and reclaim any overpaid withholding tax.)

Business Registration as 개인사업자

While some freelancers operate as unregistered individuals (미등록 프리랜서) for small-scale or occasional work, many choose to register as a sole proprietor (개인사업자) with the National Tax Service via Hometax. Registration gives access to meaningful advantages: the ability to issue proper tax invoices (세금계산서) to corporate clients, eligibility for the simplified VAT scheme if below KRW 48 million revenue, deduction of business expenses (필요경비) against income, and a professional business registration number (사업자등록번호). Common deductible expenses for Korean freelancers include home office costs, equipment, software subscriptions, professional development courses, internet, and business communication costs.

4대보험: Social Insurance for Korean Freelancers

Korea's four major social insurances (4대보험) cover National Health Insurance (국민건강보험 — NHI), National Pension (국민연금 — NP), Employment Insurance (고용보험 — EI), and Industrial Accident Insurance (산재보험 — IAI). Self-employed freelancers enroll in NHI as local subscribers (지역가입자), with premiums calculated based on income AND assets including real estate and vehicles — meaning Korean freelancers who own property may have higher health insurance premiums than they expect. National Pension contributions for self-employed individuals are approximately 9% of income (split differently than for employees). Employment Insurance and Industrial Accident Insurance are generally optional for self-employed freelancers unless they opt in voluntarily.

Korea's NTS Hometax system (hometax.go.kr) is one of the most digitally advanced tax filing platforms in the world. The NTS pre-fills much of the 종합소득세 return using withholding receipts submitted by your clients — making May filing significantly easier when your Notion income records match the NTS pre-filled data.

Frequently Asked Questions: Notion for Korean Freelancers

How do I register as a freelancer in South Korea?
Most Korean freelancers operate either as an unregistered individual (개인) for small-scale work or register as a sole proprietor (개인사업자) with the National Tax Service (NTS) via Hometax (hometax.go.kr). Registration as an individual business owner gives access to expense deductions and simplifies VAT compliance. Many freelancers also obtain a business registration number (사업자등록번호) to issue proper tax invoices (세금계산서) to corporate clients. Registration can be completed online via the NTS Hometax portal or in person at a local tax office.
What is the 3.3% withholding tax in Korea?
When a Korean company pays a freelancer for business income, it is required by law to withhold 3.3% of the gross payment before remitting the balance. This consists of 3.0% income tax (소득세) and 0.3% local income tax (지방소득세 — resident surtax). The company then issues a withholding tax receipt (원천징수영수증) to the freelancer. At year-end, the freelancer files a comprehensive income tax return (종합소득세) in May, reconciling all income and the withheld amounts against the actual tax liability. If actual tax is lower than what was withheld, the NTS refunds the excess.
How do Korean freelancers file taxes?
Korean freelancers file a comprehensive income tax return (종합소득세 신고) every May 1-31 via the NTS Hometax website (hometax.go.kr) or the Hometax mobile app. The return covers all income sources including freelance income, interest, rental income, etc. Business income earners deduct allowable business expenses (필요경비) to reduce taxable income. The NTS pre-fills much of the return with data from withholding receipts submitted by clients throughout the year, making filing significantly easier when your own income records are well-organized.
Do I need to register for VAT in South Korea?
Korean VAT (부가가치세, 부가세) is 10% standard rate. Freelancers below KRW 48 million annual revenue may qualify as simplified taxable persons (간이과세자) with reduced VAT obligations and simplified quarterly returns. Above KRW 48 million, you become a general taxable person (일반과세자) required to file full quarterly VAT returns in January, April, July, and October via Hometax. Most freelancers starting out qualify for simplified status and upgrade as their business grows.
What is the National Health Insurance situation for Korean freelancers?
Self-employed Korean freelancers participate in the National Health Insurance system (국민건강보험) as local subscribers (지역가입자) rather than as employees. Premiums are calculated based on income plus assets — including property and vehicles — which can make health insurance premiums a significant and sometimes surprising cost for freelancers who own property. National Pension (국민연금) contributions are also payable by self-employed individuals, typically around 9% of declared income. Both NHI and NP premiums are trackable as deductible expenses in your 종합소득세 return.
What are the best freelance platforms in South Korea?
The leading domestic freelance platforms in South Korea are Kmong (크몽) — the largest general freelance marketplace similar to Fiverr — and Wishket (위시켓), which focuses on IT and software development projects. Other platforms include Rallit for tech talent, Freemoa for design and creative work, and Saramin for professional services. Korean freelancers targeting international clients commonly use Upwork, Toptal, and Fiverr alongside direct LinkedIn outreach and referral networks.
