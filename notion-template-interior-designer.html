It rarely teaches solo practitioners and small-studio designers how to simultaneously manage multiple live projects with divergent timelines, track vendor lead times across dozens of trade relationships, collect client approvals before items are ordered, and keep budgets from quietly running over — all while doing the creative work that actually builds your reputation.

No single view of what stage each project is at, what is pending client approval, what needs to be ordered before the install window closes, or which project is at risk of slipping. The Solution

A Complete Interior Design Business OS in Notion

FreelanceOS Pro for Interior Designers connects your project tracker, client brief database, vendor and supplier CRM, FF&E sourcing log, budget tracker, mood board library, site visit notes, and invoice history — all in one relational Notion workspace built for how a design practice actually runs.

Project Tracker — Discovery → Design → Sourcing → Installation → Closeout

A master project database that gives every active engagement its own record with a clear stage pipeline — Discovery, Concept Design, Design Development, Sourcing, Procurement, Installation, and Closeout — so you always know exactly where each project stands in your studio's workflow. Each project record links to the client brief, the FF&E sourcing log, the budget tracker, and the invoice history, creating a single-click view of the full project without switching between apps or hunting through email folders. Track key milestone dates, the contracted design fee, the project square footage or room scope, and any special notes about access, storage, or install complexity that affect scheduling and subcontractor coordination. Filter your project board by stage to see at a glance which projects are in active sourcing, which are awaiting client approvals before anything can be ordered, and which are approaching their install window without all items confirmed as delivered and received. This is the command center for your entire studio — the one place you open every morning to understand what the day demands across every live project on your roster, without relying on memory or calendar reminders to reconstruct your priorities from scratch.

Client Brief & Design Preferences Database — Style, Budget, Timeline & Approval Log

A structured database for every client relationship that captures the full design brief — preferred style references, material tolerances (allergies, sustainability requirements, pet and family considerations), budget range by category, project timeline, rooms in scope, and any non-negotiable aesthetic or functional requirements that emerged from the discovery conversation. The preferences database is linked directly to the project tracker so that when you open a project, the client's brief is one click away — no more re-reading the intake email from four months ago to remember that this client specifically did not want brass hardware or that they need a home office that converts to a guest bedroom. Track every approval milestone in the same record — which selections have been presented, which have been approved in writing, which are pending revision, and which were rejected with the client's stated reason — so the approval log grows alongside the project and provides clear documentation if scope creep or change order disagreements arise later in the engagement. Maintaining this brief database also makes your onboarding calls more efficient, since you have a consistent set of fields to fill in rather than hoping you captured everything in a meeting note that may live in a different app.

Vendor & Supplier CRM — Products, Lead Times, Trade Discounts & Order History

A complete vendor relationship database tracking every trade account, showroom relationship, and supplier you work with — furniture manufacturers, fabric houses, lighting showrooms, tile and stone suppliers, flooring distributors, window treatment vendors, art sources, and accessory suppliers from both domestic and import channels. Each vendor record holds the account rep name and direct contact information, your trade account number, the discount percentage or trade program tier you have earned, typical lead times by product category, minimum order requirements or freight qualifications, return and damage claim policies, and a linked order history showing every past purchase from that vendor across all your projects. Knowing the typical lead time for a specific vendor's upholstery program before you promise an install date to a client prevents one of the most common and most avoidable causes of timeline failure in interior design projects — committing to a schedule before confirming that eight-week lead time on the custom sofa or the fourteen-week wait on the dining table. The CRM also helps you identify which vendor relationships are generating the most project value and which trade accounts deserve more deliberate relationship investment.

FF&E Sourcing Tracker — Furniture, Fixtures & Equipment by Room and Project

A detailed line-item database for every piece of furniture, fixture, and equipment selected across all active projects, organized by project and by room so you can see the full sourcing picture at any level of zoom — from the complete studio view of everything in procurement down to every single item specified for the primary bedroom of one project. Each line item holds the item name and description, the room and project it belongs to, the vendor and trade account, the trade price, the client price with your markup applied, the quantity required, the order date or target order date, the expected lead time in weeks, the estimated delivery date, and the current order status — Not Yet Ordered, Purchase Order Placed, In Production, Shipped, At Receiver, Delivered, Installed, or Damaged and Replacing. Filter by delivery date to prepare your weekly receiving checklist and coordinate storage with your receiving warehouse, filter by status to see everything still outstanding before an upcoming install window, or filter by vendor to prepare a consolidated order that qualifies for freight minimums. A well-maintained FF&E tracker means you are never caught off guard when a client walks through an installation and asks why the dining chairs have not arrived — you know the answer before you walk in the door.

Budget vs Actual Spend Tracker — Committed, Ordered, Invoiced & Remaining by Category

A project-level budget tracker that gives you and your client real-time visibility into where the budget stands across every spend category — furniture, lighting, window treatments, rugs, flooring, tile and stone, plumbing fixtures, hardware, art and accessories, plants and greenery, labor, design fees, and reimbursable expenses. For each category you record the allocated budget amount, the committed spend (items selected and client-approved but not yet ordered or invoiced), the ordered spend (purchase orders placed with vendors representing real financial exposure), the invoiced spend (amounts billed to the client for FF&E or design services), and the remaining available balance calculated automatically from those inputs. Watching the committed column grow as approvals are collected — before orders are placed and before money changes hands — is the early warning signal that prevents budget overruns, because catching a problem at the approval stage when selections can still be swapped or scaled back is infinitely better than discovering it at closeout when everything has already been ordered, received, and installed. Share a filtered view of the budget tracker with clients during monthly or milestone check-in calls so every budget conversation is grounded in real data rather than vague estimates from memory.

Mood Board Reference Library — Image Embeds, Links & Concept Notes by Project

A centralized visual reference library where every piece of design inspiration — whether it came from a Pinterest pin, a manufacturer's product page, a saved Instagram post, a shelter magazine tear sheet, a client email attachment, or your own photography from a past project — is logged, linked to the relevant project, and tagged with room, style direction, material type, and color palette. Each entry holds the image URL or embed link, the original source, the project and room it references, a concept note explaining what specific element of the image informs the design direction and how it will be interpreted or adapted for this particular client and space, and a status field indicating whether the reference has been incorporated into a formal presentation or is still in the exploratory inspiration pool. Instead of hunting across five platforms to reconstruct the design vision during a client presentation or a design review call, your entire reference library for a project is in one filterable Notion database — structured, searchable, and permanently connected to the project record that contextualizes every image within the broader design narrative.

Site Visit Notes Template — Observations, Photos Links, Measurements & Action Items

A standardized template for every site visit — initial site survey and measurement session, concept presentation walk-through, vendor measurement visit for custom window treatments or built-ins, delivery and installation inspection, or final punch list review — that ensures you capture the right information consistently regardless of how fast-moving or chaotic the visit turns out to be. Each site visit note captures the visit date, the project and room scope covered, the type of visit, all attendees (including contractors, trades, or client representatives present), key observations organized by room or phase, links or references to photos taken during the visit, any measurements confirmed or corrected during the visit, open questions that need follow-up before the next phase can proceed, and a structured action items list with the assigned responsible party and a due date for each item. Having a consistent, timestamped record of every site visit protects you professionally in situations where a contractor claims a measurement was communicated incorrectly, a client remembers a verbal approval differently than it occurred, or a damage claim requires documentation of pre-existing conditions that existed before your work began — all scenarios that arise in active design practices.

Invoice & Proposal Tracker — Design Fees, Trade Markup, Retainer Status & Payment History

A complete billing history across every active and past project — initial design fee proposals, project retainer invoices, FF&E trade markup billing for each sourcing phase, reimbursable expense billing, change order invoices when scope expands beyond the original agreement, and final project closeout statements — all organized in one database linked to the relevant project record for full context. Each invoice entry holds the invoice number, the linked project, the billing type (design fee, retainer, FF&E markup, expense reimbursement, or change order), the invoice amount, the date sent, the payment due date, and the payment status — Draft, Sent, Partial Payment Received, Paid in Full, or Overdue and requiring follow-up. Track your retainer balance separately for each project, showing how much of the initial retainer has been drawn down against design time and expenses and how much remains before a replenishment invoice is triggered by the terms of your contract. Knowing exactly what has been billed, what is outstanding, and what is coming due across all your projects simultaneously gives you the financial picture of your studio that informs cash flow planning, the decision to take on a new project, and the conversation with any client whose invoice has aged beyond your standard payment terms.

Frequently Asked Questions

Questions From Interior Designers & Design Studio Owners

How do interior designers use Notion to manage projects?

Interior designers use Notion to track every active project from initial discovery through final closeout in a single relational workspace. A project tracker database holds each project's current stage, client name, scope summary, key milestones, and linked records from the vendor CRM, FF&E sourcing log, budget tracker, and invoice history. Because all databases are linked in Notion, a designer can open any project and immediately see what has been ordered, what the budget looks like, what approvals are pending, and what the next site visit action item is — without switching between apps or hunting through email. Notion works especially well for designers managing three to eight projects simultaneously, where the cognitive load of keeping every detail in memory becomes unsustainable and begins to produce costly mistakes in ordering, scheduling, and client communication.

Can Notion replace project management software for interior designers?

For solo designers and small studios, Notion can absolutely replace or significantly reduce reliance on dedicated project management software. Tools like Studio Designer, Ivy, or Houzz Pro offer purpose-built features but come with per-seat subscription costs, learning curves, and rigid database structures that do not always match how a solo practice actually works day to day. Notion gives you a fully customizable workspace that adapts to your workflow — your stage names, your room categories, your vendor fields, your billing types — rather than forcing you into someone else's template. The tradeoff is that Notion requires upfront setup investment and does not have specialized features like automated purchase order generation or direct trade account integrations. For designers who want flexibility, a lower cost base, and a system they can genuinely customize, Notion is an excellent operational foundation that scales with a growing studio.

How do I track FF&E orders in Notion?

In the FreelanceOS Pro FF&E Sourcing Tracker, each item has its own database record with the item name, category (furniture, fixture, or equipment), room assignment, project link, vendor or supplier, item description, trade price, client price, lead time in weeks, order date, expected delivery date, and current order status. You can filter the database to see all outstanding orders for a specific project, all items from a specific vendor, or everything that has a delivery date in the next thirty days. This gives you a live snapshot of what is in transit, what is back-ordered, and what still needs to be ordered before an install window — without digging through email chains with ten different vendor reps to piece together the same information. A well-maintained FF&E tracker is one of the highest-leverage operational improvements a solo design practice can make, paying dividends in smoother installations and more confident client conversations.

What's the best way to manage client approvals in Notion?

The most effective approach is to track approvals as status fields within your project and FF&E databases in Notion. Each key item or design decision — a furniture selection, a material specification, a finish choice, a layout revision, or a change order — can carry an approval status field (Pending Approval / Approved / Revision Requested / Rejected) along with an approval date and any client comment or condition attached to the approval. When a client approves via email, you log the approval in Notion and link or attach the email as supporting documentation that timestamps the decision. This creates a chronological approval record tied to the specific project that you can reference if scope creep or change order disputes arise later in the project — and in an industry where verbal approvals happen constantly, having written documentation of what was agreed and when is essential professional protection that most designers wish they had built in from the start.

Can I track vendor lead times in Notion?

Yes. The Vendor and Supplier CRM in FreelanceOS Pro includes a lead time field on each vendor record where you note the typical lead time in weeks for their most common item categories — upholstery, case goods, lighting, tile, stone, window treatment fabrication, and custom accessories. When you create a new FF&E sourcing record for an item from that vendor, you can reference the lead time to calculate the order-by date that aligns with your installation window and work backward to identify when the purchase order needs to be placed. For designers who regularly work with vendors that have eight to sixteen week lead times on upholstery or custom case goods, tracking this systematically prevents the situation where an install date is committed to a client before the lead times have even been confirmed — creating an impossible timeline that damages both the client relationship and your reputation with the trade vendor who gets pressured to rush production.

How do interior designers track budgets in Notion?

The FreelanceOS Pro Budget vs Actual Tracker gives each project a master budget broken down by category — furniture, lighting, window treatments, flooring, tile and stone, accessories, labor, design fees, and reimbursables. For each category you record the allocated budget, the committed spend (items selected and client-approved but not yet ordered), the ordered spend (purchase orders placed representing real financial exposure), the invoiced spend (amounts billed to the client), and the remaining available balance. This running tally means you and your client always know exactly where the project stands financially at any moment during the engagement, rather than discovering at closeout that the budget was exceeded two months ago when selections were being approved faster than anyone was tracking the cumulative total. Catching a budget problem at the approval stage when you can still swap a selection or have a budget conversation is far preferable to delivering difficult financial news at project end.

Is Notion good for managing mood boards and design inspiration?

Notion works very well as a centralized mood board and inspiration library, especially for designers who currently have references scattered across Pinterest, Instagram saves, email attachments, Houzz ideabooks, and physical tear sheets with no connection to which project or room they belong to. In the FreelanceOS Pro Mood Board Reference Library, each entry is linked to a specific project and tagged with a room, style direction, material type, and color palette so the library is filterable and searchable rather than just a pile of images. You can embed image URLs directly in Notion, paste links from Pinterest or manufacturer websites, write concept notes explaining what element of the image informs the design direction, and attach client-shared inspiration photos as files. Filtering the library by project gives you all visual references for that project in one scrollable view — far more useful during a client presentation than switching between five platforms to reconstruct a design direction you established months earlier in the project.

How do I manage client invoices and proposals in Notion as a designer?

The Invoice and Proposal Tracker in FreelanceOS Pro tracks every billing event across your active projects — initial design fee proposals, retainer invoices, trade markup billing on FF&E orders, reimbursable expense invoices, change order billing when scope expands, and final project closeout statements. Each record holds the invoice number, the linked project, the billing type, the amount, the date sent, the due date, and the payment status — Draft, Sent, Partial Payment Received, Paid in Full, or Overdue. You also track your retainer balance for each project, showing how much of the initial retainer has been drawn down against design time and how much remains before a replenishment invoice is triggered by your contract terms. This tracker gives you a complete billing picture across all active projects simultaneously and does not replace your accounting software — it adds the project-context layer that connects financial data to the design work and decisions that generated it, making your billing conversations with clients more clear and more defensible.
