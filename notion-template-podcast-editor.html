Client and show name, show format (solo/interview/co-hosted/narrative/roundtable/hybrid), typical episode length (raw and finished — the ratio matters for quoting), publishing frequency, average episodes per month you edit, delivery format (MP3 at spec/WAV/both), publishing platform (Spotify/Apple Podcasts/Buzzsprout/Libsyn/RSS direct), service tier (basic edit only/full production/show notes included/transcript included/audiogram included), rate per episode or monthly retainer, payment cadence, next episode expected, last episode delivered, and relationship status (active/on hold/completed). 📋 Episode Delivery Pipeline

Per episode: show and client, episode number, episode title, raw file received (date and file size), guest names if interview, raw recording notes (technical issues flagged at intake — "mic cut at 14:23", "background noise from 8:00 to 12:00"), edit complexity estimate (easy/medium/complex — noise removal, multiple guests, heavy filler removal required), agreed turnaround, in-edit status, delivered date, revision requested, revision delivered, approved, and invoiced. Your complete episode production log from raw receipt to final approval — never lose track of where any episode stands across any of your client shows.

🛠️ Service Menu & Pricing

Your podcast editing service tiers with pricing clarity: Basic Edit (remove filler words and long pauses, basic noise reduction, level audio, export to MP3 at spec — typical range $0.75 to $2.50 per finished minute), Standard Edit (basic plus room tone fill, smoothing, light music bed intro/outro, chapter markers — priced per episode by length bracket), Premium Edit (standard plus heavy noise treatment, multi-track mixing, SFX, branded intro produced, show notes written). One-off services: guest audio cleanup for bad recordings, podcast trailer production, back-catalog editing at bulk pricing, YouTube audiogram creation. Rush fee policy by turnaround window. The pricing reference that makes quoting new clients consistent and confident.

🎛️ Technical Requirements Library

Per client show, store their exact delivery specs: export format (MP3 at 128/192/320kbps mono or stereo), integrated loudness target (LUFS — -16 for Spotify standard, -19 for Apple Podcasts, -14 for some YouTube optimization), true peak ceiling (-1.0dBTP standard), sample rate (44.1kHz standard), file naming convention (ShowName_EP###_YYYYMMDD), metadata tags required, show notes format (plain text/Markdown/Google Doc), and transcript delivery format if included. One reference library that eliminates repeated back-and-forth on specs — especially valuable when managing 8 to 15 active shows with different delivery requirements across every one of them.

📁 Asset & File Management Log

Per show: where raw files are received (Dropbox folder/Google Drive/Dropbox Transfer/WeTransfer/cloud storage bucket), where finished files are delivered (client Dropbox/upload portal), archival policy (how long you keep raw and finished files), music licensing (what royalty-free music this show uses — Artlist/Musicbed/Soundstripe subscription), sound effects library, and DAW template session file location (for shows where you maintain a master template in Adobe Audition/Logic/Reaper/Hindenburg). The asset map that prevents the "which folder was that?" scramble across 10 active client shows.

💼 Podcast Editing Business Dashboard

Active shows (ongoing clients), episodes delivered this month, monthly revenue, revenue by service tier (basic/standard/premium/one-off), average episode turnaround time (days from raw receipt to delivery), revision rate (percentage of episodes requiring a revision — a high revision rate signals a briefing or quality issue worth investigating), on-time delivery rate (your reliability metric clients notice), new client inquiries this month, source of new clients (referral/Podcast Editor Academy community/Facebook group/LinkedIn/Upwork/direct outreach), and annual revenue goal. The complete business view of your audio editing practice.

Frequently Asked Questions

Questions From Freelance Podcast Editors

How is this different from a template for podcasters or content creators?

This template is built specifically for the post-production business — the freelance podcast editor who gets hired by podcasters, production companies, and brands to do the audio work. It tracks your client shows, episode delivery pipeline, technical specs per show, service tiers and pricing, and your audio business income. It is not a content planning or podcast hosting tool for show hosts — it is the operational workspace for the audio professional on the other side of the raw file upload. If you are a podcast host managing your own show, a general content creator template is a better fit. If you are an editor getting paid to process other people's audio, this is built for you.

How does the technical requirements library eliminate back-and-forth with clients?

The technical requirements library stores per-show delivery specs: export format (MP3 at 128/192/320kbps, mono or stereo), integrated loudness target (LUFS — -16 for Spotify, -19 for Apple Podcasts, -14 for YouTube optimization), true peak ceiling (-1.0dBTP standard), sample rate (44.1kHz standard), file naming convention, metadata tags required, and show notes format if included. Once you build this library for each client show, you reference it for every episode rather than re-reading email threads or asking the client again. For editors managing 5 to 15 active shows simultaneously, this library alone is worth the price of the template — because the alternative is finding the spec information buried in a 3-month-old onboarding email every single time you export.

How do I price podcast editing services using this template?

The service menu lets you build out your tiers: Basic Edit (filler and pause removal, noise reduction, level audio, export to spec — typical range $0.75 to $2.50 per finished minute), Standard Edit (basic plus room tone fill, smoothing, music bed intro/outro, chapter markers — priced per episode by length bracket), Premium Edit (standard plus heavy noise treatment, multi-track mixing, SFX, branded intro produced, show notes written). Plus one-off services: guest audio cleanup, trailer production, back-catalog editing, and audiogram creation. You can also record your actual time per tier for a few months and calculate your effective hourly rate — which usually reveals that some tiers are priced correctly and others are significantly underpriced relative to the work involved.

Can I track different service tiers across different client shows?

Yes. The client show tracker records the service tier for each show — basic edit only, full production, show notes included, transcript included, audiogram included — along with the rate per episode or monthly retainer and payment cadence. The business dashboard then shows revenue broken down by service tier across all clients, which tells you which tier generates the most income and whether you are pricing each tier appropriately for the actual time it involves. Many podcast editors discover through this analysis that their premium tier clients are their most profitable because the scope is clear and revision rates are lower, even though the per-episode rate looks higher at first glance.
