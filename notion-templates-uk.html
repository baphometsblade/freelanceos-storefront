UK vs US Freelancing — Why Generic Templates Fall Short

The UK and US freelance markets are both large and sophisticated, but they operate on fundamentally different administrative frameworks. Understanding these differences is critical to choosing the right tools.

Tax Year Timing

The US tax year runs 1 January to 31 December. The UK tax year runs 6 April to 5 April — a full three months out of sync. Every US-built template assumes January planning, Q1 tax prep, and April filing. UK freelancers using these tools are constantly adapting dates and reminders. FreelanceOS is built from the ground up on the UK fiscal calendar.

IR35: The UK's Unique Contractor Legislation

There is no US equivalent of IR35. American contractors don't face legislation that examines whether their working arrangements look like employment. UK contractors — particularly in IT, finance, engineering, and consulting — must assess every contract for IR35 risk. Since the 2021 off-payroll working reforms, medium and large private sector clients make the IR35 determination rather than the contractor. This is a significant compliance burden that demands specific tools, not generic US-style freelance advice.

VAT: Different Threshold, Different Process

In the US, sales tax varies by state and is generally not applicable to B2B services between businesses. In the UK, VAT at 20% applies to most professional services once you cross the £90,000 threshold — and it must be collected from clients, submitted quarterly, and paid to HMRC. The UK also has the Making Tax Digital (MTD) requirement, which mandates digital record-keeping for VAT-registered businesses. FreelanceOS tracks your rolling income to warn you when you approach the VAT threshold.

National Insurance: Class 4 for Self-Employed

Self-employed UK freelancers pay Class 4 NI at 6% on profits between £12,570 and £50,270, and 2% above that. This is calculated annually via self-assessment — unlike employees who pay NI monthly via PAYE. Planning for this annual NI liability requires keeping aside the right percentage of income throughout the year, which FreelanceOS Pro's income tracker is designed to support.

Self-Assessment: The UK Freelancer's Annual Obligation

Every UK self-employed freelancer must complete an annual self-assessment tax return with HMRC. Here is a complete overview of what you need to prepare.

What You Need to Report

  • Total self-employment income (turnover) for the tax year
  • All allowable business expenses, categorised correctly
  • Any employment income if you also hold an employed position
  • UK property income, interest, and dividends if applicable
  • Capital gains from the disposal of assets
  • Foreign income (converted to GBP at HMRC rates)

Key Self-Assessment Deadlines

  • 5 October: Register for self-assessment if you haven't before (or if you received new self-employment income last year)
  • 31 October: Paper self-assessment return deadline
  • 31 January: Online self-assessment return deadline AND payment of any tax owed AND first Payment on Account
  • 31 July: Second Payment on Account (advance payment towards next year's tax bill)

Payment on Account Explained

Payment on Account is one of the most surprising aspects of UK self-employment for newcomers. Once your tax bill exceeds £1,000, HMRC requires you to pay advance contributions towards next year's tax — spread across 31 January and 31 July. Each Payment on Account is 50% of your previous year's tax bill. In your first big earning year, you may owe three times what you expected: the current year's tax plus two advance payments. IR35 (the off-payroll working rules) is UK legislation designed to ensure contractors who work like employees pay similar taxes. If HMRC determines you are "inside IR35" on a contract, your client deducts income tax and National Insurance before paying you — essentially treating you as an employee for tax purposes. Since April 2021, medium and large private sector clients are responsible for making IR35 determinations (called a Status Determination Statement).
