Freelancing in Czech Republic: The 2026 Landscape for OSVČ

The Czech Republic is one of Central Europe's most developed economies and a growing hub for technology, creative industries, and digital services. Prague, the capital, is home to Central European operations of major global technology companies — Google, Cisco, IBM, Microsoft, and Red Hat all have significant presence — creating strong demand for skilled freelancers and contractors in IT, consulting, and professional services. The Czech unemployment rate is consistently among the lowest in the EU, reflecting a highly skilled and competitive labor market where self-employment is well-established and respected.

The Czech Gaming Industry: A Unique Freelance Market

The Czech Republic's gaming industry is a point of national pride and a significant source of freelance work. Globally recognized studios based in the Czech Republic include:

  • CD Projekt — The Witcher series, Cyberpunk 2077; the parent company of one of the world's most acclaimed RPG studios
  • Warhorse Studios — Kingdom Come: Deliverance; a homegrown Czech studio with an international reputation
  • Bohemia Interactive — ArmA series, DayZ; based in Prague with decades of simulation and military game development
  • 2K Czech, Keen Software House, and dozens of smaller studios and indie developers

This ecosystem creates ongoing freelance demand for programmers (C++, Unreal Engine, Unity), artists (3D modeling, texture work, concept art), sound designers, QA testers, and localization translators across Czech Republic, with some projects hiring internationally.

OSVČ Registration: How Czech Freelancers Set Up

OSVČ (Osoba Samostatně Výdělečně Činná) is the standard legal structure for self-employed freelancers in the Czech Republic. Registration is straightforward: visit the local Trade Licensing Office (Živnostenský úřad) or use the online unified registration portal. Once registered, you receive an IČO (business identification number — Identifikační číslo osoby). Most IT, design, marketing, and consulting freelancers qualify for the free trade license category (volná živnost), which covers a wide range of intellectual and consulting services without requiring professional certification.

Upon registration as OSVČ, you must also register with ČSSZ (Czech Social Security) within 8 days and with your chosen health insurance provider. These registrations are separate from the trade license but are typically handled together or in quick succession.

Jako OSVČ v České republice musíte podat daňové přiznání do 1. dubna následujícího roku (nebo do 1. července s daňovým poradcem), platit zálohy na sociální a zdravotní pojištění a odvádět daň z příjmů ve výši 15 % (nebo 23 % nad zákonem stanovenou hranicí).

(As an OSVČ in the Czech Republic, you must file your income tax return by April 1 of the following year (or July 1 with a tax advisor), pay advances on social and health insurance, and pay income tax at 15% (or 23% above the statutory threshold).)

The Lump-Sum Expense Deduction (Paušální výdaje)

One of the most valuable features of the Czech tax system for freelancers is the paušální výdaje — a lump-sum expense deduction that allows OSVČ to deduct a fixed percentage of income without documenting individual expenses. The rates are:

  • 80% — agricultural activities and certain craft professions
  • 60% — trade and manufacturing businesses (most IT, design, development, and marketing freelancers qualify under volná živnost)
  • 40% — other self-employment income and professional services not covered above

For most Czech tech and creative freelancers, the 60% lump sum means only 40% of gross income is subject to income tax and social/health insurance calculations. This dramatically reduces the effective tax burden compared to full expense itemization for freelancers without very high actual business costs.

CZK vs EUR: Managing the Currency Gap

The Czech Republic is a member of the European Union but has not yet adopted the Euro. The Czech Crown (CZK) remains the national currency, and the exchange rate with EUR fluctuates based on Czech National Bank (ČNB) policy and market conditions. Many Czech freelancers working with EU clients price in EUR and receive EUR payments, which then need to be converted to CZK for tax reporting. Tracking the ČNB exchange rate on the date of each invoice or payment receipt is important for accurate tax records.

Czech freelancers with significant EUR income can open EUR accounts at Czech banks (Fio banka is popular for its low fees and EUR IBAN) or use Wise business accounts to hold EUR and convert strategically rather than at the worst possible moment.

Key Cities and Hubs for Czech Freelancers

Prague dominates the Czech freelance market, hosting the vast majority of Czech tech companies, international offices, agencies, and startup clients. Brno — Czech Republic's second largest city — has a thriving IT and startup scene, and is home to the Brno Tech Hub and a large university-educated tech workforce. Ostrava is growing as a digital economy hub in Moravia-Silesia. Prague's relatively lower cost of living compared to Western European cities makes it attractive for digital nomads relocating from Germany, the Netherlands, or the UK.

Frequently Asked Questions: Notion for Czech Freelancers (OSVČ)

How do I register as an OSVČ in Czech Republic?
To register as an OSVČ (Osoba Samostatně Výdělečně Činná), visit the local Trade Licensing Office (Živnostenský úřad) or use the online unified registration portal (jednotné kontaktní místo). You will receive a trade license (živnostenský list) and a business identification number (IČO). Most IT, design, marketing, and consulting freelancers qualify for the free trade license category (volná živnost) under group 70 — Production, trade, and services not covered by schedules 1-3. Within 8 days of starting, you must also register with ČSSZ and your chosen health insurance provider.
What taxes do Czech freelancers pay?
Czech freelancers pay income tax (daň z příjmů fyzických osob) at 15% on income up to 36 times the average monthly wage (approximately CZK 1.9 million in 2026) and 23% above that. Most OSVČ use the lump-sum expense deduction (paušální výdaje) — typically 60% of income for volná živnost holders — reducing taxable income significantly. OSVČ also pay social insurance contributions to ČSSZ and health insurance premiums to their chosen insurer separately from income tax.
What is the lump-sum expense deduction for OSVČ?
The paušální výdaje allows Czech OSVČ to deduct a fixed percentage of income without itemizing actual expenses: 80% for agricultural and specific craft activities, 60% for trade and manufacturing businesses (most IT, design, marketing, and consulting freelancers qualify here under volná živnost), and 40% for other self-employment activities. For a volná živnost freelancer earning CZK 1,000,000, the 60% lump sum means only CZK 400,000 is taxable — saving significant preparation time and often resulting in lower overall tax than itemizing actual expenses.
Do Czech freelancers pay VAT (DPH)?
Czech VAT (DPH — daň z přidané hodnoty) is 21% standard rate (with reduced rates of 12% and 0% for specific categories). OSVČ with turnover below CZK 2 million in any consecutive 12-month period are not required to register for VAT — they operate as non-taxable persons (neplátci DPH). Above CZK 2 million, VAT registration is mandatory and quarterly or monthly VAT returns must be filed. Some OSVČ register voluntarily to reclaim VAT on business purchases even below the threshold.
What is ČSSZ and how much do Czech freelancers contribute?
ČSSZ (Česká správa sociálního zabezpečení) is the Czech Social Security Administration. OSVČ pay mandatory pension insurance (důchodové pojištění) contributions — approximately 29.2% of the assessment base (which is half of taxable income, subject to minimum and maximum limits). Monthly advance payments are required, with an annual settlement after the tax return is filed. Optional sickness insurance (nemocenské pojištění) allows OSVČ to claim sick pay benefits but adds additional premiums. The minimum monthly advance for 2026 pension insurance is approximately CZK 3,900 for primary OSVČ.
What are the best freelance platforms in the Czech Republic?
Czech freelancers use a mix of international and local platforms. Internationally, Upwork is widely used for tech and design, Toptal for senior developers and designers, and LinkedIn for direct outreach to EU and global clients. Domestically, Brigáda.cz and Jobscout24.cz have freelance project listings. Many Czech freelancers find the most valuable projects through community networks — Prague's startup community Slack groups, Czech Facebook groups for OSVČ, and direct referrals. Czech Republic's EU membership means freelancers can freely work with any EU client without work permits.
