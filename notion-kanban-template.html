The Problem

Why Your Current Setup Isn't Working

You didn't fail at project management. The tools failed you. Here's what's actually going wrong — and why a properly structured Notion kanban system solves each one.

⚙️
Trello and Jira are too heavy for solo or small-team use

Jira was built for 50-person engineering teams with dedicated scrum masters. Trello lacks the relational database depth to link tasks, clients, invoices, and timelines in one place. You end up managing the tool instead of your work — paying for features you'll never touch while missing the connections that would actually help you ship.

📥
Tasks pile up in "To Do" with no priority system

When everything is in the same column with no priority differentiation, you default to whatever feels urgent rather than what's actually important. A task that came in at 9am gets attention over a high-value deliverable due tomorrow. Without a built-in triage system, your "To Do" column becomes a graveyard of things that never move — and the important work gets buried under noise.

🗂️
No visibility across multiple projects simultaneously

Running three client projects at once with separate Trello boards or folders means constant tab switching and mental context-loading. You can't see at a glance which project is closest to deadline, which client has a task waiting on your approval, or whether you're overcommitted this week. The lack of a unified cross-project view turns multi-tasking into multi-struggling.

♻️
"Done" tasks never archived, so boards become cluttered

Within a month of starting a new project management system, your "Done" column has 140 cards and your active board is buried under completed work. Without a structured archive and retrospective process, completed tasks accumulate until the board is so cluttered that you start a fresh one and abandon the old system entirely — losing all historical context and repeating the cycle in sixty days.

How It Works

Three Steps to a Running Kanban System

Set up once, run indefinitely. The template gives you the structure — you just add your projects and get to work.

1
Add all your projects to the Master Board

Open the Master Project Board and create a card for every active project — client work, personal projects, and anything else you're actively tracking. Add the client name, deadline, priority level, and project type. The board shows you the full landscape of your work in a single kanban view. You can see overloaded periods, upcoming deadlines, and project mix at a glance within the first five minutes.

2
Break each project into tasks on its kanban

Click into any project and land on its dedicated Task Kanban. Add every deliverable and task for that project — design revisions, copy drafts, calls, research, whatever the work actually involves. Assign due dates, priority tags, and estimated hours to each task. The Priority Matrix and Weekly Sprint Board automatically pull from these per-project boards so you never have to manually curate your cross-project views.

3
Run your weekly triage to keep everything moving

Every Monday, open the Weekly Sprint Board to see what's on your plate for the week. Check the Blocked and Waiting Board to follow up on anything stalled. Review the Backlog Triage System to promote any ideas whose time has come. Use the Priority Matrix to decide what gets your focused morning hours. This weekly ritual takes fifteen to twenty minutes and ensures that nothing falls through the cracks — ever.
