The Problem

Running a Freelance Editing Business Is Two Full-Time Jobs at Once

Freelance editors bring expertise in language, style, and structure. But the business side — tracking manuscripts, billing by word count, managing client preferences — requires a different kind of system.

✏️

Manuscript status tracked across emails

Six manuscripts in various stages of editing, all tracked through email subject lines and inbox search. Which one has a deadline this Friday? Which client is waiting for the editorial report you started last week? The answer is somewhere in your inbox, and finding it takes longer every month as the volume compounds.

📚

Style preferences memorized per client

One client uses AP style with a custom list of exceptions. Another wants Chicago with specific vocabulary preferences. A third is academic with APA citations. You remember most of it — until you are deep in a manuscript and realize you are not sure whether this client uses the Oxford comma or not, and the email exchange where you agreed on it is buried in a thread from eight months ago.

💰

Per-word billing calculated manually each invoice

Word count billing requires knowing the manuscript word count, your agreed per-word rate, and any agreed minimum or maximum — all from memory and email. Getting it right every time without a systematic calculation reference is a small but recurring source of invoice uncertainty that erodes client confidence.

📝

Editorial reports rebuilt each time

Every manuscript completion means writing an editorial report from a blank document, trying to remember what sections your most demanding clients expect, how formal the language should be, and whether this particular client wants structural observations or line-level notes or both. The result is inconsistent reports that vary in quality and completeness depending on how much time you have at the end of the project.

The Solution

A Complete Copy Editor OS in Notion

FreelanceOS Pro for Copy Editors connects your editorial project database, client style guide library, editorial report templates, client CRM, common errors log, professional reading list, invoice tracker, and query tracker in one Notion workspace built for freelance editors running a serious editing business.

Editorial Project Database — Client, Manuscript Type, Word Count, Deadline & Rate

A relational project database that serves as the control center for an entire freelance editing portfolio, giving editors a single place to track every active and completed manuscript without relying on email as a filing system. Each record holds the client name, manuscript title, manuscript type (Book / Article / Thesis / Marketing / Academic / Website Copy / Technical), word count, deadline, agreed rate (per word / per hour / flat fee), rate amount, current status (Awaiting Manuscript / In Progress / First Pass Complete / Awaiting Feedback / Revisions / Complete / Invoiced / Paid), and a relational link to the client's style guide in the style guide library. The status pipeline is designed around the actual stages of editorial work rather than generic project management stages. Filtering by status each Monday shows exactly what is due this week, what is awaiting client feedback, and what needs an invoice sent. The manuscript type field matters for editorial business development: filtering by type shows whether your portfolio skews toward books, academic editing, or marketing copy — information that informs specialization decisions and rate-setting over time. Word count tracking across completed projects gives you data for estimating turnaround time on new projects in the same manuscript type category, making your project proposals more accurate and your client communication more professional from the moment of engagement.

Style Guide Library — AP, Chicago, Oxford & Custom Client Preferences Per Client

A client-specific style guide database that replaces the combination of memory and buried email exchanges that most freelance editors use to track style preferences, giving you instant access to every relevant style decision for any client at any point during editing. Each style guide record connects to the client's record in the CRM and holds the primary style guide (AP / Chicago / Oxford / APA / MLA / Custom), a do-list of style preferences specific to this client, a don't-list of style choices the client has rejected, vocabulary preferences (preferred terminology, words to avoid, brand-specific spelling), citation format details, and notes on tone and voice expectations. The do/don't structure matches how editorial style decisions actually accumulate in a client relationship: through manuscript feedback and revision conversations where the client says "we always capitalize this term" or "please don't use serial comma in our marketing materials." Capturing these decisions in a searchable database means you reference the style guide before editing rather than discovering preferences in the revision round. For editors who work with the same clients across multiple projects, the style guide library builds across years of engagements into a comprehensive profile of each client's editorial identity — which is the foundation of a professional client relationship and a primary reason clients return to editors who remember their preferences rather than relitigating the same style questions on every new manuscript.

Editorial Report Template — Structural Issues, Line Edit Observations & Recommendations

A structured editorial report template embedded in Notion that produces consistent, professional manuscript assessments rather than blank-document reconstructions that vary in quality depending on end-of-project fatigue. Each report record connects to the project it belongs to and includes the manuscript summary section (scope, word count, type, what the edit addressed), structural issues section (organization, argument flow, chapter structure, narrative arc — for developmental and substantive edits), line edit observations section (patterns in sentence-level writing quality, recurring issues, areas of particular strength), consistency notes section (character names in fiction, terminology in nonfiction, formatting consistency), and recommendations for revision section (the two to five most important changes the author should make before the manuscript is ready for its next stage). The structured sections serve two purposes simultaneously: they organize your editorial thinking as you complete the manuscript, and they produce a report the client finds genuinely useful rather than a list of tracked changes without context. Editorial reports are a significant differentiator for freelance editors competing for ongoing client relationships: clients who receive thorough, consistent reports return for subsequent projects at higher rates than those who receive only a marked-up manuscript file. The template reduces the time to produce a professional editorial report from ninety minutes to thirty minutes while improving consistency across your entire client portfolio.

Client CRM — Relationship Notes, Manuscript History, Rate History & Turnaround Expectations

A relational client database that serves as the relationship memory for your entire editorial client portfolio, ensuring that every interaction, preference, and agreement is documented where you can find it rather than distributed across email threads that become harder to search as the client relationship grows. Each client record holds the client name, contact information, how the relationship started (referral source or platform), active and past manuscripts linked from the editorial project database, current per-word or hourly rate, rate history (documenting rate increases over time with dates), expected turnaround time (how quickly the client typically needs manuscripts returned), communication preferences (email / phone / form submission), payment terms (Net-15 / Net-30 / on delivery), and relationship notes covering anything relevant to working with this client professionally. The rate history field is particularly valuable for freelance editors who raise rates annually or when their expertise deepens: having documented rate history per client means you know exactly when you last raised your rate with each client and what the current agreed rate is, which prevents the uncomfortable situation of sending an invoice with a rate the client does not recognize. Relational links to the manuscript history mean you can see every project completed for a client from the client record itself, which is invaluable for long-term client relationships spanning years and dozens of manuscripts.

Common Errors Log — Patterns by Manuscript Type for Process Improvement

A professional development database that captures recurring patterns in the editorial work you encounter across manuscripts, building a personal reference resource that improves your efficiency and accuracy over time. This is an internal tool for process improvement — not a record of individual client critique — designed to help you notice patterns in your own work and in the types of manuscripts you most frequently edit. Each entry holds the error category (punctuation / consistency / grammar / structure / style / citation), a description of the specific pattern (passive voice overuse in academic manuscripts, comma splice frequency in first-time authors, inconsistent hyphenation in technical writing), the manuscript type where this pattern appears most frequently, and a note on the most efficient editorial intervention for addressing it. Over time the common errors log becomes a personally curated editorial reference that reflects your actual experience rather than generic grammar guides. It informs how you scope new projects in the same manuscript category — if you know that technical writing manuscripts in your portfolio consistently have terminology inconsistency issues, you factor that into your time estimate for the next technical project. It also supports your professional development planning: if a particular error category appears frequently and requires significant editorial time, that is a signal for targeted professional development to improve your efficiency in that area and your value to clients in that manuscript type.

Professional Reading List — Craft Books, Grammar References & Continuing Education

A curated professional reading and continuing education database that treats editorial skill development as the ongoing investment it is, giving you a structured place to track craft resources, grammar references, and professional development activities rather than a scattered collection of bookmarks and good intentions. Each reading list entry holds the title, author, category (Grammar Reference / Craft / Style Guide / Industry / Professional Development / Continuing Education), format (Book / Course / Webinar / Conference / Article / Podcast), status (To Read / In Progress / Complete / Reference), key takeaway notes after completion, and applicability notes identifying which manuscript types or client categories this resource most directly improves. Core grammar and style references — The Chicago Manual of Style, Garner's Modern English Usage, The Copyeditor's Handbook, Merriam-Webster — are logged here with edition number and notes on how you use them in your practice. The continuing education tracking functionality is particularly valuable for editors who maintain professional certifications or memberships with CE requirements: logging completed webinars, workshops, and courses with completion dates creates a professional development record that supports applications for senior editorial roles or speaking engagements at editing conferences. The reading list also serves as a recommendation resource: when a client asks about editorial resources for their team, you have a curated list organized by category rather than a mental fumble for titles you half-remember and cannot immediately locate.

Invoice & Income Tracker — Per-Word Billing, Monthly Revenue & Per-Project Fees

A financial tracking database that brings together invoicing and income visibility for the specific billing structures freelance editors use — per-word rates, hourly rates, and flat fees — in one organized system that replaces the combination of spreadsheets and memory that most freelance editors rely on for financial tracking. Each invoice record connects to the project and client from the project database and CRM, holds the billing type (Per Word / Per Hour / Flat Fee), the calculated amount (with a notes field for the word count × rate calculation that produced the number), the invoice date, due date, payment status (Sent / Paid / Overdue / Partially Paid), and payment received date. The per-word billing notes field captures the word count and rate at the time of invoicing — critical documentation for manuscripts where the final word count differs from the estimated word count in the initial agreement. Monthly income views aggregate completed payments by month, giving you quarterly and annual revenue visibility that feeds both tax preparation and business planning. Filtering by payment status weekly surfaces every overdue invoice without inbox archaeology: you immediately see who owes you money and when payment was due, which is the starting point for every professional follow-up conversation. For editors who work with clients in different countries or currencies, the invoice record includes a currency field and notes on exchange rate or payment method.

Query & Submissions Tracker — Markets, Submission Dates & Response Tracking

A submissions management database for freelance editors who also write, covering query letters to literary agents, article pitches to publications, essays to literary magazines, and guest posts to industry platforms — all the outbound writing activity that runs in parallel with client editing work and often falls through the cracks when editorial deadlines dominate the calendar. Each submission record holds the market or publication name, the piece submitted or pitched, the type of submission (Query Letter / Article Pitch / Essay / Short Story / Guest Post / Op-Ed), the submission date, the expected response window, current status (Submitted / Under Consideration / Request for Full / Rejected / Accepted / Published), the response received date, and notes on the submission guidelines or editor name. The response window field combined with the submission date lets you see at a glance which submissions are still within the expected response window and which have gone silent long enough to warrant a polite follow-up or withdrawal. For editors who pitch articles about the craft of editing to industry publications — Editor & Publisher, ACES, The Subversive Copy Editor blog network — tracking submissions professionally builds the publication record that supports conference speaking submissions, professional association leadership, and eventual book proposals about editorial methodology. The tracker works equally well for editors who write fiction or creative nonfiction alongside their client work, supporting the full spectrum of outbound writing activity that most productive editors maintain alongside their editing practice.

Frequently Asked Questions

Questions From Freelance Editors & Editorial Professionals

How does the editorial project database handle different billing structures — per word, per hour, and flat fee?

The billing type field in each project record has three options: Per Word, Per Hour, and Flat Fee. For per-word projects, the rate field holds your per-word rate and the notes field captures the word count and calculation reference at the time of agreement — so when the manuscript arrives and you invoice, the math is documented alongside the project rather than reconstructed from memory. For hourly projects, the rate field holds your hourly rate and the status pipeline includes an In Progress status where you log hours worked in the notes field. For flat-fee projects, the rate field holds the agreed total and the invoice tracker connects directly to the project record for payment tracking. The word count field in the project database also enables post-project analysis: over time you can see your average word count per manuscript type, which improves your time estimation for future projects in the same category. Many freelance editors find that this historical data shifts how they price new projects — the word count and turnaround data from completed projects is more reliable than instinct when scoping a new engagement of similar scope.

How does the style guide library work when a client has highly specific custom preferences?

The style guide library is designed around the reality that every significant client relationship eventually produces a custom layer of style preferences on top of the base style guide. The do-list and don't-list fields are free-text fields where you capture specific style decisions as they emerge from manuscript feedback: this client always capitalizes Product Manager, this client never uses serial comma, this client prefers "which" to "that" in restrictive clauses even though Chicago says otherwise. These fields accumulate over the course of a client relationship into a rich, searchable reference that reflects years of editorial collaboration. Before starting any manuscript for an existing client, you open their style guide record and review the current preferences — a practice that takes two minutes and prevents the revision cycles that result from forgetting an agreed-upon preference. New client relationships start with a style guide record created at intake, populated with whatever preferences emerge from the initial client questionnaire, and built out as the first project progresses. The vocabulary preferences field is particularly useful for clients in specialized industries where terminology has specific meaning: medical, legal, technical, and financial clients often have precise vocabulary requirements that AP or Chicago does not address.

Is this template appropriate for developmental editors, not just copy editors?

Yes. The template was designed to work across the full spectrum of editorial specializations. Developmental editors will find the editorial report template particularly well-suited to their work: the structural issues section and recommendations for revision section are exactly the categories developmental editing addresses at the manuscript level. The manuscript type field supports developmental editing categorizations (Book / Novel / Memoir / Narrative Nonfiction / Academic Monograph) and the project status pipeline can be customized to match the stages of a developmental editorial engagement (Developmental Read / Structural Assessment / Report Delivered / Revision Round / Final Assessment). Copy editors and proofreaders use the same core database structure with different status stages and different emphasis in the editorial report: line edit observations replace structural issues as the primary report section. Many freelance editors who offer multiple service tiers — developmental editing, copy editing, and proofreading — use the manuscript type and rate fields together to see their revenue breakdown by service type, which informs specialization decisions and pricing strategy.

How does the common errors log work without becoming client-critical or unprofessional?

The common errors log is designed explicitly as an internal process improvement tool, not a client critique record. The design principle is pattern recognition rather than individual attribution: you log categories of errors and the manuscript types where they appear most frequently, not individual manuscripts or clients. An entry might say "passive voice overuse — frequent in academic thesis manuscripts in humanities disciplines" with notes on the most efficient intervention, but it would not reference a specific client's thesis or attribute the pattern to a named individual. This distinction matters for the professional integrity of your editorial practice: the log is for your professional development, not for building a file on client writing quality. The practical benefit is process efficiency: if you notice through the log that business writing manuscripts from corporate clients consistently have specific punctuation issues, you develop a focused editing pass for those issues that you run consistently on that manuscript type, which makes you faster and more reliable in that specialty. Over time, the log also surfaces where your own editorial attention may need strengthening — if you find that citation format errors are appearing in your completed manuscripts, that is a signal to invest professional development time in citation style mastery.

Can I use this template for managing academic editing clients alongside commercial clients?

Yes. The manuscript type field in the editorial project database includes Academic as a category, and the style guide library accommodates the full range of academic citation styles — APA, MLA, Chicago Notes-Bibliography, Chicago Author-Date, AMA, and others — through the primary style guide field and the custom preferences notes. Academic editing has specific characteristics that the template supports well: longer manuscripts with extended turnaround windows, multi-stage projects (copy editing followed by proofreading after author revisions), and institutional clients (universities, journals, publishers) alongside individual academic clients (PhD students, faculty, independent researchers). The rate structure supports the per-word pricing common in academic editing markets, including the ability to note different rates for different document types (journal article versus dissertation chapter) within the same client relationship. The professional reading list can be filtered to show resources specifically relevant to academic editing — style guide updates, discipline-specific terminology guides, citation software documentation — supporting the specialized knowledge base that academic editing clients expect from their editors.

How does the query tracker support editors who also write?

The query tracker is a standalone database designed specifically for outbound writing activity — query letters to literary agents, article pitches to publications, essays to literary journals, and guest post pitches to industry platforms. It runs in parallel with the editorial project database rather than being connected to it, which reflects the reality that your writing career and your editing business serve different clients and have different workflow rhythms. The status pipeline (Submitted / Under Consideration / Request for Full / Rejected / Accepted / Published) matches the actual response stages of literary submissions, and the expected response window field combined with the submission date tells you when a submission has been outstanding long enough to follow up or withdraw and resubmit elsewhere. For editors who pitch articles about craft and editorial methodology to professional publications — a valuable visibility strategy for building a professional reputation — the tracker ensures that pitches are followed up on systematically rather than forgotten after the initial send. The publication record that accumulates in the tracker also has practical value for professional development: when submitting to editorial conferences as a speaker or applying for senior editorial roles, having a documented list of publications with dates and titles is immediately available rather than reconstructed from memory.
