What Makes a Notion GTD Template Actually Good

After testing dozens of templates, here are the characteristics that separate genuinely useful GTD systems from elaborate procrastination tools:

Database Design

Relational Architecture

Tasks, projects, areas, and contexts should be separate databases linked by relations — not tags or select fields. Relations enable rollups, which are what make dashboards actually work.

Capture Speed

Sub-3-Second Capture

A good template has a mobile-accessible inbox view where you can add a new task in under 3 seconds. If capture is slow or requires typing on a tiny screen, you'll revert to sticky notes.

Reviews

Built-In Review Rituals

Daily, weekly, and monthly review templates pre-built with checklists. The reviews are what keep the system clean — without them, your inbox becomes a graveyard.

Clarity

Next-Action Focus

Every task should have a clear "next action" — a single, physical, visible action. The template should enforce this with a required "Next Action" property rather than relying on willpower.

Common GTD Notion Template Mistakes to Avoid

If you're building your own Notion GTD template, watch out for these common pitfalls that kill productivity instead of boosting it:

  • Using tags instead of relations. Tags can't roll up. You can't build a "tasks by project" count with a tag — you need a relation property linking to a Projects database.
  • No weekly review page. GTD without weekly reviews is just a fancy to-do list. Build the review ritual into the template itself.
  • Too many views. Eight filtered views that you never use is worse than two views you actually open daily. Start minimal and add views only when you feel the pain of not having them.
  • Mixing reference and action items. Reference material (meeting notes, research, SOPs) should live in a separate database from actionable tasks. Mixing them creates cognitive overhead every time you process your inbox.
  • No someday/maybe list. If your brain knows there's no safe place to park ideas for later, it will keep interrupting you with them. The someday/maybe list is not optional.
  • Ignoring the two-minute rule. If an item takes less than two minutes, do it immediately during clarify. Don't let small tasks pile up in your system.

How to Set Up Your Notion GTD Template in 30 Minutes

Once you have a solid template, setup should be fast. Here's the process:

  1. Duplicate the template to your own Notion workspace. Use "Duplicate to workspace" on the template page.
  2. Set up your Areas of Responsibility. Add the three to seven major areas of your life and work (e.g., Client Projects, Business Dev, Health, Finance, Learning).
  3. Do a brain dump into the Inbox. Set a 20-minute timer and capture every open loop you can think of — tasks, ideas, worries, errands, projects. Don't organize yet, just capture.
  4. Process your Inbox to zero. Go through each item: clarify whether it's actionable, assign it to a project or area, add a next action, and set any deadlines.
  5. Configure your Today view. Add a filter for items due today or flagged as your current focus. This becomes your daily starting point.
  6. Schedule your weekly review. Block 60–90 minutes every Friday afternoon or Sunday evening on your calendar. The first one will take longer; subsequent ones average 45 minutes.

Notion GTD vs. Other GTD Apps

People often ask whether to use Notion for GTD or a dedicated app like OmniFocus, Todoist, or Things 3. Here's an honest comparison:

  • OmniFocus: More powerful for task perspectives and quick capture, but lives in isolation. Your projects and reference material are elsewhere. $99/year.
  • Todoist: Fast and cross-platform, great for simple GTD, but lacks the relational database layer needed for project-to-task linking and custom dashboards. $5–$8/month.
  • Things 3: Beautiful and intuitive, but Mac/iOS only and no relations between databases. One-time $50 purchase.
  • Notion GTD: Requires more setup, but everything lives in one place. Your client notes, meeting records, project docs, and tasks are all cross-linked. For freelancers and knowledge workers, this integration value is huge.

If you're already using Notion for notes, docs, and client management, adding GTD to the same workspace almost always wins over a separate app.
