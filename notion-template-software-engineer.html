Sprint Planning That Doesn't Suck

The sprint tracker uses a board view with columns for Backlog, Sprint Backlog, In Progress, Review, and Done. Each task row includes a Story Points number property, a Sprint tag, and a linked project or feature. Filter the board to your current sprint in one click and carry incomplete items to the next sprint by updating the sprint tag — no ceremony, no stand-up theater.

Velocity tracking is handled via a rollup that calculates total story points completed per sprint. After three sprints, you'll have a real baseline to work from — no spreadsheet gymnastics required. The PR Review Checklist is embedded as a template button inside the sprint board: click once to generate a per-PR checklist covering unit tests, edge cases, documentation, security impact, and performance considerations. For side projects, this same board becomes your personal agile system without any team overhead. One engineer running a clean sprint cadence makes a dramatic difference in how much actually ships versus how much just sits in a growing "someday" list.

Learning That Sticks

The Learning Log is a database where each row represents one learning resource: book, course, YouTube series, documentation deep-dive, or conference talk. Properties include Topic Category (Frontend, Backend, DevOps, AI/ML, Security, Career), Status (Not Started, In Progress, Completed, Abandoned), URL, Source, Start Date, Completion Date, and a rating. Engineers who track their learning outpace those who don't — not because of magic, but because visibility creates accountability.

From Individual Contributor to Staff Engineer

Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) for Personal Use

Most engineers only encounter ADRs in formal team contexts, but maintaining personal ADRs is one of the most underrated career habits. When you make a technical decision — choosing a library, picking an architectural pattern for a side project, deciding how to structure a service — document it using the ADR format: Status, Context, Decision, Consequences, and Alternatives Considered.

A library of personal ADRs is powerful at review time and interview time. You can point to specific technical decisions you made, explain the trade-offs you weighed, and demonstrate the kind of written technical reasoning that distinguishes senior engineers from junior ones. Interviewers and promotion committees are looking for evidence of this judgment — most engineers don't have it organized anywhere accessible.

The FreelanceOS ADR database keeps all of this in one searchable place. Filter by project, by status (Accepted, Deprecated, Superseded), or by technology to find decisions quickly when context comes up in a conversation or pull request.

Interview Prep Command Center

The interview prep section is a multi-database system built to cover every dimension of technical interviewing. The DSA Problem Log tracks your LeetCode and coding practice with fields for Problem Name, Difficulty, Category (Arrays, Trees, Graphs, Dynamic Programming, etc.), Status (Not Started, Attempted, Solved), and Time Complexity notes. A gallery view groups problems by category, making it immediately obvious where your gaps are.

The System Design Notes database stores your deep dives: design a URL shortener, design Twitter's feed system, design a distributed cache. Each entry holds your approach notes, trade-off analysis, key components, and scale considerations. The Company Research database creates one page per target company — tech stack, engineering culture notes, interview format details sourced from Glassdoor or engineering blogs, and your recruiter contact.

The STAR Story Bank is where behavioral prep lives. Log 10-15 past accomplishments in STAR format (Situation, Task, Action, Result) and tag each story with the leadership principles or competencies it demonstrates. When an interviewer asks "tell me about a time you influenced without authority," you pull from a real, rehearsed library instead of improvising under pressure.
