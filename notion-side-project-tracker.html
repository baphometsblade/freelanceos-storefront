Why Side Projects Die (It's Not What You Think)

The graveyard of side projects is full of good ideas with terrible infrastructure. The problem is almost never motivation, technical skill, or the quality of the idea. Side projects die for three specific reasons:

Reason 1: The next step isn't obvious. You sit down on a Saturday morning with 90 minutes to work, and you waste 30 of them trying to remember where you left off and what you were supposed to do next. By the time you get oriented, the window is half gone. You make a little progress, then close the laptop with a vague sense of guilt.

Reason 2: No visibility across projects. When you have three side projects going simultaneously — as most active makers do — it's impossible to hold all of them in your head. One project gets all the attention for two weeks, then you guilt yourself into working on another, and the first one stalls. Without a system that shows you all three at once, you can't make rational prioritization decisions.

Reason 3: No ritual to re-engage. The two-week gap becomes a four-week gap. You forget the context, you feel intimidated by the cold start, and the project quietly joins the graveyard. A weekly review ritual — even ten minutes — prevents gaps from compounding.

A well-structured Notion side project tracker addresses all three problems directly.

The Ideal Notion Side Project Tracker Structure

The architecture that works is simpler than most people expect. You need three things: a project master database, a task database linked to projects, and a weekly review ritual.

The Project Master Database

Every side project gets a single row in this database. The key properties that make it useful:

  • Status: Active / On Hold / Shipped / Archived. Keep this honest. "Active" means you've worked on it in the past two weeks.
  • Next Action: A text field with the single most specific next task. Not "work on backend" — "add user authentication to the login endpoint." The more specific this is, the faster you can re-engage.
  • Last Touched: A date field you update every time you work on the project. Create a formula that calculates "days since last touched" — this becomes your guilt score and your prioritization signal.
  • Time Allocation: How many hours per week is this project supposed to get? This forces you to be honest about what you can actually maintain.
  • Revenue: For projects that have launched, monthly revenue. Even $0/month as a number focuses the mind wonderfully.
  • Target Launch Date: The date you're aiming to have something shippable. Not "someday." A specific date.

The single most powerful field: "Days Since Last Touched" calculated formula. Sort your project gallery by this field descending and you immediately see which projects are at risk of dying. A project that hasn't been touched in 21 days needs a scheduled session this week, full stop.

The Task Database (Linked to Projects)

Each task links to exactly one project in the project database. This is the key architectural decision — tasks without project links are noise. When you filter the task database to a specific project, you see everything that needs to happen for that project, in priority order.

Key task properties: status (not started / in progress / done), estimated time (in hours — be honest), priority (P1 / P2 / P3), and a "blocking?" toggle. Sort by priority + blocking to generate your next work session agenda automatically.

The Weekly Review Ritual

Create a Notion page template for your weekly project review. Every Sunday or Monday, open a new instance of this template and fill in: which projects were active this week, what was completed, what's the next action for each active project, and whether any projects need to be put on hold or deactivated. This ritual takes 15–20 minutes and is what separates productive makers from perpetual idea hoarders.

How Many Side Projects Should You Track?

The honest answer: fewer than you think. The most productive makers have one primary project (90% of focus) and at most one secondary project (10% of attention). Three "active" projects almost always means three slowly dying projects.

Use Notion's status field to be ruthless about this. "Active" means it gets dedicated time every week. Everything else is either "on hold" (consciously paused until the primary project ships) or "archived" (not happening, and that's okay). An archived project isn't a failure — it's a decision.

The Dashboard View That Changes Everything

Build a gallery view of your project database, sorted by "days since last touched" descending, with a cover image and the "next action" text visible in the card. This is your home screen for side project management. At a glance, every Monday morning:

  • What projects exist and their status
  • Which ones haven't been touched in too long
  • The exact next action for each active project
  • Revenue for any projects that have launched

This view costs you nothing to maintain once it's built. It earns back focus every single work session by eliminating the "where was I?" overhead.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should each side project get its own Notion workspace?

Generally no. Separate workspaces mean separate contexts to switch between, separate tabs to open, and less ability to see across projects. Keep everything in one workspace with good database organization. The exception is if a project grows to the point where you have collaborators — then a separate workspace or a shared team space makes sense.

How do I stop starting new projects and actually finish existing ones?

The "days since last touched" formula is your main lever here. Make a rule: no new project gets started until all "active" projects are either shipped or consciously archived. Write this rule in your Notion workspace where you'll see it. The accountability is to your past self, which turns out to be quite effective.

Can I use this for non-technical side projects?

Absolutely. The structure works for any project type — writing a book, building a newsletter, starting a physical product business, learning a skill for monetization. The architecture is project-type agnostic.
