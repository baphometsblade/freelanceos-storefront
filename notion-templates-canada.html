Canadian Freelance Context: Key Administrative Realities

CRA Self-Employment Income Reporting

Canadian freelancers report self-employment income on the T1 General return using Form T2125 (Statement of Business or Professional Activities). This form requires your gross income, deductible business expenses, and net business income. The deadline for self-employed individuals is June 15, though any taxes owing are still due April 30 to avoid interest charges.

Accurate income tracking throughout the year makes T2125 preparation straightforward. FreelanceOS Pro's income database, filtered by calendar year, provides the gross income figure in seconds. The expenses log covers all common deductible categories — home office, equipment, software, professional development, and more.

HST/GST Registration

You must register for GST/HST once your worldwide taxable supplies exceed $30,000 CAD in a single calendar quarter or over four consecutive calendar quarters. Below this threshold, you are a "small supplier" and registration is optional. Many Canadian freelancers choose to register voluntarily once they expect to surpass this level, since GST/HST paid on business expenses becomes creditable (an Input Tax Credit).

Provincial considerations matter: Ontario, Prince Edward Island, New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, and Newfoundland and Labrador apply HST (a combined federal-provincial tax). British Columbia, Alberta, Saskatchewan, and Manitoba apply GST separately from provincial sales tax. Quebec has its own QST system. Knowing your applicable rate is essential for correct invoicing.

RRSP Contributions as a Tax Strategy

One of the most powerful tax strategies available to Canadian freelancers is maximising RRSP (Registered Retirement Savings Plan) contributions before the annual deadline (60 days after December 31). RRSP contributions are deductible from taxable income — in a high-income year, a strategic contribution can drop you into a lower marginal tax bracket. FreelanceOS Pro's income tracking helps you see your year-to-date gross in real time, making RRSP planning much easier.

Quebec: Different Rules

Freelancers operating in Quebec face additional complexity. Quebec administers its own provincial income tax (separate T1 equivalent filed with Revenu Québec), the Quebec Sales Tax (QST, currently 9.975%), and different rules around social contributions. Quebec-based freelancers typically work with a comptable who understands both federal and provincial requirements.
