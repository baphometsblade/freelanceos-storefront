Canadian vs US Freelancing — Why Generic Templates Fall Short

The Canadian and US freelance markets share a border and a language, but they operate on fundamentally different administrative systems. Understanding these differences is essential to choosing tools that actually fit your business.

CRA vs IRS: Two Very Different Tax Authorities

US-built templates assume IRS quarterly estimated payments, Schedule C reporting, and US state tax considerations. Canadian freelancers file with the CRA, complete Form T2125 (Statement of Business or Professional Activities), and deal with a federal-plus-provincial tax structure unique to Canada. The quarterly instalment schedule, the T1 filing deadlines (April 30 and the June 15 extension for self-employed), and the GST/HST system all operate differently from US tax obligations. FreelanceOS is built from the ground up for the CRA system.

GST/HST: A Sales Tax System Unlike Any Other

The US has no national sales tax. State sales tax generally does not apply to B2B professional services. Canada has GST/HST, a value-added tax that applies to most professional services once you cross the $30,000 CAD threshold. Once registered, you must charge the correct rate (5%, 13%, or 15% depending on the province of supply), file GST/HST returns, remit the net tax to the CRA, and track Input Tax Credits on your business purchases. Quebec adds a separate QST layer, requiring two separate registrations, two returns, and two remittances. FreelanceOS monitors your rolling taxable supply and tracks ITCs so GST/HST never becomes a surprise.

CPP: Paying Both Sides of the Contribution

American self-employed individuals pay self-employment tax (15.3% on net earnings) to cover both employee and employer Social Security and Medicare contributions. Canadian self-employed individuals pay CPP contributions at 11.9% (both employee and employer portions) on net earnings between $3,500 and $68,500 — plus CPP2 contributions above that. Unlike the US system, CPP contributions are partially deductible: the employer-equivalent half reduces your taxable income on your T1 return. FreelanceOS Pro's CPP estimator handles this calculation automatically so you can set aside the right amount from every invoice.

Provincial Tax: A Complexity US Freelancers Don't Face

While US state income taxes vary, the federal-plus-state structure is broadly similar across the country. In Canada, Quebec operates its own completely separate income tax system, requiring a separate provincial return (TP-1) filed with Revenu Quebec rather than the CRA. Other provinces piggyback on the federal T1 return but at significantly different rates — from Alberta's flat 10% to Ontario's graduated rates reaching 13.16%. FreelanceOS includes a provincial rate reference table covering all 13 jurisdictions so you can estimate your combined effective rate with accuracy.

T4A Slips: A Reporting Layer That Doesn't Exist in the US

US clients issue 1099-NEC forms to contractors paid $600 or more. Canadian clients issue T4A slips to freelancers paid $500 or more. The CRA cross-references T4As against your T1 filing, which means your income records must reconcile with what your clients report. Missing or incorrect T4As can trigger CRA review requests. FreelanceOS Pro's T4A database tracks expected slips by client and amount, flags mismatches before you file, and keeps your records audit-ready year-round.

CRA Tax Obligations: A Complete Guide for Canadian Freelancers

Canadian self-employment comes with distinct CRA obligations that vary by your income level, province, and business structure. Here is a comprehensive overview.

Form T2125 — Statement of Business Activities

Every Canadian freelancer who earns self-employment income files Form T2125 as part of their T1 personal income tax return. T2125 captures your gross income, allowable business expenses by category, net income, and home-office details. The CRA uses T2125 to calculate your taxable business income, which feeds into your federal and provincial tax calculation and your CPP contribution base. FreelanceOS Pro's expense categories map directly to T2125 line items — so when tax season arrives, you're not reorganising your records from scratch.

Quarterly Tax Instalments

Unlike employees who have tax withheld from every paycheque, freelancers pay tax in annual or quarterly lump sums. If your net tax owing (federal plus provincial) is more than $3,000 in the current year and was also more than $3,000 in either of the two preceding years, the CRA requires quarterly instalment payments. Instalments are due on March 15, June 15, September 15, and December 15. Missing instalments triggers instalment interest charges at the prescribed rate plus 4%. FreelanceOS Pro's CRA calendar tracks all four instalment dates with 30-day advance reminders, and the income estimator helps you calculate the correct instalment amount using the prior-year method or current-year estimate method.

GST/HST Returns and Remittances

Once registered for GST/HST, you file returns on an annual, quarterly, or monthly basis — determined by your annual taxable revenues. Annual filers (revenues under $1.5M) file by June 15 and remit by April 30. Quarterly filers submit one month after each quarter ends. Monthly filers (revenues over $6M) file one month after each calendar month. Input Tax Credits (ITCs) on business expenses reduce your net tax remittance. Accurate ITC tracking — supported by FreelanceOS Pro's expense database — is how freelancers ensure they're not overpaying GST/HST to the CRA.

Key CRA Deadlines for Self-Employed Canadians

  • February 28: T4A slips due from clients who paid you $500+ in the prior calendar year
  • March 15: First quarterly tax instalment due (if required)
  • April 30: T1 personal tax return deadline AND payment of any balance owing (even if you qualify for the June 15 filing extension, tax owing is still due April 30)
  • June 15: T1 filing deadline for self-employed individuals and their spouses (extended from April 30, but tax owing is still due April 30)
  • June 15: Second quarterly tax instalment due
  • September 15: Third quarterly tax instalment due
  • December 15: Fourth (final) quarterly tax instalment due
  • December 31: RRSP contribution room year-end for new contributions (contributions made in first 60 days of following year also count toward prior year)

RRSP and TFSA: The Freelancer's Tax-Planning Tools

Without an employer pension plan or group benefits, Canadian freelancers carry the full burden of retirement planning. The Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP) is the most powerful tool available: contributions are deductible from your taxable income (reducing your tax bill immediately), and growth is tax-sheltered until withdrawal. Your RRSP contribution room is 18% of your prior year's earned income — for a freelancer earning $80,000 net, that is $14,400 of deductible RRSP room. The Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA) complements the RRSP with tax-free growth and withdrawals, with annual contribution room of $7,000 for 2026 (lifetime room accumulating from age 18). FreelanceOS Pro's retirement tracker helps you monitor both contribution rooms and plan optimal contributions to minimise your annual tax bill.
