Freelance Reference Guide

Freelance Glossary: 80 Essential Terms Every Freelancer Should Know

Vocabulary is power. Freelancers who know the language negotiate better contracts, write tighter agreements, avoid scope creep, and close more deals. Knowing what a kill fee is before you ever need one prevents a $3,000 mistake. Understanding value-based pricing before you start a client call means you stop leaving money on the table. Language isn't just semantics in freelancing — it's the difference between a profitable project and a disaster you can't legally escape.

This glossary covers 80 essential freelance terms across contracts, pricing, finance, client management, and business development. Bookmark it. Share it with clients before you send a contract — it pre-educates them on the terms they'll sign. Link to it in your proposal when you mention kill fees or revision rounds. Every term here is defined with practical context, not textbook fluff, because knowing a term is useless if you don't know when or how to use it.

A

Finance

Accounts Receivable

Money owed to you by clients for work you have already completed and invoiced but not yet been paid for. Tracking accounts receivable tells you how much revenue is outstanding at any given moment and when each payment is due. Slow-paying clients who push your AR high can create serious cash flow problems — you might look profitable on paper while simultaneously unable to pay your own bills. A healthy freelance business keeps a close eye on AR and follows up on overdue invoices within 48 hours of their due date, not weeks later.

Pricing

Anchor Pricing

A psychological pricing strategy where you present a higher-priced option first, so that subsequent options appear more affordable by comparison. In freelancing, anchor pricing is used when structuring service packages: by leading with a premium tier at, say, $5,000, your $2,500 standard package feels like a bargain. The anchor sets the reference point in the client's mind, shifting their perception of what's reasonable. This technique is most effective when the anchor price is credible — meaning the premium tier must offer real additional value, not inflated fluff.

Contract

At-Will Contract

A contractual arrangement where either party can terminate the agreement at any time, for any reason, without penalty — provided they give any notice required by the contract. In freelancing, at-will clauses are common in ongoing service agreements and retainers. The danger of an at-will arrangement without a kill fee is that a client can cancel the day before a deliverable is due and owe you nothing. Always pair an at-will termination clause with a kill fee provision specifying that partial completion triggers partial payment. Read this clause carefully before signing anything.

B

Finance

Billable Hours

The hours you actually charge to a client — as opposed to non-billable time spent on sales calls, admin work, professional development, or internal business tasks. Most experienced freelancers find that only 60–70% of their total working hours are billable, which means your effective hourly rate is lower than your stated rate. For example, if you charge $100/hour but only bill 5 of your 8 working hours per day, your actual effective rate is $62.50/hour. Tracking billable vs. non-billable time is essential for pricing accurately and identifying where your non-revenue time actually goes.

Business

BATNA (Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement)

Your best available option if negotiations with a client fall apart and no deal is reached — originally a concept from the Harvard Negotiation Project. In freelancing, knowing your BATNA before you enter any rate negotiation gives you genuine leverage. If you have two other leads in your pipeline, your BATNA is strong and you can walk away from a low-balling client without fear. If you have no other work lined up, your BATNA is weak and you're more likely to accept unfavorable terms. Improving your BATNA means always maintaining a pipeline — never negotiate from desperation.

Pricing

Bundled Services

A pricing structure where you package multiple related services together and sell them as a combined offering, often at a slight discount compared to purchasing each service individually. Bundling benefits both parties: the client gets a more comprehensive solution at a perceived better value, and you increase your average contract size and reduce the time spent re-pitching the same client for additional work. A web designer might bundle brand identity, website design, and a launch copywriting package. Bundling works best when the services are logically sequential and the client already needs all of them.

C

Client Management

Client Brief

A structured document, completed by the client, that captures the essential context for a project: goals, target audience, brand guidelines, examples of what they like, timeline, budget, and success metrics. A thorough client brief is the foundation of a good project — missing or vague briefs are the single biggest cause of revisions and disputes. Always send a brief questionnaire before starting any project. If a client resists filling it in, that's a red flag about how collaborative (or not) the working relationship will be. Some freelancers charge a brief consultation fee specifically to get this information properly documented.

Contract

Contract Retainer

A legally binding agreement in which a client pays a set monthly fee to secure a specified amount of your time or deliverables on an ongoing basis. Unlike a project contract, a retainer is continuous — it renews each month and provides predictable, recurring revenue. A contract retainer should specify exactly what the monthly fee covers: number of hours, specific deliverables, response time commitments, and what constitutes additional billable work beyond the retainer scope. The contract should also include termination notice periods — typically 30 days — so neither party can cancel without warning.

Finance

Cash Flow

The movement of money in and out of your freelance business over time — specifically, when you receive payments versus when you have to pay expenses. Cash flow is not the same as profit. A freelancer can be profitable on paper (lots of billable work completed) but cash-flow negative (clients haven't paid yet) and unable to cover their own rent or software subscriptions. Managing cash flow means tracking exactly when invoices are due, chasing late payments immediately, and maintaining a cash reserve of at least 2–3 months of living expenses to smooth out the gaps between project payments.

Business

Churn Rate

The rate at which clients stop working with you over a given period — typically measured monthly or annually. High churn forces you into a constant cycle of finding new clients, which is expensive in both time and energy. Reducing churn by even a small margin has an outsized impact on revenue: keeping one retainer client an extra six months is worth more than landing three new one-off projects. Churn is reduced by delivering consistent quality, maintaining proactive communication, and regularly demonstrating the value you're providing. Track it by noting how many active client relationships you lose each quarter compared to how many you had at the start.

Business

Cold Outreach

The practice of contacting potential clients who have not previously expressed interest in working with you — via email, LinkedIn, direct message, or phone. Cold outreach is the most direct form of lead generation but also the lowest-response-rate channel if done poorly. Effective cold outreach is highly personalised, concise, and leads with value rather than a sales pitch. Instead of "I'm a designer looking for clients," try "I noticed your homepage has three conversion blockers — here's a quick audit." Research your prospects before reaching out, reference something specific about their business, and make it easy for them to say yes with a low-commitment ask like a brief call rather than immediately pitching a large project.

Finance

Commission

A fee paid as a percentage of a transaction or sale that you help facilitate — as opposed to a flat fee for your time or output. Some freelancers operate on commission-based arrangements, particularly in sales, marketing, or affiliate contexts. Commission structures can be lucrative if you deliver results, but they carry income risk because your pay depends entirely on outcomes outside your direct control. If you accept a commission arrangement, ensure the tracking, attribution, and payment terms are crystal clear in your contract — including what happens to commission on deals that close after your engagement ends.

Contract

Copyright Assignment

A clause in a freelance contract that transfers legal ownership of the creative work you produce to the client. By default in most jurisdictions, you retain copyright as the creator — a copyright assignment explicitly transfers those rights. Copyright assignment should only take effect upon receipt of full payment, not upon delivery of the work. This is your most powerful protection against non-payment: if you don't get paid, you still own the work and the client cannot legally use it. Some freelancers charge a premium specifically for copyright assignment, particularly in high-value industries like advertising, publishing, or software.

D

Pricing

Day Rate

A fixed fee charged for a full day of your work, regardless of the specific hours clocked — typically 7–8 hours. Day rates are common in industries like film production, consultancy, photography, and event work where clients need a dedicated block of your time rather than a deliverable. Your day rate should be significantly higher than 8× your hourly rate because it guarantees your entire day to one client, preventing you from taking other work. A common mistake is setting a day rate that's simply 8× your hourly figure without accounting for the exclusivity premium. Day rates also simplify billing: no timesheets, no hour-tracking disputes.

Client Management

Discovery Call

An initial conversation with a prospective client, usually 20–45 minutes, where you learn about their project, goals, budget, and timeline — and they learn about your process. The discovery call is not a free consultation; it's a mutual vetting exercise. Go in with a prepared list of questions, listen more than you talk, and use it to disqualify as well as qualify: if the client's budget is a fraction of your rate, or they want revisions "until they're happy" with no defined scope, the discovery call is where you find out and walk away gracefully. Never begin work without one.

Contract

Deliverable

A specific, tangible output that you agree to produce and hand over to the client as part of a project. Deliverables should be defined with enough precision that there is no ambiguity about what "done" looks like — not "a website" but "a 5-page responsive website in WordPress, including homepage, about, services, blog, and contact pages, with one round of revisions." Vague deliverables are the root cause of most scope creep, because the client's interpretation of "done" will expand to fill whatever space you leave undefined. List every deliverable explicitly in your SOW and mark everything not on that list as out of scope.

Client Management

Due Diligence

The research and vetting process you conduct before agreeing to work with a client — checking their legitimacy, financial health, reputation in the industry, and history with past freelancers. Due diligence prevents you from taking on clients who are known late-payers, who have a reputation for scope creep, or who are financially unstable and might not be able to pay. Google the company, check LinkedIn for any unusual signals, look for reviews on platforms like Clutch or Trustpilot, and ask in your freelancer networks whether anyone has worked with them before. Ten minutes of research before signing can save months of chasing payment.

E

Finance

Escrow

A financial arrangement where a neutral third party holds funds until agreed conditions are met — typically used on freelance platforms like Upwork or for high-value one-off projects where trust has not yet been established. The client deposits payment into escrow at the start of the project, and the funds are released to you upon successful delivery and approval. Escrow significantly reduces the risk of non-payment because the money already exists and is committed. For off-platform work, you can arrange escrow-like terms by requiring a 50% deposit before starting, which functions as a partial escrow without a formal third-party intermediary.

Contract

Engagement Letter

A formal letter or short document that confirms the scope of work, fees, timeline, and terms agreed between you and a client before a full contract is drafted. Engagement letters are common in professional services like accounting, legal, or consulting but are increasingly used by freelancers in all fields as a lightweight alternative to a full contract for smaller projects. An engagement letter is legally binding even without a formal contract attached — it constitutes a written agreement between two parties. It's faster to produce than a full SOW and can be the starting point that protects you when a client wants to begin immediately without waiting for contracts.

F

Contract

Fixed-Price Contract

A contract where you agree to deliver a specific scope of work for a predetermined total fee, regardless of how many hours it actually takes you. Fixed-price contracts reward efficiency — if you complete the work faster than expected, your effective hourly rate is higher. The risk is scope creep: any expansion of the project eats into your fixed fee without additional pay. Protect yourself with a detailed SOW that defines exactly what is included, a change order clause for out-of-scope requests, and a thorough understanding of the project before agreeing to a fixed price. Fixed-price contracts are generally preferred by clients because they eliminate budget uncertainty.

Pricing

Freelance Rate Card

A document listing your services and corresponding prices — a standardised menu of what you offer and what each item costs. Rate cards are typically shared with prospects who ask "what do you charge?" before a discovery call, giving them context without requiring a bespoke proposal for every enquiry. A well-designed rate card positions you as professional and organised, signals that your pricing is considered (not made up on the spot), and helps disqualify prospects whose budget doesn't align with your fees before you invest time in a call. Rate cards don't have to be completely rigid — they can note that custom quotes are available for complex or ongoing engagements.

Business

Full-Service Retainer

A retainer arrangement where you take on a broad, ongoing remit for a client — handling multiple related functions or service areas rather than a single defined task per month. For example, a full-service marketing retainer might include strategy, copywriting, social media management, and reporting all bundled together under one monthly fee. Full-service retainers command a significant premium because they require more time and expertise, and because they make you highly integrated into the client's business. The risk is that the scope can expand gradually without additional pay — always define what "full service" means specifically in your contract.

G

Business

Ghost Writer

A writer who produces content — articles, books, speeches, social posts, newsletters — that is published under the client's name without credit. Ghostwriting is extremely common in business content, thought leadership, and publishing. Ghostwriting commands a premium over bylined writing precisely because you surrender attribution; you should charge at least 20–30% more for ghostwritten work. Make sure your contract explicitly states that the client acquires full rights to the content upon payment and that you agree not to publicly claim authorship. Ghostwriting is a legitimate, high-value service — not something to be embarrassed about offering.

Pricing

Going Rate

The typical market price for a specific type of freelance work within a given industry or geography — the range of fees that clients generally expect to pay and that freelancers generally charge. Knowing the going rate in your niche is important for positioning: charging significantly below it signals inexperience or low quality, while charging significantly above requires clear justification through your portfolio, specialisation, or results. The going rate is a floor, not a ceiling — experienced freelancers with strong track records regularly charge two to three times the going rate by niching down, specialising, and building proof of results. Research going rates via industry surveys, freelancer communities, and direct conversations with peers.

H

Pricing

Hourly Rate

The fee you charge per hour of work — the most common pricing model for new freelancers, though not necessarily the most lucrative. To calculate a sustainable hourly rate, start with your target annual income, divide by the number of billable hours you realistically expect to work per year (typically 1,000–1,200 for a solo freelancer), then add 30–40% to cover taxes, slow periods, and business expenses. The fundamental problem with hourly pricing is that it punishes expertise: the faster you get at your craft, the less you earn per project. This is why experienced freelancers migrate toward fixed-price, value-based, or retainer models. If you use hourly pricing, always estimate hours conservatively and track your time with a tool like Toggl.

Client Management

Handover Document

A document prepared at the end of a project that summarises everything the client needs to know to use, maintain, or build on what you've created — logins, file locations, technical instructions, ongoing maintenance tasks, and any known issues. A thorough handover document demonstrates professionalism and reduces the volume of post-project support requests you receive. It also protects you legally by documenting that you completed and transferred the agreed deliverables. Some freelancers include one handover document in their project fee; others charge separately for comprehensive documentation packages. Either way, it's an underrated tool for generating referrals because it leaves a lasting positive impression.

I

Finance

Invoice

A formal billing document sent to a client requesting payment for services rendered, listing the work completed, the amount owed, payment due date, accepted payment methods, and your business details. A proper invoice should also include a unique invoice number (for your records and theirs), the client's legal business name, the services or deliverables covered, and any applicable taxes. Send invoices immediately upon completing work — delays in invoicing directly delay payment. Include late payment terms on every invoice so clients know exactly what happens if they miss the due date. Use invoicing software (or the FreelanceOS invoice tracker) to monitor which invoices are outstanding at all times.

Contract

Intellectual Property (IP)

The legal rights attached to creative works, inventions, and proprietary content you produce — including copyright, trademarks, and patents. As a freelancer, you automatically own the IP of everything you create unless a contract explicitly transfers those rights to the client. This is one of the most commercially significant clauses in any freelance contract, yet it's routinely overlooked or misunderstood. Always clarify IP ownership before starting work: does the client want a license to use your work, or full assignment of all rights? License arrangements can be more lucrative because you can license the same IP multiple times, while assignment is a one-time transfer.

Contract

Indemnification Clause

A contract provision where one party agrees to protect the other from financial losses, legal claims, or damages arising from specified circumstances. In freelance contracts, indemnification clauses typically state that you will not be held responsible for losses the client suffers if they misuse your work, and that the client will not hold you liable for their business decisions. Overly broad indemnification clauses can expose you to significant financial risk — if a client's contract requires you to indemnify them against any and all claims, you could theoretically be liable for losses far exceeding your project fee. Always read indemnification clauses carefully, and consider capping your liability to the amount of your project fee.

Business

Income Diversification

The practice of generating revenue from multiple sources — different clients, income streams, or product types — rather than relying on a single client or revenue channel. Income diversification reduces the risk that losing one client causes a catastrophic revenue drop. A well-diversified freelance business might include two or three retainer clients, occasional project work, a passive income product, and affiliate revenue. The rule of thumb: no single client should represent more than 30–40% of your total revenue. Above that threshold, you're not a freelancer anymore — you're a de facto employee without the benefits, and losing that client could devastate your business overnight.

K

Contract

Kill Fee

A contractually specified payment you receive if the client cancels a project after work has already begun. Typically calculated as a percentage of the total project fee — 25% for early-stage cancellations, up to 50% for mid-project terminations, and sometimes 100% for cancellations after delivery. The kill fee compensates you for work completed and for the opportunity cost of the time you blocked out — time that could have been allocated to another paying project. Many freelancers discover they need a kill fee only after a client cancels without it, leaving them with nothing for weeks of work. Include the kill fee clause in your contract template so it's always there, even for projects that seem rock-solid.

Business

KPIs (Key Performance Indicators)

Measurable metrics used to evaluate whether a project, campaign, or business activity is achieving its goals. In a client context, KPIs might include website conversion rates, email open rates, search rankings, or revenue generated by the work you produced. Always agree on KPIs at the start of every project, before work begins — not at the end when the client is deciding whether they got value. Defined KPIs give you objective proof of success, make it easier to justify your fees, and give you concrete data to feature in case studies. For your own business, track KPIs like monthly revenue, billable hours, client retention rate, and average project value.

L

Finance

Late Payment Fee

A penalty charge automatically applied to invoices that are not paid by their due date — typically expressed as a percentage of the outstanding amount per month (e.g., 1.5–2% per 30 days) or as a flat fee. Including a late payment fee clause in your contract and your invoice is not just about the money — it's a powerful signal that you operate a professional business and that your payment terms are not suggestions. The mere existence of a late payment fee significantly improves on-time payment rates, because clients know there are financial consequences to delay. Always state the late payment fee explicitly on every invoice, even if you choose not to enforce it every time.

Business

Lead Generation

The process of attracting and identifying prospective clients who might be interested in hiring you — through content marketing, social media, referrals, SEO, cold outreach, speaking, or platform presence. Lead generation is one of the most time-intensive parts of freelancing, especially early in your career when your reputation and referral network are not yet established. The most sustainable lead generation strategy is content that compounds over time — a portfolio site with SEO-optimised case studies, a consistent LinkedIn presence, or articles that rank for terms your ideal clients search. Paid lead generation (like ads) works but requires ongoing investment; organic methods take longer to build but pay off indefinitely.

Business

LinkedIn Presence

Your professional identity and content activity on LinkedIn — the world's largest B2B professional network and one of the most effective organic lead generation channels for freelancers. A strong LinkedIn presence includes a compelling headline that describes who you help and what results you deliver (not just your job title), a detailed about section with social proof, and regular posts that demonstrate your expertise. LinkedIn posts that provide genuinely useful insight attract inbound enquiries from prospects who are pre-sold on your expertise before they even contact you. Consistency matters more than volume: one high-value post per week outperforms five mediocre ones.

M

Finance

Milestone Payment

A payment structure where the total project fee is split into multiple instalments, each tied to the completion of a specific stage or deliverable — for example, 30% upfront, 40% at the mid-point review, and 30% upon final delivery. Milestone payments protect both parties: the client doesn't pay everything upfront before seeing any work, and you don't do everything before receiving any payment. For longer projects, milestone payments are essential for maintaining healthy cash flow and ensuring that if a client disappears mid-project, you've already been paid for the work completed. Always tie each milestone payment to a clear, documented deliverable — not to a time period.

Business

MOQ (Minimum Order Quantity)

The smallest project size or minimum monthly spend you will accept from a client. Setting a minimum order quantity (or minimum project fee) prevents you from filling your calendar with small, low-value work that takes just as much admin overhead as larger projects. A minimum fee signals that you are a professional, not a budget option — and it filters out clients who are unlikely to value your work appropriately. Many experienced freelancers set a project minimum of $1,000–$3,000 as a floor, below which they simply do not take work. As your demand grows, raising your minimum is one of the fastest ways to increase average project value without working more hours.

Contract

Master Service Agreement (MSA)

A comprehensive overarching contract that establishes the general terms and conditions governing the entire relationship between you and a client — covering payment terms, IP ownership, confidentiality, dispute resolution, liability limits, and termination. Once an MSA is in place, individual projects can be kicked off with a simple Statement of Work that references the MSA, rather than renegotiating all terms each time. MSAs are particularly valuable for ongoing client relationships because they reduce legal friction on each new project and give both parties a clear framework for how disagreements are handled. If a large client presents you with their MSA, read every clause carefully before signing.

N

Contract

NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement)

A legally binding contract in which one or both parties agree not to disclose confidential information shared during the course of a working relationship. Clients often request NDAs before sharing sensitive business information — strategy documents, unreleased product details, financial data, or client lists. As a freelancer, signing an NDA is generally reasonable and expected; just make sure you understand what "confidential" means in the specific agreement. Watch for overly broad NDAs that prevent you from mentioning the client in your portfolio — this is particularly common with large companies and can significantly hamper your ability to showcase your best work. Negotiate a clause allowing you to list them as a client even if you can't show the work itself.

Finance

Net 30/60/90

Payment terms specifying that full payment is due 30, 60, or 90 days after the invoice date. Net-30 is the most common B2B standard; net-60 and net-90 are often imposed by larger corporations with centralised accounts payable departments. Net-60 and net-90 terms can seriously damage a freelancer's cash flow — if you complete a $5,000 project and wait 90 days for payment, you're effectively lending that money to the client for three months. Always try to negotiate net-30 or shorter, especially with new clients. When you must accept longer terms, factor the waiting period into your fee, and use milestone payments to ensure some cash arrives sooner.

Business

Niche Specialisation

The deliberate choice to focus your freelance services on a specific industry, audience, or type of work — rather than being a generalist who serves anyone. A freelance copywriter who specialises in SaaS companies commands higher rates than a general copywriter, because clients pay a premium for domain expertise. The counterintuitive reality of niching is that narrowing your offer expands your market — being known as the best at one specific thing generates more referrals and inbound leads than being "pretty good" at everything. The most profitable freelancers typically have both a service niche (what they do) and an industry niche (who they do it for). The narrower the niche, the less price competition you face.

O

Client Management

Onboarding

The structured process of welcoming a new client and setting up the working relationship before project work begins — including collecting necessary information, getting contract and payment sorted, establishing communication preferences, setting expectations, and sharing your process. A smooth onboarding process makes clients feel confident they made the right choice and reduces the volume of questions and clarifications needed mid-project. Systematising your onboarding with a checklist or template reduces the time you spend on admin for every new client, ensures nothing gets missed, and creates a consistently professional first impression. Poor onboarding is one of the most common causes of chaotic, scope-creep-heavy projects.

Business

Opportunity Cost

The value of the best alternative you give up when you choose one course of action over another. In freelancing, opportunity cost matters when evaluating whether to take on a low-paying project: if accepting it means you're too busy to pursue or complete a higher-paying opportunity, the real cost of the low-paying project is the revenue you didn't earn from the better option. Opportunity cost is also why filling your calendar at any price is a mistake. A freelancer who is fully booked at $60/hour has zero capacity to pursue $150/hour clients. Protecting bandwidth for higher-value opportunities is a deliberate strategic choice, not laziness.

Contract

Out-of-Scope Work

Tasks or deliverables requested by the client that fall outside what was agreed in the original Statement of Work or contract. Out-of-scope requests are the primary driver of scope creep, and your contract should explicitly address how they're handled. The correct response to any out-of-scope request is a change order — a brief written document describing the additional work, the additional fee, and the impact on the timeline. Never complete out-of-scope work as a "favour" without documenting it, because what starts as a favour becomes an expectation, and then the client starts treating it as included. Respond to out-of-scope requests promptly and professionally: "Happy to add that — I'll send a change order for the additional fee and timeline adjustment."

P

Business

Proposal

A formal document presented to a prospective client that outlines your understanding of their problem, your proposed solution, the scope of work, timeline, fee, and terms. A strong proposal is a sales document as much as a project document — it should tell a story that goes from "here's the problem you have" to "here's exactly how I solve it and why I'm uniquely positioned to do so." Proposals that restate the client's situation back to them in their own words win significantly more often than generic proposals that lead with your credentials. Keep proposals focused: a 3-page proposal that clearly addresses the client's specific situation outperforms a 20-page template every time.

Client Management

Project Brief

A document that captures all the essential information needed to execute a project successfully — goals, audience, tone, existing assets, constraints, examples of what the client likes, and specific requirements. The project brief is typically completed or co-created with the client before work begins. A comprehensive project brief dramatically reduces the number of revision rounds needed because it aligns your understanding with the client's vision before a single hour of billable work is invested. If a client can't or won't complete a brief, that's your cue to ask more probing questions on the discovery call and document the answers yourself — then send them back to the client for confirmation before starting.

Business

Portfolio

A curated collection of your past work showcasing your skills, style, and the results you've achieved for clients — typically hosted on a personal website, Behance, LinkedIn, or a PDF. Your portfolio is your most powerful sales tool: it answers the client's question "can this person do what I need?" before they ever speak to you. A great portfolio leads with outcomes, not just aesthetics — not just "here's a logo I designed" but "here's a rebrand that increased this company's conversion rate by 23%." Three excellent, well-documented case studies outperform twenty undescribed samples. Keep your portfolio updated and ruthlessly cut anything that no longer represents your best work or ideal client type.

Finance

Passive Income

Revenue generated from assets that work while you're not actively trading time for money — digital products, templates, online courses, affiliate commissions, licensing, or stock assets. Passive income is the most effective antidote to the freelance feast-or-famine cycle because it generates revenue regardless of whether you have active client projects. The term "passive" is slightly misleading: building a passive income stream requires significant upfront investment of time and effort. But once the asset exists and is discoverable, it continues generating revenue with minimal ongoing work. Even $300–$500/month in passive income provides a meaningful financial cushion that reduces the desperation to accept bad clients during slow periods.

Finance

Payment Terms

The specific conditions under which you expect to be paid — including when payment is due (net-7, net-30, etc.), how it should be made (bank transfer, card, PayPal, etc.), any deposit required upfront, and any penalties for late payment. Payment terms must appear in your contract, your proposal, and on every invoice you send. Inconsistency in stating payment terms is one of the most common causes of late payment: if the contract says net-30 but the invoice says "due upon receipt," clients default to whichever interpretation benefits them. Standardise your payment terms across every touchpoint. For new clients, requiring a 30–50% deposit upfront before starting is entirely professional and widely accepted.

Business

Pitch Deck

A visual presentation — typically in PowerPoint, Keynote, or Google Slides — used to pitch your services, a project concept, or a partnership proposal to a client or stakeholder group. Freelancers in strategy, creative, or consulting roles often use pitch decks for large contracts or agency relationships. A strong pitch deck tells a story in 10–15 slides: the problem, the opportunity, your approach, your credentials, your team (if applicable), your proposed solution, and a clear ask. Avoid cramming slides with text; use visuals, data, and headlines. A pitch deck is not the same as a proposal — the deck is the narrative, the proposal is the detailed follow-up document.

R

Business

Retainer

A recurring monthly fee paid by a client in exchange for a defined amount of your time or services each month. The retainer model is the most financially stable form of freelance income because it provides predictable, recurring revenue that doesn't depend on constantly winning new projects. A single retainer client paying $2,000/month provides $24,000/year of base revenue before any project work — the equivalent of winning 24 small one-off projects annually. Retainers work best when the client has ongoing, consistent needs (content, maintenance, social media, bookkeeping, etc.) and when the scope is clearly defined so the retainer doesn't slowly expand without additional compensation.

Pricing

Rate Sheet

A document listing your pricing for different services, deliverable types, or time commitments — often more detailed than a rate card, specifying tier-based pricing, rush fees, licensing rates, or volume discounts. Rate sheets are particularly common for photographers, videographers, editors, and other creative professionals who have complex pricing structures depending on usage rights and deliverable specifications. Having a rate sheet prevents you from pricing on the fly, which almost always leads to undercharging because you haven't fully thought through all the time and resources involved. Review and update your rate sheet at least annually — many freelancers underestimate how much their market rate has increased as they gain experience.

Finance

Revenue

The total income generated by your freelance business before deducting taxes or expenses — the top-line number representing all money received from clients during a given period. Revenue is different from profit (revenue minus expenses) and from cash flow (when money actually arrives in your account). Tracking revenue monthly allows you to identify growth trends, seasonal patterns, and whether your pricing strategy is working. Many freelancers focus only on individual project fees without tracking cumulative revenue — this makes it harder to set annual income targets, plan for tax, or recognise when you're growing or contracting. Record all revenue in a dedicated business account and review it monthly.

Business

Referral Fee

A commission paid to someone who refers a paying client to you — typically 10–20% of the first project fee or the first month's retainer. Referral fee arrangements incentivise your network to actively send clients your way and reward those who do. Referrals are the highest-converting form of lead generation because the prospect arrives pre-sold on you through a trusted recommendation. Some freelancers formalise referral arrangements with agents, fellow freelancers, or complementary service providers; others keep it informal and pay a surprise "thank you" fee when a referral converts. If you set up formal referral arrangements, document the terms in writing — including whether fees apply to repeat work from the referred client.

Contract

Revision Round

A defined cycle in which you present work to the client, receive their feedback, and make specified changes. The number of revision rounds included in a project fee is one of the most important terms to define in your SOW, because unlimited revisions are one of the most common paths to unprofitable projects. A common approach is two included revision rounds with additional rounds billed at an hourly rate. Be specific about what a "revision" means: a round of revisions is structured feedback on a complete draft, not an ongoing daily dialogue or incremental micro-changes. Clients who have clearly defined revision limits tend to think their feedback through more carefully before submitting it.

S

Contract

Scope Creep

The gradual expansion of a project beyond the originally agreed-upon deliverables — usually without additional pay. It starts small: "Can you just tweak the logo?" or "Could we add one more page?" — but compounds quickly into hours or days of unbilled work. The antidote is a detailed Statement of Work that defines exactly what is included and explicitly what is not. When scope creep occurs — and it will — use it as a trigger to send a change order, document the additional request, and renegotiate the project fee before proceeding. Saying "I'd love to help with that — let me send you a change order" is professional, not difficult. Clients who respect you will accept it; clients who don't are revealing something important about how the rest of the relationship will go.

Contract

SOW (Statement of Work)

A detailed document that defines exactly what work will be performed, what deliverables will be produced, the timeline, payment terms, revision rounds, out-of-scope exclusions, and any other project-specific conditions. The SOW is the most important document in any freelance project — it is the legally binding description of what the client is paying for and what you are agreeing to produce. A vague SOW is the root cause of most freelance disputes, scope creep situations, and non-payment conflicts. Write your SOW with the mindset that if you and the client disagree on something mid-project, this document is what you'll both refer to. When in doubt, add more specificity — not less.

Contract

SLA (Service Level Agreement)

A formal commitment within a contract specifying the minimum standard of service you will deliver — including response times, availability, turnaround times, and uptime or performance metrics. SLAs are most common in technical freelancing (web development, IT support, hosting management) and ongoing service retainers. An SLA protects the client by defining what "good service" means in measurable terms, and it protects you by setting clear expectations upfront. If you're asked to include an SLA, make sure every metric is realistically achievable — committing to a 2-hour response time when you're a solo freelancer working across time zones is a contract clause you'll routinely violate.

Business

Subcontracting

Hiring another freelancer or agency to complete a portion of work that you've contracted to deliver to a client. Subcontracting allows you to take on larger projects than you could handle alone, offer services outside your core expertise, or scale your output during busy periods. Check your client contracts before subcontracting — many include clauses that require written approval before you involve third parties. When you subcontract, you remain fully responsible for the quality and delivery of the work; you can't blame the subcontractor in a dispute with your client. Have your own contract with your subcontractors, including IP assignment and confidentiality clauses aligned with your client contract's requirements.

Business

Sole Trader

The simplest legal business structure for a self-employed individual, where you operate the business yourself under your own name or a trading name, with no legal separation between you and the business. As a sole trader, all profits are yours — and so is all personal liability for debts or legal claims against the business. Sole trader status is the default for most freelancers starting out, and it's administratively simple — you declare income on your personal tax return and pay self-employment taxes. As your income grows, many freelancers transition to a limited company structure (LLC, Ltd, etc.) to limit personal liability and access potential tax efficiencies. Consult an accountant when your revenue approaches a threshold where incorporating makes financial sense.

T

Business

Time Tracking

The practice of recording how you spend your working hours — specifically which client, project, or task each block of time is allocated to. Time tracking is essential for hourly billing (to produce accurate invoices), for fixed-price projects (to understand your true effective rate and whether you quoted correctly), and for general business intelligence (to see where your non-billable time actually goes). Most freelancers who start tracking time are surprised to discover how much is consumed by admin, email, and revisions they aren't billing for. Tools like Toggl Track, Clockify, or Harvest make tracking easy. Even a week of honest time tracking will tell you more about your business than months of guesswork.

Contract

Terms & Conditions

The general legal provisions that govern the business relationship between you and your clients — covering payment terms, intellectual property, liability, dispute resolution, termination, governing law, and other standard protections. Terms & conditions are often included as a standard attachment to your contract or SOW rather than negotiated project by project. Having standard T&Cs in place saves significant time because you don't need to redraft legal protection from scratch for every new client. Many freelancers use a lawyer-reviewed template T&Cs document and update it annually. Send your T&Cs with every new client engagement and get written confirmation that the client has read and accepted them before starting work.

Client Management

Trial Project

A small, paid, low-commitment project used to test the working relationship between you and a new client before committing to a larger engagement. A well-structured trial project allows both parties to evaluate compatibility — communication style, feedback quality, payment reliability, and project management expectations — without the risk of a major commitment. A trial project should always be paid at your full rate, not discounted as a "trial." If a client insists on a free or heavily discounted trial, that's a strong signal about how they view your value and how the relationship is likely to go. Agree upfront that completing the trial successfully triggers a specific next step.

U

Business

Upsell

Offering a client an additional, higher-value service or upgrade to what they originally purchased — either at the point of sale or during an ongoing engagement when you identify an adjacent need. Upselling is not manipulative when it's genuinely relevant: a web designer spotting that a client's copywriting is weak and offering their copywriting service is solving a real problem. Upselling to existing clients is dramatically more efficient than finding new clients — the client already trusts you, knows your quality, and has no acquisition cost. Always position upsells as solutions to problems you've observed, not as an attempt to increase your own revenue. "I noticed X is costing you Y — would you like me to address that as part of an expanded scope?" is the right framing.

Business

Unique Value Proposition (UVP)

A clear, concise statement that communicates what makes you distinctly valuable to your ideal client — specifically, what problem you solve, for whom, and why you're the best person to solve it compared to the alternatives. A strong UVP is specific, not generic: "I'm a freelance designer" is not a UVP. "I help SaaS companies reduce churn through UX improvements — I've done it for eight clients with an average 18% improvement in activation rates" is a UVP. Your UVP should be instantly legible on your LinkedIn headline, website homepage, and the opening line of any proposal. If a prospect can't understand in 10 seconds why you're different from every other freelancer in your field, you don't have a UVP yet.

Finance

Utilisation Rate

The percentage of your available working hours that are spent on billable client work — calculated as billable hours divided by total working hours, expressed as a percentage. A utilisation rate of 70% means 70% of your working hours are being billed to clients. Utilisation rate is one of the most important metrics for freelance financial health, because it directly determines your actual income relative to your theoretical maximum. Below 60% usually means you're spending too much time on non-billable activities like sales and admin. Above 80% is the point where you risk burnout because there's no bandwidth for business development, professional development, or rest. Aim for 65–75% as a sustainable target.

V

Pricing

Value-Based Pricing

A pricing model where you charge based on the measurable value you deliver to the client — not the hours you spend or the going market rate. If your strategic work helps a client generate $200,000 in new revenue, charging $500 for it (the hourly equivalent for a few days of work) is a dramatic undervaluation of the outcome. Value-based pricing requires you to quantify your impact — to understand and articulate what the work is worth to the client's business, not just what it costs you to produce it. It's the most profitable pricing model available to experienced freelancers, but it requires the confidence to have direct conversations about business goals, revenue impact, and ROI. Start by asking every new client: "What does success look like, and what is that worth to your business?"

Client Management

Vetting a Client

The process of evaluating a prospective client before agreeing to work with them — assessing their legitimacy, financial reliability, communication style, project clarity, and fit with how you work. Vetting is not just due diligence on a company; it's also a qualitative assessment of whether this particular human will be a pleasant and professional person to work with. Red flags include: unwillingness to sign a contract, resistance to paying a deposit, vague project briefs, excessive urgency, requests for spec work, and talking negatively about previous freelancers. The cost of taking on a bad client — in time, stress, and unpaid invoices — is almost always higher than the cost of passing on the work entirely. Vet every client, even if you're desperate for work.

W

Client Management

Whiteboard Session

A collaborative working session — in person or virtual — where you and the client (or team) work through a problem, strategy, or creative concept together in real time, often using a shared whiteboard tool or physical board. Whiteboard sessions are powerful for discovery, strategy development, and workshop-format engagements because they generate alignment and buy-in from participants. In a freelance context, whiteboard sessions are a premium, billable service that should be priced accordingly — not offered as a free "kickoff call." A well-facilitated 3-hour strategy session can command $500–$2,000+ depending on your niche and the complexity of what you're solving. Always provide a written summary of decisions and next steps after every whiteboard session.

Contract

Work-for-Hire

A legal doctrine where a work created by a freelancer is considered — from the moment of creation — to belong to the client rather than to the creator, because it was specifically commissioned under those terms. Work-for-hire is distinct from IP assignment in that ownership never passes through the creator's hands at all. Work-for-hire arrangements eliminate your ability to use the work in your portfolio or license it to others, which justifies charging a significant premium — typically 20–50% more than standard project fees. If a client's contract contains "work made for hire" language and they're not offering additional compensation for it, negotiate. Your creative output has ongoing commercial value, and permanently surrendering all rights to it is not a standard deliverable.

Z

Contract

Zero-Hours Contract

A contractual arrangement where an employer (or client) is not obligated to offer a minimum amount of work, and the worker is not obligated to accept any work offered. Zero-hours contracts are most common in the UK but exist in various forms globally. In a freelance context, they represent an informal "on-call" arrangement without guaranteed volume. Zero-hours arrangements benefit the client (maximum flexibility) at the expense of the freelancer (no income certainty). If you find yourself effectively operating on zero-hours terms — consistently available but with no guaranteed monthly income — it's worth reframing the conversation as a retainer discussion. A minimum monthly commitment from the client, even at reduced volume, is significantly more valuable than perpetual uncertainty. Know your BATNA before that conversation.

Frequently Asked Questions

The most common questions about freelance terminology — answered plainly.

1 What does net-30 mean in freelancing?

Net-30 means payment is due 30 days after the invoice date. It's the standard payment term in B2B and corporate contracts, and many large businesses will default to it regardless of what you specify if you don't state otherwise. As a freelancer, always specify your payment terms explicitly in writing — net-30 is reasonable for established clients with a good payment history, but net-7 or net-14 is more appropriate for new clients where you haven't yet established trust. When you offer net terms of any kind, always include a late payment fee clause — typically 1.5–2% per month on the outstanding balance — so there is a financial consequence for missing the deadline, not just an awkward email.

2 What is a kill fee in freelancing?

A kill fee is a contractually specified payment you receive if the client cancels the project after work has already begun. It's typically 25–50% of the total contract value, though some contracts scale it based on how far along the project was when it was cancelled. The kill fee compensates you for two things: the work you already completed, and the opportunity cost of the time you blocked out for this project — time that could have been spent on a different paying client. Many freelancers discover they need a kill fee clause only after experiencing a cancellation without one. Include it in your standard contract template so it's always there, even for projects that seem low-risk.

3 What is scope creep?

Scope creep is the gradual expansion of a project beyond its originally agreed boundaries, usually without additional compensation. It rarely arrives as a dramatic demand — it starts with small, reasonable-sounding requests: "Can you tweak this one section?" or "While you're in there, could you also...?" Each individual request seems minor, but they accumulate into hours or days of unpaid work. Preventing scope creep requires a detailed SOW that explicitly defines what is included — and, critically, what is not. When scope creep happens despite that (and it will), the correct professional response is a change order: document the additional request, attach a fee and timeline adjustment, and get approval before doing the work. Clients who respect you will accept this; clients who don't are telling you something important.

4 What does IP assignment mean in a freelance contract?

IP assignment (intellectual property assignment) means you formally transfer legal ownership of the work you create to the client. By default in most jurisdictions, copyright belongs to the creator — meaning you — the moment you create something. An IP assignment clause in a contract overrides this default and explicitly hands all rights to the client. The critical protection: always ensure IP assignment is conditional on receipt of full payment, not on delivery of the work. This means if the client doesn't pay, you still own the work and they cannot legally use it — your intellectual property is your leverage. Some freelancers charge a premium specifically for IP assignment (versus licensing), which is entirely justified, as you're permanently surrendering rights that have ongoing commercial value.

5 What is a retainer in freelancing?

A retainer is a recurring monthly fee a client pays in exchange for a defined amount of your time or services each month. It's the most financially stable form of freelance income because it provides predictable revenue regardless of whether you're actively winning new projects. Retainers are sometimes called the "holy grail" of freelancing precisely because they reduce the feast-or-famine cycle that makes inconsistent project work so stressful. A well-structured retainer must clearly define scope: how many hours are included, what specific deliverables are expected, what response time commitments you're making, and what triggers additional billing beyond the retainer. Retainers without clear scope definitions tend to gradually expand without additional pay — the definition of scope creep at the relationship level.

6 What is value-based pricing?

Value-based pricing means charging based on the value you deliver to the client's business, not the hours you spend or the market rate for your service type. The fundamental logic: if your strategic copy generates $150,000 in new sales, charging the hourly equivalent of two days' work ($800) means the client captures $149,200 of the value you created. Value-based pricing corrects this by anchoring your fee to the client's outcome. It requires understanding the client's business goals well enough to quantify your impact — asking "what is this worth to your business if it works?" is not an aggressive question; it's essential information. Value-based pricing is the most profitable model available to experienced freelancers who have the confidence and portfolio to justify it.

7 How do I write a statement of work (SOW)?

A strong SOW should cover at minimum: project scope (a plain-English description of what you will do), deliverables (specific, measurable outputs with format and quantity), timeline (milestone dates and final deadline), revision rounds (exactly how many are included and what constitutes a revision), out-of-scope exclusions (explicit list of what is not included), payment terms (amount, schedule, method, late fee), and IP ownership (who owns what upon payment). The guiding principle: write it with the assumption that if you and the client ever disagree, this document is your referee. Vague SOWs cause most freelance disputes — "a website" is not a deliverable; "a 6-page responsive WordPress site with specified functionality, two rounds of revisions, and mobile optimisation" is.

8 What does billable hours mean?

Billable hours are the hours you actually charge to clients — as opposed to non-billable time spent on admin, business development, professional development, invoicing, email, and other overhead tasks that don't directly generate client revenue. Most freelancers find that only 60–70% of their working time is truly billable, which means your effective hourly rate is meaningfully lower than your stated rate. For example, charging $100/hour but only billing 5.5 of 8 working hours per day puts your effective rate at $68.75/hour — a significant gap. Tracking billable vs. non-billable time reveals this gap and gives you the data to either raise rates, reduce non-billable overhead, or restructure how you work. Tools like Toggl Track, Clockify, or the FreelanceOS time tracker make daily tracking low-friction.
