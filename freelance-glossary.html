The Freelance Glossary: 80 Essential Terms Defined (2026)

From accounts receivable to work for hire — every term you need to run a freelance business, explained in plain English with real examples.

80 Terms Defined A–W Coverage With Real Examples Updated 2026

A

3 terms

Accounts Receivable

Accounts receivable is money owed to you by clients for work already completed but not yet paid. It appears as an asset on your books and is a critical number to monitor: high AR with slow collections signals a cash flow problem. Tracking it regularly helps you identify chronically late clients and decide whether those relationships are worth maintaining.

Example: "I currently have $4,200 in accounts receivable — two invoices from last month that are past due."

Asset Handoff

An asset handoff is the formal process of delivering final files, source documents, login credentials, or other project outputs to your client at the end of an engagement. Specify handoff format, method (Dropbox, Drive, WeTransfer), and timeline in your contract. A clean, documented handoff prevents post-project disputes and creates a professional closing impression.

Example: "The asset handoff included the layered Figma file, all exported PNGs at 3x resolution, and a brand guidelines PDF."

At-Will Contract

An at-will contract is an agreement either party can terminate at any time without needing a specific reason. While it offers flexibility, pure at-will arrangements can leave you suddenly without income. Even if a contract is at-will, you should include a notice period clause (14–30 days) so neither party is blindsided by an abrupt ending.

Example: "Our arrangement was at-will, but I included a 30-day notice clause so neither of us could disappear overnight."

B

3 terms

Billable Hours

Billable hours are the hours you can directly charge a client for — time spent actively working on their project. Not all hours you work are billable: proposal writing, admin tasks, and professional development typically are not. Tracking billable vs. non-billable time is essential for calculating your real effective hourly rate and pricing future projects accurately.

Example: "I logged 22 billable hours on that project, but with research and email time I actually worked 31 hours total."

Business Expenses

Business expenses are costs you incur to operate your freelance business: software subscriptions, equipment, home office, professional courses, client-related travel. Most are tax-deductible, reducing your taxable income. Tracking them throughout the year — not just at tax time — saves significant money and stress. Most freelancers underclaim deductions.

Example: "My monthly business expenses include design software, Notion, accounting tools, and a coworking day pass — all deductible."

Business License

A business license is a government-issued permit authorizing you to legally operate a business in a specific city, county, or state. Requirements vary widely by location — some cities require even home-based freelancers to register. Fines for operating without a required license can exceed the cost of obtaining one. Check your local requirements before you start invoicing clients.

Example: "My city required a $75 home occupation permit as a business license before I could legally operate from home."

C

6 terms

Case Study

A case study is a detailed write-up of a client project highlighting the problem, your approach, and measurable results. Case studies are more persuasive than portfolios alone because they demonstrate process and outcomes, not just polished visuals. A single compelling case study can generate more inbound interest than months of social media posts.

Example: "My SaaS rebrand case study showed how a clearer identity helped the client increase trial signups by 34% — it's my best-performing portfolio piece."

Client Brief

A client brief is a document capturing the client's goals, requirements, target audience, timeline, and constraints before work begins. A thorough brief prevents misaligned expectations and midstream scope changes. Always require a completed brief — or send your own intake questionnaire — before starting any project. It protects both of you.

Example: "Before writing a single word, I sent the client a 12-question intake form to understand tone, audience, and competitive landscape."

Cold Outreach

Cold outreach is proactively contacting potential clients who don't know you and haven't expressed interest — cold emails, LinkedIn messages, or cold calls. Effective cold outreach is personalized, focuses on the prospect's problem rather than your credentials, and has a clear low-friction ask. Volume rarely works; specificity and relevance do.

Example: "I sent 20 personalized cold emails to DTC brands last month and landed 3 discovery calls — a 15% reply rate."

Commission

In freelancing, commission refers to either (a) being hired to create a specific work for a client (a "commissioned project"), or (b) earning a referral fee when you send business to another freelancer or agency. Referral commission rates typically range from 10–20% of the deal value and should always be documented in a written agreement.

Example: "I have a 15% referral commission agreement with a developer friend — when I refer clients to him, he pays me a cut."

Contract

A contract is a legally binding written agreement that defines the scope, deliverables, timeline, payment terms, revision limits, IP ownership, and termination conditions for a project. Every client engagement — regardless of how small or how well you know the client — needs a signed contract. Verbal agreements are nearly impossible to enforce. Build yours with the Contract Wizard.

Example: "A new client wanted to start immediately. I waited until the signed contract came back before doing any work."

CRM (Client Relationship Management)

A CRM is a system for tracking clients, leads, deal stages, communications, and follow-up actions. For freelancers, even a simple Notion-based CRM can prevent leads from slipping through the cracks and reveal patterns in your sales pipeline. The freelancers with stable income tend to have a reliable system for tracking every opportunity.

Example: "My Notion CRM shows every active lead, their status, last contact date, and projected deal value at a glance."

D

5 terms

Day Rate

A day rate is a fixed fee for a full day of work (typically 7–8 hours). Day rates are common in videography, photography, consulting, and on-site engagements. They simplify billing for short projects and protect you from hourly tracking overhead. Your day rate is typically calculated as your hourly rate multiplied by 7, with a slight discount for the volume commitment.

Example: "My day rate for on-site brand shoots is $1,200. Half-days are $750."

Deductions

Tax deductions are eligible business expenses that reduce your taxable income. Common freelance deductions include home office, internet, software, equipment, professional development, and health insurance premiums (in the US). Most freelancers underclaim — keeping clean records all year and working with a tax professional are two of the highest-ROI habits you can build.

Example: "After legitimate deductions for home office and software, my taxable income dropped by over $9,000."

Deliverable

A deliverable is a specific, tangible output you agree to produce — a logo file, a website page, a blog post, a video edit. Deliverables should be listed explicitly in your contract with format, quantity, and quality expectations defined. Vague deliverables are the single most common cause of scope disputes, revision overruns, and unhappy clients.

Example: "Project deliverables: 1 homepage design, 3 interior page templates, mobile prototype. Not included: copywriting or stock imagery."

Deposit

A deposit is an upfront payment — typically 25–50% of the total project fee — paid before work begins. Deposits weed out non-serious clients, provide coverage if a project is cancelled, and stabilize your cash flow during the project. Requiring a deposit is standard professional practice. Never begin significant work without one.

Example: "I require a 50% deposit before starting any project over $500. It has virtually eliminated last-minute cancellations."

Discovery Call

A discovery call is an initial conversation with a prospective client to understand their needs, goals, timeline, and budget — and to assess mutual fit. It is not free consulting; it is a screening conversation. Prepare specific questions, come with a clear agenda, and follow up with a written recap. A good discovery call saves hours of wasted proposal writing.

Example: "I use a 25-minute discovery call before writing any proposal. It tells me within 10 minutes whether this is a viable client."

E

2 terms

Estimate

An estimate is an informal, non-binding approximation of what a project will cost before the full scope is defined. Unlike a quote, an estimate doesn't lock in a price. Use estimates early in conversations to establish ballpark figures, and always clarify that your final price may change once you have a complete brief. Including an expiry date on estimates is also good practice.

Example: "Based on what you've described, my estimate is $3,000–$4,500. I'll give a firm quote after our discovery call and full brief."

Expenses

In a project context, expenses are costs you incur on a client's behalf that you pass through to them — stock photos, printing, software licenses purchased for the project, client travel. List these as a separate line item on your invoice. Define in your contract whether expenses are passed through at cost or with a markup (10–20% is typical for agencies).

Example: "I billed $240 in pass-through expenses — stock photos and a fonts license — on top of my design fee."

F

5 terms

Final Delivery

Final delivery is the formal transfer of completed, approved work to the client — the last milestone of a project. It should happen after all revisions are approved and the final invoice is paid in full. Make final delivery contingent on final payment in your contract; once you hand over the files, your primary piece of leverage is gone.

Example: "My contracts are explicit: source files transfer only after the final invoice is paid. No exceptions."

Freelance Invoice

A freelance invoice is a formal payment request sent to clients after work is delivered. It should include your name and business info, client details, a unique invoice number, itemized services with amounts, the total, payment due date, and payment method. Professional invoices get paid faster than informal emails. Build one in 60 seconds with the Invoice Builder.

Example: "Invoice #042 to Acme Corp: March retainer services, $2,500. Due April 14 via ACH."

Freelance Portfolio

A freelance portfolio is a curated collection of your best work shown to prospective clients. Strong portfolios show outcomes (context, problems solved, results achieved) rather than just final visuals. Quality beats quantity: five in-depth case studies outperform thirty shallow samples every time. Curate ruthlessly and keep it current.

Example: "My portfolio has 6 case studies — each one shows the brief, my process, the final work, and client results."

Freelance Rate

Your freelance rate is what you charge for your services, whether hourly, by project, or by retainer. It must cover living expenses, taxes, unpaid business time, and your desired profit margin. Most freelancers undercharge significantly when starting out. Use the Rate Calculator to determine a sustainable number you can confidently defend.

Example: "After running the numbers properly, I raised my hourly rate from $65 to $110 — and didn't lose a single client."

Freelance Retainer

A freelance retainer is an ongoing arrangement where a client pays a fixed monthly fee for a set amount of your time or a defined set of deliverables. Retainers create predictable income, reduce the time spent finding new clients, and deepen client relationships. Landing two or three retainer clients fundamentally changes the stability of a freelance business.

Example: "Three retainer clients pay me $5,400/month combined. That's my guaranteed baseline before any project work comes in."

G

1 term

Gross Income

Gross income is your total revenue before any deductions, taxes, or expenses are subtracted. It's the top-line number — what you invoiced and collected. Freelancers often confuse gross income with what they actually keep. After taxes (25–35%), business expenses, and time spent on non-billable tasks, your effective net can be significantly lower. Knowing both numbers is essential for realistic financial planning.

Example: "My gross income last year was $94,000, but after taxes and expenses my net was closer to $61,000."

H

2 terms

Hourly Rate

Your hourly rate is the price you charge per hour of work. It's the simplest pricing model but also the most limiting — it caps your income at hours worked and penalizes you for becoming more efficient. Most experienced freelancers shift toward project or value-based pricing. Even so, your hourly rate remains a useful internal benchmark for evaluating whether project-based bids are actually profitable.

Example: "I list my hourly rate as $130/hr on my website but primarily use it to backstop project quotes."

Holding Fee

A holding fee (also called a reservation fee) is a payment made by a client to reserve your calendar for a future project. It compensates you for turning away other work while holding the dates. Holding fees are typically non-refundable if the client cancels and credited toward the project fee if the work proceeds.

Example: "A client wanted to lock in two weeks in August, so I charged a $500 holding fee to secure those dates exclusively for them."

I

3 terms

Intellectual Property (IP)

Intellectual property refers to the legal ownership of original creative work — designs, code, writing, photography. By default in many jurisdictions, you own the IP of everything you create as a freelancer until you contractually transfer it. Always define IP ownership explicitly: does the client receive full copyright upon payment, or a usage license only? The answer dramatically affects your pricing.

Example: "My contract specifies that the client receives full copyright only after the final invoice is paid in full — not before."

Invoice

An invoice is a formal payment request you send after delivering work or reaching a billing milestone. It documents what was delivered, the agreed amount, and the payment deadline. Invoices are legal documents — keep copies of every invoice sent, and mark each as paid when payment clears. The Invoice Builder creates professional invoices in under 60 seconds.

Example: "I send invoices via email with a PDF attachment and follow up automatically 3 days before the due date."

Invoice Number

An invoice number is a unique sequential identifier assigned to each invoice (e.g., INV-001, INV-002). Invoice numbers are essential for tracking payments, referencing documents in client communications, and bookkeeping. Corporate clients also need them to process payments through their accounts payable systems. Always include a unique invoice number on every invoice you send.

Example: "When the client said payment was pending approval, I referenced Invoice #127 from April 3rd to confirm the specific document."

K

2 terms

Kickoff Call

A kickoff call is the first official working meeting after a contract is signed and deposit received. Use it to align on project goals, review the timeline, establish communication norms, and answer any open questions. Send a written recap within 24 hours. A well-run kickoff dramatically reduces confusion, scope disputes, and mid-project surprises.

Example: "Every new project starts with a 45-minute kickoff call. I send a written recap the same day so we're both aligned on paper."

Kill Fee

A kill fee is compensation you receive when a client cancels a project after work has begun. It compensates you for the income you turned down to take the project. Kill fees are typically 25–50% of the remaining project balance. Include a kill fee clause in every contract — without it, a cancelled project leaves you with nothing for the time already invested.

Example: "The client cancelled two weeks into a $6,000 project. My kill fee clause paid me 50% of the remaining $3,600 balance."

L

3 terms

Late Fee

A late fee is a penalty charge applied to invoices paid after the due date. Common rates are 1.5–2% per month on the overdue balance. Stating your late fee policy clearly in contracts and on invoices dramatically improves on-time payment. Most clients will never actually pay a late fee — the mere existence of the clause gets invoices paid faster.

Example: "My invoices state a 1.5% monthly late fee after 30 days. I've only had to apply it twice in three years."

Lead

A lead is a prospective client who has shown some interest in hiring you — via inbound inquiry, referral, or response to your outreach. Not all leads become clients; qualifying them early (budget, timeline, decision-maker, fit) is important. A healthy freelance business maintains a consistent flow of new leads each month so your pipeline never runs dry.

Example: "I got 7 new leads in March: 3 from referrals, 2 from my website, 2 from LinkedIn posts."

LLC (Limited Liability Company)

An LLC is a business structure that separates your personal assets from your business liabilities. If a client sues your LLC, your personal savings are generally protected. Many freelancers form an LLC once their income reaches $50–75k/year. LLCs also add credibility with enterprise clients. Formation costs $50–500 depending on state, with ongoing annual fees.

Example: "I formed an LLC in my second year of freelancing to protect personal assets and appear more professional to larger clients."

M

2 terms

Milestone

A milestone is a predefined project checkpoint at which a deliverable is due and/or a payment is triggered. Structuring projects with 2–4 milestones reduces your financial exposure if a client disappears and gives clients clear visibility into progress. Milestone-based payments are strongly recommended for any project over $1,500.

Example: "The website project had 3 milestones: signed contract (40%), homepage approval (30%), final build delivery (30%)."

Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)

MRR is the total predictable income you receive every month from retainers, subscriptions, or repeating client arrangements. It is the most important metric for freelance financial stability. A strong MRR base means your income doesn't collapse every time a project ends. Building MRR is the fastest path from feast-and-famine to sustainable freelancing.

Example: "My MRR is $6,200 from four retainer clients. Everything else I earn each month is upside."

N

5 terms

NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement)

An NDA prohibits one or both parties from sharing confidential information with third parties. Clients may ask you to sign one before sharing sensitive business data; you may ask a client to sign one if you're sharing proprietary processes. Read NDAs carefully — some include broad non-compete language that could limit your ability to work with similar clients in the future.

Example: "The startup asked me to sign an NDA before sharing their product roadmap. I had a lawyer review it before signing."

Net 15

Net 15 is a payment term meaning the invoice is due 15 days after the issue date. It's a short payment window, appropriate for smaller projects or established client relationships with strong payment history. If a client repeatedly misses Net 15 terms, consider requiring a deposit upfront or shortening to Net 7 for that client.

Example: "I use Net 15 for recurring clients who pay reliably. New clients get Net 7 until they've proven their reliability."

Net 30

Net 30 means payment is due 30 days after the invoice date. It's standard for larger companies and corporate clients but can strain cash flow when you have several outstanding invoices. To accelerate collections, consider an early payment discount: "2/10 Net 30" means 2% off if the client pays within 10 days.

Example: "My enterprise client is Net 30, so I invoice on the 1st of every month and expect payment by the 30th."

Net 60

Net 60 means payment is due 60 days after invoicing. Common with large corporations and government clients, it's generally unfavorable for freelancers. If a client insists on Net 60, price in a slow-pay premium (5–10%) and require a substantial upfront deposit to maintain cash flow during the long wait for payment.

Example: "The Fortune 500 client was Net 60, so I required a 40% deposit upfront and added a 10% slow-pay premium to my rate."

Niche

Your niche is the specific intersection of service, industry, or client type you're known for. "Freelance copywriter" is broad. "Email copywriter for e-commerce brands" is a niche. Niching down typically allows you to charge more, get referred more easily, and build authority faster. Generalists compete on price; specialists compete on expertise — and expertise wins.

Example: "I niched down to Webflow development for SaaS startups. My rates went up 40% within six months of making that decision."

O

3 terms

Offboarding

Offboarding is the process of properly wrapping up a client engagement — final file delivery, collecting a testimonial, issuing the final invoice, and closing the relationship with intentionality. A great offboarding experience is one of the highest-ROI activities in freelancing: it generates referrals, repeat work, and positive word-of-mouth. Most freelancers skip it entirely.

Example: "My offboarding checklist: final delivery, feedback form, testimonial request, referral ask, invoice. Takes 20 minutes. Generates results for months."

Onboarding

Client onboarding is the process of getting a new client set up and aligned for a successful project — collecting assets, gaining tool access, setting expectations, and scheduling the kickoff. A well-documented onboarding process signals professionalism and prevents the back-and-forth confusion that derails projects in their first week. Templates make this fast and repeatable.

Example: "My onboarding process: welcome email, Notion client portal access, kickoff call invitation — all sent within 24 hours of the signed contract."

Out-of-Scope Work

Out-of-scope work is anything a client requests that falls outside what was agreed in the original contract. Completing it for free trains clients to expect unlimited additions and erodes your margin. The professional response is to acknowledge the request, write a change order, and price it separately. "That falls outside our original scope — I can add it for $X" is a normal, professional sentence.

Example: "The client asked for two extra landing pages after the project started. I wrote a change order for the additional scope."

P

6 terms

Payment Terms

Payment terms define when and how you get paid: deposit percentage, milestone schedule, final payment timing, accepted payment methods, and due dates. Clear payment terms in your contract and on every invoice are the most effective single change you can make to improve cash flow. Vague terms lead to delayed payments and uncomfortable chaser conversations.

Example: "My terms: 50% deposit before work starts, 50% upon final delivery. Wire or ACH only. Net 7 on the final invoice."

Personal Brand

Your personal brand is your professional reputation — the associations people make when they hear your name. For freelancers, a strong personal brand generates inbound leads, enables premium pricing, and creates career resilience. It's built through consistent, visible expertise: a professional website, content creation, and repeatedly delivering work that people talk about.

Example: "My personal brand as 'the Notion-focused copywriter' has driven more inbound leads than all my cold outreach combined."

Portfolio

A portfolio is your showcase of completed work, used to demonstrate capability to prospective clients. For most creative freelancers it's a website; for developers it includes live projects and repos. Keep your portfolio current, remove weak older work, and tailor it toward the type of projects you want to attract more of. The work you show is the work you'll be hired for.

Example: "I narrowed my portfolio to fintech and SaaS projects only. Within 90 days, 80% of inbound leads were from those sectors."

Progress Payment

A progress payment is an installment tied to a project milestone rather than all upfront or all at completion. Breaking a project into 2–4 progress payments reduces your risk of non-payment and aligns client investment with visible progress. On larger projects, progress payments are standard professional practice rather than an exception to negotiate.

Example: "The $12k branding project: $4k at signing, $4k at concept approval, $4k at final delivery."

Proposal

A freelance proposal is a document presenting your understanding of a client's problem, your proposed solution, timeline, and price. It's your key sales document. A strong proposal demonstrates that you listened, addresses the client's specific situation, and makes a clear case for your approach. Generic proposals lose to specific ones every time. Use the Proposal Generator to build yours fast.

Example: "My proposal always opens with a 3-sentence description of the client's problem as I understand it. It signals that I was paying attention."

Purchase Order (PO)

A purchase order is a formal document issued by a client organization authorizing you to begin work and committing them to pay your invoice. Corporate clients use POs through their procurement systems. When a client provides a PO number, reference it on your invoice. Always verify the PO amount matches your agreed fee before starting work.

Example: "The corporate client's AP team required me to reference PO #8842-B on all invoices before they could process payment."

Q

2 terms

Quarterly Taxes

Quarterly estimated taxes are payments freelancers in the US make four times per year to the IRS (and state tax authorities) to cover their tax liability as income is earned. Due dates are typically April 15, June 15, September 15, and January 15. Missing them can result in underpayment penalties. A common rule of thumb: set aside 25–30% of every payment received in a dedicated savings account.

Example: "I keep a separate tax savings account and move 28% of every invoice payment into it. Quarterly tax bills are never a surprise."

Quote

A quote is a formal, binding price offer for a defined scope of work. Unlike an estimate (which is approximate), a quote locks in your price for specific deliverables. Most clients need a written quote before approving a purchase. Mark quotes with an expiry date ("valid for 30 days") to protect yourself from honoring old pricing as your rates increase.

Example: "My quote for the e-commerce build is $6,400. This quote is valid for 30 days based on the scope in your brief."

R

5 terms

Rate Card

A rate card lists your services and associated prices, sometimes shared publicly on your website or sent to agencies you work with. It establishes that your pricing is structured rather than negotiated on the fly. Rate cards pre-qualify leads (anyone who balks at your rates self-selects out), save time on proposals, and signal professionalism.

Example: "I publish a rate card on my website for standard services. It pre-qualifies leads before they even contact me."

Referral

A referral is when an existing client, colleague, or contact recommends you to someone who then becomes a client. Referrals convert at dramatically higher rates than cold leads because they carry built-in trust. Always ask for referrals proactively after successful projects, and consider offering referral incentives to your best advocates. A referral program is one of the most underutilized marketing tools in freelancing.

Example: "About 60% of my new business comes from referrals. I ask at every offboarding: 'Is there anyone else you'd recommend me to?'"

Retainer

A retainer is an ongoing monthly arrangement where a client pays a fixed fee for access to your services — either a set number of hours or defined deliverables per month. Retainers are the cornerstone of financial stability in freelancing. Predictable monthly income enables better planning, more reinvestment in the business, and reduced time spent on constant client acquisition. See the full guide at freelance-retainer-template.html.

Example: "My primary retainer client pays $2,200/month for 10 hours of strategy work. It's my most efficient and most profitable arrangement."

Revision

A revision is a client-requested change to delivered work. Without limits in your contract, clients can request unlimited revisions indefinitely. Standard practice is to include 2–3 revision rounds in your project fee, with additional rounds billed at your hourly rate. Clearly define what constitutes a revision versus a new requirement to prevent boundary erosion.

Example: "My design contracts include 2 revision rounds. The client requested a third; I billed it at $95/hr."

Rush Fee

A rush fee is a premium charge for work requiring faster-than-standard turnaround. It compensates you for rescheduling other work, working outside normal hours, and the stress of compressed timelines. Rush fees typically run 25–100% above your standard rate depending on urgency. Never waive a rush fee as a goodwill gesture — it teaches clients that urgency is free.

Example: "The client needed the deck redesigned in 24 hours. Normal rate plus 50% rush fee: $975 total. They said yes immediately."

S

6 terms

Scope

Scope defines exactly what is and is not included in a project — the specific deliverables, formats, number of pages or features, revision rounds, and any explicit exclusions. A clearly written scope is the foundation of every successful client engagement. The vagueness of the scope is directly proportional to the likelihood of conflict. Define it explicitly, in writing, before work begins.

Example: "Scope: 1 homepage design, 3 interior templates, mobile-responsive, WordPress build. Not included: copywriting, stock photos, or SEO."

Scope Creep

Scope creep is the gradual expansion of a project beyond the original agreement — typically through small incremental requests that individually seem minor but collectively consume significant unbilled hours. Prevent it with a clear contract, a habit of writing change orders for additions, and the professional willingness to say "that's outside our scope — here's what it would cost to add."

Example: "What was scoped as 5 pages slowly grew to 12 with no change orders. That's scope creep, and it cost me 40 hours of uncompensated work."

Self-Employment Tax

Self-employment tax is the 15.3% US tax covering Social Security and Medicare for self-employed individuals. As a freelancer, you pay both the employee and employer portions. You can deduct half of it on your federal return, but you must account for it when pricing your work. Combined with income tax, most US freelancers should budget 28–35% of gross income for taxes.

Example: "On top of income tax, I pay self-employment tax on every dollar earned. I budget for a combined 30% tax rate across all federal and state obligations."

Sole Proprietor

A sole proprietor is an individual operating a business without a separate legal entity. It's the default structure for new freelancers — no registration required in most states, file taxes on Schedule C. The downside is unlimited personal liability. Most freelancers graduate to an LLC once income is consistent, though the decision depends on your specific situation and risk tolerance.

Example: "I freelanced as a sole proprietor for two years, then formed an LLC when I crossed $60k/year and wanted personal liability protection."

Statement of Work (SOW)

A Statement of Work is a formal, detailed document that defines the scope, deliverables, timeline, milestones, responsibilities, and payment terms for a specific project. More comprehensive than a basic contract, an SOW is common for larger or longer-term engagements. A signed SOW is your primary defense if a dispute arises over what was agreed.

Example: "Before the 3-month content strategy engagement, both parties signed an 8-page Statement of Work covering every deliverable and payment milestone."

Subcontractor

A subcontractor is someone you hire to help deliver work you've contracted to produce for a client. You remain responsible for the subcontractor's work quality and timely delivery. Verify that your client contract permits subcontracting (or doesn't prohibit it), and always have a written agreement with your subcontractor covering deliverables, deadlines, payment, and IP transfer.

Example: "I subcontracted the development work to a trusted developer while I managed the client relationship, design, and delivery coordination."

T

3 terms

Testimonial

A testimonial is a written or video endorsement from a satisfied client describing their experience and the results you delivered. Testimonials are powerful social proof that reduce hesitation in new prospects. Collect them immediately after project completion while enthusiasm is high. Ask specific questions ("What result did we achieve together?") to get useful specifics rather than vague praise.

Example: "After every project I send a 3-question feedback email and request permission to use their response as a testimonial."

Time Tracking

Time tracking is recording exactly how long you spend on each project or task. Even if you charge fixed project rates, tracking time reveals your real effective hourly rate, exposes underpriced projects, and sharpens future estimates. Many freelancers are genuinely shocked to discover how much unbillable overhead their "profitable" projects actually require once they start measuring.

Example: "Time tracking revealed that a $2,500 project I thought was profitable was netting me $38/hr after revisions and communication."

1099 (Form 1099-NEC)

A 1099-NEC is a US tax form that clients send you by January 31st for any year they paid you $600 or more. It reports your freelance income to the IRS. You're responsible for taxes on all freelance income regardless of whether you receive a 1099 — income under $600 is still taxable, just not separately reported by the client. Keep your own income records independent of what 1099s you receive.

Example: "I received 1099s from four clients in January. Combined with my own income log, those records go straight to my accountant."

U

2 terms

Upsell

An upsell is offering an existing client an additional or upgraded service beyond what they originally hired you for. Examples: offering a brand guidelines document after completing a logo, or proposing an email nurture sequence to a client who hired you for a landing page. Upsells to existing happy clients convert far more efficiently than finding new ones and don't require sales pipeline work.

Example: "After every branding project I offer a brand guidelines document as an upsell. About 40% of clients add it, at an average of $800 each."

Upwork

Upwork is a freelance marketplace where businesses post projects and hire freelancers. It's a common entry point for new freelancers to build verified work history and client reviews. Platform fees (up to 20%) and competitive bidding compress rates significantly over time. Many freelancers use Upwork early to build a portfolio and social proof, then transition to direct client relationships where margins are much higher.

Example: "I used Upwork for 18 months to accumulate 25 reviews and $45k in verified earnings, then moved to direct clients and doubled my effective rate."

V

1 term

Value-Based Pricing

Value-based pricing means charging based on the economic value your work creates for the client — not the time it takes you. A homepage redesign that generates $300,000 in additional revenue for a client is worth dramatically more than 40 hours of your time. Value-based pricing requires understanding the client's business goals and being able to quantify ROI. It enables significantly higher rates and rewards expertise over speed.

Example: "I priced the rebrand at $18,000 — not based on my hours, but because a clearer brand would help them close $500k in annual contracts."

W-9

A W-9 is a US tax form providing your legal name, address, and Taxpayer Identification Number (or SSN) to a client so they can issue a 1099 at year-end. Clients typically request a W-9 before processing their first payment to you. Keep a signed, filled-out W-9 ready to send on demand — it's often needed before a check or ACH transfer can be processed.

Example: "The agency couldn't process my first invoice until I sent a completed W-9. I now include it proactively with every new contract."

Warm Lead

A warm lead is a prospective client who already knows who you are, was referred by someone they trust, or has shown prior interest in your work. Warm leads convert at dramatically higher rates than cold outreach with far less effort. Building visibility through content, referrals, and community participation creates a steady stream of warm leads over time — the real flywheel of a sustainable freelance business.

Example: "A warm lead from a past client's referral closed in 3 emails and one call. An equivalent cold lead would have taken weeks of follow-up."

White-Label

White-labeling means producing work delivered to end clients under another company's brand — you remain invisible to the final client. Agencies commonly white-label freelancers for services they don't deliver in-house. White-label work provides consistent volume with no sales effort required, but you typically can't use it in your portfolio and rates are usually lower. It's a useful income stream alongside direct client work.

Example: "I do white-label SEO content for two agencies. They handle clients; I deliver the work each month. No sales, just execution."

Work for Hire

Work for hire is a legal doctrine where the creator of a work assigns full copyright ownership to the client as part of the engagement. When you sign a work-for-hire agreement, you cannot use the work in your portfolio, cannot resell it, and have no claim to authorship. Some clients require it. When you're asked to give up IP rights entirely, that should be reflected in a higher price — you're selling something extra.

Example: "The client wanted full work-for-hire terms: no portfolio use, complete IP transfer. I added a 25% premium to the quote to compensate."
