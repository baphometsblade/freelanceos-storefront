Why Indie Hackers Need a Better Notion Setup

The indie hacker community has a shipping problem. Not a talent problem. Not an idea problem. A system problem. Thousands of technically excellent builders start projects that quietly die on a half-filled Trello board, a Notion page with one section filled in, or a GitHub repo with eight commits before real life took over.

The builders who consistently ship — the ones with multiple products live, growing MRR, and a reputation in the community — almost universally have one thing in common: they have a system that makes the work visible and the next step obvious.

Notion is the right tool for indie hackers when it's set up correctly. The problem is that most indie hackers set it up wrong. They create a maze of pages instead of an integrated workspace. They track some things but not others. They have no weekly review ritual that keeps projects alive. And they start over every six months with a new "system."

This guide covers the right way to use Notion as an indie hacker — and the specific template setup that supports consistent shipping.

The Indie Hacker Notion Architecture

Before getting into specific features, let's talk about the structure that makes an indie hacker workspace actually work. The key insight is that indie hackers have a fundamentally different workflow than either traditional knowledge workers or project managers. You need:

  • A product portfolio view — all your products, their status, their MRR, and their health in one place
  • Per-product roadmaps — what's shipping next for each product, not just what might happen someday
  • A launch tracker — where are you distributing, what's the feedback, what are the numbers
  • An idea inbox — captured immediately, reviewed weekly, not acted on impulsively
  • A revenue dashboard — MRR, churn, new signups, and net revenue visible without opening Stripe

The mistake most indie hackers make is building these as five separate sections with no connections between them. A product in your portfolio should link directly to its roadmap, its launch tracker, and its revenue line. When you click into a product, you should see everything relevant to that product immediately.

Database 1: Product Portfolio

Your master product database. Every product or project — live, in development, or paused — lives here. Key properties: status (idea / building / launched / paused / killed), MRR, launch date, tech stack, primary distribution channel, and a link to the product roadmap.

The gallery view of this database is your home base. At a glance: what's live, what's building, what's making money. This is the first thing you open on Monday morning.

Database 2: Roadmap (Linked to Products)

Each roadmap item links to a product in the portfolio database. This means you can filter your roadmap to "only show tasks for Product X" or see everything building across all products. Status: backlog / this sprint / in progress / shipped. Include a "Why This?" property — if you can't articulate why a feature matters for growth or retention, it probably doesn't need to be built right now.

Database 3: Launch Log

Every time you launch something — a new product, a major feature, a Product Hunt post, a Hacker News Show HN — it gets an entry here. Track the date, platform, reach, signups/revenue generated, and key learnings. Over time this database becomes the most valuable thing you own. You'll see patterns: which launch channels actually work for your audience, which ones are noise.

Database 4: Revenue Dashboard

Manual monthly revenue logging by product. Yes, you can connect Stripe to analytics tools, but there's value in manually reviewing and entering your numbers each month — it keeps you honest about what's working. Track MRR, new MRR, churned MRR, and net new MRR. A simple formula gives you month-over-month growth percentage.

Database 5: Ideas Inbox

The most important database you're probably not maintaining. Every product idea, feature idea, marketing idea, and potential project gets captured here immediately — not acted on. A weekly review ritual where you tag, prioritize, and archive ideas keeps this useful. The best ideas survive multiple reviews. The bad ones quietly move to archive.

The Weekly Shipping Ritual

Systems don't ship products. Habits do. The most important ritual for an indie hacker isn't a sophisticated Notion setup — it's a weekly review that keeps your work moving forward. Here's the structure that works:

Monday (20 minutes): Open your product portfolio. For each active project, check one task to the "in progress" status. Write the single most important outcome for the week at the top of your weekly note. Block focus time on your calendar.

Wednesday (10 minutes): Mid-week check-in. Are you on track? Does anything need to move to next week? Are there blockers to remove?

Friday (30 minutes): Weekly review. Update task statuses. Log any revenue changes. Move completed items to the launch log if applicable. Write three sentences about what you shipped, what you learned, and what's next. Then review your ideas inbox and archive anything that no longer excites you.

This ritual takes less than an hour per week and is the difference between projects that ship and projects that die. The documentation matters less than the consistency.

What About Obsidian, Linear, and Other Tools?

Indie hackers are notorious for tool-switching. There's always a shinier system. Here's the honest answer: the best tool is the one you actually use. Notion wins for most indie hackers not because it's technically superior to Linear or Obsidian but because it's flexible enough to handle the full range of what you need — product management, knowledge base, CRM, financial tracking — without requiring you to maintain five separate apps.

Linear is excellent if you have a team and need issue tracking. Obsidian is excellent if you're a writer who needs a zettelkasten. Neither of them replaces a product portfolio database or a revenue dashboard. For solo builders, Notion's breadth wins over any tool's depth.
