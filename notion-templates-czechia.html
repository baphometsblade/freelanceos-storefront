Czech Trade License (Živnostenský List) System

Most Czech freelancers operate under a živnostenský list (trade license) as OSVČ (Osoba Samostatně Výdělečně Činná — self-employed person). Free trades (volné živnosti) — IT, consulting, design, marketing, writing, translation — require no professional qualifications. Register at a živnostenský úřad (trade office) or online via Czech POINT. Cost: CZK 1,000 (approx €40). Processing: 1–3 business days. The template includes a trade license tracker, activity code (ŽO) reference, and registration checklist — start to finish in under a week.

OSVČ Tax System: 15% Flat Tax + 60% Paušál

Czech OSVČ pay: flat 15% income tax on profit. Key option: výdajový paušál (flat-rate expense deduction) — instead of tracking all expenses, claim 60% of revenues as a flat deduction (for most volná živnost activities). This means you pay 15% tax on only 40% of your revenue. Social insurance (ČSSZ) and health insurance are mandatory with minimum monthly contributions. New OSVČ benefit from reduced social contributions in year 1. The template tracks revenue, paušál calculations, quarterly advance tax payments, and annual social/health insurance settlement.

The Czech Tech Economy: Central Europe's Quiet Tech Leader

The Czech Republic's technology sector has quietly become one of the most respected in Central and Eastern Europe, with Prague often ranked alongside Warsaw and Bucharest as the top tech hubs in the region. The country's track record spans everything from global cybersecurity (Avast, founded in Prague in 1988, became one of the world's largest antivirus providers before merging with NortonLifeLock in 2022) to developer tools (JetBrains, creators of IntelliJ IDEA and PyCharm, was founded in Prague by Russian developers who recognized the Czech environment as ideal for a software company) to modern SaaS (Productboard, founded in Prague, raised $125M and built a product management platform used by companies including Microsoft and Disney).

The country's engineering and computer science educational infrastructure — centered on Czech Technical University (ČVUT) in Prague, Masaryk University in Brno, and Charles University — produces graduates who are competitive globally. International technology companies consistently rate Czech engineers highly on both technical depth and communication skills, which has made Prague a preferred location for European R&D centers.

Czech Republic's trade license system (živnostenský list) is one of the more freelancer-friendly in the EU: registration takes days, costs roughly €40, and the flat 60% expense deduction (výdajový paušál) makes tax calculation simple without requiring detailed bookkeeping for most freelancers. This simplicity has contributed to a healthy independent contractor culture in Czech tech — many experienced Czech engineers and designers work as OSVČ rather than as employees, maintaining flexibility while serving both Czech and international clients.

Prague's quality of life is compelling for an EU capital: lower cost of living than Western European capitals (a comfortable apartment is €700–1,200/month in Prague, less in Brno), excellent public transportation, rich cultural life, central European location (1–2 hour flights from most EU capitals), and a strong English-speaking professional community in tech. Brno offers much of Prague's tech opportunity at 20–30% lower cost. For international freelancers considering EU relocation, the combination of Czech trade license ease, EU legal framework, CZK currency (stable, managed by the CNB), and Prague quality of life makes Czech Republic an increasingly attractive option in the EU landscape.

FAQ

Czech Freelancer Questions

How do Czech freelancers register for taxes?

Czech freelancers (OSVČ) must: (1) Obtain a živnostenský list (trade license) at a živnostenský úřad or online via Czech POINT — CZK 1,000 fee, 1–3 business day processing. (2) Register at the tax office (finanční úřad) for income tax — usually done simultaneously with the trade license via Czech POINT. (3) Register for social insurance (ČSSZ) and health insurance (your chosen zdravotní pojišťovna, e.g., VZP). The entire process can be completed in under a week online. A daňový poradce (tax advisor) is recommended for the first year to set up quarterly advance payments correctly.

What is a živnostenský list in the Czech Republic?

A živnostenský list (trade license) is the legal authorization required to operate as a self-employed professional (OSVČ) in the Czech Republic. It specifies the licensed activity (živnostenský obor) and is issued by the živnostenský úřad (trade licensing authority). For most freelance activities in IT, consulting, marketing, design, and writing, the relevant license is a volná živnost (free trade) under the category "Výroba, obchod a služby neuvedené v přílohách 1 až 3 živnostenského zákona" (Production, trade, and services not listed in Annexes 1-3 of the Trade Licensing Act). The license is renewed automatically unless cancelled, and there is no annual renewal fee.

What is OSVČ in Czech Republic?

OSVČ stands for Osoba Samostatně Výdělečně Činná — literally "person independently gainfully active," equivalent to "self-employed" in English. An OSVČ can be either a "hlavní OSVČ" (primary self-employment, main income source) or "vedlejší OSVČ" (secondary self-employment, alongside employment). The distinction matters for social insurance: hlavní OSVČ has higher minimum contribution requirements. As an OSVČ you pay 15% flat income tax, mandatory ČSSZ social insurance contributions, and health insurance contributions. You file an annual daňové přiznání (tax return) typically by April 1 of the following year (extended to July if using a daňový poradce).

How do Czech freelancers receive international payments?

The most popular options: Revolut (very high adoption in Czech Republic — a CZK account with instant EUR/USD conversion and low transfer fees is the standard setup for Czech freelancers with international clients), Wise (strong for USD/GBP to CZK conversion with transparent fees, widely used alongside Revolut), SEPA credit transfer to Czech bank account in EUR (works well for EU clients paying in EUR — fast, cheap), and SWIFT transfer for USD or other currency clients. PayPal is available but fees are generally higher. Most Czech freelancers with international income use Revolut or Wise as the primary international receipt account, then transfer CZK to their Czech bank for expenses.

Is Revolut popular in Czech Republic?

Yes — Czech Republic has one of the highest Revolut adoption rates per capita in Europe. Revolut's CZK support (Czech Koruna was added to Revolut's supported currencies) made it particularly useful for Czech users: they can hold CZK, EUR, and USD simultaneously, convert between them at interbank rates, and use the Revolut card domestically and abroad. For Czech freelancers with international income, the Revolut setup is typically: receive USD/EUR from international clients → hold in Revolut → convert to CZK when rate is favorable → transfer to Czech bank account for expenses. Revolut's instant conversion notifications and rate alerts make this approach feel very controlled.

What is the 60% expense deduction for Czech freelancers?

The výdajový paušál (flat-rate expense deduction) allows Czech OSVČ to deduct 60% of their revenues as expenses without needing to track or document individual expenses. The 60% rate applies to most free trades (volné živnosti) including IT, consulting, design, and marketing. This means: if you earn CZK 1,500,000 in a year, you can deduct CZK 900,000 as expenses and pay 15% income tax on the remaining CZK 600,000 — that's CZK 90,000 in income tax, or an effective tax rate of 6% on gross revenue. The paušál cannot be combined with actual expense tracking — you choose one approach per tax year. There are also income caps above which the paušál becomes less advantageous; consult a tax advisor for current limits.
