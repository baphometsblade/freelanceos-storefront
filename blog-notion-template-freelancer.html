Setting Up Your Free Notion Client CRM

A client CRM in Notion is one of the highest-value things you can build. At its core, it's a database where each row is a client, and each property tells you something useful about that relationship.

Here's the exact structure that works for most freelancers:

1

Create your Clients database

Start a new database in Notion (full-page, not inline). Each row = one client or prospect. Add these core properties:

  • Name (Title) — company or person name
  • Status (Select) — Lead, Active, Paused, Churned
  • Contact Email (Email)
  • Niche (Select) — their industry vertical
  • Monthly Value (Number, formatted as currency)
  • Last Contact (Date)
  • Next Follow-up (Date)
  • Notes (Text) — quick context about the relationship
2

Create views that match how you work

The power of Notion's CRM isn't the database itself — it's the views. Set up:

  • Board view grouped by Status — see your pipeline at a glance
  • Table view filtered to Active — your current client roster
  • Calendar view by Next Follow-up — never miss a check-in
  • Gallery view — visual overview if you work with brand-heavy clients
3

Link clients to projects and invoices

Create a relation property that connects each client to their projects (in your Projects database) and a separate relation to your Invoices database. Now when you open a client page, you see their full history instantly.

Invoice Tracking in Notion That Actually Works

Most freelancers either use a dedicated invoicing tool (FreshBooks, Wave, QuickBooks) or they just... don't track invoices properly. Both approaches have costs. Dedicated tools add monthly SaaS fees and another login. Sloppy tracking leads to unpaid invoices slipping through the cracks — a real business killer.

A Notion invoice tracker won't replace your accounting software for tax purposes, but it's an excellent real-time dashboard for cash flow. Here's how to build it:

The Invoice Database Structure

Your Invoices database needs these properties:

Property Type Purpose
Invoice # Title e.g. INV-2026-042
Client Relation → Clients DB Links to your CRM
Amount Number (currency) Invoice total
Status Select Draft / Sent / Paid / Overdue
Issue Date Date When you sent it
Due Date Date Net 15 / Net 30 / etc.
Paid Date Date When payment landed
Days to Pay Formula dateBetween(prop("Paid Date"), prop("Issue Date"), "days")

The Formulas That Make It Powerful

Add a rollup to your Clients database that sums the "Amount" from all related invoices with Status = "Paid". Now your client record shows lifetime revenue automatically. Add another rollup for "Sent" status to see outstanding receivables per client.

Create a filtered view called "Overdue" that shows invoices where Due Date is before today and Status is not Paid. This becomes your weekly accounts receivable check — takes 3 minutes instead of 30.

Project Management for Freelancers in Notion

Freelance project management has different needs than agency or startup PM. You're typically handling 3–10 concurrent client engagements, each at different stages, with no dedicated project manager. Your system needs to be lightweight but comprehensive enough that nothing falls through.

The Projects Database

Set up one Projects database where each row is a client engagement or deliverable:

  • Project Name — descriptive, client-prefixed (e.g., "Acme Corp — Q2 Content")
  • Client — relation to your Clients database
  • Type — One-time, Retainer, Ongoing
  • Status — Not Started, In Progress, In Review, Complete, Archived
  • Start Date / Due Date — the project timeline
  • Budget — project value (connects to invoicing)
  • Priority — High / Medium / Low

Tasks: Sub-items vs. Linked Database

For task management within projects, you have two approaches in Notion. The simpler one: use sub-items (available on all plans) directly inside each project. Each sub-item is a task with a checkbox, due date, and assignee (if you have subcontractors).

The more powerful approach: create a separate Tasks database with a relation back to Projects. This lets you build a unified "My tasks today" view that pulls tasks from all active projects in one place — filtered to due date = today and status = not done. This is the view most productive freelancers start their workday in.

4

Build your weekly review view

Create a filtered + sorted view of Projects called "This Week" — filter to Status = In Progress, sort by Due Date ascending. Every Monday morning, this view tells you exactly what needs to be delivered this week across all client engagements. No spreadsheet. No calendar archaeology. Just one view.

Building a Lead Pipeline That Actually Converts

Most freelancers' sales pipeline is "I posted on LinkedIn and hoped." That works sometimes, but it doesn't scale. A Notion lead pipeline brings structure to the outreach and follow-up process — especially important when you're juggling client work at the same time.

Your pipeline stages should reflect your actual sales process. A typical flow:

Stage What it means Key action
Prospect Identified but not contacted Research and qualify
Contacted Initial outreach sent Set follow-up reminder
Intro Call Discovery call scheduled Prepare questions, send agenda
Proposal Sent Scope + pricing delivered Follow up in 3 days if no response
Negotiation Back and forth on terms Know your floor before this stage
Won / Lost Deal closed or passed Log reason, keep "Lost" for retargeting

The Notion board view (grouped by stage) is perfect for this. You can drag cards between columns as deals progress. Add a "Next Action" date property so you can filter to "prospects requiring follow-up today" — an extremely high-value view that most freelancers don't have.

🎯

Pro tip: Log a "Lost Reason" on every deal you don't close. After 20 lost deals, patterns emerge. Most freelancers discover that 60–70% of their losses are on price — which means the fix is either qualifying better upfront or productizing your offers more clearly.
