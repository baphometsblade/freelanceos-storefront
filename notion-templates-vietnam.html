VND/USD Multi-Currency Income Tracker

Vietnamese Dong (VND) remains one of Asia's strongest local currencies for purchasing power, but most Vietnamese freelancers working internationally invoice in USD, AUD, GBP, or EUR. Track income in the original foreign currency alongside the VND equivalent at the exchange rate on the day of transfer. 📋

PIT / TNCN Tax Tracker (Thuế Thu Nhập Cá Nhân)

Vietnamese Personal Income Tax (Thuế Thu Nhập Cá Nhân, TNCN) applies to freelance income. The standard approach for individual freelancers providing services is the contractors withholding method: clients withhold 7% of service fees above VND 2 million per payment. If you register as a household business (hộ kinh doanh), progressive PIT rates apply (5% on income up to VND 60M/year; 10%, 15%, 20%, 25%, 30%, and 35% on higher bands up to VND 960M+). Track withholding amounts per client, total taxable income, and annual PIT declaration deadline (Q1 of the following year). A tax code (mã số thuế) from the General Department of Taxation is required for formal registration.

📄

Electronic Invoice (Hóa Đơn Điện Tử) Notes

Vietnam's e-invoice (hóa đơn điện tử) system became mandatory for businesses from July 2022. If you are registered as a household business or company, you are required to issue e-invoices for services provided to Vietnamese businesses. Individual freelancers (not registered as a business) typically use a "purchase voucher" (phiếu chi) provided by the client, who declares the payment and withholds the applicable tax. Track whether each client is managing tax withholding on their side or whether you need to file independently.

MoMo / ZaloPay / Domestic Payment Tracker

Vietnam's domestic payment ecosystem is dominated by MoMo (leading digital wallet), ZaloPay (connected to Zalo, Vietnam's dominant messaging platform), VNPay, and direct bank transfers. For domestic freelance payments — especially from smaller clients or startups — MoMo is common for faster transfers. Track which clients pay via which channel, transfer fees, and settlement timing. For international payments, Wise and Payoneer are the standard tools for Vietnamese freelancers receiving USD from international clients, offering competitive exchange rates versus traditional bank wire transfers.

TopDev / ITviec / Vietnamese Platform Tracker

Vietnam's tech freelance ecosystem includes both international and local platforms. TopDev.vn is the leading IT and tech job/freelance platform for the Vietnamese market — used heavily by developers, testers, and data professionals. ITviec.com is the second major tech platform. Lancer.vn and Freelancerviet.vn serve the broader Vietnamese freelance market (design, writing, marketing). International: Upwork and Fiverr are widely used by Vietnamese developers and designers serving global clients. Track which platform each client or project originated from, platform fee rates, and payout schedules.

HCMC / Hanoi / Da Nang Ecosystem Notes

Vietnam's freelance ecosystem has three main hubs: Ho Chi Minh City (HCMC/Saigon) is the commercial and startup capital with the largest tech ecosystem — home to startups like MoMo, VNPay, Tiki, and Masan, plus the HCMC District 1 and 7 co-working scene (Toong, Dreamplex, Base Camp). Hanoi is the government and enterprise tech hub with strong demand for backend developers and systems engineers. Da Nang is Vietnam's fastest-growing digital nomad destination, with lower living costs and a growing co-working community (Big C Building, CoWorking, The Hive). Note which city your clients are based in and adapt communication style accordingly (direct Northern style vs. relationship-first Southern style).

Vietnam's Growing Freelance Economy

Vietnam is one of Southeast Asia's most dynamic digital economies, with a population of 98 million people, a median age of 30, and one of the highest rates of internet penetration in the region. The country's rapid economic development — sustained GDP growth averaging 6–7% annually over the past two decades — has fueled a thriving tech sector and a rapidly professionalizing freelance economy.

Ho Chi Minh City (HCMC), often called Saigon, is Vietnam's commercial capital and startup hub. Companies like MoMo (Southeast Asia's largest digital wallet), VNPay (payment infrastructure), Tiki (e-commerce), and the Vietnamese offices of global firms like Google, Lazada, and Shopee are headquartered or have major operations here. The District 1 and District 7 areas have become centers for Vietnam's tech startup ecosystem, with co-working spaces, accelerators, and networking events drawing developers, designers, and digital marketers.

Hanoi, Vietnam's capital, has a stronger government and enterprise technology sector. State-owned enterprises and government digitalization projects drive demand for backend developers, system engineers, and IT consultants. The city's Hoan Kiem and Cau Giay districts have established co-working scenes, and Hanoi is home to many of Vietnam's largest technology companies including FPT Software, Viettel (telecom), and VNPT.

Da Nang has emerged as Vietnam's — and increasingly Southeast Asia's — most accessible digital nomad destination. With lower living costs than HCMC or Hanoi, fast internet, beautiful beaches, and a growing co-working ecosystem, Da Nang attracts both international digital nomads and Vietnamese freelancers seeking a better quality of life outside the major cities. The city hosts a growing number of tech startups and has positioned itself as Vietnam's "Silicon Riviera."

Vietnamese freelancers working internationally benefit significantly from the VND-to-USD exchange rate: with 1 USD equivalent to roughly 25,000 VND, international clients paying USD rates can provide comfortable income for living and saving in Vietnam. This has driven rapid growth in internationally-focused freelance work, particularly in software development, UI/UX design, content writing (both Vietnamese-language and English), and digital marketing services.

Tax compliance for Vietnamese freelancers involves understanding the distinction between individual freelancer status and business registration. Most freelancers working with Vietnamese corporate clients will have tax withheld at source (7% of service fees above VND 2 million). For international income, freelancers must self-declare and pay PIT on foreign-source earnings. The General Department of Taxation's portal (thuedientu.gdt.gov.vn) handles online registration and filing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do freelancers in Vietnam need to pay income tax?
Yes. Vietnamese freelancers earning income from services are subject to Personal Income Tax (Thuế Thu Nhập Cá Nhân, TNCN). For individuals providing services to Vietnamese businesses: if your fee per payment exceeds VND 2 million, the client is required to withhold 7% before paying you (under the contractors withholding method). For international income (clients paying in USD or other foreign currencies), you are responsible for self-declaring and paying PIT on that income. Progressive PIT rates apply if you register as a business: 5% on annual income up to VND 60M, rising through six bands to 35% on income above VND 960M/year. Register your tax code (mã số thuế) through the General Department of Taxation portal. Consult a local ke toan thue (tax accountant) for your specific situation.
How do Vietnamese freelancers receive international payments?
Vietnamese freelancers working with international clients primarily use Wise (TransferWise), Payoneer, and PayPal to receive foreign currency payments. Wise offers competitive exchange rates close to the mid-market rate and is widely used for USD-to-VND conversions. Payoneer is popular with freelancers on Upwork and Fiverr. Funds are transferred to Vietnamese bank accounts — Vietcombank, Techcombank, MB Bank, and VPBank are commonly used for international transfers due to their SWIFT capability and favorable forex handling. Some freelancers maintain USD accounts temporarily before converting to VND to take advantage of favorable rates.
What are the best freelance platforms for Vietnamese developers and designers?
Vietnamese tech freelancers use a mix of international and local platforms. For international work: Upwork and Fiverr are the most popular, with Toptal for senior/expert-level developers. For the domestic Vietnamese market: TopDev.vn is the leading IT and tech freelance and job platform; ITviec.com is the second major tech platform. Lancer.vn and Freelancerviet.vn serve the broader Vietnamese freelance market (design, writing, marketing). For design specifically: Behance and Dribbble build international visibility. Content writers in Vietnamese can find domestic clients through direct outreach to e-commerce companies and digital agencies.
Is Ho Chi Minh City or Hanoi better for freelancers in Vietnam?
Both cities have strong freelance ecosystems with different characteristics. HCMC has the largest startup and tech ecosystem, the most co-working spaces (Toong, Dreamplex, Base Camp, and dozens more), and the highest density of international companies and clients. Hanoi has a strong government and enterprise technology sector with steady demand for backend developers and system engineers. Da Nang has emerged as Vietnam's most popular lower-cost alternative — many Vietnamese freelancers choose Da Nang for its quality of life, cost of living, and growing digital nomad community while working remotely for clients in HCMC, Hanoi, or internationally.
What is MoMo and how do Vietnamese freelancers use it?
MoMo is Vietnam's leading digital wallet platform with over 30 million users. Vietnamese freelancers may receive payments from domestic clients via MoMo for smaller or faster transactions, especially with SME clients or startups. ZaloPay (integrated with Zalo, Vietnam's dominant messaging platform with 75+ million users) is the second major digital wallet. For larger invoice payments from domestic clients, bank transfer (chuyen khoan) remains the standard method. MoMo does not support cross-border transactions, so for international payments, Wise and Payoneer are the primary tools.
Do Vietnamese freelancers need to register a business?
Not necessarily. Individual freelancers in Vietnam can operate under two main frameworks: as an individual (cá nhân) paying contractors withholding tax when working with Vietnamese businesses, or by registering as a household business (hộ kinh doanh) for more formal legal status and the ability to issue official invoices. For freelancers working primarily with international clients who pay in foreign currency, individual status with proper annual tax declaration is commonly used. If you expect to scale your income or work regularly with large Vietnamese corporations requiring formal invoices, household business registration is worth considering. Always consult a Vietnamese tax accountant (kế toán thuế) for guidance on your specific situation.
