How to Calculate Your Freelance Income

Understanding your freelance income isn't just about multiplying your hourly rate by hours worked. There are layers — gross revenue, taxable income, net take-home, and effective hourly rate — and most freelancers are fuzzy on at least one of them. This calculator handles all four so you can make confident financial decisions.

Gross vs. Net Freelance Income

Gross revenue is the total amount clients pay you before any deductions. Net income is what you actually keep after paying taxes and covering business expenses. The gap between gross and net is typically 30–40% for US freelancers — meaning someone billing $100,000 might take home $60,000–$70,000 after everything. Knowing both numbers is essential for setting realistic financial goals.

The Billable Hours Reality

One of the most common mistakes freelancers make is calculating income as if 100% of their time is billable. It never is. Between client acquisition, admin tasks, invoicing, marketing, and professional development, most freelancers are only billable for 50–60% of their working hours. This calculator lets you set your actual billable hours rather than total hours, giving you a more honest income projection.

Self-Employment Tax for US Freelancers

Unlike employees who split Social Security and Medicare taxes with their employer, freelancers pay both halves — the full 15.3% self-employment tax. This is on top of federal income tax. A freelancer in the 22% federal bracket effectively pays around 37% in combined taxes before state taxes. The good news: you can deduct half of self-employment tax from your gross income, and business expenses reduce your taxable income further.

Business Expenses That Reduce Taxable Income

Every legitimate business expense reduces your taxable income dollar-for-dollar. Common deductions include: home office (dedicated workspace — either simplified $5/sq ft method or actual expenses), software subscriptions (Adobe, Notion, project management tools), equipment (computers, cameras, peripherals), professional development (courses, books, conferences), health insurance premiums, and retirement contributions. Tracking these consistently can reduce your effective tax rate by 5–10 percentage points.

Income Smoothing Strategies for Irregular Freelance Income

The biggest financial challenge for freelancers isn't how much they earn — it's the unpredictability. Three strategies help: (1) Retainer clients — even one client on a monthly retainer provides a stable floor; (2) Separate accounts — keep a business checking and a tax savings account, move 25–30% of every deposit immediately; (3) Income smoothing — pay yourself a fixed "salary" from your business account monthly, letting the balance absorb feast-and-famine cycles. This calculator's monthly take-home figure gives you a good target for your self-imposed salary.

Freelance Income FAQs

How much do freelancers make on average?
Freelance income varies widely by niche and experience. Entry-level freelancers often earn $30,000–$50,000/year while building their client base. Mid-level professionals typically earn $60,000–$100,000. Experienced specialists in high-demand fields (software development, UX design, copywriting, consulting) regularly earn $100,000–$200,000+. The median full-time freelancer in the US earns approximately $65,000–$75,000 annually, according to various industry surveys.
How do I calculate my freelance hourly rate?
Start with your desired annual net income. Add your estimated annual overhead (software, equipment, etc.). Add 25–30% for taxes. Divide by your realistic billable hours (typically 1,000–1,200 hours/year for full-time freelancers). The result is your minimum viable rate. Then compare to market rates in your niche and adjust accordingly. Our Rate Calculator handles this math in detail.
What taxes do freelancers pay?
US freelancers pay: (1) Self-employment tax — 15.3% on the first ~$160,000 of net earnings (covers Social Security and Medicare); (2) Federal income tax — 10–37% depending on taxable income bracket; (3) State income tax — varies by state (0% in TX, FL, NV; up to 13.3% in CA). Total effective rates commonly range from 25–38% for full-time freelancers. You can deduct half of self-employment tax, qualified business income (up to 20% via QBI deduction), and legitimate business expenses to reduce your taxable income.
How do I save for taxes as a freelancer?
The golden rule: move 25–30% of every payment into a separate tax savings account the day you receive it. Treat it as untouchable. Pay quarterly estimated taxes using IRS Form 1040-ES — due mid-April, mid-June, mid-September, and mid-January. Track business expenses in real-time (not at tax season). Consider a SEP-IRA to defer up to 25% of net self-employment income. If you're consistently earning $80k+ net, consult a CPA about S-Corp election, which can reduce self-employment tax significantly.
What's a realistic income for a new freelancer?
Most new freelancers earn $25,000–$45,000 in their first year while building their client base and reputation. Expect the first 3–6 months to be slower as you land your first clients. A realistic goal for year one is 15–20 billable hours per week at $30–$60/hour depending on your niche, improving each quarter. By year two or three, hitting $60,000–$80,000 is achievable in most freelance fields. Landing 1–2 retainer clients in the first year is a major accelerator for income stability.
