Why Track MRR in Notion at All?

If you have a Stripe account, you already have revenue data. If you use Baremetrics, ChartMogul, or ProfitWell, you have beautiful dashboards. Why build an MRR tracker in Notion?

There are three compelling reasons. First, context. Stripe shows you revenue in isolation. A Notion MRR tracker lives inside your broader business workspace — alongside your product roadmap, your customer notes, and your goals. Seeing revenue in context of what you've been building and who you've been talking to is significantly more useful than a number in a separate app.

Second, multiple revenue streams. Most solopreneurs and indie hackers have more than one revenue source — perhaps a SaaS product, a consulting engagement, and a digital product. Stripe tracks each separately. A Notion MRR tracker can aggregate across all revenue types and sources into a single monthly picture.

Third, the habit of looking. When your revenue dashboard is in the same place as your weekly planning, you look at it every week. When it's in a separate analytics tool, you only look when you're worried. The friction of switching tools is higher than you think, and frequent review of revenue data is what turns numbers into decisions.

The Notion MRR Tracker: Database Structure

The cleanest MRR tracker in Notion uses two databases: a monthly snapshot database and (optionally) a customer/subscription database. Here's how to build it:

Database 1: Monthly Revenue Snapshots

One row per month. This is your core historical record. Here are the properties that matter:

  • Month (title): "2026-05" format for easy sorting. Always YYYY-MM so alphabetical sort equals chronological sort.
  • Starting MRR (number): MRR at the first day of the month. Equal to the previous month's Ending MRR.
  • New MRR (number): Revenue from new customers or new subscriptions this month.
  • Expansion MRR (number): Revenue from upgrades or upsells to existing customers.
  • Churned MRR (number): Revenue lost from cancellations. Enter as a positive number.
  • Contraction MRR (number): Revenue lost from downgrades. Enter as a positive number.
  • Ending MRR (formula): Starting MRR + New MRR + Expansion MRR − Churned MRR − Contraction MRR
  • Net New MRR (formula): New MRR + Expansion MRR − Churned MRR − Contraction MRR
// Ending MRR formula in Notion: prop("Starting MRR") + prop("New MRR") + prop("Expansion MRR") - prop("Churned MRR") - prop("Contraction MRR") // Net New MRR formula: prop("New MRR") + prop("Expansion MRR") - prop("Churned MRR") - prop("Contraction MRR") // MoM Growth % (requires previous month's MRR in a separate field): (prop("Ending MRR") - prop("Starting MRR")) / prop("Starting MRR") * 100

Key Views to Create

Table view (default): All months sorted chronologically. Shows your full revenue history. This is your source of truth.

Gallery view: Each month as a card with the ending MRR as the card heading. Good for a quick visual sense of trajectory.

Filtered view — Current Year: Filter to only show rows where the Month contains the current year (e.g., "2026"). This is your annual view without historical noise.

Database 2: Customer / Subscription Database (Optional)

For businesses with subscription customers you can actually name (rather than anonymous SaaS users), a customer database linked to the monthly snapshot adds significant value. Each customer row has their MRR contribution, subscription start date, plan tier, and status (active / churned / paused).

A rollup property on the monthly snapshot database can then sum the MRR from all active customers in a given month, cross-checking your manual entry with the bottom-up calculation. This is especially useful for catching calculation errors and understanding customer-level churn patterns.

Beyond Basic MRR: Metrics That Matter

Once you have the basic MRR tracker working, add these metrics to deepen your understanding of your business health:

Churn Rate

Monthly churn rate = Churned MRR ÷ Starting MRR × 100. Add this as a formula property. A healthy B2B SaaS has under 2% monthly churn; consumer SaaS under 5%. If your churn rate is higher, no growth strategy fixes it — you have a retention problem.

Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)

Add a "Marketing Spend" property to your monthly snapshot. Your CAC for the month = Marketing Spend ÷ New Customers. Compare this to your ARPU (average revenue per user) to understand your payback period.

Annual Run Rate (ARR)

Simple formula: Ending MRR × 12. Not the same as actual annual revenue (which includes churn effects), but a useful benchmark for fundraising conversations and financial planning.

Lifetime Value Estimate (LTV)

Average MRR per customer ÷ Monthly Churn Rate. If your average customer pays $30/month and you have 3% monthly churn, estimated LTV = $30 ÷ 0.03 = $1,000. Compare LTV to CAC — you want LTV:CAC above 3:1 for a healthy unit economics.

Frequently Asked Questions

What if I don't have recurring revenue yet?

The same structure works for non-recurring revenue — just relabel "MRR" as "Monthly Revenue" and "New MRR" as "New Sales." The analytics principles (growth rate, revenue by source, expense tracking) apply equally to any revenue model.

Can Notion formulas do everything I need?

For the metrics described here, yes. Notion formulas can handle the MRR calculations, growth percentages, and churn calculations without any external tool. Where Notion falls short is automated data import (you'll enter numbers manually) and complex visualization. For most solo businesses, manual entry is fine — it forces you to actually look at the numbers.

Should I also use Baremetrics or ProfitWell?

If you have 50+ paying customers and your business depends on granular subscription analytics, yes — dedicated tools do this better. Before that threshold, Notion's flexibility as a combined business OS outweighs the marginal improvement in revenue reporting.
