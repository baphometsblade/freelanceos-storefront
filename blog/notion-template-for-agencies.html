Why Agencies Are Moving to Notion (vs. Asana, ClickUp, Monday)

Dedicated project management tools like Asana, ClickUp, and Monday.com were built for large, structured teams with fixed workflows. They work — but they come with tradeoffs that hurt small agencies specifically:

  • Per-seat pricing adds up fast. Monday.com starts at $9/seat/month but the useful features don't unlock until the $16+ tiers. For a 5-person agency, that's $80–100/month minimum.
  • No client-facing documentation. You can't put your proposals, SOWs, and project briefs in the same place as your tasks — so everything stays fragmented.
  • Rigid structure. These tools assume everyone works the same way. Agencies serving different client types (retainer, project-based, hourly) need more flexibility.

Notion's advantage is that it's a genuinely blank canvas. You can build a client database, link it to a project tracker, attach a retainer schedule, and share a client-specific view — all for free (or $8/month on Plus). The learning curve is real, but a good Notion template for agencies eliminates most of it.

The key insight: Notion isn't project management software. It's a workspace OS. When you set it up as an agency OS — with the right databases and linked views — it becomes more powerful than any dedicated tool for small teams.

6 Must-Have Notion Databases for Agencies

Most agencies that struggle with Notion either have too few databases (not enough structure) or too many (overwhelming). Here are the six that every agency needs, and why:

1. Client Database

Your master list of all clients — active, past, and prospect. Each client record should link to their projects, retainer details, contacts, and notes. This is the hub of your entire agency workspace. Every other database relates back to it.

2. Project Tracker

A Kanban or table view of all active work, organized by client. Each project card should have a status (proposal, active, review, complete), due date, assigned team member, and linked deliverables. Filtering by client gives you an instant picture of what's happening with any account.

3. Task Manager

Granular tasks live here — linked to projects and assigned to people. This is where your team works day-to-day. Use a board view for sprint-style work or a list view sorted by due date for deadline-driven work. The key is linking each task to both a project and a person.

4. Retainer Tracker

One of the most underused databases — and one of the most valuable. Log every recurring client engagement: monthly value, renewal date, deliverables per month, and payment status. Agencies consistently discover 10–20% of retainer revenue slipping through the cracks once they build this.

5. Proposal & SOW Library

Store every proposal and statement of work as a Notion page, linked to the relevant client. You can build fill-in-the-blank templates that make new proposals a 20-minute job instead of a two-hour one. Add a status field (draft, sent, won, lost) to track your close rate.

6. Agency CRM

A lightweight pipeline tracking leads from first contact to signed contract. For most small agencies, this is a simple Kanban with stages: Lead, Qualified, Proposal Sent, Negotiating, Closed Won/Lost. Link CRM records to your client database when deals close.

How to Set Up a Client Portal in Notion

A Notion client portal is a shared Notion page (or set of pages) that you give clients access to. Done right, it replaces email chains, Google Drive folders, and status update meetings. Here's how to set it up:

Step 1: Create a master "Clients" database

Each row is one client. Each row has its own Notion page (this is the portal for that client). Inside that page, you'll nest everything client-specific: their project tracker, shared files, meeting notes, and a deliverables checklist.

Step 2: Build the client page template

Create a template inside your Clients database that automatically populates when you add a new client. Include: a welcome section, a project status section (filtered view of your project tracker), a file repository, and a communication log.

Step 3: Configure sharing permissions

In Notion, open the client's page and click "Share." You can share via email (gives them an account-linked view) or via public link with "Can view" permissions. For clients who need to leave comments, set it to "Can comment." Avoid giving clients "Can edit" unless they're actively contributing to the workspace.

Step 4: Customize per client

One of the advantages of Notion over dedicated client portals is the ability to make each workspace feel tailored. Add the client's logo, use their brand colors as cover images, and make sure the project names match what they call things — not your internal naming system.

Pro tip: Set a weekly recurring reminder to update each client portal. A portal that's 2 weeks out of date is worse than no portal — clients will stop checking it. 15 minutes of updates per client per week is all it takes.

Managing Retainers in Notion

Retainer management is one of the highest-value things you can systematize in Notion. Most agency owners are surprised to discover they're losing meaningful revenue simply from not tracking this well. Here's a simple but effective retainer tracker setup:

Create a "Retainers" database with these fields:

  • Client (relation to your client database)
  • Monthly value (number)
  • Renewal date (date)
  • Deliverables per month (text or relation to projects)
  • Payment status (select: Pending, Paid, Overdue)
  • Notes (text)

Create a filtered view showing all retainers renewing in the next 30 days — this becomes your monthly revenue forecast. Create another view filtered by "Overdue" to catch payment issues early. The financial clarity alone often pays for a paid template ten times over.

Team Collaboration in Notion for Agencies

Notion's collaboration features have improved significantly in 2025–2026. Here's how to structure team workflows effectively:

Use assigned views, not shared chaos

The biggest mistake agencies make is dumping everything into one database and letting everyone work from the same view. Instead, create filtered views for each team member showing only their assigned tasks. Create a "My Tasks Today" view filtered by Assignee = Current User and Due Date = Today. Team members see what matters to them; nothing else clutters the view.

Use page comments for async communication

Notion's comment feature is underused. Rather than sending a Slack message about a specific deliverable or document, leave a comment directly on the relevant Notion page. This keeps context attached to the work — not buried in a chat thread three weeks later.

Weekly team dashboard

Create a team home page that aggregates: tasks due this week (across all projects), retainers renewing this month, and open proposals. This becomes the one page everyone checks Monday morning to align on priorities. Notion's linked database views make building this straightforward once your databases are set up correctly.
