Why Your Rate Is Your Most Important Business Decision

Undercharging is the single most common mistake freelancers make — and it is a slow poison for your business. When you charge too little, you take on more work to cover your income goals, leave less time for each client, deliver worse results, and create a spiral where you are always busy but never profitable.

Most new freelancers make the mistake of anchoring their rate to what they earned as an employee, or to what they think "feels fair" without accounting for the true cost of self-employment. Here is what a freelance rate has to cover that a salary does not:

  • Self-employment tax: 15.3% on top of income tax in the US — you pay both halves of Social Security and Medicare
  • Health insurance: $300–800/month for a solo plan, not covered by an employer
  • Business expenses: Software, equipment, internet, co-working space, professional development
  • Non-billable time: Business development, admin, invoicing, networking, marketing — typically 40-50% of your work week
  • Paid time off: Vacation, sick days, and holidays you are not billing for
  • Retirement savings: No employer 401(k) match — you fund it entirely

A general rule: your freelance rate should be roughly 2-3x your hourly equivalent as an employee to achieve the same take-home pay and quality of life. If you earned $50/hour as an employee (a $100k/yr salary), your freelance floor is around $100-150/hr.

The uncomfortable truth: If a client pushes back hard on your rate, they are usually not your ideal client. Clients who value good work pay for it. Clients who only care about price will always be difficult to work with and the first to demand scope creep.

The 3 Rate-Setting Formulas

Use all three formulas, then take the highest result. That is your starting rate. Going lower puts you at risk of building an unsustainable business.

Formula 1: The Income Goal Formula

Start with what you need to earn, then work backwards to an hourly rate.

Income Goal Formula
Step 1: Annual income goal × 1.3 (add 30% for taxes) = Gross target
Step 2: Gross target + Annual business expenses = Total revenue needed
Step 3: Total revenue needed ÷ Billable hours per year = Minimum hourly rate
Example: $80,000 target × 1.3 = $104,000 gross. Add $12,000 expenses = $116,000 needed. ÷ 1,000 billable hours = $116/hr minimum.

For billable hours: a full-time freelancer typically bills 15-25 hours per week. Use 1,000 hours/year (about 20 hours/week for 50 weeks) as a conservative estimate. If you are part-time, use 500 hours.

Formula 2: The Market Rate Research Formula

Find what the market pays for your skill level and position yourself in the right band.

  1. Search for your skill on Upwork and filter to top-rated freelancers — look at the rate range in your niche
  2. Check Bureau of Labor Statistics or Glassdoor for the employee salary equivalent in your skill and location, then apply the 2-3x multiplier
  3. Ask in Slack communities or Reddit (r/freelance, r/forhire) what others charge for similar work
  4. Check Bonsai or Malt rate guides for your specific niche

Position yourself at the 50th percentile of market rates at minimum. If you have strong samples and proven results, aim for the 75th percentile. The goal is not to be the cheapest option — it is to be competitive for mid-to-senior talent.

Formula 3: The Cost-Plus Formula

Add up the actual cost of completing a typical project, then add your desired profit margin.

Cost-Plus Formula
(Your time × your floor rate) + Direct expenses + Overhead allocation + Profit margin = Project price
Example: 10 hrs × $75/hr = $750 + $50 stock photos + $25 overhead = $825 cost. Add 30% profit = $1,072 project price = $107/hr effective rate.

This formula is most useful for project-based pricing. It ensures you never lose money on a project even with unexpected scope, and it builds in a profit cushion above your labor cost.

Hourly vs. Project Rates vs. Retainers

How you structure your pricing affects your income as much as the rate itself. Each model has real trade-offs.

Hourly Rate

  • Safe when scope is undefined
  • Easy to track and justify
  • Penalizes your efficiency — you earn less as you get faster
  • Clients track your hours and question your time
  • No incentive to innovate or find faster solutions
  • Best for: Ongoing support, undefined scope, maintenance contracts

Project Rate (Recommended)

  • Rewards efficiency — earn more as you get faster
  • Clients focus on results, not hours
  • Easier to budget for both sides
  • Scope creep is the main risk — requires clear contracts
  • Lets you price on value, not time
  • Best for: Defined deliverables — websites, designs, writing, audits

Monthly Retainer

  • Predictable recurring income — best for cash flow
  • Client pays for access and availability
  • Define clearly what is included per month
  • Harder to win initially but best long-term model
  • Best for: Ongoing content, social media, fractional roles, SEO

Value-Based Pricing

  • Price based on the business outcome, not your time
  • Highest earning potential for experienced freelancers
  • Requires confidence in quantifying ROI
  • Not suitable for early-stage freelancers
  • Best for: Consultants, senior strategists, CRO specialists

The evolution path: Start with hourly to understand how long projects take. Move to project rates once you can estimate accurately. Add monthly retainers as clients see ongoing value. Explore value-based pricing once you can articulate and quantify the business impact you create.

How to Raise Your Rates (3 Strategies)

Most freelancers raise their rates far too rarely. As a rule: raise your rates annually, or any time you have a wait list of clients. Here are three specific strategies.

Strategy 1: New rate for new clients only (lowest risk)

When you decide to raise your rate, apply the new rate immediately to any new clients. Honor the old rate for existing clients for 60-90 days, then send a rate increase notice giving them 30 days' warning. This is the gentlest approach and almost never causes client loss. Most existing clients are used to annual price adjustments in every other service they use.

Strategy 2: Annual inflation adjustment (easiest to justify)

Set the expectation from your first contract: your rates adjust annually on [date]. A 10-15% annual increase tracks well above inflation and feels fair to clients who value you. Put this language in your standard contract so it is never a surprise: "Rates are reviewed annually each January. Clients will receive 30 days' notice of any adjustment."

Strategy 3: Value-based repricing (highest increase potential)

If you can quantify the business outcome you created for a client — $50,000 in additional revenue from a campaign you ran, a website that doubled their conversion rate, content that drives 80% of their inbound leads — you can reprice based on that value, not your time. This approach can double or triple rates overnight but requires you to know and articulate your ROI clearly.

Rule: Never lower your rate for a client who pushes back. If a client says they cannot afford your rate, offer a reduced scope, not a reduced rate. Lowering rates signals that your original quote was arbitrary. Offering a smaller scope signals that your rate is real and that scope and budget are the variable, not your value.

Scripts for Raising Rates

Copy and adapt these messages. The key is to announce, not ask permission. You are informing clients of a business decision, not requesting their approval.

Email: Annual rate increase notice

Subject: Rate Update — Effective [DATE] Hi [NAME], I wanted to give you advance notice that my rates will be increasing to $[NEW RATE]/hr (or $[NEW RATE] per project) effective [DATE], 30 days from today. This adjustment reflects rising costs and the continued investment I make in skills and tools to deliver better results for clients like you. Your current rate of $[OLD RATE] remains in effect for any work completed or invoiced before [DATE]. If you have upcoming projects you'd like to kick off before the rate change, I'm happy to prioritize those. Just reply here or send over the brief. As always, thanks for the continued trust — it's genuinely great working with you. [Your name]

Response to a client who pushes back on your rate

Hi [NAME], Thanks for letting me know — I understand budget is a real constraint. My rate reflects the quality and speed I bring to this type of project, and I'm not able to come down on it. What I can do is work within your budget by scoping the project differently. For $[THEIR BUDGET], here's what I could deliver: [REDUCED SCOPE]. If that works for your needs, I'm happy to proceed. If you need the full scope, I'd suggest revisiting when the budget allows — I'll hold space in my calendar. Let me know what makes the most sense for you. [Your name]

Script for raising rates mid-retainer (for long-term clients)

Hi [NAME], I'm reaching out to share a rate adjustment that will take effect after our current retainer term ends on [DATE]. Starting [NEW DATE], my monthly retainer will be $[NEW AMOUNT] (up from $[OLD AMOUNT]). This reflects a ~[X]% adjustment, consistent with annual changes across my client roster. I genuinely value our working relationship and wanted to give you as much notice as possible so you can plan accordingly. If you'd like to lock in a longer-term retainer at the current rate before the change, I'm open to a 6- or 12-month arrangement. Just let me know. Looking forward to continuing the work. [Your name]

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate my freelance hourly rate?

Use the income goal formula: take your target annual income, add 30% for taxes and 20% for expenses, then divide by your billable hours (use 1,000 hours/year for full-time). Example: ($80,000 target + 30% tax buffer = $104,000) + $12,000 expenses = $116,000 total needed ÷ 1,000 hours = $116/hr minimum rate.

What is a good freelance hourly rate for beginners?

Beginner rates vary by skill. Entry-level: virtual assistants $18-35/hr, writers $30-50/hr, designers $40-65/hr, marketers $40-70/hr, developers $50-80/hr. Never go below $25/hr even as a complete beginner — below that rate you cannot sustain a freelance business after accounting for taxes, expenses, and non-billable time.

Should I charge hourly or by project as a freelancer?

Project rates are almost always better than hourly for experienced freelancers. Project rates reward your efficiency, remove clients' incentive to micromanage your hours, and let you price based on value delivered. Use hourly only for ongoing support retainers or when project scope is genuinely undefined at the outset.

How do I raise my freelance rates without losing clients?

The safest approach: apply your new rate to new clients immediately, give existing clients 30-60 days' notice of the increase. Most long-term clients accept a 10-20% annual increase if you have delivered consistent value. Frame it as an annual adjustment, not an arbitrary change, and never apologize for it.

How many billable hours should I expect as a freelancer?

A realistic full-time freelancer averages 15-25 billable hours per week — roughly 750-1,250 hours per year. The rest of your working time goes to business development, admin, invoicing, marketing, and non-billable project work. Use 1,000 hours/year as a conservative planning number when calculating your required hourly rate.
