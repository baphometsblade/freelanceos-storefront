Notion for Small Business: The Complete 2026 Setup Guide

Small businesses spend an average of $1,200 per year on project management, CRM, and task management tools — most of which overlap with each other. Notion can replace all of them for a fraction of the cost. This guide walks through exactly how to set up Notion as your small business operating system: the 7 databases every small business needs, how to connect them, and how to build the dashboard that gives you a real-time view of your business.

Why Notion Beats Spreadsheets for Small Business

Google Sheets and Excel are powerful tools, but they have a fundamental limitation: they're flat. Each spreadsheet is an island. Client data lives in one sheet, projects in another, invoices in a third — and connecting them requires complex VLOOKUP formulas or manual copy-pasting. When data changes in one sheet, the others don't update automatically.

Spreadsheets

  • Data siloed in separate files
  • Manual cross-referencing required
  • No visual project views
  • Formulas break when data moves
  • Difficult to share selectively
  • No linked views or rollups

Notion

  • All databases linked in one workspace
  • Automatic rollups across databases
  • Board, Calendar, Gallery, Timeline views
  • Properties always connected to data
  • Shareable by page with custom access
  • Real-time updates across all views

Beyond the structural advantages, Notion combines your databases with rich documentation. A client record can contain meeting notes, a shared portal, project briefs, and contract summaries — not just a row of data. Your business knowledge lives alongside your business data, in the same tool.

The 7 Databases Every Small Business Needs in Notion

These seven databases form the core of a small business operating system in Notion. Each one is valuable on its own, but the real power comes from linking them together.

1

Contacts (CRM)

Your master database of everyone you do business with — clients, prospects, vendors, partners, and referral sources. Essential properties:

  • Name, Company, Email, Phone, Website
  • Relationship Type (Client, Prospect, Vendor, Partner)
  • Status (Lead / Active / Past / Archived)
  • Source (how they found you or you found them)
  • Total Revenue (rollup from Invoices)
  • Last Contact Date, Next Follow-up Date
2

Projects

Every active and past project your business has run. This is the operational heart of your business in Notion. Essential properties:

  • Project Name, Status, Priority
  • Client (Relation to Contacts)
  • Start Date, Due Date, Completion Date
  • Project Value (your fee for this project)
  • Tasks (Relation to Tasks database)
  • Invoices (Relation to Invoices)
  • % Complete (formula based on task completion)
3

Invoices

Every invoice you've sent, with payment tracking. This replaces a basic invoicing spreadsheet. Essential properties:

  • Invoice Number, Status (Draft / Sent / Paid / Overdue)
  • Client (Relation to Contacts)
  • Project (Relation to Projects)
  • Amount, Date Sent, Date Due, Date Paid
  • Days Overdue (formula: today - due date if unpaid)
  • Payment Method, Notes
4

Contracts

Track every contract and agreement. For small businesses, contracts often get lost in email threads. Centralizing them in Notion means you can always find the terms you agreed to. Essential properties:

  • Contract Name, Status (Draft / Sent / Signed / Expired)
  • Client (Relation to Contacts)
  • Project (Relation to Projects)
  • Contract Value, Start Date, End Date
  • Signed Date, Document Link
  • Auto-renewal flag, Notice period
5

Team Tasks

Your task management system. Whether you're solo or have a small team, a centralized task database linked to projects prevents work from falling through the cracks. Essential properties:

  • Task Name, Status (Backlog / To Do / In Progress / Done)
  • Project (Relation to Projects)
  • Assignee (Person property for team members)
  • Priority (High / Medium / Low), Due Date
  • Estimated Hours, Actual Hours
  • Blocking / Blocked By (Relation to other tasks)
6

Content Calendar

If your business publishes content — blog posts, social media, newsletters, videos — a content calendar database keeps it organized. Essential properties:

  • Content Title, Type (Blog / Social / Email / Video)
  • Platform, Status (Idea → Draft → Review → Published)
  • Publish Date, Author
  • Target Keyword (for SEO content)
  • Distribution checklist (checkboxes for each platform)
7

Finances

An income and expense tracker that gives you a running picture of business financial health. This works alongside (not instead of) accounting software for most businesses. Essential properties:

  • Transaction Name, Type (Income / Expense)
  • Category, Amount, Date
  • Payment Method, Tax Deductible (checkbox)
  • Month (formula to extract month from date)
  • Linked Invoice or Receipt

Step-by-Step: Setting Up Your Small Business Notion Workspace

Here's the recommended order for building your Notion business system from scratch:

  1. Create your workspace structure first. Before building any databases, decide on your top-level page structure. A good starting structure: Home (dashboard), Clients, Projects, Finance, Content, Team, and Archive. Create these as top-level pages in your sidebar.
  2. Build the Contacts database. This is your foundation. Create it in the Clients section. Don't add relation properties yet — just the basic fields (name, company, email, status). Add your existing clients as records.
  3. Build the Projects database. Add the Relation property pointing to Contacts. Now link each project to its client. Create a Board view grouped by Status for your Kanban project board.
  4. Build the Tasks database. Add a Relation to Projects. For each active project, add the immediate next tasks. Create a Table view sorted by Due Date and filtered to show only incomplete tasks.
  5. Build the Invoices database. Add Relations to both Contacts and Projects. Go back to your Contacts database and add a "Total Revenue" Rollup property that sums the Amount field from linked Invoices.
  6. Build the Contracts and Content databases. These are simpler and don't require as many cross-database connections. Add Relations where relevant.
  7. Build your Finances database. Keep this simple — a straightforward income/expense log with category and date. Use formulas to calculate monthly totals and year-to-date summaries.
  8. Build your Home dashboard. Create linked views from each database on a single home page, filtered to show only what's immediately relevant: active projects, tasks due this week, overdue invoices, upcoming follow-ups. This is the page you open every morning.

Important: Build all databases before linking them. It's much easier to add Relation properties once all the databases exist than to set up Relations to databases you haven't created yet. The order above (Contacts → Projects → Tasks → Invoices) ensures each database exists before you try to link to it.

The Business Dashboard: Your Daily Command Center

The most powerful page in your Notion workspace is the Home dashboard. It's not a database itself — it's a page that displays filtered views from all your databases side by side, giving you a real-time overview of your business without opening multiple tabs.

A well-built small business dashboard should show:

  • Active projects — filtered view of projects with Status = Active, showing client, due date, and % complete
  • This week's tasks — filtered view of tasks due in the next 7 days, sorted by priority
  • Overdue invoices — filtered view of invoices with Status = Sent and Due Date < today
  • Upcoming follow-ups — filtered view of contacts where Next Follow-up is this week
  • Revenue this month — rollup or linked view from your Finances database showing MTD income
  • Content due — filtered view of content calendar items due in the next 14 days

This dashboard replaces the morning scroll through email, spreadsheets, and project management tools. One page, everything you need to know about your business right now.

Notion Pricing for Small Business: What Plan Do You Need?

For solo business owners and freelancers, Notion's free plan is sufficient to build everything described in this guide. The free plan includes unlimited pages, blocks, and basic databases.

Consider upgrading to Notion's Plus plan ($10/month) when you need:

  • Unlimited file uploads (the free plan has a 5MB file size limit)
  • 30-day version history (useful for tracking changes to contracts and financial data)
  • More guests on shared pages (up to 10 on Plus vs. 10 read-only on free)
  • Automations and API access

For teams of 2–10 people, the Business plan ($18/user/month) adds team-wide database sharing, advanced permissions, and audit logs.
