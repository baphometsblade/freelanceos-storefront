This is a step-by-step hands-on tutorial, not just a concept guide. Every section has something for you to actually do in Notion right now. By the end, you will have a working personal workspace system. Open Notion alongside this tutorial and follow along as you read.

Before you start: Sign up at notion.so (free), install the desktop app from notion.so/desktop, and open it up. This tutorial assumes you are starting from a fresh, empty Notion workspace.

Getting Set Up: Account, App, and Interface

Creating Your Account

Go to notion.so and click "Get Notion free." Sign up with your Google account, Apple ID, or email address. After verifying your email, you will be taken through a brief onboarding flow asking about your use case (personal, work, school) and whether you are using it alone or with a team. Choose whatever fits best — you can change everything later.

Install the Desktop App

The web browser version works fine, but the desktop app is noticeably faster, especially with larger workspaces. Download it from notion.so/desktop for Mac or Windows. Once installed and logged in, the desktop app and web version stay in sync — changes in one appear immediately in the other.

Understanding the Three-Zone Interface

Notion's interface has three key zones you need to know:

  • Left sidebar: Your navigation panel. Shows workspace name at the top, then your Favorites (starred pages), followed by your page hierarchy. Click the + icon to create new pages. Click the triangle next to any page to expand its sub-pages. Drag pages up and down to reorder.
  • Center content area: The main editing canvas where you write, add blocks, and view databases. This takes up most of the screen and is where you do all your actual work.
  • Top right header: Share, comment, history, search, and AI buttons. The Share button controls who can see and edit the current page. The clock icon gives access to version history. The AI sparkle opens Notion AI.
Try This Now

Click the + button in the sidebar, create a new page called "Notion Tutorial Practice," and add a house emoji as its icon by clicking the icon area next to the title. This is your sandbox page for this tutorial.

Tutorial Part 1 — Pages and Blocks

Creating Your First Page

You just created a page above. Now let's fill it. Click inside the content area below the title and start typing. Notice how a blinking cursor appears and you can type freely. The first thing you write becomes a text block. Press Enter to create a new block below it.

The Slash Command — Your Most Important Tool

The slash command is how you access every block type in Notion. On a new empty line, type / and a menu appears. You can either scroll through the block types or start typing to filter them. Type /h1 and press Enter to insert a Heading 1. Type /todo to insert a checkbox. Type /call to insert a Callout block. You will use the slash command constantly.

Try This Now

On your practice page, use the slash command to add: a Heading 1 that says "My Practice Page," a bulleted list with three items, a To-do list with two checkboxes, and a Callout block with an emoji and a short message. Check off one of the to-do checkboxes when you are done.

Organizing with Headings

Heading blocks (H1, H2, H3) are the backbone of a well-organized Notion page. H1 is the largest, used for major page sections. H2 and H3 are used for subsections within those sections. Any time your page has more than a few paragraphs, add headings to structure it — they also auto-generate a table of contents if you add a /toc block.

Toggle Blocks — Expandable Sections

Type /toggle to create a toggle block. The toggle heading is always visible; click the triangle to expand or collapse the content inside. Use toggles for reference information you want collapsed by default, FAQs, meeting agendas with details hidden until needed, or completed sections you want to archive visually without deleting.

Try This Now

Create a toggle block on your practice page. Title it "Click to expand." Inside the toggle, add three bullet points. Close the toggle. Notice how only the title is visible — click it to reveal the content. This is how you create expandable sections in any Notion page.

Rearranging Blocks

Every block can be moved by hovering over its left edge until a six-dot drag handle appears. Click and drag that handle to move the block up, down, or to the side of another block. Moving a block next to another block creates a two-column layout automatically — one of Notion's most useful layout features. You can create three or four columns the same way by dragging more blocks to the right.

Callout Blocks — The Perfect Highlight Tool

Callout blocks create visually distinct highlighted boxes that stand out from regular text. They are perfect for important warnings, tips, key information, or anything you want a reader to notice immediately. Type /callout, add your text, then click the emoji on the left to customize it. Use the warning emoji for alerts, a lightbulb for tips, a star for important notes.

Tutorial Part 2 — Your First Database

Inline vs. Full-Page Databases

Notion databases come in two forms. An inline database is embedded inside a page alongside other content — great for smaller databases that support a document. A full-page database lives as its own page in the sidebar — better for large, central databases you access frequently. For this tutorial, we'll start with an inline database, then move to full-page for the workspace build.

Creating Your First Database

On your practice page, type /table and select "Table — Inline" (not the simple table, which is just a grid). A new database appears with two default columns: Name and Tags. Click the database title ("Untitled") and rename it to "My First Database."

Try This Now

Create an inline table database on your practice page. Rename it "My First Database." Add three rows by pressing Enter or clicking "+ New" at the bottom. Give each row a name: "Task 1," "Task 2," "Task 3."

Adding Properties to Your Database

Properties are the columns of your database. Click the + button at the top right of your database to add a new property. You will see the property type picker. Let's add several key property types:

  • Click + and choose Select. Name it "Status." Click the property, then add options: "Not Started" (grey), "In Progress" (yellow), "Done" (green). Now click on a row's Status cell and choose an option.
  • Click + again and choose Date. Name it "Due Date." Click any row's Due Date cell and pick a date from the calendar picker.
  • Click + again and choose Checkbox. Name it "Complete." Click the checkbox on any row to toggle it.
  • Click + again and choose Number. Name it "Hours." Set the format to "Number." Type a number in any row's Hours cell.
Try This Now

Add all four property types above to your database. Then fill in at least two rows with real values for each property — a status, a due date, a checkbox state, and a number of hours. Notice how the data is typed and formatted automatically for each property type.

Opening a Database Record as a Full Page

Click on any row name in your database. A panel opens on the right showing that record's full-page view. This is where each database entry has its own Notion page — you can add any content here: notes, images, sub-tasks, reference links. Press Cmd/Ctrl + Enter to open the record in full-page view instead of the side panel. Click the back arrow to return to the database.

Understanding Multi-Select vs. Select

Select properties allow one value from a predefined list. Multi-select properties allow multiple values simultaneously — perfect for tags. If you want to categorize a project as both "Design" and "Client Work," use Multi-select. If you want a single Status value like "In Progress," use Select. Beginners often use Multi-select when they should use Select — the key question is: "Can this item be in two states simultaneously?"

The Relation Property

The Relation property type links your database to another database. We will use this in depth in Part 5. For now, know that it is what makes Notion relational — it is the property type that connects Projects to Tasks, Clients to Projects, and Notes to Sources.

Tutorial Part 3 — Database Views

Why Views Are the Most Underused Notion Feature

Most Notion beginners spend all their time in Table view and miss 80% of what makes Notion powerful. Views let you see the same database data in completely different visual formats — and each view can have its own filters and sorts. You will add four views to your database now.

Adding a Board (Kanban) View

Click "+ Add a view" at the top left of your database. Choose "Board." Notion automatically groups the board by the first Select property it finds — in our case, Status. Your database now shows as a Kanban board with columns for "Not Started," "In Progress," and "Done." Drag cards between columns to update their status. Name this view "By Status."

Try This Now

Add a Board view to your database grouped by Status. Drag one of your rows from "Not Started" to "In Progress." Notice how the Status property updates automatically. Drag it back. This is the core of Kanban-style task management in Notion.

Adding a Calendar View

Click "+ Add a view" again, choose "Calendar." Set it to display by Due Date. The calendar now shows your database entries on the days they are due. Click on any date to add a new entry directly from the calendar. Entries without a due date are listed at the top as "No date." Name this view "Calendar."

Adding a Gallery View

Add another view and choose "Gallery." The gallery shows each record as a card. If any records have a cover image set, it displays prominently. You can change what appears on each card by clicking "Properties" at the top and toggling which properties show. Gallery view is excellent for visual content — recipe collections, portfolio items, reading lists with book covers. Name this view "Gallery."

Adding a List View

Add a final view and choose "List." The list view is the most compact format — just the title and a few key properties per row. No columns, no cards. Best for large reference databases you scan quickly. Name this view "List."

Filters on Views

Each view can have its own independent filter. With the Board view selected, click Filter at the top and add a rule: "Status is not Done." Now your board only shows active tasks — completed tasks are hidden automatically. Switch to the Table view — all tasks are still visible there because the filter only applies to the Board view.

Sorts on Views

Click Sort on any view and add a sort: Sort by Due Date ascending. Your tasks now appear in chronological order by deadline. Add a second sort: by Status. Tasks are now sorted by due date first, then by status within the same date. Each sort level refines the ordering further.

Pro Tip

Name your views descriptively: "This Week (Due)" for a calendar filtered to this week's deadlines, "Active Board" for a kanban filtered to In Progress and Not Started, "All Tasks" for an unfiltered table. Good view names turn your database into a self-documenting system.

Tutorial Part 4 — Templates

Two Types of Templates in Notion

Notion has two kinds of templates that beginners often confuse. Workspace templates are pre-built page or database structures from the Notion template gallery — you duplicate them into your workspace. Template buttons are buttons inside your own databases that create new entries pre-populated with a structure you define. We will cover both.

Using Workspace Templates from the Gallery

Click the + button in the sidebar, then look for "Templates" in the onboarding view, or navigate to notion.so/templates. Browse the gallery and find a template that interests you — "Personal Task Manager," "Meeting Notes," or "Project Tracker." Click "Get template" or "Duplicate" to copy it to your workspace. The template appears as a new page in your sidebar. Explore how it is built — this is one of the fastest ways to learn advanced Notion techniques.

Creating a Template Button for Database Entries

Template buttons are the more powerful — and more frequently used — type. They automate the creation of consistently structured database records.

To create a template button for your database:

  1. In Table view, click the dropdown arrow next to the blue "New" button at the top right of the database.
  2. Select "+ New template."
  3. You are now in the template editor for this database. Add a title at the top (this becomes the default record name pattern if you want).
  4. Below the title properties, add the content structure you want every new record to start with: a heading "Notes," a heading "Subtasks," a to-do list with placeholder steps, a callout for important context.
  5. Pre-fill any properties: if every new task should start as "Not Started" status, set the Status property to "Not Started" in the template.
  6. Name the template something clear like "Standard Task" or "New Project."
  7. Click "Back" to return to the database.
Try This Now

Create a template button for your practice database. Include a Heading called "Task Notes," a to-do list with three placeholder steps, and a Callout block for blockers. Set the default Status to "Not Started." Then create a new entry using this template and see how it pre-fills your structure automatically.

Template Buttons on Regular Pages (Not Just Databases)

You can also add a template button directly to a regular page — not just inside databases. Type /button and select "Template button." Define what content the button creates when clicked. For example: a weekly review template button on your Home page that creates a new structured review page each week when clicked, with all your standard headings and sections pre-filled.

Tutorial Part 5 — Linking Databases

This is where Notion becomes genuinely powerful and genuinely different from every other tool in this category. Linking databases with Relation properties creates a connected workspace where data flows between your systems without manual duplication.

Setting Up Two Databases to Link

First, create two databases as separate full-page databases in the sidebar:

  1. Click + in the sidebar, choose "Table." Name it "Projects." Add properties: Status (Select: Planning / Active / Complete), Client (Text), Deadline (Date).
  2. Click + in the sidebar again, choose "Table." Name it "Tasks." Add properties: Status (Select: To Do / In Progress / Done), Priority (Select: High / Medium / Low), Due Date (Date).

Add a few sample projects in your Projects database and a few sample tasks in your Tasks database.

Adding the Relation Property

Go to your Tasks database. Click the + button to add a new property. Choose Relation. A dialog appears asking which database to connect to. Choose "Projects." Make sure "Show on Projects" is enabled — this creates the bidirectional link. Name this property "Project." Click "Add relation."

Now each task in your Tasks database has a "Project" field. Click on any task's Project cell and search for one of your project names. Select it. That task is now linked to that project.

Try This Now

Add the Relation property to your Tasks database linking to Projects. Then link two or three of your tasks to one of your projects. Now open that project record in full-page view — you will see a section at the bottom showing all the tasks linked to this project. This is the bidirectional relation in action.

Adding a Rollup to Aggregate Child Data

A Rollup property reads data from the records linked through a Relation property and aggregates it. Go to your Projects database. Click + to add a new property. Choose Rollup. Configure it:

  • Relation: "Tasks" (the relation we just created)
  • Property: "Status" (which property from Tasks to roll up)
  • Calculate: "Count all" (count how many tasks are linked)

Name this property "Task Count." Now each project shows a live count of how many tasks are linked to it. Change the Calculate to "Percent checked" if your task completion uses a Checkbox property — this gives you an instant completion percentage for each project.

Tutorial Part 6 — Notion AI

Enabling Notion AI

Notion AI is an add-on feature available on Notion's paid plans (Plus and above). To enable it, go to Settings (click your workspace name in the top left, then Settings) and look for the AI add-on option. You can trial Notion AI for free for a limited number of uses before committing to the add-on subscription.

Using Ask AI on a Page

Open any Notion page that has substantial content — a project brief, a set of meeting notes, a long document. Click the AI sparkle button in the top right corner (labeled "Ask AI"). A chat interface appears on the right side of the page. Type a question about the page's content: "What are the key action items in this document?" or "Summarize this project brief in three bullet points." Notion AI reads the current page's content and answers specifically about what is there — not generic AI answers, but answers grounded in your actual notes.

Try This Now

On any page with a few paragraphs of text, click Ask AI and type: "Summarize this page in two sentences." See how the AI reads and condenses your actual content rather than giving a generic response. Then try: "What are the main points on this page?"

Using AI to Write

On an empty line in any Notion page, press Space. A small AI menu appears. Choose "Draft with AI" and type your prompt. For example: "Write a project kickoff email to a new client starting a 3-month web design project. Tone: professional but friendly." Notion AI generates a full draft. Edit it to add your specific details, client name, project specifics, and personal voice. The draft is a starting point, not a final product — but it eliminates the blank-page problem.

Summarizing Meeting Notes

This is one of the highest-value Notion AI use cases. After writing or pasting in meeting notes, select all the text, then use the AI menu (or the AI button) to "Summarize." You get a clean 3-5 bullet executive summary of the key points and decisions. Or try "Extract action items" — AI identifies every task and commitment mentioned in the notes and returns a clean checklist.

Autofilling Database Properties

In any database, you can add an AI-powered property that automatically analyzes each record and fills in a value. Add a new property and choose "AI autofill." Configure it: choose which other properties to analyze, and what you want to generate (a summary, tags, a status assessment). Run the autofill on all records and watch it populate. Excellent for adding summaries to a large notes database or auto-tagging entries by content.

Tutorial Part 7 — Building a Complete Freelancer Workspace

Now we put it all together. Here is how to build a functional freelancer workspace in Notion — the architecture used in FreelanceOS Pro — from the ground up using everything you have learned in this tutorial.

The Architecture: Four Core Databases

A complete freelancer workspace needs four databases linked together:

  1. Clients DB — one record per client. Properties: Name, Status (Active/Past/Lead), Industry, Contact Email, Notes.
  2. Projects DB — one record per project. Properties: Name, Client (Relation to Clients), Status (Planning/Active/Complete), Deadline, Revenue.
  3. Tasks DB — one record per task. Properties: Name, Project (Relation to Projects), Status, Priority, Due Date, Estimated Hours.
  4. Notes DB — one record per note or meeting. Properties: Name, Related Project (Relation to Projects), Date, Type (Select: Meeting Notes / Research / Idea / Reference).

Building the Home Dashboard

Create a new full-page page titled "Home." This is your Notion hub. Add four linked database views using /linked, one for each database, with targeted filters:

  • Active Projects: Linked view of Projects, filtered to Status = Active. Gallery view showing project name and deadline.
  • Tasks This Week: Linked view of Tasks, filtered to Due Date = This Week, Status not Done. Board view grouped by Status.
  • Recent Notes: Linked view of Notes, sorted by Date descending, limit to 5 entries. List view.
  • Active Clients: Linked view of Clients, filtered to Status = Active. Table view showing name, industry, and linked projects count (via Rollup).

Result: Your Home page now shows a live dashboard — active projects, tasks due this week, recent notes, and active clients — all updating automatically as you work in the underlying databases. This is the power of linked databases and views in a real workspace.

The Rollup Chain

Add Rollup properties to make your databases self-reporting:

  • In Clients: Rollup of Projects relation — "Count all" projects per client, and "Sum" of revenue per client.
  • In Projects: Rollup of Tasks relation — "Count all" tasks, "Count checked" for complete tasks, "Sum" of estimated hours.
  • The result: every client record shows total project count and total revenue. Every project record shows task count and completion percentage. No manual tracking required.

10 Pro Tips to Level Up Your Notion

You have learned the fundamentals. Here are ten techniques that separate intermediate Notion users from power users.

  1. Cmd/Ctrl + P for Quick Find: Use this keyboard shortcut to jump to any page in your workspace in under two seconds. Once you have dozens of pages, this is your primary navigation tool — faster than clicking through the sidebar every time.
  2. Create a capture inbox: Add a simple page called "Inbox" or "Capture" at the top of your sidebar. Use it as a dumping ground for everything — ideas, links, tasks, notes — that you capture quickly and process later. Keeping your main databases clean and reviewing your inbox during a daily or weekly review session is more sustainable than perfectly categorizing everything on the fly.
  3. Group your Board views strategically: Board view does not have to group by Status. You can group by Priority (a board with Low / Medium / High columns), by Client (a board with one column per client), or by Due Date week. Click "Group" at the top of any Board view and change the grouping property.
  4. Use @date reminders: When you mention a date inline with @, you can set a reminder on that date. Type @tomorrow and then click the date mention — you will see an option to set a notification time. Notion will send you a reminder at that time on that date.
  5. Synced blocks for repeated content: If the same information needs to appear on multiple pages — a team standup agenda, a recurring disclaimer, your current weekly focus — create it as a synced block. Changes made anywhere update everywhere the block is used automatically.
  6. Use the Web Clipper for research: Install the Notion Web Clipper browser extension. Configure it to clip into a Research database with Date and URL properties. Every time you find a useful article, clip it in two clicks. Your research is now searchable, filterable, and linkable to the projects it informs.
  7. Linked database filters beat creating new databases: When you want a "subview" of your data (like "Tasks filtered to Project X"), create a linked database view with a filter instead of creating a new database. One source of truth, many views. New databases mean duplicated data and maintenance headaches.
  8. Use the formula property for dynamic calculations: Notion formulas can calculate due date urgency (if days until due < 3, show "Urgent"), compute whether a project is over budget, or combine two text properties. Formula syntax takes practice but unlocks powerful dynamic properties that update automatically.
  9. Set up database templates for every recurring record type: Every database should have at least one template. Task template with your standard subtasks. Meeting notes template with your standard agenda sections. Project template with your standard phases. The 5 minutes to set up a template saves hours across dozens of future records.
  10. Review and simplify your workspace monthly: Notion workspaces tend to grow organically and become cluttered. Schedule a monthly 30-minute "workspace review" — archive databases you have not used, simplify hierarchies that have gotten too deep, remove properties from databases you never actually fill in. The best Notion systems are the ones that stay lean, not the ones that try to track everything.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to learn Notion?
Most users can learn Notion's basics — pages, blocks, and a simple database — in an afternoon of hands-on practice. Becoming comfortable with multiple views, filters, and relations takes about one to two weeks of regular daily use. Building a sophisticated multi-database workspace system (like the one in Part 7 of this tutorial) takes one to three months for most people. The learning curve is real but manageable if you start simple and add complexity only when you feel the need for it.
Is there an official Notion tutorial?
Yes. Notion has an official help center at notion.so/help with written tutorials organized by topic, and an official YouTube channel with video walkthroughs of every major feature. When you first create a Notion account, the onboarding flow walks you through the basics interactively. The Notion template gallery at notion.so/templates also includes "starter kits" that come with usage explanations built into the pages — exploring these is one of the best self-directed tutorials available.
What should I build first in Notion?
Start with a simple Tasks database with just Name, Status (Select), and Due Date properties, and add a Board view grouped by Status. This gives you immediate practical value — a working Kanban task board — while teaching you the most important Notion concepts: databases, properties, and views. Once using the Tasks database feels natural (usually after a week or two), add a Projects database and link them with a Relation property.
Can I import my data from other apps into Notion?
Yes. Notion supports importing from Evernote (ENEX files), Trello (JSON export), Asana, CSV files (imported as databases), Confluence, Google Docs, Word documents, HTML files, and plain text files. Go to Settings and Members from the top left, then click "Import" in the left nav of the settings panel. Evernote and CSV imports are the most commonly used and generally work well. Trello imports bring in boards as Notion boards with reasonable fidelity.
Is Notion good for students?
Yes — Notion is extremely popular among university students. Common student setups: a Courses database with one entry per class, an Assignments database linked to Courses with due dates and grade properties, a Reading List database for papers and books, and research notes pages linked to a bibliography database. Notion offers free access to a more generous plan for students and educators through their Education plan — verify your student email at notion.so/students to access it.
What is the difference between a page and a database in Notion?
A page is a free-form canvas for any content — text, images, embedded blocks, and databases. A database is a specialized type of page that organizes its sub-pages as structured records with typed properties (dates, statuses, checkboxes, numbers). You can think of a database as a page where all the "sub-pages" inside it are organized in a filterable, sortable, viewable table, board, calendar, or other format. All database records are technically Notion pages — you can open any record and see its full page content — but they are also structured data entries with typed fields.
