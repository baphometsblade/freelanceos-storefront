Quarterly Taxes for Freelancers: How to Pay Estimated Taxes in 2026

Updated July 2026 · By FreelanceOS Team
Home › Blog › Quarterly Taxes for Freelancers

Tax disclaimer: This article covers general US federal tax concepts for educational purposes. Tax laws change and individual situations vary significantly. Always consult a qualified tax professional (CPA, enrolled agent, or tax attorney) for advice specific to your situation. Nothing in this article constitutes tax, legal, or financial advice.

Most people who transition from employment to freelancing get hit by the same surprise: a large tax bill at the end of the year because they assumed taxes would be handled the same way they were when they had a paycheck. They were not.

As a self-employed freelancer, nobody withholds taxes from your income. You are responsible for calculating and paying your own taxes — and the IRS expects you to do it four times per year, not once. This system is called estimated quarterly taxes, and getting it right is one of the most important financial habits you can build as a freelancer.

What Are Estimated Quarterly Taxes?

The US tax system operates on a pay-as-you-go basis. Employees have taxes withheld from each paycheck throughout the year, so they are continuously paying their tax obligation. Freelancers and self-employed individuals have no withholding, so the IRS requires them to make estimated tax payments four times per year to approximate the same effect.

You are required to make quarterly estimated tax payments if you expect to owe at least $1,000 in federal taxes for the year after accounting for any withholding and credits. Most active freelancers earning $20,000+ per year will easily exceed this threshold.

What quarterly taxes cover:

  • Self-employment tax — 15.3% on net self-employment income (this is the largest tax surprise for new freelancers — it covers both the employee and employer FICA contributions)
  • Federal income tax — your marginal rate on taxable income (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, or 37% depending on income level)
  • State income tax — varies by state; 0% in Florida, Texas, and Washington; up to 13.3% in California
The Big Surprise for New Freelancers

Self-employment tax (15.3%) is often the shock. As an employee, you paid 7.65% in FICA and your employer paid the other 7.65% — you never saw the employer's portion. As a freelancer, you pay both halves. The good news: you can deduct half of self-employment tax as an above-the-line deduction on your return.

2026 Quarterly Tax Deadlines

The IRS divides the year into four estimated tax periods with payment due dates. Note that the periods are not equal in length.

If a deadline falls on a weekend or federal holiday, it moves to the next business day. Always verify the current year's deadlines at irs.gov — they shift slightly year to year.

Calendar Tip

Add all four quarterly tax deadlines to your calendar the first week of January every year. Set a 2-week reminder for each. This gives you time to calculate your payment, log into IRS Direct Pay, and confirm receipt before the deadline.

How Much to Pay: The Safe Harbor Rule

The question freelancers ask most often is: "How much should I pay each quarter?" The answer depends on which calculation method you use. There are two approaches:

Method 1: Safe Harbor (Recommended for Most Freelancers)

The Safe Harbor rule lets you avoid underpayment penalties by paying a percentage of last year's tax liability rather than trying to precisely calculate this year's taxes each quarter.

  • If last year's AGI was $150,000 or less: pay at least 100% of last year's total federal tax liability, split into 4 equal payments
  • If last year's AGI was above $150,000: pay at least 110% of last year's total federal tax liability, split into 4 equal payments

Example: Last year you paid $8,000 in total federal taxes and your AGI was under $150,000. Safe harbor means paying $2,000 per quarter ($8,000 / 4). Even if you earn significantly more this year, you will not be penalized for underpaying as long as you make those four $2,000 payments on time.

The advantage: you do not need to track your income and expenses in real time to calculate quarterly payments. You just look at last year's return and divide by 4.

Method 2: Actual Estimate (Better If Your Income Is Declining)

Calculate your estimated annual tax liability based on actual YTD income and expected full-year income, subtract known deductions and credits, then pay 25% of the result each quarter.

This method is more work but protects you from overpaying if your income is lower this year than last year. Use tax software (TurboTax, H&R Block, TaxAct) or a CPA's worksheet to run the calculation.

The Tax Set-Aside Rule

The most practical system for freelancers is not calculating precise quarterly amounts at all — it is setting aside a percentage of every payment the day you receive it, deposited into a dedicated tax savings account that you never touch except to make quarterly payments.

Recommended set-aside rates by income level:

  • Under $40,000 net income: set aside 20-25%
  • $40,000-$80,000 net income: set aside 25-30%
  • $80,000-$150,000 net income: set aside 30-35%
  • $150,000+ net income: set aside 35-40% and work with a CPA

When the quarterly deadline arrives, your tax account has the money. You pay from that account, not from your operating account. This eliminates the "I spent my tax money" problem entirely.

Separate Bank Account

Open a separate high-yield savings account named "Tax Reserve." Transfer your set-aside percentage immediately when you receive any payment. Treat this account as off-limits for anything other than tax payments. The interest you earn on it while waiting to pay is a bonus.

How to Pay Estimated Taxes to the IRS

Recommended

IRS Direct Pay (directpay.irs.gov)

Free, no registration required, available 24/7. Log in with your SSN, date of birth, and previous year's tax info for identity verification. Select "Estimated Tax" and the tax year. Pay directly from your checking or savings account. Payments post within 1-2 business days. Get a confirmation number immediately. This is the fastest and easiest option for most freelancers.

Scheduling Ahead

EFTPS (Electronic Federal Tax Payment System)

eftps.gov — requires registration (takes 7-10 business days to receive your PIN by mail). Once enrolled, lets you schedule all four quarterly payments at the start of the year. Best if you like to set everything up once and not think about it again. Used by many small business owners and their CPAs.

Mobile

IRS2Go App

The IRS official mobile app allows estimated tax payments via Direct Pay from your smartphone. Available for iOS and Android. Same underlying system as directpay.irs.gov.

Traditional

Form 1040-ES by Mail

Fill out a 1040-ES payment voucher, write a check to "United States Treasury," and mail to the IRS address for your state (listed in the 1040-ES instructions). Allow 5-7 business days for delivery. Not recommended given the availability of faster electronic options, but valid if you prefer paper.

State Quarterly Estimated Taxes

In addition to federal quarterly taxes, most states with income tax also require estimated quarterly payments from self-employed residents. State deadlines often mirror the federal schedule but not always — check your state's department of revenue website for specifics.

States with no income tax (no state quarterly payments required): Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire (on wages only), South Dakota, Tennessee (on wages only), Texas, Washington, Wyoming.

States with the highest income tax rates (extra planning needed): California (up to 13.3%), New Jersey (up to 10.75%), Oregon (up to 9.9%), Minnesota (up to 9.85%), Massachusetts (flat 5% but 9% on certain capital gains).

Most state tax agencies offer online payment portals similar to IRS Direct Pay. Search "[your state] estimated tax payment online" to find the official portal.

Common Quarterly Tax Mistakes Freelancers Make

Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not paying at all in year one — many new freelancers don't know about quarterly taxes until they file their first return and owe a large bill plus penalties. Start making estimated payments from your very first freelance payment.
  • Paying quarterly but forgetting state taxes — federal payments do not cover state taxes. If your state has income tax, you need to make state estimated payments separately.
  • Using your operating account for taxes — money sitting in your main business account tends to get spent. Keep your tax reserve in a separate account that you psychologically treat as untouchable.
  • Forgetting the self-employment tax — income tax at your marginal rate is only part of what you owe. Self-employment tax (15.3% on net SE income) often catches new freelancers off guard and makes their total tax rate much higher than they expected.
  • Not tracking deductible expenses — your quarterly payment is based on net profit (income minus deductible expenses), not gross income. Every legitimate business expense reduces the amount you owe. Track everything.
  • Missing the deadlines — late payments incur penalties even if you pay in full eventually. Set calendar reminders 2 weeks before each deadline, not the day of.

Frequently Asked Questions

When do freelancers pay quarterly taxes in 2026?
The four estimated tax payment deadlines for 2026 are: April 15 (Q1: January-March income), June 16 (Q2: April-May income), September 15 (Q3: June-August income), and January 15, 2027 (Q4: September-December income). If a deadline falls on a weekend or holiday, it moves to the next business day. Always confirm current deadlines at irs.gov.
How do I calculate quarterly estimated taxes as a freelancer?
The safest method is the Safe Harbor rule: pay at least 100% of last year's total tax liability divided into 4 equal payments (or 110% if last year's AGI exceeded $150,000). This protects you from underpayment penalties regardless of what you earn this year. Alternatively, estimate your actual current-year tax liability based on YTD income and deductions, and pay 25% of that each quarter.
What happens if I don't pay quarterly taxes?
If you owe more than $1,000 in federal taxes and did not make adequate estimated payments during the year, the IRS charges an underpayment penalty. The penalty rate changes quarterly (tied to the federal short-term rate + 3%). It is not catastrophic — usually a few hundred dollars — but it is entirely avoidable with proper quarterly payments.
How do I pay quarterly estimated taxes to the IRS?
The easiest method is IRS Direct Pay (directpay.irs.gov) — free, no registration needed, pay directly from your bank account, get a confirmation number immediately. EFTPS (eftps.gov) requires enrollment but lets you schedule all four payments at once. You can also pay via the IRS2Go mobile app or by mailing a check with Form 1040-ES.
What is the self-employment tax rate for freelancers?
The self-employment tax rate is 15.3% on net self-employment income up to the Social Security wage base (confirm the current limit at irs.gov). This covers both the employee and employer portions of Social Security (12.4%) and Medicare (2.9%). Above the wage base, only the 2.9% Medicare portion applies. You can deduct half of self-employment tax as an above-the-line deduction on your tax return, which reduces your adjusted gross income.
