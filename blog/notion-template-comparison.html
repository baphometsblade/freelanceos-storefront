Quick Overview: What Each Creator Focuses On

Before the detailed comparison, it helps to understand the core focus of each creator. These products aren't interchangeable — they're designed for different primary users.

  • FreelanceOS — Built specifically for freelancers and small agencies running their entire business out of Notion. The focus is operational: client management, project tracking, invoicing, financial dashboards, and proposals. Business infrastructure, not personal productivity.
  • Easlo — A wide-ranging Notion creator with templates spanning personal productivity, life management, and business. Strong visual design and a large community. Best known for the Notion OS and various productivity bundles.
  • Thomas Frank — YouTube creator with a large following, known for the Ultimate Brain and related productivity/second brain templates. Focus is on knowledge management, note-taking, and personal productivity systems. Also produces content on Notion techniques.

The overlap is real, but the sweet spots are distinct. Understanding this upfront saves time reading the detailed comparison.

FreelanceOS: Designed Around Running a Business

FreelanceOS was built with one primary question: what does a freelancer actually need to manage their business in Notion, end to end? The answer shaped every decision in the template architecture.

Strengths

  • Fully connected client-project-invoice hierarchy. This is the feature most business-focused templates claim and few actually deliver well. In FreelanceOS, opening a client record shows you all their projects, all their invoices, total revenue, and recent contact history — all automatically maintained.
  • Free tier that actually works. The Lite version isn't a stripped-down demo — it's a functional client and project tracker that many freelancers use indefinitely without ever upgrading. This is rare in the template space.
  • Priced simply. FreelanceOS Pro is $29. AgencyOS is $49. There are no bundles with features you don't need, no subscription model, no recurring charges. One-time purchase.
  • Vertical focus means fewer compromises. Because the entire product is designed for one use case (running a freelance business), there are no "personal life" pages mixed in with your client tracker, no habit trackers sitting next to your invoice database. The workspace is clean and purpose-built.

Weaknesses

  • No personal productivity layer. If you want a full second-brain system, reading lists, or habit tracking alongside your business tools, you'd need to build that yourself or combine FreelanceOS with another template.
  • Smaller creator community. Easlo and Thomas Frank have much larger followings, more YouTube tutorials, and more community-generated resources. FreelanceOS has better documentation for its specific use case but less community content around it.
  • Narrower scope. If you're not primarily a freelancer or small agency owner, FreelanceOS isn't the right fit — it's optimized for that specific workflow.

Easlo: Strong Design, Wide Scope

Easlo has built one of the largest Notion template businesses, with products ranging from personal productivity to business management. The most popular products are the Notion OS (a complete life + work operating system) and various specialty templates.

Strengths

  • Beautiful, polished design. Easlo templates consistently look excellent. If aesthetics matter to how you feel using a workspace (and for many people they do), Easlo is at the top of the market.
  • All-in-one life + work options. If you want a single Notion workspace that handles personal goals, reading lists, habit tracking, AND business management, Easlo's bundles offer this in a coherent system.
  • Large library. Easlo covers a wide range of needs. Whatever specific Notion template you're looking for, there's a good chance Easlo has a version of it.
  • Active community and updates. Regular template updates, community Discord, and Notion tutorials make Easlo a strong option if you want ongoing support and engagement.

Weaknesses

  • Business management depth varies. For freelancers specifically, the business-focused templates in Easlo's lineup are less specialized than FreelanceOS. The client and project tracking is present but not as deeply connected to invoicing, proposals, and financial dashboards.
  • Pricing complexity. Easlo offers individual templates, bundles, and subscription tiers. The best value often requires buying a bundle with more than you need. It can be harder to figure out the right purchase.
  • Scope can feel overwhelming. The "complete life OS" approach is a strength for some users and a source of anxiety for others. If you just want a clean freelance business tracker, the personal productivity features can feel like noise.

Thomas Frank: Best for Knowledge Workers and Second Brain

Thomas Frank's templates are designed around his "second brain" and knowledge management philosophy. The flagship product, Ultimate Brain, is an extensive personal knowledge management system built in Notion. His productivity content on YouTube has driven significant awareness.

Strengths

  • Best-in-class knowledge management. If your primary use case is note-taking, literature reviews, project-based research, or managing a large knowledge base, Thomas Frank's templates are genuinely outstanding. No other template creator goes as deep here.
  • Exceptional documentation and tutorials. The YouTube library is extensive. If you get stuck on any part of the system, there's almost certainly a video that addresses it.
  • Task management is well-developed. The task system in Ultimate Brain is more sophisticated than most Notion templates — with time blocking, priority systems, and weekly reviews built in.
  • Regular updates and improvements. The template has been through multiple major revisions and continues to be actively maintained.

Weaknesses

  • Not designed for freelance business operations. Ultimate Brain isn't a client tracker. It doesn't have an invoicing system, a proposal pipeline, or a financial dashboard. Using it to manage a freelance business requires significant customization.
  • Complexity. Ultimate Brain is a comprehensive system that takes meaningful time to learn and set up. For someone who just wants to track clients and projects, it's significantly more than they need.
  • Price point. Ultimate Brain is priced higher than FreelanceOS and most Easlo templates. The price is justified by the scope, but if you're a freelancer who just needs business management, you're paying for a lot of features you won't use.

Who Should Choose Which

Freelancers & Agency Owners

Choose FreelanceOS

If your primary goal is managing your freelance business — tracking clients, projects, invoices, proposals, and revenue — FreelanceOS is the most purpose-built option. The free Lite version lets you verify it works for your workflow before spending anything. The Pro upgrade at $29 adds the financial dashboard, proposal pipeline, and advanced CRM features that growing freelancers actually need.

Life + Work Integration

If you want a single Notion workspace that combines personal goals, habits, and life management with your work tracking, Easlo's all-in-one systems are the best option. The design quality is excellent and the breadth of coverage is hard to match. For pure freelance business management, FreelanceOS goes deeper — but Easlo is the better choice if the personal productivity layer matters to you.

Students & Knowledge Workers

Choose Thomas Frank

If your primary use case is knowledge management, research notes, literature review, or building a second brain, Thomas Frank's Ultimate Brain is the best template on the market for that job. It's priced at a premium and complex to set up, but nothing else goes as deep on this specific use case. If you're a freelancer who also writes, researches extensively, or manages a large knowledge base, this might be worth layering alongside a business-focused template.

Can You Use More Than One?

Yes, and some people do. It's perfectly reasonable to use FreelanceOS for business operations and Thomas Frank's system for personal knowledge management, maintaining them as two separate Notion workspaces or as separate sections of the same workspace. The overlap (both have task management) can be managed by choosing one as the authoritative task system and using the other for its specialty.

Combining FreelanceOS with Easlo is less common because they cover similar ground — if you're primarily a freelancer, FreelanceOS is more specialized; if you want the full life OS, Easlo is more comprehensive. Buying both would mostly result in duplication.

Are these templates compatible with each other?
Not directly — each is a standalone Notion workspace you duplicate and customize. However, because Notion workspaces are flexible, you can manually copy databases or pages from one template into another if you want specific elements. There's no technical incompatibility, just the practical work of merging different structures.
Which template is best for a content creator?
For content creators, FreelanceOS's sister product CreatorHQ is the most purpose-built option — it includes a content calendar, brand deal tracker, and performance tracking alongside the standard business management layer. Easlo also has content-focused templates. Thomas Frank's system is better suited to knowledge management than content production workflows.
Do these templates work on mobile?
All three work in Notion's mobile app. The experience is somewhat more limited on mobile than desktop (some views and layouts display differently on small screens), but all core functionality — adding records, updating statuses, checking tasks — works fine on mobile for all three.
