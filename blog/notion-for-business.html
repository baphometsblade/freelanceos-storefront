The average small business runs on 8–12 different software tools. Email for communication. Asana or Trello for tasks. Google Docs for documents. Airtable for databases. Notion, at its best, replaces most of these with a single connected workspace.

What makes businesses switch:

  • Everything in one place. Docs, databases, wikis, and project management — no context-switching between 5 different apps.
  • Flexible enough to match your process. You build the system; Notion doesn't force you into a predetermined workflow.
  • Scales from solo to team. Start as a single user, invite collaborators later, set granular page permissions as you grow.
  • Genuinely affordable. Free for most solo use cases. $10/month per user for teams — a fraction of what dedicated PM + doc + wiki tools cost separately.

Setting Up Notion for a Solo Business vs Small Team

Solo business setup

When you're working alone, simplicity is everything. Use the free Notion plan. Start with a Project Tracker and a Client CRM — those two databases cover 80% of your daily business operations. Add a Financial Tracker when you hit tax season or want to track profitability. Add Meeting Notes and SOPs as the business grows. A solo setup should take 2–3 hours to build from scratch, or under 5 minutes with a pre-built template.

Small team setup (2–10 people)

Teams need two additional things: clear ownership and permission structure. Every task should have an Assignee property. Set up page-level permissions so team members see what's relevant to them without accessing sensitive financials or client notes they don't need. Use Notion's Teams workspace with the Plus plan ($10/member/month) to get shared team pages, unlimited guests, and 30-day page history.

Team Permission Structure

Create a "Team Space" at the workspace level for shared resources (SOPs, meeting notes, project boards). Keep sensitive items (finances, client contracts, salary info) in a separate page with restricted access. Notion's permission system works at the page level — a team member can have edit access to one page and no access to a sibling page in the same area.

Notion Permissions and Sharing

Understanding Notion's permission model is essential for any business use:

  • Full member: Can see and edit all pages in the workspace unless specifically restricted. Full members are billed users on paid plans.
  • Guest: Can access only the specific pages they're invited to. Ideal for clients reviewing deliverables or contractors working on specific projects. Guests are free on all plans (up to 10 on Plus).
  • Page share (public link): Share a read-only or editable page via public URL — useful for client-facing portals, public SOPs, or shared project dashboards.

Best practice: give clients guest access to their project page, not to your entire workspace. They see their deliverables, timeline, and meeting notes — nothing else.

Notion for Agencies

Agencies need everything a small business needs plus a client portal system and multi-project capacity planning. The key upgrade: a Master Projects database with a "Client" relation, a "Team Member" relation (linked to a Staff database), and a Capacity view showing how many active projects each team member owns. This prevents over-commitment and makes resource planning visible at a glance.

Notion for Freelancers

Freelancers need a leaner setup than an agency, but with just as much structure. The must-haves: a CRM tracking leads through to paid clients, a project tracker with deliverable checklists, and a financial tracker that makes quarterly tax prep painless. All three should be connected via relations so opening any client record shows their full project and payment history. Frequently Asked Questions

Can Notion replace tools like Asana or Monday.com for business?
For small businesses and freelancers, yes — Notion can replace Asana, Monday, and ClickUp for project management. It lacks some advanced automation and reporting features of dedicated PM tools, but its flexibility and all-in-one nature (combining docs, wikis, and databases in one place) makes it a better fit for teams that want to consolidate their tools.
Is Notion good for client management?
Yes. Notion works very well as a lightweight CRM for managing clients. You can build a contacts database with client status, project history, and communication notes. Link it to a Projects database to see all work per client. For most freelancers and small businesses with under 100 active clients, Notion's CRM capabilities are more than sufficient.
How much does Notion cost for a business?
Notion has a free plan that covers most solo business needs. The Plus plan is $10/member/month and includes unlimited file uploads, 30-day history, and guest access. The Business plan is $15/member/month with advanced permissions, SAML SSO, and 90-day history. Most freelancers and small businesses can run entirely on the free plan.
What is the ROI of using Notion for business?
Teams using Notion typically replace 3–5 separate tools: a project manager, a wiki/documentation tool, a note-taking app, a CRM, and sometimes a spreadsheet tool. At $10–50/month per tool, that's a $30–$250/month saving per team member. Beyond cost, the larger ROI is time: less context-switching, fewer lost files, and a single source of truth for all business information.
