The Notion Annual Review Template: How to Actually Use the End of Year Productively

The 5-part annual review in Notion — Life Audit, 12 Reflection Questions, Financial Review, Goals Review, and 2027 Planning. Do this once a year and the compound effect is extraordinary.

June 2026  ·  16 min read
Home › Blog › Notion Annual Review Template

Most people spend the last week of December doing one of two things: celebrating the holidays, or frantically trying to hit deadlines before the calendar flips. Almost nobody does what the most intentional, high-performing people quietly do: stop, look back, and deliberately design what comes next.

The annual review is not a productivity hack or a corporate exercise. It's the oldest planning technology humans have — intentional reflection at a natural cycle boundary. We do it at midnight on January 1st, usually with champagne and vague resolutions, then forget about it by February 3rd. The Notion annual review system in this guide does the same thing, but with structure that actually works.

Here's what intentional annual reviews do that casual "new year's resolutions" don't:

  • They break the hamster wheel — forcing you to stop running and look at where you've been running to
  • They reveal patterns invisible at the day-to-day level but obvious when you zoom out to 12 months
  • They prevent drift — the gradual, unnoticed shift from the life you want to the life that's just happening to you
  • They reset priorities — giving you permission to change direction based on what you've learned, rather than continuing by default

The five-part system in this guide takes 3-4 hours. Do it once and the clarity you gain is worth a month of productivity hacks. Do it every year and the compound effect over a decade is extraordinary.

When to Do This

Best window: December 27-31. The year is fresh in your memory, the holidays have quieted down, and the new year hasn't started yet. Block 3-4 hours in a single sitting or two 2-hour sessions. Do it alone, somewhere quiet, without your phone. This is thinking time, not multitasking time.

The 5-Part Notion Annual Review System

1
Life Audit — Where Are You Actually?
Before reviewing the year or planning the next, you need an honest snapshot of where you are right now — across all the domains of life, not just work. The life audit is a 10-area self-assessment that takes about 20 minutes and often reveals things you've been ignoring.

Rate each area from 1 to 10 — 1 being "this is a genuine crisis" and 10 being "couldn't be better." Be honest, not aspirational. The goal is accuracy, not optimism.

Career / BusinessRate 1-10
FinancesRate 1-10
Health / FitnessRate 1-10
Relationships (Family)Rate 1-10
Relationships (Friends)Rate 1-10
Romance / PartnershipRate 1-10
Personal GrowthRate 1-10
Fun / RecreationRate 1-10
Home EnvironmentRate 1-10
Contribution / PurposeRate 1-10

For any area rated 6 or below: write 2-3 sentences answering "What would a 10 look like in this area?" This is not about guilt — it's about honesty. A common pattern: Career is an 8, Health is a 4. The career success was built at the expense of the health. Knowing this is the first step to changing it.

In Notion: Create a table with the 10 areas, your rating for each, and a text column for your "what would a 10 look like" notes. Include this same table in every year's review so you can see how ratings change year over year.

2
Year in Review — 12 Questions to Close the Year
These 12 questions are designed to extract the signal from 12 months of noise. Some will be easy to answer. Some will make you uncomfortable. The uncomfortable ones are usually the most important.
1What were your 3 biggest wins this year? (Career, personal, relationships — any domain counts.)
2What were your 3 biggest disappointments? (Not failures — disappointments, things that didn't go how you hoped.)
3What did you spend too much time on? (Activities, people, platforms, thought patterns.)
4What did you not spend enough time on? (Things that matter but kept getting deprioritized.)
5What's one thing you'd do differently if you could re-run this year?
6What are you most proud of — not your biggest achievement, but what fills you with genuine pride?
7What did you learn this year? (Skills, insights about yourself, lessons from mistakes.)
8Who had the biggest positive impact on you this year, and did you tell them?
9What habit served you best this year? (The practice that most supported your wellbeing or performance.)
10What habit hurt you most? (The pattern you kept returning to despite knowing it wasn't serving you.)
11What would 12-months-ago-you be most surprised to know about your life right now?
12If you had to describe this year in one word, what would it be?

In Notion: Each question gets its own text block under a toggle in your Year in Review section. Write without editing — draft freely, then refine. Aim for 3-5 sentences per question minimum. The quality of your answers depends on your honesty, not your writing ability.

3
Financial Review — An Honest Look at the Money
Most people avoid looking at their finances with this level of honesty. That avoidance is expensive — both financially and psychologically. The annual financial review takes about 30 minutes and gives you the clearest picture of your financial year that you can get.

Open your bank account, your accounting software (Wave, FreshBooks, QuickBooks), and your Notion Revenue Dashboard. Work through each of these:

Income vs Goal What did you set as your income target for this year? What did you actually earn? What was the gap or surplus? Why?
Income Sources Breakdown What percentage came from each client, project type, or revenue stream? Which was most stable? Which was most lucrative per hour?
Top 3 Expenses What were your three largest expense categories? Were they worth it? Would you spend the same next year?
What You'd Cut If your income dropped 30% tomorrow, what would you cut first? This reveals what's essential vs. comfortable habit.
Savings Rate What percentage of your gross income did you save or invest this year? Is this higher or lower than last year?
Net Worth Change Total assets minus total liabilities in January vs. today. Is the gap growing or shrinking? Why?

The financial review is not about judgment. It's about data. The person who knows exactly what they earned, spent, and saved is able to make clear financial decisions. The person who avoids this information makes decisions from anxiety instead.

4
Goals Review — What Did You Actually Achieve?
Pull out the goals you set at the beginning of this year. If you didn't set goals at the beginning of the year — that's important information, and it's one of the things you'll change this time. For each goal, do this honest assessment:

For each goal from last year, answer:

Did you achieve it? Yes / Partially / No — with a brief honest explanation of what happened.
Effort rating (1-10): How hard did you actually try? Be honest — "I tried" is not the same as "I consistently worked toward this."
Outcome rating (1-10): How satisfied are you with what you achieved relative to this goal?
What did you learn? Regardless of whether you hit it — what does this goal teach you about what you value, what you're capable of, or how you work?
Keep, drop, or transform? Does this goal belong in next year's plan? In the same form? Or does it need to change?

The goals review often reveals something important: many people set goals they don't actually want. The goal was set based on external expectation, comparison to someone else, or a version of yourself that no longer applies. Reviewing goals honestly gives you permission to let those go.

It also reveals the opposite: goals you cared deeply about but deprioritized all year under the pressure of daily urgency. These are the ones that most deserve intentional planning time in the next year.

5
Next Year Planning — Designing 2027
You've reviewed where you are (Life Audit), reflected on what happened (Year in Review), assessed your finances (Financial Review), and evaluated last year's goals (Goals Review). Now you design intentionally.

Step 1: Choose Your Word of the Year

Before setting specific goals, choose one word that captures the spirit of the year you want to create. A word of the year is not a goal — it's a guiding intention. When you face a decision about how to spend your time, whether to take an opportunity, or how to respond to a challenge, your word provides a north star.

Powerful words of the year include: Depth (doing fewer things more deeply), Momentum (building consistent forward progress), Rest (recovering and rebuilding after a period of overwork), Create (prioritizing creative output over consumption), Expand (pushing past comfortable limits), Focus (ruthless prioritization), Presence (being fully in what you're doing).

Choose your word by asking: "What quality, if I embodied it fully in 2027, would make the most difference?" That's your word.

Step 2: Set 3-5 Annual Goals

Three to five goals — not ten. Every goal you add dilutes the others. The best annual goal is:

  • Specific enough that you'll know when you've achieved it
  • Meaningful enough that you'll still care about it in July
  • Ambitious enough that achieving it would actually change something
  • Realistic enough that it's achievable with consistent effort, not luck

Spread your goals across life areas based on your Life Audit — if Health was a 4, at least one goal should address that. If Career was an 8, maybe Career doesn't need a stretch goal this year.

Step 3: Break Each Goal into a Q1 Milestone

An annual goal without a Q1 milestone doesn't start until February. For each annual goal, identify the specific progress you need to make in January-March for the annual goal to be on track. This becomes the question you ask yourself in every weekly review: "Am I on track for my Q1 milestones?"

Example Goal Breakdown

Annual Goal: Launch a digital product that generates $2,000/month in passive income by December 2027
Q1 Milestone: Complete product research and outline by January 31; complete first version by March 31; soft-launch to email list by April 7
First 3 actions in January: Survey existing audience about their biggest problems (Jan 5), research 3 competing products (Jan 10), outline product format and scope (Jan 15)

Step 4: The First 3 Actions in January

The final step: for each goal, identify the first 3 concrete actions you'll take in January. Not "start working on it" — specific, datable actions. The first actions are usually the hardest. Getting specific about them eliminates the vagueness that causes most plans to stall before they start.

Building the Annual Review in Notion

The Notion setup for your annual review is simple and reusable:

  1. Create a page called "Annual Reviews" in your Notion workspace.
  2. Inside, create a sub-page for each year: "2026 Annual Review," "2027 Annual Review," etc.
  3. Each year's page has 5 toggle sections — one per part of this review — that expand to reveal your answers.
  4. Link the Goals section to your Notion Goals database, so you can see last year's goals inline.
  5. Link the Financial Review section to your Revenue Dashboard database, so income figures pull automatically.
  6. Add a "Previous Reviews" section at the bottom of each year's page with links to past years — so you can read 2024's review while writing 2026's.

Create the template once (with the toggle structure, the Life Audit table, and the 12 questions already written). Duplicate it each December, update the year in the title, and fill it in during your review session. Over time, you build a searchable archive of every year's review — one of the most valuable personal documents you can have.

Make It Annual, Not Optional

Add "Annual Review" as a recurring event in your Google Calendar for December 28-29 every year, blocking 4 hours. Treat it like any important client deadline. The people who do this consistently report it as the highest-value 4 hours they spend all year — not despite the lack of immediate output, but because of the clarity it creates for everything that follows.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a Notion annual review?
A Notion annual review is a structured reflection and planning session built inside Notion — typically done in the last week of December or first week of January. It uses Notion pages, toggle blocks, and linked databases to guide you through reviewing the past year (wins, disappointments, patterns, lessons) and planning the next year (goals, themes, Q1 priorities). The Notion format keeps all your annual reviews in one place, searchable and comparable year over year.
When should I do my annual review?
The best window for an annual review is December 27-31 — after the holidays but before the new year pressure hits. This gives you quiet time to reflect while the year is still fresh. The second-best window is January 1-7. Avoid doing it before Christmas (too much happening) or after January 15 (momentum for the new year has already been established without the review's direction). Block 3-4 hours in a single sitting, or spread across two 2-hour sessions.
What questions should I ask in an annual review?
The 12 most powerful annual review questions: (1) What were my 3 biggest wins? (2) What were my 3 biggest disappointments? (3) What did I spend too much time on? (4) What did I not spend enough time on? (5) What's one thing I'd do differently? (6) What am I most proud of? (7) What did I learn this year? (8) Who had the biggest positive impact on me? (9) What habit served me best? (10) What habit hurt me most? (11) What would 12-months-ago-me be most surprised about? (12) One word to describe this year.
What is a life audit in an annual review?
A life audit is a systematic rating of different life areas to identify where you're thriving and where you're neglecting yourself. Rate each area from 1-10: Career/Business, Finances, Health/Fitness, Family Relationships, Friendships, Romance, Personal Growth, Fun/Recreation, Home Environment, and Contribution/Purpose. Areas rated under 6 need a deliberate plan for improvement. The life audit prevents career success from masking deterioration in other important life areas.
How do I build an annual review in Notion?
Build your Notion annual review as a page with 5 toggle sections — one for each part of the review. Each toggle expands to reveal the prompts for that section. Link the Goals Review section to your Goals database, and the Financial Review section to your Revenue Dashboard. Add a 'Previous Reviews' section at the bottom with links to past years' reviews. Create this page once as a template and duplicate it each year.
What is a word of the year and how do I choose one?
A word of the year is a single word that captures the spirit or theme of the year you want to create. Unlike specific goals (which are measurable outcomes), a word of the year is a guiding intention. Good examples: 'Depth' (doing fewer things more deeply), 'Momentum' (building forward progress), 'Rest' (recovering from overwork), 'Create' (prioritizing creative output). Choose your word by asking: what would make next year fundamentally different from this year, in one word?
How is an annual review different from goal setting?
Annual review and goal setting are two different activities that work together. The annual review looks backward — it's a structured retrospective of what happened, what worked, what didn't, and why. Goal setting looks forward — it's a planning exercise for what you want to create. The annual review should come first, because reviewing the past year reveals patterns and lessons that change what goals make sense for the next year. Setting goals without reviewing is like navigating without looking at where you've been.
