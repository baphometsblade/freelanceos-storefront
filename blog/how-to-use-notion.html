1. What Is Notion?

Notion is an all-in-one workspace that combines notes, databases, tasks, wikis, and project management into a single, highly flexible tool. Used by more than 30 million people worldwide — from solo freelancers to Fortune 500 engineering teams — Notion has become the default choice for anyone who wants one tool instead of five.

The best description of Notion is not a formal definition. It is this: Notion is a Lego set for your productivity system. The pieces — blocks, pages, databases — are simple. What you build with them is entirely up to you. A freelancer builds a client dashboard and invoice tracker. A startup builds their entire company wiki, roadmap, and hiring pipeline. A student builds a second brain for all their notes and research. Same tool, completely different outputs.

What makes Notion different from every other productivity tool in this space:

  • Flexibility: Unlike Trello (boards only) or Evernote (notes only), Notion adapts to almost any workflow you can imagine — without requiring code or IT support.
  • Relational data: You can link databases together — connect your Projects database to your Tasks database to your Clients database — creating a lightweight relational system without a dedicated database tool.
  • All-in-one: Docs, tasks, databases, wikis, calendars, and AI assistance — in a single workspace with a single login. No more tab-switching between Google Docs, Trello, Evernote, and Airtable.
  • Collaboration: Share pages or entire workspaces with teammates, clients, or the public. Control permissions at the page level.
  • Templates: Thousands of community-built templates for every conceivable workflow, plus official Notion templates for everything from product roadmaps to personal journals.

Notion at a glance: Free plan available. Paid plans start at $10/month (Plus) and $15/month (Business). Available on Mac, Windows, iOS, Android, and web browser. Notion AI is an add-on feature available on paid plans.

2. How to Get Started with Notion

Creating Your Account

Head to notion.so and sign up with your email, Google account, or Apple ID. The process takes under two minutes. You'll be prompted to choose a plan — start with the free plan. You can upgrade anytime, and the free plan is genuinely useful for personal use and evaluation.

Free Plan vs. Plus Plan

The free plan gives you unlimited pages and blocks for personal use, up to 10 guest collaborators, and 7 days of page history. The Plus plan ($10/month) adds unlimited team members, 30-day history, unlimited file uploads, and better collaboration features. For most beginners and solo users, the free plan is more than enough to get started and build out a complete personal system.

Desktop App vs. Web Browser

Notion works in your web browser at notion.so, but the desktop app — available for Mac and Windows — is faster, supports better keyboard shortcuts, and provides limited offline access to recently opened pages. Download it from notion.so/desktop. There are also excellent iOS and Android apps for mobile access and quick capture on the go.

Understanding the Notion Interface

When you first open Notion, you see three main areas:

  • Sidebar (left): Your navigation hub. Shows your workspace name, starred pages (Favorites), private pages, shared pages, and any team spaces you belong to.
  • Page content (center): The main editing area. Everything you write, every database you create, every block you add appears here.
  • Page actions (top right): Share settings, version history, comments, and AI assistance buttons.

Click the + button next to any section in the sidebar to create a new page. Hover over any page in the sidebar to reveal options for duplicating, deleting, or moving it. Drag pages to reorder them or nest them under other pages as sub-pages.

Navigation tip: Use Cmd/Ctrl + P (Quick Find) to search and jump to any page in your workspace instantly — this becomes the most-used shortcut in your entire Notion workflow once you have more than a handful of pages.

3. Notion's 5 Core Building Blocks

a. Pages — The Canvas for Everything

Everything in Notion lives on a page. A page is a blank canvas — you can put any kind of content on it: text, headings, images, embeds, to-do lists, databases, or even other pages nested inside it (called sub-pages). Pages can be as simple as a grocery list or as complex as an entire company employee handbook.

Every page has a title at the top and an optional icon and cover image. Click the emoji area to the left of the title to add an icon. Click "Add cover" to add a header image from Unsplash or your own files. Below the title, the entire page is yours to fill with blocks. There is no predefined structure — you create it.

Pages can be nested inside other pages as sub-pages, creating a natural hierarchy. A client page might contain sub-pages for the project brief, meeting notes, invoice log, and delivery files — all in one place.

b. Blocks — The Atoms of Every Page

Everything on a Notion page is a block. When you type text, that is a text block. A heading is a heading block. Every image, bullet point, checkbox, code snippet, quote, callout, embed, or database is its own independent block that can be moved, styled, deleted, or duplicated on its own.

To add a new block, press Enter to create a new line, then either start typing or press / (the slash command) to open the block picker menu. The block menu lets you search for any block type by name — type /call to instantly find the Callout block, /code to insert a code block, /table to create a simple table.

You can rearrange blocks by hovering over the left edge of any block until the six-dot drag handle appears, then dragging it up, down, or to the side of other blocks. Blocks dragged next to each other automatically create multi-column layouts — a Notion feature many beginners miss entirely.

Essential block types to know:

  • Text — basic paragraph text, the default block type
  • Heading 1, 2, 3 — section headers of decreasing size (/h1, /h2, /h3)
  • Bulleted list, Numbered list — organized lists that support nesting
  • To-do list — checkboxes for tasks (/todo)
  • Toggle list — expandable/collapsible content section (/toggle)
  • Callout — highlighted box with a customizable emoji icon (/callout)
  • Quote — styled block quote with a left border
  • Code — code snippet with language-specific syntax highlighting
  • Divider — horizontal line to separate sections
  • Image — upload an image, paste a URL, or search Unsplash
  • Embed — embed content from external services (YouTube, Figma, Google Maps, etc.)
  • Table of contents — automatically generated TOC from page headings
  • Button — a template button that creates new content on click
  • Database — the most powerful block type (full section below)

c. Databases — The Heart of Notion

Databases are what separate Notion from every other note-taking app. A Notion database is a structured collection of items (called "entries" or "pages"), each with properties — typed fields that hold specific kinds of data. Think of it as a spreadsheet where every row can also open into its own full Notion page, with any content you want inside it.

A Projects database might have rows for each project, with columns for Name, Status (Active/Complete), Client, Deadline, and Revenue. But clicking on any project row opens a full page with the project brief, meeting notes, deliverables checklist, and attached files — all linked to that single database record.

Property types you will use constantly:

  • Title — the name of the record (required on every database)
  • Text — plain text field for notes or descriptions
  • Number — numeric value (supports formatting: currency, percentage, etc.)
  • Select — single-choice dropdown with color-coded options
  • Multi-select — multiple tags/labels from a set of options
  • Date — date picker, supports date ranges and reminder notifications
  • Checkbox — boolean true/false toggle
  • URL, Email, Phone — typed fields with appropriate formatting
  • Person — assign a workspace member to a record
  • Files & Media — attach files or images to a record
  • Relation — link this record to a record in another database (the magic of Notion)
  • Rollup — aggregate data from a related database (count, sum, average, etc.)
  • Formula — calculated field using Notion's formula language
  • Created time / Last edited time — automatic timestamps

Databases can be created as full-page databases (their own page in the sidebar) or inline databases (embedded on an existing page). Full-page databases are better for large, frequently used collections. Inline databases are great for smaller reference lists embedded in a document.

d. Views — See Your Data Your Way

One of Notion's most powerful — and most underused — features is views. Every database can be displayed through multiple views, each showing the same data in a completely different way. Adding a new view never changes or loses any data — it is just a different lens on the same underlying records.

  • Table view — classic spreadsheet grid. Best for data entry, seeing all properties at a glance, and bulk editing.
  • Board view (Kanban) — cards grouped into columns by a Select property (usually Status). Drag cards between columns to update their status. Best for task workflows and project pipelines.
  • Calendar view — shows records on a calendar by a Date property. Best for deadline management and scheduling.
  • Gallery view — card grid that prominently displays a cover image. Best for visual content like recipe collections, portfolio items, or book libraries.
  • List view — compact list with minimal visible properties. Best for large reference databases you browse quickly.
  • Timeline view — horizontal Gantt-style bars showing records across a time axis. Best for project planning with start and end dates. Requires Plus plan.

Add a new view by clicking + Add a view at the top left of any database. You can name each view and each view remembers its own filters, sorts, grouped properties, and visible columns independently.

The power of views: Your Tasks database can have a Table view for data entry, a Board view (grouped by Status) for daily workflow, a Calendar view for deadline planning, and a List view filtered to just High Priority items — all looking at the exact same data from the exact same database.

e. Relations and Rollups — Linking Your Workspace Together

Relations and rollups are what transform Notion from a sophisticated note app into a genuine workspace operating system. A Relation property creates a bidirectional link between two databases — records in Database A can be connected to records in Database B. A Rollup property then aggregates data from those linked records.

Classic example: You have a Projects database and a Tasks database. Add a Relation property in Tasks that points to Projects. Now you can connect each task to its parent project. Then add a Rollup property in Projects that counts the number of tasks linked to each project, or sums their estimated hours. This creates a live view of project progress without any manual updating.

Extend this further: add a Clients database with a Relation to Projects, and a Rollup in Clients that shows total revenue from all linked projects. You have just built a lightweight CRM, project management system, and financial dashboard — all from three linked Notion databases.

4. Your First Notion Setup: Step-by-Step

The best way to learn Notion is to build something real with it right now. Here is how to create a simple personal productivity system in about 15 minutes — a solid foundation you can expand over time.

1

Create Your Home Page

Click the + button in the sidebar to create a new page. Title it "Home." Click the emoji area to add a house icon. This is your personal dashboard and navigation hub. Add a Callout block at the top with your current priorities or a motivating personal mission statement. Everything else in your workspace will link from here.

2

Create a Tasks Database

On your Home page, type /database and select "Table — Inline." Title it "Tasks." Add properties: Status (Select: Not Started / In Progress / Done / Blocked), Priority (Select: High / Medium / Low), Due Date (Date), and Notes (Text). These four properties cover 95% of task management needs. Delete the default "Tags" property — you can add it back later if needed.

3

Add a Kanban Board View

Click "+ Add a view" at the top of your Tasks database. Choose "Board." The board automatically groups by Status, creating columns for each status option. Rename this view "By Status." Now drag tasks between columns to update their status visually. This is your daily task management view.

4

Add a Calendar View for Deadlines

Click "+ Add a view" again and choose "Calendar." Set it to display by Due Date. Name this view "Calendar." Now you can see which tasks are due on which days at a glance. Click any empty day to add a new task with that due date pre-filled — faster than the table for calendar-based planning.

5

Create Your First Template Button

In the Table view, click the dropdown arrow next to the blue "New" button and select "+ New template." Name it "Standard Task." Configure it: add a heading ("Details"), a checkbox list for sub-steps ("Research / Draft / Review / Deliver"), and a text block for notes. Now every new task you create using this template starts fully structured — no setup time each time.

6

Link Your Tasks to a Projects Database

Create a new full-page database by clicking + in the sidebar and choosing "Table." Title it "Projects." Add: Status (Select: Planning / Active / Complete), Client (Text), Deadline (Date), Revenue (Number — format as currency). Then go back to Tasks, add a new property, choose "Relation," and link it to Projects. Now tasks can be connected to their parent projects, and you can add a Rollup in Projects to count active tasks per project.

The golden rule of starting with Notion: Do not build what you think you might need someday. Build only what you need right now, then add complexity when you feel the specific pain of not having it. A simple system you actually use is infinitely better than a complex system you maintain but never open.

5. Notion's Most Useful Features

Template Buttons

Template buttons are one of the most powerful and underused features in Notion. A template button creates a new page (or a structured block of content) pre-populated with your defined layout — with a single click. They eliminate the friction of setting up recurring items from scratch. Create a weekly review template, a meeting notes template, a new client onboarding template, or a project kickoff structure. Once it is set up, every future instance is perfect and consistent.

To create a template button, type /button and choose "Template button" from the block menu. Define a name, then design the content that gets created each time it is clicked. You can add headings, checkboxes, callouts, database relations — anything you want to appear in each new entry.

Linked Database Views

A linked database is a filtered and customized view of an existing database, displayed inside a different page. It shows live data from the source database — any updates to the source immediately appear in the linked view. Type /linked and select "Linked view of database" to create one, then choose which database to link.

This lets you show a filtered slice of your Tasks database on your Home page — for example, only tasks due this week with High Priority — without duplicating data. The data lives in one canonical database; you view relevant subsets of it anywhere.

Filters and Sorts

Every database view supports independent filters and sorts. Click Filter at the top of any database to add a condition: Status = In Progress, Due Date = This Week, Priority = High, or any combination. Click Sort to order records: by Priority descending, then Due Date ascending, for example. Filters and sorts are saved per-view — your Board view can have different filters than your Table view, both looking at the same underlying database.

This feature transforms an overwhelming 200-record database into a focused, useful tool. Without filters, every database eventually becomes noise. With filters, every view shows exactly what you need right now.

Toggle Lists

Toggle blocks create expandable content sections. The heading is always visible; click it to reveal the content hidden beneath. Toggles are perfect for FAQs (the question is always visible, click to expand the answer), reference information you do not need constantly, completed project sections you want to archive visually, or long notes where you want progressive disclosure. Type /toggle to insert one anywhere.

Callout Blocks

Callout blocks are highlighted boxes with a customizable emoji. They draw attention to important notes, warnings, tips, or critical information in the flow of your writing. Click the emoji on the left side of a callout to change it — the warning sign for alerts, a lightbulb for tips, a checkmark for completed items, a fire for urgent items. Type /callout to add one.

Inline Mentions

Type @ anywhere in Notion to mention three types of things:

  • @person — mention a workspace member. They receive a notification if they have access to the page.
  • @date — insert a date inline (with optional reminder notifications). Perfect for adding a follow-up date in the middle of a paragraph: "Circle back on @next Tuesday to check on this."
  • @page — create an inline link to any other Notion page. The linked page name appears as a clickable reference, and Notion automatically updates it if the page is renamed.

Synced Blocks

A synced block is content that appears on multiple pages but is edited from a single source — changes made anywhere automatically update everywhere the block is used. Perfect for a "Team Updates" section you want on your Home page, your team's Wiki page, and your Weekly Review page simultaneously. Type /synced to create a synced block, add your content, then copy the synced block to other pages using the "Copy and sync" option.

Notion Web Clipper

The Notion Web Clipper is a browser extension — available for Chrome, Firefox, and Safari — that lets you save any webpage to Notion with one click. Choose which page or database to save it to. The clipper captures the title, URL, and optionally the full page content. Essential for research workflows, reading lists, competitive intelligence, and saving inspiration for later use.

6. Notion for Common Use Cases

Task Management — Replacing Todoist or Things 3

Notion excels at task management once you have set up a Tasks database with the right properties. The Board view is your daily driver — drag tasks between "Not Started," "In Progress," and "Done" columns just like a Kanban board. The Calendar view shows upcoming deadlines. The Table view gives you a full data view for weekly planning sessions.

The key advantage over dedicated task managers: your tasks live in the same workspace as your project notes, reference documents, and client information. No more context-switching between apps to find the meeting notes that tell you what you are supposed to be working on.

The key tradeoff: dedicated task apps like Todoist are faster for mobile capture, have better reminder and recurring task systems, and require zero setup. Notion's task system requires 30 minutes of setup but then exceeds what any dedicated task app can match in flexibility.

Note-Taking — Replacing Apple Notes or Evernote

Notion is a powerful note-taking tool for people who want organized, searchable, structured notes over raw speed. The block-based editor handles everything from quick jots to long-form research beautifully. A Notes database with tags, dates, and statuses gives you filterable, searchable notes that Apple Notes cannot match in organization.

Where Apple Notes and Evernote still win: instant capture speed, especially on mobile, and more reliable offline access. Notion's mobile app has improved significantly but still lags dedicated note apps for sub-10-second capture. Many power users maintain both: Apple Notes for quick mobile capture, Notion for organized long-form notes and reference material.

Project Management — Replacing Trello or Asana for Small Teams

For freelancers and small teams, Notion replaces Trello or Asana admirably. A Projects database with Timeline view (Gantt-style), linked to a Tasks database and a Clients database, covers 90% of project management needs for teams up to about 10 people. The entire client journey — proposal, active work, invoicing, delivery — can be managed from a single Notion workspace.

Where Notion falls short for larger teams: task dependencies, workload management by team member, and dedicated reporting dashboards are all more mature and purpose-built in tools like Asana, Linear, or Monday.com. If your team needs critical-path dependencies or capacity planning, look at dedicated PM tools.

Wiki and Documentation — Replacing Confluence

Notion is exceptional as a team wiki. Nested pages create a natural documentation hierarchy. Toggles and headings make long documents skimmable. The ability to embed live databases inside wiki pages means your documentation can be dynamic — your "Team Members" wiki page shows a live People database, not a static list that goes out of date.

Teams that have used Confluence and found it slow, clunky, and hard to maintain find Notion transformative. The lower barrier to writing and organizing content means teams actually keep their wikis up to date rather than letting them go stale.

Personal CRM — Replacing Airtable for Relationship Tracking

A Contacts database in Notion — with properties for company, role, relationship strength, last contacted date, how you met, and a notes field — creates a lightweight CRM system. Add a linked Interactions log database and you can track every conversation and follow-up. Linked to a Clients database and a Projects database, you have a complete client relationship management system inside your existing workspace, without a separate CRM subscription.

7. Notion AI: What It Can Do

Notion AI is an optional add-on feature that embeds AI assistance throughout your workspace. It is available for an additional fee on top of your Notion plan. Rather than switching to a separate ChatGPT window, AI assistance is available directly in every page and database you work in.

Notion AI's key capabilities:

  • Write from a prompt: Click the AI sparkle button (or press Space on an empty block) and describe what you need. "Write a client proposal for a 3-month web design project." Notion AI generates a structured, full-length first draft. Always edit before sending, but the starting point saves significant time.
  • Summarize: Select a long block of text, an entire page, or paste in a long email thread and ask AI to summarize it in bullet points or a single paragraph. Ideal for meeting notes, research articles, and long documents you need to process quickly.
  • Translate: Select any text and ask AI to translate it into another language — useful for international client communications or working with multilingual teams.
  • Extract action items: Paste in meeting notes and ask "What are the action items?" AI scans for every commitment, task, and follow-up mentioned and returns a clean checklist. This alone can save 10 minutes per meeting.
  • Ask AI about your pages: The "Ask AI" button at the top right of any page lets you ask questions specifically about that page's content. "What deliverables did we agree to in this project brief?" pulls the answer from your actual notes rather than generating a generic response.
  • Autofill database properties: AI can analyze a database record's content and automatically fill a Summary property, assign Tags, or assess a Status. Instead of manually summarizing 50 research notes, select them all and run an AI autofill — done in seconds.
  • Improve writing: Select any text and choose "Make longer," "Make shorter," "Fix grammar," "Make more professional," "Simplify language," or "Change tone to friendly." These editing tools are genuinely useful for polishing client-facing documents.

How to use Notion AI effectively: The highest-value use cases are not generating content from nothing — it is processing and restructuring content you have already captured. Use it to summarize, extract, and organize. AI-generated first drafts save time; AI-processed notes from real meetings and real work save even more.

9. 8 Beginner Mistakes to Avoid in Notion

These are the most common ways beginners undermine their own Notion experience — and exactly how to avoid each one.

Mistake 1: Building Too Complex Too Fast

This is the most common Notion failure mode. Beginners watch a YouTube tutorial, see an impressive 12-database workspace, and spend an entire weekend recreating it — then never actually use it because it is too overwhelming to maintain. Build only what you need right now. Add complexity when you feel the specific pain of not having it. The most beautifully organized Notion workspace that you never open is worth nothing. A simple, imperfect system you use daily is worth everything.

Mistake 2: Not Using Template Buttons

If you are manually recreating the same page structure over and over — setting up identical headings every time you write meeting notes, rebuilding the same project structure for each new client — you are wasting 5-15 minutes every single time. Template buttons eliminate this entirely. Build your standard structures once as templates, then create perfect instances in one click. This single habit change typically saves beginners several hours per month within the first few weeks.

Mistake 3: Nesting Pages Five Levels Deep

Notion allows infinite nesting, which tempts beginners into deep hierarchies: Home > Work > Clients > Active > Q3 > Acme Corp > Projects > Website Redesign. By the time you find what you need, you have clicked eight times. The solution: use flatter structures with better naming, and rely on Quick Find (Cmd+P) to jump directly to any page rather than navigating the sidebar tree. A well-named page at the top level is easier to find than the same page buried five levels deep.

Mistake 4: Only Ever Using Table View

Many beginners create a database, see Table view, and never discover that it is just one of six ways to look at the same data. They add 50 tasks to a table and find it overwhelming, then conclude that Notion is not better than their old spreadsheet. The fix: add a Board view grouped by Status for daily task management, add a Calendar view for deadline visibility, add a filtered List view for urgent items. The same database becomes dramatically more useful across multiple views.

Mistake 5: Not Linking Databases Together

If your Projects database and your Tasks database exist in complete isolation from each other, you are missing the core value proposition of Notion. The real power comes from Relations — linking tasks to their projects, linking projects to their clients, and using Rollups to see aggregated data without manual updating. Beginners who skip Relations are using Notion as an expensive notepad. Beginners who master Relations have a genuine workspace operating system.

Mistake 6: Expecting Notion to Be Fast for Quick Capture

Notion is a deep organizational tool, not a fast capture tool. The mobile app is slower to open and input into than Apple Notes, Google Keep, or a dedicated task app. Setting expectations correctly prevents frustration: use Notion for organizing, processing, and working with information, and use a faster dedicated app for quick on-the-go capture. Many productive Notion users inbox everything to Apple Notes, then process it into Notion during their daily review session.

Mistake 7: Rebuilding Your Old System Inside Notion

Beginners who migrate from Evernote try to recreate their Evernote notebook hierarchy inside Notion. Beginners from Trello try to rebuild their exact Trello boards. This approach misses what Notion actually is — a relational, database-driven workspace. Start fresh with a beginner's mind. Ask: "What do I need to track, what are the connections between these things, and what decisions do I need these systems to support?" The answer rarely looks like your old system.

Mistake 8: Never Using Filters and Sorts

A database without filters becomes useless as soon as you have more than 30 records — it is just an overwhelming wall of information. Beginners add tasks and projects until their databases are cluttered, then blame Notion for being disorganized. Filters are the cure. Filter your daily task view to "Status = In Progress AND Due Date = This Week." Filter your Projects view to "Status = Active." Suddenly every database surface shows only what is relevant right now. Filters and sorts are not optional power features — they are the core of how a well-designed Notion system works.

Frequently Asked Questions About Notion

Is Notion free?
Yes, Notion has a generous free plan that works well for individual users. It includes unlimited pages and blocks for personal use, up to 10 guest collaborators, and 7 days of version history. The Plus plan ($10/month) adds unlimited team members, 30-day version history, unlimited file uploads, and priority support. The Business plan ($15/month per member) adds SAML SSO, private team spaces, and advanced analytics. For most beginners and solo users, the free plan is sufficient to evaluate whether Notion fits your workflow and to build a complete personal productivity system.
Is Notion good for beginners?
Notion has a moderate learning curve — steeper than Apple Notes or Google Docs, gentler than tools like Airtable or Coda. The concept of blocks and databases can feel unfamiliar at first. The key to success as a beginner: start smaller than you think you should. Use Notion just for simple notes for the first week. Add a basic database in week two. Add views and filters in week three. Beginners who try to build a complex relational system on day one almost always get overwhelmed and quit. Beginners who start small and add complexity gradually almost always become power users within a month.
Can Notion replace Google Docs?
For personal and small team use, yes — Notion can replace Google Docs for most document types. It handles long-form writing, rich formatting, embedded images, tables, comments, and sharing with granular permissions. Where Google Docs still wins: real-time collaboration on large documents with many simultaneous editors (20+ people working on the same doc simultaneously), suggesting mode and tracked changes for editorial workflows, and tighter integration with other Google Workspace tools like Drive and Gmail. If you are a freelancer, solopreneur, or small team, Notion can fully and comfortably replace Google Docs.
Is Notion better than Evernote?
For most users in 2026, yes — Notion offers significantly more flexibility, better organization, relational databases, and a much more generous free plan than Evernote's current offering. Evernote still has faster mobile capture, better email-to-note features, and more reliable search for scanned documents. But for building an organized, interconnected personal knowledge system, Notion's capabilities far exceed what Evernote offers at comparable price points. Many former Evernote power users — including longtime users who were committed to Evernote for years — have permanently switched to Notion.
Does Notion work offline?
Partially. Notion's desktop app caches recently viewed pages so you can read them without an internet connection. However, creating new pages or editing existing ones while offline is unreliable — changes may or may not sync correctly when you reconnect. Notion is fundamentally a cloud-first application built around real-time sync. If you need robust, reliable offline-first note-taking — for example, if you regularly work in locations without internet — Obsidian (which stores all files locally as plain Markdown files) is a better fit for that specific requirement.
Can you use Notion for project management?
Yes, and many freelancers and small teams use Notion as their sole project management tool successfully. A well-configured Tasks database with Board, Calendar, and Timeline views — linked to a Projects database and a Clients database — handles most project management needs for teams of up to about 10 people. Notion's limitations for larger teams: no native task dependencies (you cannot say "Task B cannot start until Task A is complete"), limited built-in workload reporting, and no dedicated portfolio-level project tracking. For teams that need those features, dedicated tools like Asana, Linear, or Monday.com are better suited.
What is the difference between Notion and Obsidian?
These two tools serve very different kinds of note-takers, though they overlap in some use cases. Notion is cloud-based, collaboration-friendly, built around databases and structured data, with a visual block-based interface that most people find approachable. Obsidian is a local-first Markdown editor with a powerful bidirectional linking system and a graph view showing connections between notes — it is offline-first, privacy-focused, and deeply loved by the personal knowledge management community. Notion is the better choice for teams, structured project management, and mixed work and personal workflows. Obsidian is the better choice for private, deeply interlinked, long-form research and writing. Many power users actually use both: Obsidian for their personal research and writing library, Notion for team collaboration and client-facing work.
