What Is a Notion Wiki?

A wiki is a collection of interconnected pages where you document processes, policies, reference information, and procedures. In Notion, a wiki is typically a nested page structure — a home page with links to sections, each section containing sub-pages.

Unlike a database (which stores records with properties), a wiki is document-oriented. Think of it as a well-organized internal manual. You read and update it; it does not power dashboards or filtered views in the same way a database does.

Personal Wiki vs. Team Wiki

Personal Wiki (Solo Freelancer)
  • Your processes and SOPs
  • Scripts and templates you reuse
  • Client onboarding checklists
  • Tool setup instructions
  • Resource references (rates, legal, tools)
  • Accessible only to you
Team Wiki (Agency / Contractors)
  • Brand guidelines and tone of voice
  • Project workflow and approval processes
  • Contractor onboarding materials
  • Tool access and login processes
  • Meeting cadences and communication norms
  • Shared with team members

This guide focuses on the personal freelance wiki — the most valuable starting point. Once your own wiki is solid, expanding it for a team is straightforward.

How to Structure a Freelance Wiki

The most effective freelance wiki covers four categories:

  • SOP Library: Step-by-step processes for recurring tasks (client onboarding, project kickoff, invoice process, offboarding).
  • Client Handbooks: Client-specific notes — their brand voice, communication preferences, revision limits, key contacts, and password/access notes.
  • Resource References: Your rate card, contract template links, legal resources, tool logins, subscription list, and quick-reference cheat sheets.
  • Script Templates: Email scripts you reuse constantly — proposals, follow-ups, testimonial requests, scope creep responses, and offboarding messages.

Building the Hierarchy in Notion

Two approaches exist for building wiki structure in Notion:

Option 1: Nested Pages

Pure page hierarchy — a home page with linked sub-pages, which contain their own sub-pages. Simple, fast, and easy to navigate. Best for solo freelancers.

↳ Follow-Up Scripts
↳ Testimonial Request Scripts

Option 2: Database + Pages (Hybrid)

Use a database for SOPs (so you can tag by category, filter by status, and mark items as "needs update"), and nested pages for everything else. More powerful but slightly more setup. Recommended if you have 20+ SOPs or plan to share with contractors.

Start Simple Build Option 1 first. You can always convert a nested page structure to a database later — Notion makes this easy with the "Turn into database" option. Do not over-engineer the first version.

5 Essential Wiki Pages for Freelancers

Page 01
Client Onboarding SOP
Every step from signed contract to first deliverable. Includes: welcome email text, project setup checklist, tools to share access to, and the kickoff call agenda.
Page 02
Proposal & Pricing Reference
Your current rate card, package structures, common scope additions, and the logic behind your pricing. Removes guesswork when quoting.
Page 03
Scope Creep Response Scripts
Pre-written email responses for the most common scope expansion requests. Removes the "ugh, how do I say this" moment that often leads to doing work for free.
Page 04
Tool Access & Logins Reference
What tools you use, what each one does, and (with a link to your password manager) how to access them. Invaluable when onboarding contractors or recovering access after a lockout.
Page 05
Annual Review Template
A structured page for your year-end review: revenue vs. goal, top clients, top projects, what to start/stop/continue, and goals for next year.

Wiki vs. Second Brain: Different Purposes

These terms get conflated but they serve different functions:

  • Wiki: Operational reference. Stable, structured, process-oriented. You consult it when doing work. It describes how your business runs.
  • Second Brain: Personal knowledge capture. Dynamic, exploratory, idea-oriented. You add to it when learning. It captures what you are thinking and discovering.

A freelancer can benefit from both. Start with the wiki (higher immediate ROI), then build a second brain if you find yourself needing a personal research and knowledge system. They can coexist in the same Notion workspace with clear separation.
