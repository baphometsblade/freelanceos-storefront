What a Notion Project Management Template Actually Needs

Most Notion PM templates floating around the internet are glorified task lists. They have a Projects database and a Tasks database — and that's where they stop. That's not project management; that's a to-do list with extra steps.

A real Notion project management system needs five components working together:

  • A Projects database — with status, deadline, phase, client relation, and contracted value
  • A Tasks database — linked to projects, with dependencies, priority, assignee, and due dates
  • Multiple views — Kanban board, table, timeline/Gantt, and calendar at minimum
  • A status workflow — defined stages that reflect how work actually moves in your business
  • A Master Dashboard — a home page that surfaces active projects, overdue tasks, and upcoming deadlines without navigating

The difference between a working system and a template graveyard is whether those five components are connected and whether you actually open it every day. Setup matters, but the daily driver experience matters more.

Project Phases: Structuring Work from Brief to Delivered

Project phases are the highest-level view of where a project stands. They're different from task status — phases describe the stage of the overall project, not the status of individual deliverables. A well-designed Notion template separates phases from task statuses so you can see both simultaneously.

Phase 1

Discovery

Brief received, scope defined, proposal drafted. No billable work has started.

Phase 2

Kickoff

Proposal accepted, contract signed, onboarding complete. Work can begin.

Phase 3

Production

Active delivery phase. Tasks are being worked on and submitted for review.

Phase 4

Review

Deliverables submitted to client. Awaiting feedback or approval rounds.

Phase 5

Wrap-Up

Final files delivered, invoice sent, project closed and archived.

In a Notion Projects database, Phase is a Select property. Combined with a Status property, you get two-dimensional visibility: you can see that a project is in the Production phase (macro) and that three of its tasks are In Review (micro). This is the structure that makes Notion genuinely useful for project management rather than just task storage.

Tip: Create a Projects view filtered to Phase = "Wrap-Up" as your billing trigger. Any project that moves to Wrap-Up gets an invoice created the same day. This one filter view eliminates the most common freelancer cash flow problem: forgetting to bill because wrap-up always feels chaotic.

Task Dependencies: Building a Proper Blocking System

Task dependencies are where most Notion templates fall short. The reason is that Notion's native dependency system — drawing connecting lines in Timeline view — is purely visual. It doesn't block a task from being started; it just shows the intended sequence. For many freelancers that's enough. For projects with strict sequencing, you need to go one step further.

The Blocking Relation Method

In your Tasks database, create two Relation properties: Blocking and Blocked By, both pointing to the same Tasks database. Set the Blocking relation to sync with Blocked By (Notion will do this automatically when you enable the two-way sync). Now you can link Task B to Task A as "Blocked By Task A." A filtered view — Tasks where "Blocked By is empty" — shows exactly what's free to work on right now.

This is simpler to maintain than it sounds. You only need to set the dependency once at project kickoff, during the planning phase when you're breaking the project into tasks. After that, as tasks complete, the blocked tasks automatically become visible in your working queue.

Using Timeline View for Dependency Visualization

Notion's Timeline view (available on paid plans) is the closest thing to a Gantt chart in Notion. You set a Date range property on your Tasks database (start date + end date), switch to Timeline view, and you can draw dependency lines by hovering between two tasks. The result is a visual representation of your project schedule that most clients find easier to understand than a table or Kanban board.

The Timeline view is particularly useful for client communication. A screenshot of your project timeline — showing phases, tasks, and sequence — takes 30 seconds to export and answers "where are we at?" without a meeting.

Gantt tip: Group your Timeline view by Project. This gives you a multi-project Gantt chart that shows all active client work on a single scrollable timeline. Color-code by Phase using the color property on your Phase select field. At a glance you can see every project, where it sits in its lifecycle, and whether any timelines are colliding.

Team Views: Managing Work Across Multiple People

If you work with subcontractors, collaborate with a partner, or manage a small team, the Tasks database needs an Assignee property — a Person property in Notion that links to workspace members. Once you have that, you unlock several views that make team coordination far less chaotic.

The Assignee Board

Switch your Tasks database to Board view, grouped by Assignee. You immediately see every person's current task load as a stack of cards. Cards with red due dates are overdue. Cards with no due date are drifting. This view makes capacity problems visible in seconds — if one person's column has twelve cards and another has two, that's information worth acting on.

The Weekly Workload View

Create a filtered view of Tasks: Date is within the next 7 days, Status is not Done. Group by Assignee. This is your weekly team standup view — instead of asking "what's everyone working on this week?", you open this view and the answer is already there. It also shows if anyone is double-booked for a deadline day.

The Bottleneck Filter

Filter Tasks to: Status = "In Review" and Assignee is not empty. This is your review queue — work that's been submitted and is waiting for your feedback or client approval. If this view gets long, you have a bottleneck. Reviewing and clearing the In Review queue before starting new work keeps the project moving faster than any other single habit.

The Status Workflow: From Not Started to Done

A status workflow is the most important design decision in a Notion project management template. The right statuses match how your work actually moves; the wrong ones create friction at every step. Here's the workflow that works for most freelancers:

Not Started → In Progress → In Review → Done  |  On Hold

Five statuses. That's it. The most common mistake is creating too many statuses — "Backlog," "Scoped," "Assigned," "Started," "Submitted," "Under Revision," "Final Review," "Approved," "Archived." Every status you add is a decision you have to make every time you update a task. Decision fatigue is real, and the result is a database where nothing gets updated because it's too much work.

The five-status system works because:

  • Not Started — everything you plan to do but haven't touched
  • In Progress — what you're actively working on right now
  • In Review — submitted to client or waiting for feedback; you're blocked by external input
  • Done — complete, approved, no further action needed
  • On Hold — paused intentionally; not abandoned, not active

A "Today's Work" view — filtered to In Progress and Not Started tasks, sorted by due date — becomes the only view you need to open in the morning. Everything else is context.

Timeline View: Notion's Gantt Chart Replacement

Notion's Timeline view (introduced as a core feature in 2024 and significantly improved through 2025–2026) is now a fully viable Gantt chart replacement for freelancers and small teams. It is not as sophisticated as Instagantt or Microsoft Project, but for most independent project managers it covers everything you actually need.

Setting Up Timeline View in Notion

  1. Add Start Date and End Date properties to your Tasks database (or use a single Date property with the date range toggle enabled)
  2. Switch to Timeline view from the view switcher in your Tasks database
  3. Set the date property to your Start/End or Date range field — Notion will render each task as a bar across the timeline
  4. Group by Project to show all tasks organized under their parent project
  5. Enable dependencies in Timeline settings to draw dependency lines between tasks

Once configured, you can drag tasks to reschedule them, extend or shrink task bars to adjust duration, and click any task to open the full detail. Changes you make in Timeline view are reflected in all other views immediately — there's no separate "Gantt mode" that gets out of sync with your task database.

Sharing Timelines with Clients

Notion pages can be shared publicly via a web link with view-only access. Sharing your project timeline with a client requires nothing more than clicking "Share" on the relevant page and sending the link. The client can see the full Gantt-style view, current task statuses, and phase progress — without needing a Notion account. This is a significant advantage over dedicated PM tools that charge per-seat for client access.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Notion do project management?
Yes — Notion handles project management very well for freelancers, small teams, and agencies. It supports Kanban boards, table views, timeline views, task databases linked to projects, and status workflows. For most independent professionals and small teams it outperforms dedicated PM tools because it integrates clients, invoices, and documentation in the same workspace.
Does Notion have a Gantt chart view?
Notion has a Timeline view that functions like a Gantt chart. You can visualize tasks or projects across a time axis, set start and end dates, and see parallel workstreams at a glance. It is not as feature-rich as dedicated Gantt tools like Instagantt, but for most freelancers and small teams the Timeline view covers everything needed to track project schedules visually.
How do I manage task dependencies in Notion?
Notion supports task dependencies natively in Timeline view — you can draw dependency lines between tasks to show that one must be completed before another begins. For a more structured approach in a Tasks database, you can create a Blocking / Blocked By relation that links tasks to each other. Combined with filtered views, this lets you see exactly which tasks are blocked and what is preventing them from starting.
