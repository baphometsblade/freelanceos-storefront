Notion Pages: The Complete Guide

Master pages, blocks, templates, and databases — everything that makes Notion the world's most flexible workspace in 2026

Home›Blog›Notion Pages Complete Guide 2026

If you have spent any time in Notion, you have heard the phrase: "everything in Notion is a page." It sounds simple. But understanding what that actually means — and why Notion designed it that way — unlocks the full power of one of the most flexible productivity tools available in 2026.

This guide covers Notion pages in depth: what they are, how they differ from databases, the 50+ content blocks you can use inside them, how templates work, how synced blocks eliminate redundant content across your workspace, and how to share and publish pages to the web. By the end, you will understand Notion at a structural level that most users never reach.

What Are Notion Pages?

In Notion, a page is the fundamental unit of all content. When you create a note, you are creating a page. When you create a project in a database, the project is a page with properties. When you build a dashboard, that dashboard is a page. Your entire Notion workspace is a hierarchy of pages, nested inside each other.

Every page has four components:

  • Icon — An emoji, uploaded image, or Notion library icon that appears in the sidebar and at the top of the page
  • Cover image — A full-width banner image at the top of the page (optional but powerful for aesthetics)
  • Title — The page name, which also appears as the heading at the top of the page content
  • Body — An unlimited canvas of blocks: text, images, databases, embeds, code, and more
The Infinite Nesting Principle: Pages can contain other pages, which contain other pages — with no depth limit. Your workspace sidebar shows this tree structure. This makes Notion powerful for organizing hierarchical information, but also the source of the most common setup mistake: nesting everything rather than using databases for structured, recurring data.

On the free Notion plan, you have unlimited pages. The 1,000 block limit that existed in early versions was removed in 2022 — today there is no ceiling on how much you can create or store in Notion across all plans.

Pages vs. Databases — The Critical Distinction

Understanding the difference between a page and a database is the single most important conceptual leap in Notion. Most new users use pages when they should use databases, and end up with a workspace that cannot be filtered, sorted, or viewed in different ways.

Pages — Use When

  • You are writing a one-time document or note
  • The content is freeform and not repeatable
  • You do not need to filter or sort the content
  • It is a unique piece of content (meeting notes for a specific meeting, a specific project brief)
  • You need rich media, embeds, and multi-column layouts

Databases — Use When

  • You have multiple items of the same type (clients, tasks, projects, posts)
  • You need to filter, sort, or group items
  • Each item has structured properties (status, date, tags, numbers)
  • You want multiple views of the same data (board, calendar, gallery)
  • You need to link items to related items in other databases

Here is the key insight: every row in a database IS a page. When you click on a task in your Task database, you open a page for that task — with all its database properties at the top and a full freeform body below. You can write paragraphs, add images, embed videos, and nest sub-pages inside a database entry, just like any other page. Databases give you structured properties + all the power of a page in one.

Page Templates in Notion

Templates in Notion solve the consistency problem. Without templates, every new page you create starts as a blank canvas. If you create client pages, each one looks different because you set it up from scratch each time. With templates, one click creates a new page with a pre-defined structure — properties set, sections in place, checklists ready.

Types of Templates in Notion

  • Notion Template Gallery templates — Community-submitted workspace templates you can import into your own Notion account. Found at notion.so/templates.
  • Database templates — Templates within a specific database. When you click "New" on a database, you can choose from custom templates you have set up for that database. Each template creates a new page with pre-set properties and content.
  • Template buttons — A special block you place on any page. One click executes a pre-defined action: adds a section, creates a set of blocks, or repeats a format. Used for daily standup formats, meeting agendas, or habit tracking blocks.

How to Create a Database Template

  1. Open any database and click the dropdown arrow next to the New button
  2. Select "+ New template"
  3. Design the page structure: add your standard sections, default properties, and placeholder text
  4. Name the template and save it
  5. Now when you create new entries, click the dropdown arrow → select your template

Nested Pages and Sidebar Organization

Pages can be nested inside other pages to any depth. In the sidebar, you see your page tree — top-level pages with triangles you click to expand sub-pages beneath them. This structure works well for hierarchical information: a company wiki organized by department, a client folder containing all project pages, a library of templates organized by type.

Sidebar Management Best Practices

  • Favorites: Right-click any page → Add to Favorites. Favorited pages appear in a Favorites section at the top of your sidebar, always accessible regardless of where they live in your page hierarchy.
  • Hide from sidebar: Pages inside databases do not appear in the sidebar. This reduces clutter — your sidebar shows your five core databases, not every entry inside them.
  • Drag to reorder: Drag pages up and down in the sidebar to set your preferred order. Most-used pages go to the top.
  • Archive rather than delete: Create an "Archive" page and move rarely-used pages inside it. They remain searchable but do not clutter your primary navigation.
The Sidebar Rule: Your sidebar should have no more than 7-10 items at the top level. Everything else lives inside those pages or inside databases. A clean sidebar is a sign of a well-structured workspace. If you can see more than 15 items in your sidebar without scrolling, you need to reorganize.

Synced Blocks — Write Once, Display Everywhere

Synced blocks are one of Notion's most powerful and underused features. A synced block is a content block that exists in multiple pages simultaneously — but it is not duplicated. It is the same block, displayed in multiple locations. When you edit it in one location, every other location that displays the block updates automatically.

How to Create a Synced Block

  1. Type /sync on any page and select "Synced Block"
  2. Add your content inside the synced block
  3. Click "Copy and Sync" on the synced block header
  4. Paste it on any other page — those pages now display the same block
  5. Edit the block anywhere and it updates everywhere

Best Use Cases for Synced Blocks

  • Navigation menus: A synced block of quick links that appears at the top of every major page in your workspace. Update the navigation once and it changes everywhere.
  • Team announcements: A shared notice (new process, policy update, upcoming deadline) that appears on every team member's home page without requiring you to update each one separately.
  • Resource libraries: A curated list of links, templates, or tools that should be accessible from multiple contexts (your project pages AND your client pages AND your dashboard).
  • Standard disclaimers or terms: Legal language, billing terms, or contact information that appears consistently across all client-facing pages.

Synced Block Limitations to Know

  • Synced blocks sync content and formatting, but NOT linked database views embedded inside them
  • You cannot sync a block across different Notion workspaces (only within the same workspace)
  • Synced blocks are visible to all workspace members — avoid putting private information in synced blocks
  • If you delete the "original" synced block, it breaks the sync for all copies

Sharing and Publishing Notion Pages

Notion offers multiple levels of sharing, from private to fully public web pages. Understanding each option helps you use Notion for client collaboration, public knowledge bases, and simple websites.

🔒

Private (Default)

Only you can see the page. Default for all new pages. Accessed only from within your Notion account.

👥

Shared with Specific People

Invite by email from the Share menu. Notion guests can view or edit depending on the permission level you set. Ideal for sharing specific pages with clients (they do not need a paid plan to view).

🏢

Shared with Workspace

All members of your Notion workspace can access the page. Best for internal team resources, wikis, and shared databases where all team members should have access.

🌐

Published to Web (Public URL)

Anyone with the URL can view the page, no Notion account required. Toggle on from Share → Publish to web. Control options: Allow comments, Allow duplication, Allow search engine indexing. Use this for public portfolios, knowledge bases, and simple websites.

Using Published Notion Pages as Simple Websites

A published Notion page is a publicly accessible webpage at a notion.site URL. It is functional as a simple website for personal portfolios, landing pages, and knowledge bases. Limitations: you cannot add custom CSS, run JavaScript, or use a custom domain natively.

For a custom domain on a Notion page, third-party tools solve this elegantly:

  • Super.so — Maps custom domains to Notion pages, adds custom CSS/JS, supports SEO metadata
  • Popsy.co — Converts Notion pages into beautiful websites with custom domains
  • Notion.site — Notion's own publish URL (notion.site/your-page-name) available on Plus plan

Page Icons and Cover Images

Icons and covers are the most visible aesthetic elements of any Notion page. Used consistently, they create a professional, cohesive workspace. Used inconsistently, they create visual noise.

Icon Options

  • Emoji — Click the icon area on any page → Emoji tab. Free, universal rendering on all devices and in all color modes. The safest choice for cross-platform consistency.
  • Notion Icon Library — Click the icon area → Icons tab. Hundreds of clean SVG icons organized by category. Fast and free, directly built into Notion.
  • Custom upload — Click the icon area → Custom tab → Upload image. Best size: 280×280px PNG with transparent background. Use for branded icons, product logos, or custom-designed icon packs.
  • External URL — Link to any image URL hosted online. Useful for pulling icons dynamically, but dependent on the external hosting staying live.

Cover Image Tips

  • Ideal cover size: 1500×600px. Notion displays the top portion — design with this crop in mind.
  • Click "Reposition" after adding a cover to adjust the vertical position of your image.
  • Built-in gallery covers range from abstract art to architectural photography to gradient patterns. The gradient options are the most versatile across aesthetic styles.
  • Notion's Unsplash integration lets you search millions of photos without leaving Notion — click the cover area → Unsplash.

Notion AI in Pages

Notion AI (available as a $10/month add-on or bundled with certain plans) adds language model capabilities directly inside every page. It is not a chatbot interface — it is integrated into the block editing experience.

What Notion AI Can Do on a Page

  • Draft new content: Type /AI and describe what you need — a proposal introduction, a meeting agenda, a project summary. AI writes a first draft in the same page.
  • Summarize existing content: Select any text → Ask AI → Summarize. Essential for condensing long meeting notes into key points.
  • Extract action items: Paste meeting transcript or notes → Ask AI to find action items. AI returns a clean bulleted list of tasks.
  • Improve writing: Select any text → Ask AI → Improve writing / Make shorter / Make longer / Fix spelling and grammar. Useful for polishing client-facing proposals.
  • Translate: Select text → Ask AI → Translate to [language]. Supports 20+ languages.
  • Q&A over your pages: Use the Notion AI sidebar to ask questions about your own workspace content. "What is the status of Project Phoenix?" returns an answer based on your actual Notion data.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a Notion page?
A Notion page is the fundamental unit of all content in Notion. Every note, document, database entry, dashboard, wiki article, and organizational structure in Notion is a page. Pages have four main elements: an icon (emoji or custom image), an optional cover image, a title, and a body that contains an unlimited number of content blocks. Pages can be nested inside other pages infinitely, creating a hierarchical workspace structure that can be as flat or as deep as you need.
What is the difference between a Notion page and a database?
A page is a freeform document where you add blocks in any order without structure imposed on the content — similar to a Google Doc or Word document but with more block types. A database is a structured collection of pages where each page (entry) has defined properties: dates, statuses, tags, numbers, relations, and more. The database view lets you filter, sort, group, and display entries in different formats (table, board, calendar, gallery, timeline, list). Every entry inside a database is itself a page — so you get both the structured properties of a database row AND the freeform content of a page in each entry.
How do I make a Notion page public?
Click Share in the top right corner of any Notion page, then scroll to the "Publish" section and toggle "Publish to web" to ON. Notion generates a public URL at notion.site/your-workspace/page-name. You can also configure whether public visitors can comment on the page, duplicate it to their own Notion account, or whether search engines should index it. The page remains public until you toggle it off. Note: if the page is a sub-page of a private parent page, the parent page's sharing settings take precedence.
Can Notion pages be used as a website?
Yes, as a basic one. Published Notion pages are publicly accessible webpages that anyone can view without a Notion account. They display your content cleanly with Notion's formatting. Limitations: no custom domain (natively), no custom CSS or JavaScript, no forms processing, and limited SEO control. For a proper website with a custom domain built on Notion, use Super.so ($16/month), Popsy.co, or Feather.so — these services take your Notion pages and publish them with custom domains, improved SEO, and some CSS customization.
What are synced blocks in Notion?
Synced blocks are content blocks that exist in multiple locations within your Notion workspace but share the same underlying data. When you edit a synced block in one location, it automatically updates everywhere else the block appears. Create them by typing /synced on any page, adding your content, then using the "Copy and Sync" button to paste the block on other pages. They are orange-outlined in edit mode so you can identify them. Best used for: navigation menus that appear across multiple pages, team announcements, shared resource lists, and any content that needs to stay identical across multiple locations.
How many Notion pages can I have for free?
Unlimited — on all Notion plans including the free plan. Notion removed its block limit in 2022. You can create as many pages, sub-pages, and database entries as you want with no ceiling. The main limitations of the free plan are: up to 10 guests (people you invite to specific pages), 7-day page version history (cannot recover edits older than 7 days), and a 5MB file upload limit per file. The Plus plan ($10/month) removes the guest limit and extends version history to 30 days.
How do I delete and recover a Notion page?
To delete: right-click the page in your sidebar and select Delete, or open the page and click the ... menu in the top right → Delete. Deleted pages go to your Trash, accessible from a link at the very bottom of your sidebar. Pages stay in Trash for 7 days on the free plan and 30 days on the Plus plan. To restore: open Trash, find the deleted page, and click Restore. To permanently delete: click Delete permanently from Trash (this is irreversible). Note: deleting a parent page moves all its sub-pages to Trash as well.
