Build a Freelance Financial Dashboard in Notion

Published May 2026  ·  Last updated: May 2026  ·  7 min read

Most freelancers have a vague sense of how their finances are doing. They know roughly what they've invoiced, have some idea of what's been paid, and try not to think too hard about expenses. This works fine until tax time, until a slow month catches them off guard, or until they realize they've been undercharging a client for two years because they never bothered to look at the total. A proper Notion financial dashboard template turns "vague sense" into actual visibility.

Why Notion Works for Financial Tracking

Notion isn't an accounting tool. It can't automatically import transactions, reconcile bank accounts, or file taxes. If you need those features, use a dedicated tool like Wave (free) or QuickBooks alongside your Notion workspace.

What Notion does extremely well is connect financial data to the context that makes it meaningful. When an invoice record links to the client and the project, you can answer questions like "how much has Client X paid us over two years?" and "which project type generates the most revenue?" in seconds. Standalone accounting software rarely surfaces these answers without running custom reports.

The other advantage is friction. Because your client tracker, project management, and invoicing are all in the same Notion workspace, creating a new invoice record is a natural part of the project workflow — not a separate task you have to remember to do in a different tool.

The Three Databases Your Financial Dashboard Needs

1. Invoices Database

This is the core of your financial tracking. Every invoice you issue becomes a record here. Essential fields:

  • Invoice Number — a formatted text field (e.g., INV-2026-047)
  • Client — Relation to your Clients database
  • Project — Relation to your Projects database
  • Amount — Number field, formatted as currency
  • Status — Select field: Draft, Sent, Partial, Paid, Overdue
  • Issue Date — Date field
  • Due Date — Date field
  • Paid Date — Date field (fill in when payment arrives)
  • Month — Formula field: formatDate(prop("Issue Date"), "YYYY-MM") — this is critical for monthly grouping

2. Expenses Database

Track every business expense that might be tax-deductible: software subscriptions, equipment, co-working fees, professional development, contractor payments. Fields:

  • Description — what was it
  • Category — Software, Equipment, Professional Services, Marketing, Other
  • Amount — Number, formatted as currency
  • Date — Date field
  • Deductible — Checkbox (yes/no)
  • Month — Same formula as Invoices

3. Monthly Summary (Dashboard)

A parent database or a dedicated dashboard page that pulls aggregated figures from Invoices and Expenses using linked database views and rollups. This is the "one page to check" that gives you your financial snapshot without scrolling through hundreds of individual records.

Building the Dashboard Views

Outstanding Invoices View

A Table view of the Invoices database filtered to Status = Sent OR Status = Partial OR Status = Overdue. Sort by Due Date ascending — the most overdue invoices appear first. Include Amount and Due Date columns. This view answers "who owes me money right now?" in under five seconds.

Use Notion's filter groups to create an "Overdue" highlight: add a second filter layer for Due Date is before Today, Status is not Paid, and give those records a distinct color using Notion's row color feature. Red rows = overdue. You won't be able to miss them.

Monthly Income View

A Table view of the Invoices database grouped by the Month formula field. Set the Amount column to show the sum for each group. This gives you monthly income totals at a glance, without any formulas to maintain — the grouping is purely mechanical.

Add a secondary filter: Status = Paid only. This shows collected revenue (cash-basis accounting), not just invoiced revenue. Switch between the two to understand the gap between what you've invoiced and what you've actually been paid.

Expense Breakdown View

A Table view of the Expenses database grouped by Category, with Amount summed per group. This tells you where your money is going. Most freelancers are surprised by their software spend when they look at it categorized — $15 here, $29 there, it adds up fast.

Net Income Calculation

Notion can't automatically subtract expenses from revenue (it can't create formulas that reference two different databases). The workaround is a simple dashboard page where you embed both the monthly income summary and the monthly expense summary as linked views, side by side. You read the totals and do the subtraction mentally — or paste them into a simple calculator formula.

Month-End Financial Review: A 15-Minute Routine

The dashboard is only valuable if you actually use it. A monthly review at the end of each month takes 15 minutes and gives you a clear picture of your financial health.

  1. Check outstanding invoices. Any invoices more than 30 days past due need a follow-up email today — not later.
  2. Verify the monthly income total. Compare collected revenue to your target. Are you on track? Ahead? Behind?
  3. Review expenses. Any subscriptions you didn't actually use this month? Any new expenses that need to be categorized?
  4. Update year-to-date running total. Keep a simple running total in a text field or callout block — "YTD as of [Month]: $X." It's a five-second update that tells you where you stand against your annual goals.
  5. Check client revenue distribution. Open the Revenue by Client view. Are you too dependent on one client? Is that a risk you're comfortable with?

Notion Financial Tracking vs. Dedicated Accounting Tools

The question comes up regularly: should I use Notion for finances, or just use QuickBooks/Wave/FreshBooks?

The honest answer is: use both, but for different things. Dedicated accounting tools handle bank reconciliation, tax categories, and formal financial statements. Notion handles operational visibility — seeing how your business is doing right now, which clients are generating income, and what's outstanding. These are different jobs, and the best setup uses both tools in their respective strengths.

Many freelancers use Notion as their primary day-to-day financial dashboard and export invoices to PDF for their accountant at tax time. The operational workflow happens in Notion; the official recordkeeping happens in the accounting tool. This split works well and avoids the complexity of trying to make Notion do something it wasn't built for.

Tax preparation tip: At year-end, create a Notion Invoices view filtered to the current year with Status = Paid. Export to CSV. This gives you a clean record of all collected revenue — exactly what your accountant needs for income reporting.
Can Notion automatically track if an invoice is overdue?
Partially. You can create a formula field that checks if Due Date is in the past and Status is not Paid, and returns "Overdue" as text. However, Notion won't automatically change the Status field or send you a notification — you need to check the view manually. Some users combine Notion with Zapier or Make to trigger email reminders when invoices become overdue.
How do I handle partial payments in Notion?
Create a "Paid Amount" number field alongside the "Invoice Amount" field. A formula field calculates the balance (Amount - Paid Amount). When a partial payment arrives, update Paid Amount and set Status to "Partial." When fully paid, set Status to "Paid" and fill in Paid Date. FreelanceOS Pro includes this structure out of the box.
Is the free template enough for tax tracking?
The free template gives you a basic invoice database that's sufficient for tracking income for tax purposes. What it doesn't include is expense tracking with deductibility flags, which is important for reducing taxable income. If expense tracking matters to you, the Pro version is worth the $29.
