What Is Self-Employment Tax?

Self-employment tax (SE tax) is the freelancer's version of FICA — the Social Security and Medicare taxes that employees split with their employers. When you work for a company, your employer pays half (6.2% SS + 1.45% Medicare) and deducts the other half from your paycheck. As a self-employed person, you are simultaneously the employee and the employer, so you pay both halves yourself — for a combined rate of 15.3%.

SE tax applies the moment your net self-employment income exceeds $400 in a calendar year. This low threshold catches many part-time freelancers and side-hustle workers by surprise. If you earned $600 doing gig work this year and did not report it, you are technically in violation of IRS requirements.

The Deduction Benefit: You can deduct exactly half of your SE tax as an above-the-line deduction on Form 1040, directly reducing your adjusted gross income (AGI) before income tax is calculated. If you owe $8,000 in SE tax, you get a $4,000 deduction — no itemizing required. This partially compensates for paying both halves.

How Schedule SE Works — The Full Calculation

Schedule SE is the IRS form you use to calculate your self-employment tax. It flows directly from the net profit on your Schedule C (business profit and loss statement). Here is the step-by-step math:

SE Tax Calculation — Worked Example

  1. Net profit from Schedule C: $60,000
  2. Multiply by 92.35% (IRS adjustment for the "employer" portion): $60,000 × 0.9235 = $55,410
  3. Multiply by 15.3% (combined SE tax rate): $55,410 × 0.153 = $8,478 SE tax owed
  4. Half SE tax deduction: $8,478 ÷ 2 = $4,239 reduction in taxable income
  5. Net tax on taxable income is then calculated on the reduced amount after this deduction and the standard deduction.

The 92.35% multiplier exists because the IRS lets self-employed people treat the theoretical "employer" half of SE tax as a business expense before calculating the tax — mirroring how employees receive their wages net of the employer's portion. It is a built-in partial offset that reduces your SE tax slightly.

For income above $168,600 (the 2026 Social Security wage base), only the 2.9% Medicare portion applies to income in excess of that threshold. High earners above $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly) also owe an additional 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax on income above those thresholds — totaling 3.8% Medicare tax on the highest-income portion.

Schedule C — Your Business Profit and Loss

Before Schedule SE can be completed, you need Schedule C — the IRS form that calculates your net profit (or loss) from freelancing. The formula is simple: Total Business Income − Total Business Expenses = Net Profit (or Loss). Your net profit feeds directly into Schedule SE.

Common Freelance Income Sources

  • Client payments for services (design, writing, development, consulting, photography)
  • Revenue from digital products (courses, templates, ebooks, software licenses)
  • Platform income (Upwork, Fiverr, Toptal, 99designs, Dribbble jobs)
  • Revenue from content monetization (YouTube AdSense, Substack, Patreon)
  • Speaking fees, workshop income, and affiliate commissions
  • Any 1099-NEC income and cash payments even without a 1099
1099-NEC Threshold: Clients must issue you a 1099-NEC if they paid you $600 or more during the year. But you must report ALL income on Schedule C, even if no 1099 was issued. Cash payments, PayPal transfers, Venmo business payments — all of it is income.

The Business vs. Personal Expense Rule

The IRS allows deductions for expenses that are ordinary (common in your industry) and necessary (helpful for your business). Mixed-use expenses — like a phone or home internet used for both work and personal activities — must be prorated by business-use percentage. Keep documentation: a phone bill with "60% business use" written on it is not documentation; a call log showing business calls is.

Quarterly Estimated Taxes — The Freelancer's Most Critical Habit

The US tax system is pay-as-you-go. Employees have taxes withheld from every paycheck automatically. Freelancers must replicate this through quarterly estimated tax payments. If you expect to owe $1,000 or more in total tax when you file (after credits and withholding), the IRS requires quarterly payments — or you face an underpayment penalty.

How to Calculate Your Quarterly Payment (Two Safe Harbor Methods)

  • Method 1 — 90% of current year tax: Estimate what you will owe this year and pay at least 90% of it across four installments. Requires good income forecasting.
  • Method 2 — 100% of prior year tax (safe harbor): Pay the exact same total federal tax you owed last year, divided equally into four payments. If your prior-year AGI exceeded $150,000, the threshold rises to 110% of last year's tax.

Most freelancers with variable income use the prior-year safe harbor — it eliminates guessing and protects you from penalties even if this year turns out to be significantly more profitable.

Quick Example: Safe Harbor Calculation

If you owed $12,000 total federal tax last year (and your AGI was under $150,000):

  • Quarterly payment target: $12,000 ÷ 4 = $3,000 per quarter
  • Set a calendar reminder for each due date and pay via IRS Direct Pay
  • If this year earns more, you may owe more at filing — but no underpayment penalty applies

How to Pay Estimated Taxes

  • IRS Direct Pay at irs.gov/directpay — free, instant bank transfer, no registration required
  • EFTPS (Electronic Federal Tax Payment System) — best for recurring scheduled payments, requires advance enrollment
  • IRS2Go app — pay directly from your phone
  • Debit/credit card via IRS-authorized processors (small convenience fee applies)
  • Mail Form 1040-ES with a check — mail early to ensure timely delivery

The underpayment penalty in 2026 is approximately 7–8% annualized (federal short-term rate plus 3%). On a $3,000 underpayment for one quarter, the penalty is roughly $50–$60. Not catastrophic, but cumulative underpayments across all four quarters add up and trigger IRS notices.

20 Tax Deductions Every Freelancer Should Be Taking

Legal deductions are the single most powerful tool for reducing your tax bill. Every dollar of legitimate business expense directly reduces your net profit on Schedule C — which reduces both your income tax and your SE tax. Many freelancers underclaim deductions out of uncertainty. Here are the 20 most valuable ones, with notes on how to document them:

🏠

Home Office

Must be used exclusively and regularly for work. Simplified method: $5/sq ft up to 300 sq ft ($1,500 max). Regular method: actual home expenses × business-use percentage. Your principal place of business.

💻

Computer & Equipment

Laptops, monitors, external drives, cameras, microphones. Deduct full cost in year purchased via Section 179, or depreciate over 5 years. Mixed personal/business use requires proration.

🌐

Internet & Phone

Deduct the business-use percentage of your monthly bills. Track call logs or estimate honestly. If you use your phone 65% for business, deduct 65% of your bill.

📱

Software Subscriptions

Notion, Adobe Creative Cloud, Zoom Pro, Slack, Figma, Asana, QuickBooks, GitHub, Canva — any software used in your freelance work. Keep annual payment confirmations.

🚗

Business Vehicle Use

2026 IRS standard mileage rate: 70 cents per mile for business driving. Track every business mile with a mileage log app. Or deduct actual vehicle expenses using business-use percentage.

✈️

Business Travel

Airfare, hotels, taxis, Uber, rental cars for primarily business trips are fully deductible. Meals during travel: 50% deductible. Personal days added to a business trip are not deductible.

📚

Education & Courses

Online courses, workshops, books, webinars, and conferences that maintain or improve your current freelance skills. Training to enter a new career field is not deductible.

💼

Professional Services

CPA fees, bookkeeper fees, attorney fees for business contracts or entity formation, and financial advisor fees related to your business. All fully deductible in the year paid.

🏥

Health Insurance Premiums

Self-employed individuals deduct 100% of health, dental, and vision premiums for themselves and family — as an above-the-line deduction, if not eligible for an employer plan.

🏦

Retirement Contributions

SEP-IRA: up to 25% of net SE income (max $69,000 in 2026). Solo 401(k): up to $23,500 employee contribution + employer match. Both reduce taxable income dollar-for-dollar.

📣

Marketing & Advertising

Google Ads, social media ads, website hosting, domain names, logo design, business cards, promotional products, and any paid promotional materials for attracting clients.

🤝

Contractor Payments

Pay subcontractors or assistants? Their fees are deductible as a business expense. Issue Form 1099-NEC to any contractor paid $600 or more in the year.

🏢

Coworking Space

Monthly memberships, day passes, and dedicated desk rentals at coworking facilities are fully deductible — no exclusive-use test required (unlike home offices).

🍽️

Business Meals

50% of meals with clients, prospects, or business partners where business is discussed. Record: date, attendees, business purpose, and restaurant. Save receipts for amounts over $75.

🔒

Business Insurance

Professional liability (E&O), general liability, and cyber liability insurance premiums for your freelance practice. Fully deductible in the year premiums are paid.

📬

Postage & Shipping

Mailing contracts, sending deliverables, shipping equipment — all business-related postage and shipping costs are deductible. Keep receipts and note the business purpose on each.

🖨️

Office Supplies

Printer paper, ink, pens, notebooks, desk organizers, and other consumable office supplies used for your business. Small individually but add up meaningfully over 12 months.

📋

Professional Memberships

Industry association dues, trade union fees, professional organization memberships, LinkedIn Premium when used for client prospecting, and similar professional subscriptions.

💳

Business Credit Card Interest

Interest charges on a credit card used exclusively for business expenses are fully deductible. Keep business and personal spending on separate cards — this distinction matters in an audit.

🏧

Business Bank Fees

Monthly maintenance fees, wire transfer fees, and transaction fees on a dedicated business bank account are fully deductible. One more reason to always maintain a separate business account.

State Self-Employment Taxes — Know Your State

Federal SE tax is only part of your total tax picture. Most states impose their own income tax on self-employment income, and the rates vary dramatically based on where you live and work.

No State Income Tax — Best States for Freelancers

These seven states have zero state income tax, meaning your SE income escapes state-level taxation entirely:

Texas, Florida, Nevada, Wyoming, Washington, South Dakota, Alaska

New Hampshire taxes dividends and interest income but not earned SE income. Tennessee eliminated its investment income tax in 2022. If you have location flexibility as a freelancer, state tax burden is worth factoring into where you live.

Retirement Accounts — Reduce Taxes While Building Wealth

Tax-advantaged retirement accounts are among the most powerful tools available to self-employed individuals. Every dollar contributed to a SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k) comes off your taxable income — simultaneously reducing your income tax for this year and building long-term wealth. Unlike deductions that require you to spend money (like buying equipment), retirement contributions grow in your own account.

SEP-IRA (Simplified Employee Pension)

  • Contribute up to 25% of net self-employment income
  • 2026 maximum contribution: $69,000
  • Contribution deadline: your tax filing deadline, including extensions (up to October 15)
  • Easy to open at Fidelity, Vanguard, Schwab, or any major brokerage
  • No annual reporting requirements for balances under $250,000
  • One of the simplest retirement accounts available

Solo 401(k)

  • Employee contribution: Up to $23,500 in 2026 ($31,000 if age 50+) — deductible dollar-for-dollar
  • Employer contribution: Up to 25% of net SE income
  • Combined maximum: $69,000 ($76,500 if age 50+)
  • Roth option available for the employee contribution portion
  • Must open the plan before December 31 to make employee contributions for the current year
  • Slightly more complex but allows larger contributions at lower income levels than SEP-IRA

Retirement Contribution Tax Savings Example

Freelancer with $100,000 net SE income contributes to a Solo 401(k):

  • Employee contribution: $23,500 (reduces taxable income immediately)
  • Employer contribution: approx. $18,587 (25% of adjusted net)
  • Total retirement contribution: $42,087
  • Federal income tax savings (assuming 22% bracket): approximately $9,260
  • Plus the retirement savings compound tax-free until withdrawal

Annual Tax Filing Checklist for Freelancers

  1. Gather all income records — 1099-NEC forms from clients, PayPal annual summaries, Stripe reports, platform payment histories
  2. Pull all expense records — Receipts, bank statements, credit card statements, mileage logs, subscription confirmation emails
  3. Complete Schedule C — Report all income; deduct all categorized business expenses; calculate net profit
  4. Complete Schedule SE — Multiply net profit by 92.35%, then by 15.3%; that is your SE tax
  5. Claim half SE tax deduction — Deduct 50% of SE tax on Schedule 1, Form 1040 (above-the-line)
  6. Claim additional above-the-line deductions — Health insurance premiums, retirement contributions, student loan interest
  7. Apply standard or itemized deduction — 2026 standard deduction: $14,600 (single), $29,200 (married filing jointly)
  8. Calculate total tax — Federal income tax by bracket + SE tax
  9. Subtract quarterly payments made — Total of all four 1040-ES payments during the year
  10. Pay balance or claim refund — File and pay any remaining amount by April 15, 2027

Frequently Asked Questions

How much should I save for self-employment taxes?
Most tax professionals recommend saving 25–30% of your net self-employment income to cover SE tax (15.3%), federal income tax (10–24% depending on bracket), and state income tax. If you live in a high-tax state like California or New York, or if you are in the 24%+ federal bracket, save 35%. The simplest system: open a dedicated savings account and automatically transfer 28% of every client payment the moment it arrives. Adjust the percentage after your first full year when you know your actual effective rate.
Do I need an LLC to deduct business expenses?
No — this is one of the most common and costly misconceptions among new freelancers. Sole proprietors can deduct all legitimate business expenses on Schedule C without forming any business entity. An LLC is a legal structure that provides personal liability protection; it is not a tax structure. A single-member LLC is treated as a "disregarded entity" by the IRS and taxed identically to a sole proprietorship by default — you still file Schedule C. You do not need an LLC to deduct your laptop, home office, software subscriptions, or any other business expenses.
What happens if I miss a quarterly estimated tax payment?
You owe an underpayment penalty, which is calculated at the IRS underpayment rate for the quarter — typically the federal short-term rate plus 3 percentage points, approximately 7–8% annualized in 2026. On a $3,000 missed payment for one quarter, the penalty is roughly $50–60. This is not a criminal offense and will not trigger an audit by itself. The IRS calculates the penalty automatically on Form 2210 when you file. The real risk is cumulative underpayments across all four quarters, which can add up to $200–400 or more and generate IRS notices.
Can I deduct my home office if I also work from coffee shops?
Yes. The home office deduction requires that the dedicated space in your home be used exclusively and regularly for business — but it does not require that you work exclusively from home. You can work from coffee shops, coworking spaces, or client offices without losing the home office deduction, as long as your home office is your principal place of business (where you do administrative work, billing, planning, client calls, etc.). The coffee shop is incidental; your home office is headquarters.
What records do I need to keep for self-employment taxes?
The IRS standard is to retain records for at least 3 years from the date you filed your return. Keep records for 6 years if you underreported income by more than 25%, and indefinitely for returns involving fraud. Practically: keep all receipts for business expenses over $75 (under $75 is helpful but not legally required), all invoices issued and received, bank and credit card statements for business accounts, mileage logs for vehicle use, 1099 forms received, and proof of quarterly estimated tax payments. Digital records stored in cloud services are fully accepted by the IRS.
Do I owe self-employment tax on passive income?
No. Self-employment tax applies only to active business income from a trade or business you materially participate in. Passive income sources — including rental income from real estate you do not actively manage, dividends and interest from investments, capital gains from selling assets, and partnership income where you are a passive investor — are not subject to SE tax. They are subject to regular income tax (and potentially the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax at higher income levels), but the 15.3% SE tax does not apply.
How does S-corporation election affect self-employment tax?
When you elect S-corp status, the business pays you a "reasonable compensation" salary, which is subject to payroll taxes (FICA — same 15.3% but split between you as employee and the corporation as employer). Additional business profits above your salary are distributed to you as shareholder distributions — these are not subject to SE tax or payroll taxes, saving you the 15.3% SE tax rate on that portion. The strategy works best when net profits exceed roughly $60,000–$80,000 annually, because you need the SE tax savings to outweigh the added costs: payroll processing, quarterly payroll tax filings, potentially a bookkeeper and CPA. Always consult a qualified CPA before making the S-corp election, as the IRS scrutinizes reasonable compensation and the rules are complex.
