The 6 Core Life Areas to Cover

A life planner without life area structure is just a task list. Life areas give your goals and habits context — you're not just tracking habits, you're deliberately nurturing specific areas of your life. Here are the six core areas most people need covered:

🏃
Health & Body
Exercise, sleep, nutrition, medical appointments, mental health, energy management. Your body is the platform everything else runs on — it deserves deliberate tracking.
💼
Career & Work
Business goals, skill development, income growth, professional relationships, creative projects. For freelancers, this includes both client work and business development.
💰
Finances & Money
Income goals, savings rate, debt, investments, financial habits. Separate from career — you might be succeeding professionally but not financially if the money doesn't stick.
❤
Relationships & People
Partner, family, friendships, community, mentors. Relationships are the area most neglected by high-achieving people — a life planner that doesn't explicitly include them will unconsciously deprioritize them.
📚
Learning & Growth
Books, courses, skills, experiences, creative exploration. Continuous learning is especially critical for freelancers and knowledge workers who need to stay current and expand their capabilities.
🌈
Recreation & Life Quality
Hobbies, travel, play, rest, experiences you want to have. This area is often omitted from productivity-focused planners — a mistake. Sustainable high performance requires genuine recovery and enjoyment.

In Notion, create a dedicated page for each life area. Each page becomes a mini-hub: goals in this area, habits to support them, key projects, relevant notes and reading. Link these pages from your main Life Planning hub so everything is one click away.

The Life Wheel Framework in Notion

The Life Wheel is a coaching tool that asks you to rate your current satisfaction in each life area on a 1–10 scale. The visual metaphor is a wheel: if your scores were literally the radius of a circle, how round — and how well would it roll? A wheel with a flat side (low score in one area) has a rough ride regardless of how good the other areas are.

Building Your Life Wheel in Notion

Create a database called "Life Areas" with these properties:

  • Area Name (Title): Health, Career, Finances, Relationships, Learning, Recreation
  • Current Score (Number, 1–10): Your honest satisfaction right now
  • Desired Score (Number, 1–10): Where you want to be in 12 months
  • Gap (Formula): prop("Desired Score") - prop("Current Score")
  • Focus Area (Checkbox): Mark the 1–2 areas you're prioritizing this quarter
  • Last Updated (Date): When you last reviewed this area's score

Update scores monthly as part of your monthly review ritual. The trend over time is more valuable than any single score — watch which areas drift down when you're busy and which ones recover when you rest.

Life Wheel Insight

Focus on your lowest-scoring area that you have actual control over — not necessarily your lowest overall. You can't score a 10 in health if you're sleeping 5 hours due to an infant, but you can improve your nutrition score. Work at the margin of what's possible.

Monthly, Weekly, and Daily Planning Layers

A life planner operates at three time horizons simultaneously. Each layer has a different job — they shouldn't duplicate each other.

Monthly Layer — Strategic
Monthly Review & Preview
At the end of each month: review Life Wheel scores, review progress on active goals, identify the top 3–5 priorities for the coming month. The monthly layer connects your annual goals to your weekly plans. Without it, the annual goals sit unreviewed while weekly planning optimizes for short-term urgency. Dedicate 45–60 minutes to your monthly review — it's the highest-leverage planning time you have.
Weekly Layer — Operational
Weekly Planning & Review
Every Sunday (or Friday afternoon): review the week that's ending, plan the week ahead. Identify 3–5 "wins" you want to achieve across all life areas — not just work. Schedule time blocks for high-priority tasks. Review habits from the past week. The weekly plan is where your monthly priorities get translated into specific calendar blocks. Time: 30–45 minutes.
Daily Layer — Tactical
Daily Dashboard & Shutdown
Every morning: review your daily task list, your schedule, and your Top 3 priorities for the day. Every evening: a 5-minute shutdown ritual — mark tasks complete, capture anything unfinished, write one sentence about the day. The daily layer is where life happens — but it should be driven by your weekly plan, not just reactive to whatever lands in your inbox that morning.

Building Your Habit Tracking System

Habits are the operational layer of life planning — the behaviors that, repeated consistently, produce the outcomes you want in each life area. A habit tracker without a life planner is just gamified behavior. A life planner without habits is just pretty goals.

The Habit Database Structure

Create a Habits database with these properties:

  • Habit Name (Title): Short, specific, behavior-based. "Read 30 min" not "Be a reader."
  • Life Area (Relation → Life Areas database): Links habit to its life area for Life Wheel scores.
  • Frequency (Select): Daily / Weekdays / 3x/week / Weekly
  • Cue (Text): The trigger that prompts this habit. "After morning coffee" or "Before lunch."
  • Current Streak (Formula): Calculated from your daily log entries.
  • Active (Checkbox): Allows you to pause habits without deleting them.

Daily Habit Logging

Create a separate Daily Log database. Each entry is one day. Each active habit is a Checkbox property. Each morning, create a new Daily Log entry (or use a template) and check off habits as you complete them. This is faster than individual habit entry and gives you a per-day record you can filter and analyze.

Habit Science Insight

Start with no more than 3 new habits at once. Habit formation requires cognitive overhead — adding too many simultaneously overwhelms the behavior change process. Once a habit becomes automatic (typically 60–90 days), add the next one.

Life Planning Through Goal Reviews

Goals live in your Goals database, tagged by life area. But goals that aren't reviewed consistently become decoration. Build reviews into your planning rhythm:

Weekly: Are this week's tasks connected to any active goal? If not, why not? This is the connection check that prevents "urgent but not important" from consuming all your time.

Monthly: For each active goal, update the progress, note what moved, and assess whether the goal still matters. Some goals get invalidated by new information — better to acknowledge this than chase outdated targets.

Quarterly: Review all life area scores. Are your quarterly priorities (the Focus Area checkboxes in your Life Areas database) still correct? Adjust them for the next quarter based on what you learned.

Annually: Full annual review (see our Notion Annual Review Template guide) — the comprehensive zoom-out that shapes the next year's life areas, goals, and priorities.

Connecting Life Planning to Business Systems

For freelancers and solopreneurs, the most powerful feature of a Notion life planner is the ability to connect personal life goals directly to business operations. This isn't possible in a paper planner or a standalone productivity app — it's uniquely enabled by Notion's relational database structure.

The Hierarchy That Makes It Work

Life Areas contain Goals. Goals can be linked to Projects. Projects contain Tasks. This hierarchy means that every task you complete traces upward to the life area it serves. When you're deciding whether to take on a new client project, you can ask: which of my active goals does this serve? If the answer is "none," that's important information.

Practical Connections in FreelanceOS

  • Income goals in the Finances life area link to revenue goals in your business dashboard, which link to the clients and projects generating that revenue.
  • Health goals have protected time blocks in your weekly plan that your client deadlines have to work around — not the other way.
  • Learning goals connect to a reading list and course tracker that feeds insights back into your business strategy.
  • Relationship goals appear in your weekly planning as recurring items alongside client tasks — ensuring personal commitments aren't perpetually deferred by work.

The result is a system where your business serves your life, rather than your life serving your business. That's not a philosophical claim — it's a structural one, built into how the databases relate to each other.

How to Start Without Overwhelm

The biggest risk with a life planner is overbuilding on day one. Here's a phased approach that gets you started immediately without the two-weekend setup trap:

Week 1: Just the Life Areas. Create six pages in Notion — one per life area. For each, write two sentences: "Current state: [honest assessment]. Desired state: [what I want in 12 months]." Nothing else. That's your life plan for now. It will be better than not having one.

Week 2: One habit per life area. Pick one small, specific habit for each life area. Add it to a simple Habits list with the frequency and your "cue." Start tracking with a daily checkbox page.

Week 3: Add weekly review. Spend 30 minutes on Sunday reviewing your habits from the week and writing your 3–5 wins for the coming week. Add them to your calendar. This is now a weekly planning session.

Month 2+: Build out goal databases and monthly reviews. Once the weekly habit is established, add the goal database structure, life wheel scores, and monthly review template. Build incrementally — each addition should be motivated by a felt need, not the desire for a complete system on day one.

Frequently Asked Questions

What should a Notion life planner include?
A complete Notion life planner should include: a Life Areas hub (pages for Health, Career, Finances, Relationships, Learning, and Personal Growth), a Goals database with life-area tagging, a Habits tracker, a monthly review template, a weekly planning template, and a daily dashboard. The key is building a hierarchy: life areas define your values and priorities, goals give direction within each area, and the weekly/daily layers provide the operational view where you actually do the work.
How does the Life Wheel work in Notion?
The Life Wheel is a classic coaching tool that evaluates satisfaction across 8–10 life areas on a scale of 1–10. In Notion, you replicate this by creating a database with each life area as an entry, adding a 'Satisfaction Score' number property (1–10), and updating it monthly. The scores over time reveal which areas are being neglected and which are thriving. Many people add a 'Desired Score' property alongside the current score to see the gap they're working to close.
How do I connect life planning to business planning in Notion?
Connect life and business planning by using relation properties that link your Life Goals database to your Business Goals database. A financial goal ('earn $150k this year') should link to a career life area goal ('grow my freelance income by 30%'). This creates a hierarchy where business projects trace back to life priorities, not the other way around. FreelanceOS is designed around this principle — your business system is embedded inside a broader life system, not separate from it.
What life areas should I track in a life planner?
The classic 8 life areas for a life planner are: Health & Fitness, Career & Work, Finances & Money, Relationships & Family, Personal Growth & Learning, Recreation & Fun, Environment & Home, and Spirituality or Meaning. You don't need all 8 — choose the ones that matter most to you right now. Most effective life planners cover 5–7 areas. The goal is to ensure no important area of life is being chronically neglected while you focus on one or two.
How do I build a habit tracker in my Notion life planner?
Build a habit tracker in Notion by creating a database with: Habit Name (title), Area of Life (select), Frequency (Daily/Weekly/3x week), Streak (formula counting consecutive days), and date properties for logging. Use a Checkbox for each day or a linked entry per day. The most durable setup uses a separate Daily Log database where each entry is one day, and each habit is a checkbox property. Filter to the current week to see your weekly habit completion at a glance.
How is a Notion life planner different from a productivity system?
A productivity system focuses on tasks, projects, and output — getting work done efficiently. A life planner encompasses all areas of life, including those that have nothing to do with productivity: relationships, health, meaning, joy. A life planner asks 'am I living well?' while a productivity system asks 'am I working efficiently?' The best setups include both — a productivity system nested inside a life planner so that work efficiency serves life quality, not the reverse.
Can a Notion life planner replace a paper planner?
For most people, yes — Notion can replace a paper planner and offer significant advantages: searchability, linked databases, automatic date filtering, and access from any device. The main advantage of paper is friction-free capture (no loading screen, no dead battery). Many people use Notion for their life planner backbone and a small notebook for daily quick capture, then transfer captures into Notion at end of day. If you love paper, consider a hybrid: paper for morning journaling, Notion for tracking and planning.
