Notion Life Planner: Build Your Complete Life OS in 2025

Your complete blueprint for building a Notion system that tracks every area of your life — goals, health, relationships, finances, and personal growth.

📅 Updated July 2025 ⏱ 18 min read 🗂 12 steps
Notion Life Planner Life OS Goal Setting Habit Tracking Personal Finance

Most productivity systems are task managers dressed up in a nice interface. They help you capture what to do and when to do it — but they don't address the bigger picture of how you're actually living. A Notion Life Planner is different. Where a productivity app focuses on tasks, a Life OS focuses on life areas: goals, relationships, health, learning, and finances all live in one connected workspace, linked together so that progress in one area reinforces progress in others.

The Life OS concept is simple but powerful. Instead of scattering your attention across five separate apps — a fitness tracker, a budget app, a journaling app, a to-do list, and a planner — everything lives in one linked Notion workspace. Your health goal connects to your habit tracker, which connects to your weekly review, which connects to your annual vision. Nothing exists in isolation. When you mark off a habit, it rolls up to your health goal. When you review your week, you're looking at the same system you'll review at month-end and year-end.

Notion is uniquely suited for building a Life OS because of its database relation system. Data flows between sections rather than sitting in silos. Your annual goals feed into quarterly OKRs, which show up in your weekly plan, which shapes your daily dashboard. Filtered views let you see exactly what you need — only today's habits, only this quarter's goals, only the people you haven't contacted in 30 days. You configure the system once and then it works for you instead of the other way around.

In this guide, you'll build 10 interconnected databases and pages covering the 5 pillars of a Notion Life OS. By the end, you'll have a system that handles every major area of your life — from annual vision setting to daily habit logging — all in one place, all connected. Whether you're starting completely from scratch or rebuilding a messy existing setup, follow these steps in order and you'll have a functioning Life OS by the end of the weekend.

The 5 Pillars of a Notion Life OS

A well-structured Life OS covers every major domain of human life without overwhelming you. These five pillars form the backbone of your system. Each one is a distinct area, but all five connect back to your central dashboard and share data with each other through Notion's relation and rollup properties.

🎯
1. Goals & Vision

Annual goals, quarterly OKRs, and a 5-year vision statement that gives direction to everything else in your system. This pillar answers: where am I going and why?

💪
2. Health & Habits

Daily habit tracker, sleep and exercise logs, nutrition check-ins, and energy tracking. This pillar answers: am I maintaining the physical foundation that makes everything else possible?

👥
3. Relationships

A People database with birthday reminders, last-contact dates, and notes on each relationship. This pillar answers: am I investing in the people who matter most to me?

💰
4. Finances

Income and expense tracker, savings goals, and a net worth log updated monthly. This pillar answers: am I building financial security and making progress toward my money goals?

📚
5. Career & Learning

Skills to learn, courses in progress, books read, and career milestones. This pillar answers: am I growing professionally and developing the skills my future self will need?

Each pillar gets its own section in your Notion workspace — a dedicated database or set of pages — but they're all linked to your central homepage and to each other. The magic of the Life OS isn't any single database; it's the connections between them.

How to Build Your Notion Life Planner

Follow these steps in order. Each step builds on the previous one. Steps 1–5 create the strategic layer (vision, goals, planning). Steps 6–8 create the operational layer (habits, weekly and daily routines). Steps 9–10 build the people and money pillars. Steps 11–12 close the loop with reviews and database connections.

1
Create Your Life OS Homepage

Build a master dashboard page — this is your morning landing page. Use a full-width layout with a meaningful cover photo (a place you want to visit, a person you love, or simply a clean gradient). Give the page a single evocative emoji icon. Inside, include: a quote or focus word for the week (use a synced block so you only update it once), linked database views filtered to today's tasks and habits, quick links to each of the 5 pillars (Goals, Health, Relationships, Finance, Learning), a "Wins" section where you add 1 win per day as a simple bullet, and your current quarter's top 3 goals displayed prominently at the top. The homepage should feel like a command center — clean, current, and motivating. Everything you need for the day should be one click away.

2
Set Up Your Annual Overview Page

Create a page called "[Year] — Annual Overview" (e.g., "2025 — Annual Overview"). This is your strategic north star for the year. Add: a goals table with rows for each life area (Health, Career, Finance, Relationships, Personal Growth) and columns for your top 1–2 annual goals per area; your word of the year — one word that captures your intention for the year (examples: Momentum, Clarity, Depth, Health); a section for 12 monthly themes — a one-word focus for each month, planned loosely in January and updated as the year evolves; and a linked database view of all goals you'll create in the next step (filtered to Timeframe = Annual). Review this page at the start of each quarter and update it. It should never be more than a few months stale.

3
Build Your Goals Database

Create a full-page database called "Goals" with these properties: Goal (title — the name of the goal), Area (select: Health / Career / Finance / Relationships / Learning / Personal), Timeframe (select: Annual / Quarterly / Monthly / Project), Status (select: Not Started / Active / Achieved / Dropped), Quarter (number: 1, 2, 3, or 4), Progress % (number with a percentage format — update manually monthly), and Outcome (text — what achieving this goal actually means for your life). Create three views: a Board view grouped by Area to see all life domains at a glance, a Board view grouped by Status to track what's active vs achieved, and a Gallery view where each goal card shows the Area and Progress % for a visual overview. You'll embed filtered views of this database throughout your system.

4
Set Up Quarterly OKR Pages

Each quarter, create a dedicated page: "Q1 2025 — [Theme Word]" (e.g., "Q1 2025 — Foundation"). At the top, copy your 3–5 annual goals. Below each, write 1–3 Objectives — ambitious, qualitative descriptions of what winning this quarter looks like. Under each Objective, list 2–3 Key Results — specific, measurable outcomes with numbers. For example: "Objective: Build a consistent exercise routine that sticks. Key Result 1: Complete 48 workouts (4 per week). Key Result 2: Sleep 7+ hours on 75 of 90 days. Key Result 3: Run one 5K." The quarterly page links to your Goals database filtered to the current quarter. Review OKRs at the 6-week mark to adjust — don't wait until the quarter ends to discover you're off track.

5
Create Monthly Theme and Focus Pages

Each month, spend 20 minutes creating a one-page Monthly Preview and Review. The review section (done on the last or first day of the month) covers: last month's 3 biggest wins, the most important lesson learned, and an honest look at what didn't get done and why. The preview section covers: this month's focus word, your 3 monthly priorities pulled from your quarterly OKRs, and your habit focus — which 1–2 habits will get the most attention this month. These monthly pages live inside your quarterly OKR page and link to your Habit Tracker database so you can see which habits were active during each month. After 12 months, you'll have a rich archive of how your year actually unfolded versus how you planned it.

6
Build Your Habit Tracker Database

Create a database called "Habits" with these properties: Habit (title), Category (select: Health / Productivity / Relationships / Learning / Mindfulness), Frequency (select: Daily / Weekdays / Weekly), Goal Link (relation to your Goals database — connect each habit to the goal it supports), and Notes (text — any context or personal rules for this habit). Create a second database: "Daily Log" with one entry per day. Each Daily Log entry has the date as its title and a checkbox for each active habit. A rollup property on the Habits database connects to the Daily Log to show your completion rate and current streak. Critical rule: limit to 5–7 active habits at any time. More than that and the system becomes a source of guilt rather than progress. Add habits gradually over months, not all at once.

7
Build Your Weekly Planning Ritual

Create a Weekly Template button in a page called "Weekly Plans." When you press the button each Sunday or Monday, it generates a new page with: This Week's Top 3 Priorities — three outcomes (not tasks) that would make the week a success; a Brain Dump area — an unstructured zone to capture everything on your mind before organizing; Calendar events — a section to list your fixed commitments for the week; a Task list — a filtered linked database view of all tasks due this week; and a Weekly Reflection section for use on Friday: what worked, what didn't, energy level 1–10, one thing to do differently next week. The ritual takes 15 minutes on Sunday and prevents the reactive, unfocused mode that kills most people's weeks. This is the highest-leverage habit in your Life OS.

8
Set Up Your Daily Dashboard

Your daily page is a minimal morning setup page — not a dumping ground for everything. Use a template button to generate it each day. Contents: Today's date as the page title (format: Mon Jul 7 2025), Today's Habit Checklist (linked view from your Daily Log for today's date), Today's Top 3 Tasks (a filtered view of your task database showing only tasks due today), Daily Gratitude — 3 things you're grateful for, written fresh each morning, and Today's Focus — one sentence describing the single most important thing you'll accomplish today. The daily page intentionally has almost nothing on it. Its job is quick setup so you actually open it every morning. A complex daily page gets avoided; a simple one gets used.

9
Create Your Relationships Database

Build a database called "People" with these properties: Name (title), Relationship Type (select: Family / Close Friend / Friend / Colleague / Mentor / Client / Acquaintance), Birthday (date), Last Contact (date — update this manually each time you meaningfully interact), Communication Style (select: Call / Text / In-Person / Email — their preferred mode), What They Care About (text — their current priorities, passions, or life events to remember), and Next Step (text — what you want to do or say next time you connect). Create two must-have filtered views: "Birthday This Month" (filter: birthday month = current month) and "Overdue Check-In" (filter: Last Contact is more than 30 days ago AND Relationship Type is Family, Close Friend, or Mentor). Review these two views every Sunday during your weekly planning ritual.

10
Build Your Personal Finance Tracker

Create three databases. Transactions: Date, Amount (number), Category (select: Housing / Food / Transport / Health / Entertainment / Business / Savings), Type (select: Income / Expense), Account (relation to Accounts), and Notes. Accounts: Account Name, Type (select: Checking / Savings / Investment / Debt), Current Balance (number), Last Updated (date). Savings Goals: Goal Name, Target Amount, Current Amount, Target Date, Progress % (formula: Current ÷ Target × 100). Build a Finance Dashboard page that embeds: a Transactions view filtered to the current month grouped by Category, a rollup of total income vs total expenses this month, your current savings rate (income - expenses ÷ income), and progress bars for each Savings Goal. Review transactions weekly (5 minutes), update account balances monthly (10 minutes).

11
Set Up Your Annual Review Template

Create a template page called "Year in Review — [Year]." Each December, use this template to capture the full picture of your year before planning the next. Include sections for: Biggest wins (5–10 things you're genuinely proud of), Biggest lessons (what you learned the hard way), Goals achieved vs missed (honest tally from your Goals database), Relationships that mattered (people who showed up for you, people you showed up for), Skills developed (what you can do in December that you couldn't do in January), Books read, places visited, How I felt this year (energy, health, happiness on a 1–10 scale), What I'm carrying forward into next year, and What I'm leaving behind. This annual review is what makes your system compound. It directly informs your next Annual Overview page (Step 2), closing the loop on the entire system.

12
Connect Everything With Relations

This is the power move that transforms a collection of databases into an actual Life OS. Add relation properties to connect: Goals ↔ Habits (so each habit shows which goals it supports, and each goal shows its supporting habits), Goals ↔ Transactions (tag financial transactions to the savings goal they fund), People ↔ Calendar Events (tag important conversations or plans to the relevant person in your People database), and Savings Goals ↔ Transactions (use a rollup to auto-calculate how much you've put toward each goal). Once these relations are in place, your dashboards update automatically, your rollups surface meaningful data, and the system starts doing cognitive work for you. Your habits are no longer floating in space — they're attached to goals. Your spending is no longer anonymous — it's tagged to a category and a purpose.

Common Mistakes When Building a Notion Life OS

Most people who build a Notion Life OS abandon it within 6 weeks. The reasons are predictable and avoidable. Here are the five most common mistakes — and how to avoid each one.

1
Building too much at once

The excitement of a new system leads people to build all 5 pillars in one weekend, populate every database, and create elaborate dashboard views — before they've used any of it. Then it collapses because it's too heavy to maintain. The fix: start with just Goals + Habits + Daily Note. Use those for 30 days. Add one pillar at a time over 3 months. The system you build slowly is the one you'll actually use.

2
Over-engineering properties

Twenty-property databases look impressive in screenshots and rot in practice. If you never fill in a property, it just creates visual noise and makes adding new entries feel like a chore. The fix: start with 3–5 properties per database — the absolute minimum you need to make it useful. Add properties only when you hit a real limitation, not in anticipation of one.

3
No review ritual

A Life OS without reviews is a garden without watering. It rots. Databases go stale. Goals get forgotten. Habits fall off. The system stops feeling relevant and you stop opening it. The fix: block 15 minutes every Sunday for the weekly review ritual (Step 7). Block 30 minutes every month-end for the monthly review. The system can't maintain itself — you have to show up.

4
Copying templates without adapting them

Templates are starting points, not final answers. A template designed for a student will have different properties than one built for a freelancer. A template for a morning person will look different than one for a night owl. The fix: use any template as a 30% starting point. Spend the first two weeks using it, noticing what you never look at and what you wish existed. Then rebuild those parts for your actual life.

5
Not linking databases

The most common reason Notion Life OS setups feel like "just fancy pages" is that the databases aren't connected. Each database is an island — useful on its own but not compounding. The fix: Step 12 in this guide is mandatory, not optional. Adding relations between Goals, Habits, People, and Finance databases is what transforms isolated records into a living system where data flows and dashboards self-update.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a Notion Life OS?

A Notion Life OS is a connected workspace that tracks all major life areas — goals, health, relationships, finances, career, and personal growth — in a single Notion workspace. Unlike isolated productivity apps, a Life OS links these areas together so your annual goals flow into weekly habits, which flow into daily actions. It replaces 5–8 separate apps with one unified system that you own and control.

How do I organise my whole life in Notion?

Start with 5 databases: Goals, Habits, People, Transactions, and a Journal (Daily Log). Build a homepage dashboard that shows filtered views from each database. Add a weekly planning template. Review daily (5 min), weekly (15 min), monthly (30 min). The system gets more useful as you add data — within 90 days it becomes genuinely indispensable because you've built enough history to see patterns.

Can Notion replace a physical planner?

Yes, for most people. Notion handles everything a physical planner does — daily notes, weekly planning, goal tracking, habit logging — plus things a physical planner can't do: full-text search across years of entries, linked databases, relation properties, and access from any device. Many people keep a physical journal for morning free-writing and use Notion for all planning and tracking. Both approaches work well.

How do I set up annual goals in Notion?

Create a Goals database with an "Area" property (Health, Career, Finance, etc.) and a "Timeframe" property (Annual, Quarterly, Monthly). Add your annual goals for each life area — aim for 1–2 per area, no more. Create a filtered view on your homepage showing only Annual goals. Each quarter, filter the database to the current quarter to see active OKRs derived from your annual goals. Review the annual goals page at the start of each quarter and adjust if circumstances change.

Is Notion good for tracking habits?

Notion is excellent for habit tracking when set up correctly. Create a Habits database linked to a Daily Log database via a relation property. Each day's log entry has checkboxes for your active habits. A rollup formula shows your completion rate and current streak. The key advantage over dedicated habit apps: your habits link directly to your goals, so you can see which habits support which annual goals — something no standalone habit app can show you.

How do I track relationships in Notion?

Build a People database with properties: Name, Relationship Type, Birthday (date), Last Contact (date), and Notes. Create two filtered views: "Birthday this month" (filter: birthday month = current month) and "Overdue check-in" (filter: Last Contact is more than 30 days ago, and Relationship Type = Friend, Family, or Mentor). Review these views every Sunday during your weekly planning ritual. The People database turns relationship maintenance from a vague intention into a concrete, actionable system.
