Notion Project Management: Build a System That Runs Your Business

One workspace for every project, task, client, and deadline. The complete Notion PM system that replaces Asana, Trello, and ClickUp for freelancers and small teams.

June 2026  ·  14 min read
Home › Blog › Notion Project Management Guide

Most freelancers start project management the same way: a combination of email threads, Google Docs, a sticky note on the monitor, and optimistic memory. This works until you have three clients simultaneously — then a deadline slips, a revision gets missed, and a client sends the dreaded "just checking in" email.

Dedicated PM tools like Asana and ClickUp exist to solve this, but they have a problem for solo operators: they're separate systems. Your project tasks are in Asana, your client notes are in Google Docs, your invoicing is in FreshBooks, and your reference materials are scattered across Drive. Nothing connects.

Notion's key advantage is integration. When your project management lives in the same workspace as your client CRM, meeting notes, invoice log, and template library, information flows between them. A project references the client record. A task links to the brief. The invoice connects to the project it belongs to. This guide builds that complete system from scratch.

Why Notion Beats Asana and Trello for Freelancers

Before building, let's be honest about the comparison. Notion isn't the best dedicated project management tool in every dimension — Asana has better reporting, ClickUp has native time tracking, Trello is simpler for pure Kanban. So why choose Notion?

Cost: Notion's free plan handles most freelancers' needs. The Plus plan is $10/month for unlimited pages, blocks, and file uploads. Asana's free plan caps at 10 users and 10 projects; their paid tier starts at $10.99/user/month. ClickUp Free is generous but their paid plans add up. For a solo operator, Notion is almost always cheaper.

Customization: Notion lets you build exactly what you need, not what the software decided you need. Don't need time tracking? Don't add it. Need a custom field for client industry? Add it in 10 seconds. Asana gives you their field structure with limited modification. Notion is a blank canvas.

Integration with your workspace: In Notion, your project management database sits next to your meeting notes, client contracts, brand guide, and personal journal. You can link a task to the brief it relates to, which links to the client record, which links to the active invoice. This cross-referencing is impossible in dedicated PM tools without integrations.

No per-seat pricing: If you occasionally collaborate with a contractor or show a client a board, Notion doesn't charge per seat for guests (up to 10 on the free plan, unlimited on Plus). Asana and ClickUp charge per user on paid plans.

When to Choose a Dedicated PM Tool Instead

Notion is the right choice for freelancers and teams up to ~15 people. If you manage teams larger than 15, need native time tracking billed to clients, require advanced portfolio reporting, or use complex dependency-chain project structures, consider ClickUp (better customization than Asana) or Asana (better reporting). For most freelancers reading this, Notion is the move.

Step 1: Build Your Projects Database

Database 1 of 2
Projects Database — The Master List
  • Project Name (Title) — descriptive name: "Acme Corp Website Redesign Q3"
  • Status (Select: Not Started / In Progress / In Review / Done / On Hold / Cancelled)
  • Client (Relation to your Clients database, or Text if you don't have one yet)
  • Deadline (Date — final delivery date)
  • Budget (Number — total project value in your currency)
  • Billed (Number — amount invoiced so far)
  • Priority (Select: High / Medium / Low)
  • Type (Select: Website / Design / Writing / Strategy / Development / Other)
  • Project Lead (Person — if you're on a team)
  • Kick-Off Date (Date — when the project started)
  • Health (Formula — see below)
  • Days Until Deadline (Formula: dateBetween(prop("Deadline"), now(), "days"))
  • Notes (Text — internal notes, quick context)
  • Brief URL (URL — link to project brief)
Active Projects (Status = In Progress) By Client (grouped) This Week's Deadlines (Deadline filter) Completed Archive

The Health formula is your early warning system. Here is a basic version using Notion's formula syntax:

Project Health Formula

if(prop("Status") == "Done", "✅ Complete", if(dateBetween(prop("Deadline"), now(), "days") < 0, "🔴 Overdue", if(dateBetween(prop("Deadline"), now(), "days") < 7, "🟡 Due Soon", "🟢 On Track")))

This formula shows four states: Complete (done), Overdue (deadline passed, not done), Due Soon (within 7 days), and On Track. Glancing at your Projects database, you instantly know which projects need attention.

Step 3: The 5 Essential Views

Views are where Notion's PM system comes alive. The same data, filtered and formatted differently, gives you completely different insights. Set these up as saved views on your Tasks database:

View 1: Kanban Board

Switch your Tasks database to Board view, grouped by Status. This is your primary working view — cards flow left to right from Not Started through In Progress, In Review, and into Done. Show the Due Date, Priority tag, and Project name on each card so you know context at a glance. You should see this view first thing each morning.

View 2: Calendar View

Switch to Calendar view, dated by Due Date. This shows you the density of upcoming deadlines across the month — visual overcommitment is obvious when 12 tasks cluster around the same day. Filter by Status not equal to Done so completed tasks don't clutter the calendar. This view is also the best to screenshot and share with clients for a project timeline overview.

View 3: Table with Active Filters

A Table view filtered to Status = In Progress, sorted by Due Date ascending. This is your "what am I working on right now" view — everything currently active, sorted by what's due soonest. Add the Priority property as the first column so high-priority items are visually prominent. This is the view to check when you sit down to work and need to pick the next task.

View 4: Gallery / Timeline View

Switch to Gallery view, showing Tasks by project. This gives you a card-based overview of tasks per project. For a rough Gantt-style timeline view without paying for Notion's Timeline view (which requires a paid plan), create a Table view filtered by project, sorted by Due Date, with a formula property showing a simple text progress bar based on task completion percentage.

View 5: Completed Archive

A Table view filtered to Status = Done, sorted by Completion Date descending. This is your track record — every finished task in reverse chronological order. Use this for: end-of-week reviews (what did I actually complete?), client reporting (here's everything we shipped this month), and personal motivation on slow days.

Step 4: Project Templates for Recurring Work

If you do recurring project types — website redesigns, monthly content packages, brand identity projects — templates eliminate the "what tasks does this project need?" thinking every time a new project starts.

In Notion, create a Template for your Projects database. When you click "New" on a project, instead of a blank page, you get a pre-structured page with:

  • Standard phases already set up (Discovery, Design, Development, Review, Launch)
  • Default tasks pre-created and linked to the project
  • A brief section with the standard questions you ask every client
  • A milestone tracker table
  • An internal notes section for team communication
  • A client communication log (date, what was communicated, any decisions made)

Build one template per project type. A "Website Build" template has 40+ standard tasks. A "Monthly Retainer" template has recurring tasks that you clone each month. A "Brand Identity" template walks through discovery, concept, design, and delivery phases with standard tasks pre-loaded.

Template Trick

When you create tasks inside a project template, they don't automatically link to the new project when you use the template. The workaround: use a database template that creates the project page with a linked Tasks view filtered to that project. Then manually add tasks from your master database to the project. Alternatively, use Notion's "Relation" button on the project page to add related tasks.

Step 5: Client-Facing Project Board

One of Notion's most underused PM features: sharing a filtered project view with clients as a public read-only page. This replaces the weekly status email and gives clients visibility without requiring them to learn Notion.

For each client, create a "Client Portal" page in Notion containing:

  • Project Overview — a brief description of the project scope and goals
  • Current Milestone — what phase you're in right now
  • Upcoming Deliverables — a simple list or table of what's coming next and when
  • Completed Work — a linked database view filtered to Done tasks, so they can see the work history
  • How to Request Changes — clear instructions so revision requests come through properly
  • Key Dates — next review date, final delivery date, payment due dates

Share this page via "Share to web" — generates a public URL with read-only access. Send this link to your client at project kickoff. Update it weekly. The result: clients feel informed without sending you "just checking in" emails, and you have a professional client experience that competitors using email-based PM don't offer.

Managing Multiple Clients Simultaneously: The Daily Dashboard

When you're running 4-6 projects across different clients, the biggest risk is task fragmentation — one client's urgent task gets missed because you were focused on another project. The Daily Dashboard solves this.

Create a new Notion page called "Daily Dashboard" containing linked database views:

  • Today's Tasks (Tasks database, filtered: Due Date = Today AND Status ≠ Done)
  • Overdue Tasks (Tasks database, filtered: Due Date before Today AND Status ≠ Done)
  • In Review (Tasks database, filtered: Status = In Review — things waiting for client feedback)
  • Active Projects Health (Projects database, filtered: Status = In Progress, sorted by Health formula)
  • This Week's Deadlines (Projects database, filtered: Deadline within 7 days)

Open this page first thing every morning. Everything requiring action today is in one view, across all clients and projects. This 5-minute morning review — checking Today's Tasks and Overdue items — is the habit that prevents missed deadlines.

Handling Project Delays and Blockers

Delays and blockers are normal in client work. The problem is when they're invisible until they become crises. The Notion PM system handles this proactively:

Blocker Protocol

When a task is blocked (waiting on client assets, waiting for feedback, waiting on a vendor), check the Blocker checkbox and fill in Blocker Notes describing: what is blocking it, who is responsible for unblocking it, and by when it needs to be resolved. Your "Blocked Items" view shows all blocked tasks at a glance.

Deadline Escalation

Add a "Revised Deadline" date property to your Projects database. If the original deadline changes, put the new date here and leave the original intact for reference. Add an "Escalation Notes" text property to explain why the deadline changed — useful for client conversations and post-project reviews.

Buffer Policy

Build your own buffer into every project by setting internal deadlines 3-5 days earlier than the client deadline. Use "Client Deadline" as the Deadline property you show clients, and "Internal Deadline" as a separate property your Kanban and Calendar views are based on. This buffer absorbs the inevitable last-minute revision round or technical issue.

The Weekly Project Review Ritual

A PM system is only as good as the review habits around it. The weekly review — 30-45 minutes on Friday afternoon or Monday morning — is what keeps the system accurate and catches problems before they compound.

Weekly review checklist in Notion (create this as a template):

  • Review all In Progress projects — are they actually in progress?
  • Check Overdue Tasks view — acknowledge and reschedule or close
  • Review Blocked Items — have any blockers been resolved? Update accordingly
  • Check next week's deadlines — are you prepared for everything due next week?
  • Archive any Done projects by changing their status to Done and removing from Active view
  • Update client portal pages with current status
  • Add next week's tasks — anything planned that isn't in the system yet
  • Review budget vs. billed on active projects

The goal isn't to spend an hour on process. It's 30 minutes of honest accounting: does the system reflect reality? If a task is listed as "In Progress" but you haven't touched it in 10 days, update it. An accurate system is useful; a stale system is noise.

Integrating Notion PM with Your Other Tools

Notion doesn't live in isolation. Most freelancers use it alongside communication tools (Slack, email), file storage (Google Drive, Dropbox), and scheduling tools (Calendly, Google Calendar). Here's how to integrate them without paid automation tools:

Slack Integration

Use the free Notion-Slack integration (available in Notion's integrations settings) to receive notifications when properties change on databases you follow. Set up notifications for: tasks moved to "In Review" (so you know to review them), projects moved to "Done" (celebration and next-steps trigger), and overdue items (daily digest).

Google Drive

Add a "Drive Folder" URL property to your Projects database. Every new project gets a Google Drive folder — paste the link in Notion. All deliverables, source files, and assets live in Drive; Notion references them. You get Notion's organizational structure with Drive's file handling, without duplicating files.

Google Calendar via Zapier

A free Zapier workflow: when a task's Due Date is set in Notion, create a Google Calendar event. This means your project deadlines appear in your personal calendar automatically. The reverse — creating a Notion task from a calendar event — also works and is useful for scheduling work blocks for specific tasks.

Calendly

Add a Calendly link to your client portal pages for easy meeting scheduling. When a client meeting is booked, create a meeting notes sub-page in the relevant client's project page in Notion. Keep the client communication log in Notion; it creates a searchable history of every interaction.

Project Health Score Formula — Advanced Version

The basic health formula covers overdue and deadline proximity. Here's a more sophisticated version that accounts for blocked tasks and budget:

Advanced Health Score

Create a "Blocked Task Count" rollup property on Projects (rollup of Tasks, filtered to Blocker = true, show: Count). Then use: if(prop("Status") == "Done", "✅ Done", if(dateBetween(prop("Deadline"), now(), "days") < 0 and prop("Status") != "Done", "🔴 OVERDUE", if(prop("Blocked Task Count") > 2, "🟠 Multiple Blockers", if(dateBetween(prop("Deadline"), now(), "days") < 7, "🟡 Due Soon", "🟢 On Track"))))

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you use Notion for project management?
Yes — Notion is an excellent project management tool, especially for freelancers and small teams. It supports Kanban boards, calendar views, linked databases for tasks and projects, and shareable client-facing pages. While it lacks some enterprise PM features like native time tracking and invoicing, its all-in-one workspace integration makes it the best choice for solo operators managing multiple client projects.
Is Notion better than Asana for project management?
For freelancers and small teams (1-5 people), Notion is typically better than Asana. Notion is cheaper, more customizable, and integrates your PM with your notes, client database, and templates. Asana has advantages for larger teams needing dedicated PM software with advanced reporting and automation. For most freelancers, Notion's flexibility and cost advantage win.
How do I set up a Kanban board in Notion?
Create a database, add a Status select property with stages (Not Started, In Progress, Review, Done), then change the view from Table to Board. Notion groups cards by the Status property automatically. Customize which properties appear on cards by clicking Board settings. Add color-coding by setting colors on each Status option in the select property editor.
How do I manage multiple clients in Notion?
The key is a Daily Dashboard page with a linked Tasks database view filtered to "Due Date = Today" across all projects. Add a Client relation to your Projects database and create per-client views. Check the Daily Dashboard each morning for a unified view of everything due today, regardless of which client it belongs to. Also create a Blocked Items view to catch anything waiting on client input.
What is a project health score in Notion?
A project health score is a formula property in your Projects database that rates each project's status. A basic version: if the deadline is passed and status is not Done, it shows Overdue. If the deadline is within 7 days, it shows Due Soon. Otherwise, it shows On Track. An advanced version also accounts for the number of blocked tasks and whether the project is over budget.
Can Notion replace ClickUp?
For freelancers, yes. Notion can replace ClickUp. ClickUp has more built-in PM features like native time tracking and Gantt charts, but Notion's all-in-one workspace means your project management lives alongside your notes, client records, and templates. ClickUp requires more configuration and has a steeper learning curve. For solo operators, Notion's simplicity and integration usually win.
How do I handle project delays in Notion?
Add a Blocker checkbox and Blocker Notes text property to your Tasks database. When a task is blocked, check the box and describe what's blocking it and who needs to act. Create a Blocked Items view filtered to Blocker = true. In your Projects database, add a formula that flags overdue projects with a red indicator. Run a weekly review to catch delays before they compound into crises.
How do I share a project status with clients in Notion?
Create a dedicated client portal page in Notion showing milestones, current status, upcoming deliverables, and completed work. Use a filtered linked database view that only shows tasks relevant to that client. Share via Notion's "Share to web" option — this creates a public read-only link your client can access without a Notion account. Update it weekly. Clients love the transparency and it dramatically reduces status-update emails.
