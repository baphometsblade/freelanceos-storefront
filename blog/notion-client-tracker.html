How to Track Clients in Notion (Free Template)

Published May 2026  ·  Last updated: May 2026  ·  7 min read

Building a Notion client tracker template is one of the highest-leverage things a freelancer can do in their first year of business. Not because client tracking is complicated, but because the default state — scattered emails, mental notes, and a vague sense of who owes you money — gets expensive fast. A single missed follow-up on a warm lead costs more than the time it would have taken to set up a proper system.

What You Actually Need to Track (and What You Don't)

Most Notion client tracker tutorials tell you to add 20 fields to your database. In practice, you'll use maybe eight of them regularly. Adding too many fields is worse than adding too few — it creates friction that makes the system feel like homework instead of a useful tool.

The fields that genuinely matter:

  • Client Name — obvious, but use the full business name, not a nickname
  • Status — this is the most important field (more on this below)
  • Primary Contact — the person you actually communicate with at the company
  • Email / Phone — contact info without having to open your email to find it
  • Source — where this client came from (referral, inbound, outbound, etc.)
  • Last Contacted — a date field; this prevents leads from going cold silently
  • Notes — a text field for anything important that doesn't fit elsewhere
  • Linked Projects — a Relation to your Projects database

Fields you might eventually want but shouldn't add immediately: contract value, timezone, preferred communication method, referral source details. Add these when you have a specific need, not speculatively.

The Status Field: The Heart of Your Client Tracker

The Status field is what turns a client list into a client tracker. Without it, you have a database. With it, you have a system you can actually manage your relationships with.

Recommended statuses for a freelancer client tracker:

  • Lead — someone you've had initial contact with but haven't done work for
  • Proposal Sent — you've pitched them; waiting for a decision
  • Active — current paying client
  • Paused — worked with before, relationship on hold, potential to resume
  • Past — completed work, no current engagement
  • Nurture — long-term relationship you want to maintain even without active work

Six statuses sounds like a lot. In practice, most of your database will be Active and Past. The others represent the stages where things fall through the cracks without a system — and that's exactly why they're there.

Building the Views That Make It Useful

The database itself is storage. The views are the interface. Here are the four views every Notion client tracker needs:

View 1: Pipeline Board

A Board (Kanban) view grouped by Status. This is your bird's-eye view of all relationships at a glance. How many leads are warm? How many proposals are pending? Are you relying too heavily on one active client? The Pipeline Board answers these questions in five seconds every time you open it.

Set this as the default view for your client database. It should be the first thing you see.

View 2: Active Clients

A Table or Gallery view filtered to Status = Active. This is your daily driver — the list of people you're currently working for. Include the Primary Contact, linked Projects, and Last Contacted date. Sort by Last Contacted ascending so the clients you haven't been in touch with recently float to the top.

View 3: Follow-Up Queue

A Table view filtered to Status = Lead OR Status = Proposal Sent, sorted by Last Contacted ascending. This view is your weekly follow-up list. Open it every Monday, check anyone you haven't contacted in more than a week, and send a quick check-in. This single habit converts more leads than any other.

The Follow-Up Queue is the most valuable view in your client tracker. Most freelancers lose deals not because prospects weren't interested — but because the freelancer got busy and stopped following up. The view makes neglect visible before it becomes permanent.

View 4: Revenue by Client

A Table view with a Rollup field that sums invoice totals linked from your Invoices database. Sorted by total revenue descending. This view tells you who your most valuable clients are — important for knowing who to prioritize for retention conversations, referral asks, and upsell opportunities.

Connecting Your Client Tracker to Projects and Invoices

A standalone client database is useful. A client database connected to your Projects and Invoices databases is significantly more powerful. The setup requires three things:

  1. Create a Projects database if you don't have one. Add a Relation property that links to your Clients database. Set it as "One project — many clients" or "One project — one client" depending on your work.
  2. Create an Invoices database (or use the one from FreelanceOS Lite). Add a Relation property linking invoices to both Clients and Projects.
  3. Add Rollup fields to your Clients database: one that counts linked Projects, one that sums Invoice totals, one that shows the most recent Invoice date.

Once these are in place, every client record becomes a complete summary of your entire relationship with that client: every project, every invoice, the total revenue, the last time you billed them. All visible on one page without navigating anywhere.

The Weekly Client Review Habit

A client tracker only works if you use it consistently. The most reliable way to build that habit is a weekly review — a 10-minute routine that keeps the database accurate and prevents relationships from going stale.

The weekly review looks like this:

  1. Open the Pipeline Board. Move any cards that have changed status this week.
  2. Open the Follow-Up Queue. Send a quick check-in to any lead or proposal you haven't touched in 7+ days.
  3. Open the Active Clients view. For any client you haven't contacted recently, note why — are you waiting on them, or have you been avoiding the conversation?
  4. Check the Revenue by Client view. Is your income diversified, or are you dangerously dependent on one client?

The whole review takes under 10 minutes once the database is populated. The value it generates — in relationships preserved, leads converted, problems spotted early — is out of proportion with that time investment.

Can I use Notion to replace dedicated CRM software?
For most freelancers and small agencies, yes. Dedicated CRMs like HubSpot or Pipedrive are built for sales teams with high lead volume and complex follow-up automation. Freelancers typically don't need that level of complexity — and Notion's flexibility means you can build exactly the system you need without paying for features you'll never use.
Does this work on mobile?
Yes. Notion's mobile app supports all database views, including Board view for the pipeline. The experience is slightly more limited than desktop, but perfectly functional for checking the Follow-Up Queue or updating a client status on the go.
How do I handle multiple contacts at the same client company?
The simplest approach is a "Contacts" sub-database linked to your Clients database. Each contact record holds one person's name, email, role, and last contacted date. The client record shows a linked view of all contacts at that company. FreelanceOS Pro includes this structure out of the box.
