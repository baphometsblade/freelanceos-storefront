Why Notion Makes a Great Lightweight CRM

Notion is not a traditional CRM. It does not have built-in email sequences, call recording, or predictive lead scoring. What it does have is everything a freelancer actually needs:

  • Flexible databases that you can structure exactly the way your business works — not the way a software company assumes it works.
  • Relations and Rollups that let you link contacts, deals, and activities together so you can see the full picture of every relationship.
  • Multiple views — kanban, table, calendar, gallery — so you can switch between a pipeline view and a follow-up calendar without any extra tools.
  • Everything in one place. Your CRM, project notes, client documents, templates, and task lists all live in the same workspace. No tab-switching, no copy-pasting between tools.

For freelancers managing up to 50 active contacts and a dozen active deals at any time, Notion is not a compromise — it is genuinely the best option available.

Building Your Notion CRM: Step by Step

Database 1
The Contacts Database

This is the core of your CRM — a record for every person you have a professional relationship with. Create a new database page in Notion and add the following properties:

Name (Title) Email (Email) Phone (Phone) Company (Text) Status (Select) Tags (Multi-select) Last Contact (Date) Source (Select) Notes (Text)

For the Status property, create these options: Lead, Warm, Proposal Sent, Active Client, Past Client, Not a Fit. For Tags, use categories like Referral Source, Agency, Enterprise, SMB — whatever segments are meaningful in your business. For Source, track where the lead came from: Referral, LinkedIn, Website, Cold Outreach, Event.

Database 2
The Deals / Pipeline Database

The Deals database tracks active opportunities — each row is a potential project or engagement. Add these properties:

Deal Name (Title) Stage (Select) Value (Number — currency) Probability (Number — %) Close Date (Date) Contact (Relation → Contacts) Notes (Text)

For the Stage property, create: Discovery, Proposal, Negotiation, Won, Lost. The Relation property to Contacts is what ties deals to real people. Set Probability as a number from 0-100 — this lets you calculate a weighted pipeline value.

Database 3
The Activities Log

The Activities database is a timestamped record of every meaningful interaction. Each row is one interaction. Properties:

Activity (Title) Type (Select) Date (Date) Contact (Relation → Contacts) Deal (Relation → Deals) Notes (Text) Follow-Up Date (Date)

For Type, create: Call, Email, Meeting, Proposal Sent, Contract Signed, Check-in. The Follow-Up Date property is critical — it powers your follow-up calendar view and ensures nothing falls through the cracks.

Linking Everything Together with Relations and Rollups

The power of a Notion CRM comes from the connections between databases. Here is how to wire them up:

  1. Link Deals to Contacts: In the Deals database, add a Relation property pointing to Contacts. This lets you open any deal and immediately see which contact it belongs to — and open any contact to see all their deals.
  2. Link Activities to both: In Activities, add Relations to both Contacts and Deals. Now every call note, email log, or meeting record connects to the right person and the right deal.
  3. Add Rollups to Contacts: Back in the Contacts database, add a Rollup property that counts the number of linked Deals per contact. Add another that shows the total value of all linked deals. Now you can see at a glance which contacts have the most pipeline value.
  4. Add a Rollup for last activity: In Contacts, add a Rollup that shows the most recent Activity date. This gives you an at-a-glance "last touched" date without opening every contact record.

3 Views Every Freelancer Needs

View 1: Pipeline Kanban (Deals database)
Group by Stage. This gives you the classic CRM pipeline view — columns for Discovery, Proposal, Negotiation, Won, Lost. Drag cards between columns as deals progress. Add Deal Value and Close Date as visible card properties.
View 2: Active Clients Table (Contacts database)
Filter by Status = Active Client. Show properties: Name, Email, Company, Last Contact Rollup, active deal count Rollup. This is your quick-reference view of who you are currently working with.
View 3: Follow-Up Calendar (Activities database)
Calendar view grouped by Follow-Up Date. Every activity with a follow-up date set appears on this calendar. Review it at the start of each week to know exactly who needs to hear from you today.

Automations: Let Notion Do the Reminders

Notion's built-in automations (available on paid plans) let you trigger actions when properties change. For a CRM, the most useful automation is:

  • When a Deal Stage changes to "Won" → create a new page in your Projects database and assign the linked Contact. This kicks off the client onboarding process automatically.
  • When a Contact's Status changes to "Active Client" → send yourself a Notion notification or update a linked project status.

On the free plan, you can achieve similar results by building a filtered view that shows only contacts whose Last Contact date is more than 30 days ago — your manual follow-up queue.

The CRM Workflow: Lead to Client to Referral

1
New lead comes in. Create a Contact record immediately. Set Status to Lead. Log the source. Add a note about context. Create a linked Activity record for the first touchpoint.
2
Discovery call scheduled. Create a Deal record linked to the Contact. Set Stage to Discovery. Add a Follow-Up Date to your Activity for after the call.
3
Proposal sent. Update Deal Stage to Proposal. Log an Activity of type Proposal Sent. Set a follow-up date 5-7 days out in the Activity record.
4
Deal closes. Update Deal Stage to Won. Update Contact Status to Active Client. Your calendar automation or Projects database view picks up the new client.
5
Project delivered. Update Contact Status to Past Client. Schedule a check-in activity 60-90 days out. Ask for a testimonial and referral at the right moment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Notion be used as a CRM?
Yes. Notion works very well as a lightweight CRM for freelancers and small businesses. You can build a contacts database, a deals pipeline, and an activities log, then link them together with Relations and Rollups to get a complete view of every client relationship. It lacks advanced automation and AI features of paid CRMs but covers 80% of what most freelancers actually need.
Is a Notion CRM better than HubSpot or Pipedrive?
For freelancers and small teams with fewer than 50 active contacts, a Notion CRM is often better than paid CRMs because it is free, flexible, and already connected to the rest of your Notion workspace. Paid CRMs become worth it when you have a sales team, need email sequences, or manage hundreds of active deals simultaneously.
What databases do I need for a Notion CRM?
A complete Notion CRM needs three core databases: a Contacts database (name, email, company, status, tags, last contact), a Deals or Pipeline database (stage, value, probability, close date), and an Activities log (call notes, emails sent, meetings). Link them with Relations and use Rollups to surface data across databases.
