Freelance Business Structure: Should You Form an LLC or Stay a Sole Proprietor?

When you start freelancing, you're automatically a sole proprietor — you don't have to do anything to "become" one. But as your income grows, the question of whether to form an LLC becomes more pressing. The choice affects your legal liability, how you pay taxes, how you're perceived by clients, and how much administrative overhead you take on.

This guide explains both structures clearly and helps you decide what's right for your situation. We'll cover the tax differences, the liability question, the real cost of forming and maintaining an LLC, and the income thresholds where the math starts to favor upgrading your structure.

1. What Is a Sole Proprietor?

A sole proprietorship is the simplest business structure. You don't need to file any formation documents — if you're freelancing under your own name or a DBA (Doing Business As), you're already a sole proprietor. There's no separation between you and the business: legally, financially, and for tax purposes, you and your business are the same entity.

How sole proprietors pay taxes

All business income flows directly to your personal tax return via Schedule C. You pay income tax on your net profit (revenue minus deductible business expenses) at your personal tax rate. You also pay self-employment tax of 15.3% on net profit up to $160,200 (2026 threshold) — this covers both the employee and employer portions of Social Security and Medicare. You can deduct half of this self-employment tax on your return.

Sole proprietors pay quarterly estimated taxes — April 15, June 15, September 15, and January 15 — to avoid underpayment penalties. This is often the biggest administrative surprise for new freelancers.

The sole proprietor liability problem

There is no legal wall between your personal assets and your business. If a client sues your business — for a missed deadline, a contracted deliverable dispute, a copyright infringement claim — they can pursue your personal bank accounts, your car, and in extreme cases your home. This risk is theoretical for many freelancers, but it's real and it grows as your client contracts grow in size and complexity.

2. What Is an LLC?

A Limited Liability Company (LLC) is a legal business structure created by filing Articles of Organization (or Certificate of Formation) with your state. It creates a separate legal entity for your business — distinct from you personally. The core benefit is in the name: limited liability.

If your LLC gets sued or incurs debt, your personal assets are generally protected. The business can lose, but your personal bank account, home, and personal property are typically shielded — provided you maintain the LLC properly (separate bank account, don't commingle funds, keep records).

How single-member LLCs are taxed by default

A single-member LLC (one owner) is taxed as a "disregarded entity" by default — which means the IRS treats it exactly like a sole proprietorship for federal tax purposes. Your business income flows to Schedule C on your personal return, and you still pay self-employment tax at 15.3%. The LLC gives you liability protection without changing your default tax treatment. This surprises many new LLC owners who expected tax benefits automatically — the tax savings require an additional election (see S-Corp section below).

4. Tax Differences for Freelancers

The default tax treatment for a sole proprietor and a single-member LLC is identical. Both pay income tax and 15.3% self-employment tax on net profit. The difference emerges only if you elect S-Corp status for your LLC.

Self-employment tax: the silent cost

Self-employment tax (SE tax) hits freelancers hard because employees split this cost with their employer — the employer pays 7.65% and the employee pays 7.65%. As a freelancer, you pay both sides: 15.3% on the first ~$160K of net profit. On $80,000 in freelance profit, that's $12,240 in SE tax alone — before income tax. This is why business structure and tax strategy matter at higher income levels.

Deductible business expenses reduce your SE tax bill: You pay SE tax on net profit (revenue minus deductible expenses). Every legitimate business expense — software, home office, equipment, professional development, business travel, health insurance premiums — reduces both your income tax and your SE tax. Track everything.

Tax deductions available to all freelancers (regardless of structure)

  • Home office deduction — if you have a dedicated workspace, you can deduct a proportional share of rent/mortgage interest and utilities
  • Self-employed health insurance — 100% of premiums deductible from gross income
  • Self-employed retirement contributions — SEP-IRA contributions up to 25% of net earnings (up to $69,000 in 2026)
  • Software and tools — subscriptions, licenses, business apps
  • Professional development — courses, books, conferences
  • Business travel — flights, hotels, meals at 50% for business purposes
  • Mileage — standard mileage rate for business driving
  • Freelance tools and equipment — computer, camera, peripherals used for business

5. When Should a Freelancer Form an LLC?

There's no single universal answer, but here are the income and situation triggers that make forming an LLC clearly worth it:

$0 – $30K/year

Likely not worth it yet

At low income levels, the filing fees and administrative overhead (annual reports, separate banking, potential state taxes) may exceed any real benefit. The liability protection is valuable but abstract — stick to good contracts and professional liability (E&O) insurance as your primary risk management tools.

$30K – $60K/year

Worth considering — especially if clients require it

At this income range, the liability protection becomes more meaningful as contract sizes grow. Some corporate clients will only sign contracts with a legal business entity (not an individual). If you're losing clients because you don't have an LLC, form one. If liability risk is low and no clients are asking, a strong contract and E&O insurance may suffice.

$60K+/year

Form an LLC and evaluate S-Corp election

Above $60K in net profit, the math on S-Corp taxation starts to make sense. The liability protection is increasingly important as contract values grow. The LLC is also a prerequisite for the S-Corp election. Most freelancers earning $60K+ net profit should have an LLC — consult a CPA to determine whether the S-Corp election is appropriate for your specific situation.

Situation triggers (regardless of income)

  • A client contract requires you to be a business entity
  • You're hiring subcontractors — LLC protects you from employment liability
  • Your work has meaningful liability exposure (legal, medical, financial, engineering work)
  • You want to open a business bank account and accept payments in your business name
  • You're building a brand that may be worth more than the freelance income itself

6. How to Form an LLC as a Freelancer

Forming an LLC is simpler than most people expect. Here's the straightforward process:

  1. Choose your state. Most freelancers form their LLC in their home state (where they live and work). Wyoming and Delaware are popular for online businesses due to low fees and privacy protections, but this creates complexity if you're not a resident — you'll need a registered agent in that state. For most freelancers, your home state is the right choice.
  2. Choose your business name. The name must be unique in your state. Search your state's Secretary of State business name database. The name must include "LLC" or "Limited Liability Company."
  3. File Articles of Organization. File online through your state's Secretary of State website. Fees range from $50 (Kentucky) to $500 (Massachusetts). Processing takes 1–10 business days in most states.
  4. Get an EIN. Apply for a free Employer Identification Number (EIN) from the IRS at irs.gov. This takes 5 minutes online. Your EIN is your business's tax ID — you'll use it instead of your SSN for business tax filings and banking.
  5. Open a business bank account. Use your EIN, LLC formation documents, and state certificate to open a separate business checking account. Mercury and Relay are popular free options for freelancers.
  6. Get an Operating Agreement. Most states don't legally require this, but it's recommended. An operating agreement documents how the LLC is run. For a single-member LLC, it's a simple one-page document. Templates are available free online.
  7. Register for state business taxes if required. Some states require LLCs to register for sales tax, state income tax, or pay annual franchise taxes. Check your state's requirements — California's $800 minimum franchise tax is the most notorious example.

DIY vs. service: You can form an LLC yourself through your state's website for just the filing fee. If you want to skip the paperwork, services like Northwest Registered Agent ($125/year) or ZenBusiness ($49/year) handle the filing and include registered agent service. Both options are legitimate — the DIY route saves money, the service route saves time.

7. S-Corp Election: Is It Worth It?

The S-Corp election (IRS Form 2553) is a tax strategy available to LLCs that can significantly reduce self-employment tax above a certain income level. Here's how it works and when it makes sense:

How S-Corp taxation works

Under an S-Corp election, you pay yourself a "reasonable salary" from the business. That salary is subject to payroll taxes (Social Security and Medicare — effectively the 15.3% self-employment tax). Any profit above that salary is taken as a distribution — and distributions are not subject to SE tax.

S-Corp example: You have $120,000 in freelance net profit. As a sole proprietor, you pay SE tax on the full $120,000 = ~$18,360. As an S-Corp with a $70,000 reasonable salary, you pay SE tax on $70,000 = ~$10,710, and take $50,000 as a distribution (no SE tax). Net SE tax savings: ~$7,650/year. Minus additional costs (payroll service ~$600/year, additional CPA time ~$1,500/year), net benefit: ~$5,550/year.

When the S-Corp election makes sense

The S-Corp election adds real administrative complexity: you need to run payroll (even if just for yourself), file quarterly payroll tax returns (940/941), and your CPA will charge more to prepare your additional tax forms (1120-S + K-1). The break-even point is typically around $60,000–$80,000 in net profit — below that, the additional costs eat the savings. Above $80,000–$100,000, the savings are material and the complexity is worth it for most freelancers.

Always consult a CPA before electing S-Corp status. The "reasonable salary" determination is not arbitrary — the IRS scrutinizes S-Corp salaries, and setting your salary too low to maximize distributions is a red flag for audit. A CPA will help you set a defensible salary based on industry norms for your role.

8. Business Banking and Financial Setup

Regardless of whether you form an LLC, every freelancer should have a separate business bank account. Commingling personal and business finances creates tax preparation nightmares, makes it impossible to accurately track business income and expenses, and — for LLCs — can "pierce the corporate veil," potentially eliminating your liability protection.

Recommended freelance banking setup

  • Business checking: Mercury Bank (free, great API for accounting integration) or Relay (free, good for expense categorization) are popular with freelancers
  • Business savings: Use a HYSA (high-yield savings account) for your tax reserve — set aside 25–30% of every payment received for quarterly estimated taxes
  • Business credit card: Earn rewards on business expenses and simplify expense tracking. Chase Ink, American Express Blue Business Cash, and Capital One Spark are commonly used by freelancers

Frequently Asked Questions

Should a freelancer form an LLC?
Most freelancers benefit from forming an LLC once they're earning consistently — typically $40,000–$60,000+ per year, or sooner if they work with clients who require contracts or have significant liability exposure. An LLC provides personal asset protection, business credibility, and the option to elect S-Corp taxation at higher income levels. Below $30K/year, a strong contract and professional liability insurance may be sufficient until income grows.
What is the difference between a sole proprietor and an LLC?
The key difference is liability protection. As a sole proprietor, there's no legal separation between you and your business — if someone sues your business, your personal assets are at risk. An LLC creates a legal wall between you and the business, generally protecting your personal savings, car, and home from business liabilities. For federal tax purposes, a single-member LLC and sole proprietor are taxed identically by default, unless the LLC elects S-Corp status.
How much does it cost to form an LLC as a freelancer?
LLC formation costs vary by state: filing fees typically run $50–$500 (national average around $130). Annual renewal fees range from $0 to $800+ depending on your state (California charges a minimum $800/year franchise tax). You can form an LLC yourself online through your state's Secretary of State website, or use a service like Northwest Registered Agent for $125/year including registered agent service.
What is an S-Corp election and should freelancers use it?
An S-Corp election allows your LLC to be taxed as an S Corporation instead of a sole proprietorship. The tax benefit: you pay yourself a reasonable salary (subject to payroll taxes) and take remaining profits as distributions, which are not subject to self-employment tax (15.3%). This saves meaningful money above $60,000–$80,000 in net profit, but adds complexity: payroll setup, additional tax filings, and higher CPA fees. Always consult a CPA before electing S-Corp status.
Do freelancers need a business bank account?
Yes — all freelancers should have a separate business bank account, even sole proprietors. Mixing personal and business finances creates tax headaches, makes expense tracking difficult, and for LLC owners, can undermine your liability protection. Open a free business checking account at Mercury or Relay in about 20 minutes using your EIN. Set up a separate savings account for your quarterly tax reserve (set aside 25–30% of every payment).
