What Is Notion?

Notion is an all-in-one workspace that combines note-taking, document creation, task management, databases, wikis, and project management in a single app. The core philosophy: everything in Notion is a block, and blocks can be anything — text, images, to-do items, databases, embedded videos, links to other pages, and more. This means Notion can function as a word processor, a spreadsheet, a Kanban board, a wiki, a calendar, and a project manager simultaneously — because the same block-based building system underlies all of them.

Who Uses Notion and Why

  • Students: Organize lecture notes, track assignments, build research databases, and manage group projects
  • Freelancers: Manage clients, projects, invoices, content calendars, and time tracking in one workspace
  • Small teams: Build wikis, manage projects, track OKRs, and replace Confluence + Trello + Google Docs with one tool
  • Writers: Organize drafts, research, reading notes, and editorial calendars
  • Productivity enthusiasts: Build second brains, GTD systems, PARA structures, and personal knowledge management systems

What Notion Is NOT

Setting expectations correctly prevents frustration:

  • Not a calendar app: Notion has a Calendar view and Google Calendar integration, but it's not a replacement for Google Calendar or Apple Calendar for event management
  • Not a spreadsheet: Notion databases look like spreadsheets but have limited formula support — use Airtable or Google Sheets for heavy data work
  • Not a reminder app: Notion has no push notifications for due dates — you won't get a popup when a task deadline passes
  • Not a chat tool: Notion has comments and @mentions but is not a Slack replacement
  • Not offline-first: Notion requires internet for most functions — if you need fully offline note-taking, consider Obsidian

Your First 10 Minutes in Notion

After signing up at notion.so, you'll land in a sample workspace with some pre-built example pages. Here's what to do first:

1

Create Your First Page

Click the + button in the left sidebar (next to "Add a page" or hovering on the left sidebar), or press Ctrl+N (Windows) / Cmd+N (Mac). A blank page appears. Type any title — "My First Page" works fine — then press Enter to move to the body. Type anything. Press Enter to create a new line.

That's a page. You've started. The entire rest of Notion builds on this foundation.

2

Learn the Slash Command — Your Most Important Shortcut

On any Notion page, press / to open the command menu. This reveals every type of block you can add to a page. Start typing to filter the list. Press Enter to insert the selected block type. This is how you add every single type of content in Notion — get comfortable with it immediately.

Try typing /h1 to add a Heading 1, /todo to add a to-do checkbox, or /image to add an image block. The slash command is the entire Notion interface condensed into one shortcut.

Understanding Pages and Blocks — The Foundation

Everything in Notion is built from two primitives:

  • Page: A container that holds blocks. Pages can live at the top level of your workspace (visible in the sidebar) or nested inside other pages. There's no limit to how deep pages can be nested.
  • Block: Any single piece of content. A paragraph of text is a block. An image is a block. A to-do checkbox is a block. A database is a block. Even other pages embedded inside a page are blocks.

This block architecture is what makes Notion so flexible. You can mix and match any block types on a single page: a heading block, then a paragraph block, then a to-do list block, then a database block, then an embedded YouTube video block — all in the same document.

Inline Editing — How Blocks Work in Practice

Click anywhere on the page to start a new text block. Press Enter to go to the next line. Type / to add a different type of block. Drag and drop any block (hover to see the drag handle ⋮⋮) to reorder content. Click the six-dot drag handle to move blocks, duplicate them, delete them, or convert them to a different block type.

The intuition: think of each line as its own atom. You can grab any atom and move it anywhere on the page, or transform it into a different type of content. This is fundamentally different from a word processor, where text is a continuous stream — and it's what makes Notion uniquely powerful for structured thinking.

Databases — The Real Power of Notion

Pages are powerful. But databases are where Notion becomes genuinely transformative. For most beginners, databases are the concept that requires a mental model shift — but once you get it, everything clicks.

What Is a Notion Database?

A Notion database is a collection of pages that all share the same set of properties. Think of it like a spreadsheet where each row is actually a full Notion page. The row is the entry. The columns are properties (metadata). But you can open any row to reveal the full page behind it — with all its block-based content.

Example: Client Database

  • Each row = one client (each row is actually a full page where you can store notes, files, meeting summaries, communication history)
  • Columns = properties that every client entry shares: Client Name, Status, Industry, Monthly Revenue, Contact Email, Date Started
  • You can filter to see only Active clients
  • You can sort by Monthly Revenue to see your most valuable clients
  • You can switch to Board view to see clients organized by Status (Active / Paused / Prospect / Archived)

This is the key insight: in Notion, your clients aren't just a list. They're a queryable, filterable, viewable-in-multiple-ways collection of full pages. This is more powerful than a spreadsheet (each row is a complete document) and more structured than free-form notes (every entry has the same properties).

Building Your First Notion Database — Step by Step

1

Create a New Database Page

Create a new page and title it "📝 My Tasks." Type /database in the body and select "Database - Full page" from the dropdown. A new database appears with one default column: "Name" (the title of each entry).

2

Add Properties (Columns)

Click the + at the top right of the database header to add a new property. Add these:

  • Status — property type: Select — options: To Do, In Progress, Done, Blocked
  • Due Date — property type: Date — enable "Include time" if you want hour precision
  • Priority — property type: Select — options: 🔴 High, 🟡 Medium, 🟢 Low
  • Notes — property type: Text — any additional context
3

Add Some Real Tasks

Add 5–10 real tasks you actually need to do. Click each row's name to open it as a full page — notice that inside each task page, you can write notes, add checklists, attach files, link to other pages. These aren't just spreadsheet rows — they're full pages that happen to also appear as table rows.

4

Try the Board View

Click "Add a view" at the top left of the database → choose "Board." Click "Group by" → select "Status." Your tasks instantly reorganize into a Kanban board with columns for To Do, In Progress, Done, and Blocked. Drag tasks between columns to update their status. The underlying data is identical — you're just seeing it differently.

5

Try Filtering and Sorting

Back in Table view, click "Filter" → Add filter → Priority is 🔴 High. Your database now shows only high-priority tasks. Click "Sort" → Add sort → Due Date, ascending. Now your high-priority tasks are sorted by nearest deadline first. Click "Filter" again to remove the filter and see all tasks. These filters are saved per view — you can set up one view that always shows today's high-priority tasks.

You've just built a task manager that rivals dedicated apps like Todoist or Things — and it's connected to the same workspace as everything else you'll build in Notion.

Notion Views — See Your Data Different Ways

Every Notion database can have multiple views. Each view shows the same underlying data through a different lens. You can have as many views as you want on the same database.

📋 Table View

Spreadsheet-like. Best for data entry, bulk editing, and seeing all properties at once. Default view for new databases.

📌 Board View

Kanban columns. Best for project management. Group by Status to drag tasks through stages.

📅 Calendar View

Shows entries on a calendar by their Date property. Best for scheduling, editorial calendars, and event planning.

🖼️ Gallery View

Shows each entry with its cover image. Best for visual collections: reading lists, portfolios, design inspiration.

📄 List View

Clean, minimal list. Best for focus lists without visual clutter. Good for quick task reviews.

📊 Timeline View

Gantt-chart style. Shows entries across time. Best for project planning with start and end dates.

The key insight: you're not choosing one view and committing. You can toggle between all views at any time. Many teams have a Board view for active work, a Calendar view for scheduling, and a Table view for data entry — all on the same database.

Notion Templates — Skip the Setup

Instead of building everything from scratch, use templates to get started fast. There are three sources:

1. Notion's Built-In Template Gallery

Click "Templates" in the left sidebar or go to notion.com/templates. Notion offers free templates for: meeting notes, project trackers, editorial calendars, book lists, habit trackers, travel planners, personal CRMs, OKR trackers, and more. Click a template and duplicate it to your workspace instantly.

2. Database Page Templates

Inside any database, click the dropdown arrow next to the "New" button and select "New template." You can create a template that pre-fills the structure of a new page. For example: every new client page automatically has sections for "Overview," "Communication Notes," "Deliverables," and "Payment History." Your template runs every time you click New — no rebuilding from scratch.

3. Template Buttons

More advanced: use the /button block to create a clickable button that generates pages, inserts blocks, or modifies database entries. Notion's Left Sidebar — Navigation Basics

Understanding the sidebar makes Notion much easier to navigate:

  • Workspace name (top left): Click to switch between workspaces if you have multiple
  • Search (Ctrl/Cmd+P): The fastest way to navigate to any page. Type a few letters and press Enter.
  • Inbox: Shows @mentions and comments directed at you across all workspace pages
  • Favorites: Pages you've starred (right-click any page → Add to Favorites) appear here — always one click away
  • Private pages: Pages only visible to you
  • Shared pages: Pages visible to your team members
  • Trash: Deleted pages remain here for 7 days (free) or 30 days (Plus) before permanent deletion
Pro tip: Notion's search (Cmd/Ctrl+P) is the fastest way to navigate. Don't scroll through the sidebar looking for a page — just press the shortcut and type a few letters of the page name. After a week of use, you'll navigate Notion almost entirely through search.

5 Beginner Notion Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Mistake 1: Building Everything Before Using Anything

Many beginners spend their first week building an elaborate workspace — 20 databases, a complex dashboard, custom formulas — before entering a single real task or note. They optimize a system for a life they don't yet live in Notion.

✅ Fix:

Set up ONE database and use it for a real week before building anything else. Start with a simple Tasks database. The needs of real use will tell you what to build next far better than any planning session.

❌ Mistake 2: Recreating Your Folder Structure

People naturally try to replicate their Google Drive folder structure in Notion: Work → Projects → Client X → 2026 → Q1 → Documents. This creates deep nesting that's hard to navigate and doesn't use Notion's actual strengths.

✅ Fix:

Use databases with filters instead of deep folder nesting. One "Projects" database is more powerful than a nested folder structure because you can filter it instantly by any property — client, status, date, type — in one click.

❌ Mistake 3: Nesting Pages Too Deep

Creating pages inside pages inside pages inside pages creates a maze that becomes impossible to navigate without remembering the exact path. Most people can't find their own content after a month.

✅ Fix:

Keep a maximum of 2–3 levels of nesting. Use relations and linked database views to connect information across different sections rather than burying it deeper. If you're creating a fourth level, it's a sign you need a database with filters instead.

❌ Mistake 4: Ignoring Views

Many beginners use only Table view for every database — and then complain that Notion "isn't that useful" for task management. Board view alone makes Notion 3x more useful for project work. Calendar view makes it 3x more useful for scheduling.

✅ Fix:

Try every view type on your task or project database within your first week. Create at least three views per major database: Table for editing, Board for status management, and one other (Calendar or List) for review. You'll likely find at least one that transforms how you use that database.

❌ Mistake 5: Not Using Templates

Building each new meeting notes page, project brief, or weekly review from scratch is slow, inconsistent, and means you forget sections you meant to include.

✅ Fix:

Spend 20 minutes in your second week of Notion building database templates for every recurring page type you create. The investment pays back within days. Meeting notes template, project page template, weekly review template, client page template — these run every time you create a new entry and take seconds to use.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Notion free?
Yes — Notion has a generous free plan with unlimited pages, unlimited blocks, unlimited guests on shared pages, and 5MB per file upload. The free plan is fully functional for individual use. Paid plans start at $12 per month for Plus, which adds 30-day page history, unlimited file uploads, and custom domain support.
What should a beginner use Notion for?
Start with one simple use case and master it before expanding. Good starting points include a to-do list (Tasks database with Status and Due Date), a reading tracker (Books database with Title, Author, Status, and Rating), or a notes database (for capturing anything you want to save). Get comfortable with pages and basic databases before building complex multi-database systems.
Is Notion better than Evernote?
For most beginners in 2026, Notion is the better choice. Its database features, linked records, and block-based editing are significantly more powerful and flexible than Evernote's note-based approach. Evernote's mobile app is faster for quick one-handed captures and works better offline. Notion requires more initial setup time but becomes more useful the more you use it — Evernote often plateaus.
Does Notion work offline?
Partly. The Notion desktop and mobile apps cache recently viewed pages for offline reading. You can read cached content without internet. However, you cannot create new pages, edit content reliably, or sync changes without an active internet connection. For primarily offline note-taking, consider using Obsidian alongside Notion for the databases.
How long does it take to learn Notion?
Basic Notion — creating pages, building simple databases, switching views, using templates — takes 1–2 hours to grasp. Building a genuinely useful personal workspace takes about a week of consistent daily use. Advanced features like formulas, relations, rollups, API integrations, and automations take several months to master. Most users find the basics pay back the learning investment within the first week.
Can I import my notes from Evernote or Google Docs?
Yes — Notion supports imports from Evernote, Confluence, Google Docs, Trello, Asana, Quip, and more. Go to Settings → Import in the left sidebar of your workspace. Most common formats (HTML, Markdown, CSV) are also supported. Large Evernote imports can take several minutes and may require some cleanup afterward, but the process is relatively smooth.
Is Notion good for students?
Excellent — Notion is one of the most widely used tools among students and academics for organizing lecture notes, tracking assignments by due date, building reading lists, managing research projects, and collaborating on group work. The free plan fully covers typical student needs, and Notion offers an Education plan at a discount for verified students and educators.
