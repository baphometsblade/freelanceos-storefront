The Best Freelance Website Builder in 2026 (Compared + Ranked)

Squarespace, Webflow, WordPress, Wix, Cargo, Format, Notion + Super.so, and GitHub Pages — honest comparison with pricing, pros, cons, and who each one is for.

Your freelance website is not optional. It is your 24/7 pitch, your portfolio, your credibility signal, and your primary SEO asset. Clients who receive your referral still Google you before replying. Without a professional website, you are invisible to a meaningful portion of potential clients — and under-positioned to the ones who do find you.

The challenge is choosing the right builder. There are now more options than ever, ranging from free developer tools to polished no-code platforms, and the wrong choice can cost you hours of wasted setup or money on features you do not need. This guide compares the eight best website builders for freelancers in 2026 — with honest assessments of who each one is actually for.

What Your Freelance Website Must Include

Before picking a tool, be clear on what you are building. Every freelance website needs these six pages:

  • Homepage: Who you are, what you do, and for whom — in 10 seconds or less. A clear headline, social proof, and a call to action.
  • Services page: Specific offerings, what is included, and pricing signals (ranges or "starting from" prices). Clients who cannot figure out if they can afford you will leave without contacting you.
  • Portfolio / Case Studies: Evidence of results, not just pretty screenshots. Before/after, metrics, client names (if permitted).
  • About page: Your background, specialization, and the story of why you do this work. Personality matters — clients hire people they trust.
  • Contact page: A simple form, your email, your location (city at minimum), and response time expectation.
  • Blog or Resources: Optional but valuable for SEO and demonstrating expertise. Even 4-6 well-written articles can drive meaningful organic traffic.

Top 8 Website Builders for Freelancers (2026)

1. Squarespace
From $16/mo — Best Overall for Visual Freelancers
Top Pick

Squarespace remains the gold standard for freelancers who want a beautiful, professional website without spending hours on setup. The templates are legitimately excellent — designed by professional designers, not as afterthoughts. Portfolio features are built in, image handling is excellent, and the editor is intuitive enough that non-technical users get a finished site in a day or two.

The SEO tools cover the basics well: meta titles, descriptions, canonical URLs, XML sitemaps, and basic structured data. Not the most powerful SEO platform available, but sufficient for most freelancers. The biggest limitation is flexibility — Squarespace is opinionated about design, which is both its strength (you cannot make it look bad) and its weakness (you cannot make it look radically different).

Pros

  • Best templates for portfolios
  • Excellent image/media handling
  • Integrated blogging + SEO
  • Ecommerce available
  • Reliable uptime

Cons

  • Limited design flexibility
  • No free plan
  • Slower than competitors
  • Less powerful SEO vs WordPress
2. Webflow
From $14/mo — Best for Designers and Developers
Best for Designers

Webflow gives designers pixel-perfect control over layout, animation, and interactions — without writing a line of code (though you can). If you want a portfolio that looks genuinely custom and demonstrates your design skill, Webflow is the right choice. The learning curve is steeper than Squarespace, but the output ceiling is much higher.

For developers, Webflow's CMS and dynamic content features make it a viable platform for client sites, not just your own portfolio. The SEO features are strong, clean semantic HTML output, and it handles page speed well. Webflow is increasingly a portfolio standard in the design community — having a Webflow site signals that you know the tool clients are using.

Pros

  • Maximum visual control
  • Clean HTML/CSS output
  • Strong SEO capabilities
  • CMS for dynamic content
  • Impressive animations

Cons

  • Steeper learning curve
  • Expensive at scale
  • Overkill for non-designers
  • Monthly cost adds up
3. WordPress.com
Free to $25/mo — Best for Writers and Bloggers
Best for SEO

WordPress.com (the hosted version) gives you the world's most powerful CMS with a managed hosting layer on top. For freelancers who plan to publish significant content, WordPress's SEO ecosystem is unmatched — plugins like Yoast and Rank Math give you granular control over every on-page SEO element. The Gutenberg editor is now genuinely good.

Note: WordPress.com is not WordPress.org (self-hosted). WordPress.com is simpler and managed; WordPress.org gives you full control but requires you to handle hosting, security, and updates yourself. For most freelancers, WordPress.com's paid plans are the right balance. On the free plan, ads will appear on your site and your URL will be yoursite.wordpress.com.

Pros

  • Best SEO ecosystem
  • Powerful blogging tools
  • Enormous theme/plugin library
  • Free plan available
  • Largest community

Cons

  • More complex to learn
  • Plugin conflicts possible
  • Maintenance overhead
  • Templates less polished
4. Wix
Free to $17/mo — Best for Beginners
Easiest Setup

Wix has improved dramatically over the past three years. The templates are professional, the AI site builder can generate a reasonable first draft in minutes, and the drag-and-drop editor is genuinely the most intuitive in the market. For complete beginners who need a professional website up quickly without learning anything technical, Wix is the fastest path.

The free plan shows Wix ads and requires a Wix subdomain — not suitable for a professional freelance site. The first paid tier removes this. SEO features have improved but still lag behind WordPress. The biggest ongoing concern with Wix is that migrating away later is difficult — your content is locked in their system.

Pros

  • Easiest drag-and-drop editor
  • AI site builder
  • Free plan available
  • Large template library
  • Integrated booking/payments

Cons

  • Hard to migrate away later
  • Weaker SEO than WordPress
  • Free plan shows Wix branding
  • Performance can be slow
5. Cargo
$13/mo — Best for Creative Portfolios
Most Creative

Cargo is a niche platform beloved by creative professionals — illustrators, photographers, motion designers, art directors. The templates are strikingly designed with unusual layouts and interactions you simply cannot achieve on Squarespace. If you want your portfolio to feel genuinely different, Cargo delivers.

Not for everyone: Cargo is opinionated about aesthetics, the editor takes some learning, and the platform is less SEO-optimized than mainstream alternatives. But for creatives in competitive visual fields where portfolio aesthetics matter enormously, Cargo stands apart.

Pros

  • Unique, distinctive templates
  • Strong creative community
  • Affordable price
  • Great for visual work

Cons

  • Limited SEO features
  • Steeper learning curve
  • Not for non-creatives
  • Smaller feature set
6. Format
From $7/mo — Best for Photographers
Photography

Format is purpose-built for photographers and visual artists. It handles large image galleries with exceptional performance, offers client proofing tools, print sales integration, and password-protected client galleries — features no general-purpose builder includes natively. If you are a photographer, Format is almost certainly your best option.

Pros

  • Purpose-built for photographers
  • Fast image galleries
  • Client proofing built in
  • Print sales integration
  • Affordable entry price

Cons

  • Not suitable for non-photographers
  • Limited blog features
  • SEO tools are basic

8. GitHub Pages
$0 — Best Free Option for Developers
Free / Dev

GitHub Pages hosts static sites for free, forever, at yourname.github.io (or a custom domain you point to it). For developers, this is the obvious choice: full code control, free hosting, integrates with your existing workflow, and signals competence to technical clients immediately.

Not suitable for non-developers. Requires knowledge of HTML/CSS or a static site generator like Hugo, Jekyll, or Eleventy. No WYSIWYG editor, no drag-and-drop, no managed backend. But if you are comfortable with version control and static sites, GitHub Pages is the most cost-effective professional option available.

Pros

  • Completely free
  • Full code control
  • Credibility signal for devs
  • Custom domain support
  • Fast static performance

Cons

  • Requires coding knowledge
  • No CMS or editor
  • Not for non-developers
  • Manual updates required

How to Build Your Freelance Website in a Weekend (6-Step Checklist)

  • Day 1, Step 1: Choose and register your domain. Buy yourname.com or yourname[service].com from Namecheap or Google Domains. Keep it simple — firstname + lastname is best.
  • Day 1, Step 2: Choose your platform and create your account. Based on the comparison above, select the builder that fits your skill level and goals. Start your trial or free plan today.
  • Day 1, Step 3: Select a template and set up your homepage. Write your headline (what you do + for whom), add a professional photo, and include one clear call-to-action button.
  • Day 2, Step 4: Build your Services and Portfolio pages. Services: list 2-4 specific offerings with brief descriptions and pricing signals. Portfolio: add 3-6 of your best projects with context and results.
  • Day 2, Step 5: Write your About and Contact pages. About: your background, specialization, and one personal element that makes you memorable. Contact: a form that goes to your email, plus your direct email address.
  • Day 2, Step 6: Connect your domain, set up basic SEO, and publish. Add meta titles and descriptions to every page. Verify in Google Search Console. Share the link immediately.

SEO Basics for Your Freelance Website

You do not need to be an SEO expert to get organic traffic from your freelance website. These basics will put you ahead of 80% of your competition:

Target local + service keywords. "Freelance copywriter [your city]" or "B2B SaaS copywriter" is more achievable than broad terms. Add your city to your homepage and contact page.
Write case studies, not just portfolio samples. A page titled "How I helped [Client Type] increase conversions by 40%" ranks for problem-based searches. Pure portfolio images do not.
Use descriptive alt text on all images. Every portfolio image should have alt text describing what it is and who it was for (without revealing confidential client details).
Get your site listed. Add your website to Google Business Profile, LinkedIn, Clutch, and relevant professional directories. Each citation improves your domain authority and local relevance.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best website builder for freelancers?
Squarespace is the best overall website builder for most freelancers because it offers professional templates designed for portfolios, reliable SEO tools, and an easy-to-use editor — all starting at $16/month. Webflow is better for designers and developers who want more control without touching code. Wix is the easiest for complete beginners.
Do freelancers need a website?
Yes. A professional website is the single most important marketing asset a freelancer can have. It is your 24/7 pitch, your portfolio, your credibility signal, and your SEO asset. Clients who find you through referrals will check your website before replying. Without one, you are invisible to a meaningful portion of potential clients.
How much does a freelance website cost?
A freelance website costs between $0 (GitHub Pages for developers) and $30-$40 per month for premium platforms with custom domains. Most freelancers spend $12-$25 per month for a professional website builder plus a custom domain ($10-$15/year). Total annual cost: $150-$350 for a professional setup.
Can I use Notion as my freelance website?
Yes. With a tool like Super.so, you can publish a Notion page as a public website with a custom domain, custom styles, and SEO settings. This works especially well for freelancers who already live in Notion and want their portfolio, case studies, and services page in the same system. FreelanceOS Pro includes a portfolio template built for this setup.
What pages does a freelance website need?
Every freelance website should have at minimum: a homepage (what you do and for whom), a services page (specific offerings and pricing signals), a portfolio or case studies page (evidence of results), an about page (your background and specialization), a contact page, and a blog or resources section to support SEO and demonstrate expertise.
