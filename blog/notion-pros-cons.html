Notion is one of the most hyped productivity tools on the internet — and for good reason. But every Notion enthusiast has also experienced the moment it lets them down: the slow loading database, the feature that doesn't quite work the way you need it to, the collaboration hiccup that interrupts your flow. This is an honest review, not a sales pitch.

We use Notion daily for our own business (FreelanceOS is built on Notion templates), so we've experienced both the genuine power and the genuine frustrations firsthand. Here's the unvarnished truth about where Notion excels and where it falls short — so you can make a clear-eyed decision about whether it's right for you.

Our position: We sell Notion templates, which means we have a financial interest in Notion succeeding. We've done our best to make this an honest review anyway — because if Notion isn't right for you, you'll find out quickly and feel misled. The pros and cons below reflect our genuine experience with the product.

1. What Notion Gets Right

Extreme flexibility — it works for nearly any use case

Notion is a blank canvas. It can be a note-taking app, a CRM, a project manager, a wiki, a content calendar, a habit tracker, or all of these simultaneously. No other tool in this price range offers this level of customization. The flip side is the learning curve — but the flexibility is genuine and it scales.

All-in-one: replaces 3-5 apps for most users

A well-built Notion workspace can replace Google Docs (documents), Trello/Asana (project management), Confluence (wiki), Airtable (databases), and Evernote (notes). The tool consolidation alone is worth significant monthly savings and cognitive overhead reduction.

Linked databases are genuinely powerful

Notion's database system — with relations, rollups, formulas, and multiple view types (table, board, calendar, gallery, timeline) — is one of the most flexible relational data tools available in a no-code environment. For freelancers managing clients, projects, and invoices in linked databases, this feature alone justifies the tool.

Generous free plan

Notion's free plan gives individual users unlimited pages and blocks. For solo users, the free plan covers almost everything you'd need. Unlike many SaaS tools, you don't hit a wall immediately — you can build a comprehensive personal system before ever considering an upgrade.

Notion AI is genuinely useful (not a gimmick)

Notion AI, added via a $8/month add-on, can draft pages, summarize databases, extract action items from meeting notes, and fill in structured data from prose. The AI is integrated directly into the writing and database experience in ways that feel natural rather than bolted on. For knowledge workers, the time savings are real.

Massive template ecosystem

Notion's template gallery has thousands of free and paid templates for virtually every use case. The quality varies enormously — but the best templates (like FreelanceOS Pro) give you a fully structured system in minutes rather than weeks. The template ecosystem lowers the barrier to getting value out of Notion significantly.

Cross-platform with good mobile apps

Notion works on web, Mac, Windows, iOS, and Android. The mobile apps have improved significantly — you can view databases, add quick notes, and check tasks comfortably on a phone. Not perfect (see cons), but functional for mobile use.

Good integrations and API

Notion integrates natively with Slack, GitHub, Figma, Google Drive, and dozens of other tools. The Notion API is well-documented and enables powerful automations through Zapier, Make, and custom integrations. For teams that want to automate their workflows, the API is a significant advantage over simpler note apps.

Price-to-value is exceptional for individuals and small teams

At $8/month per user for the Plus plan, Notion is remarkable value for what it offers. Compared to paying separately for Asana ($10.99), Confluence ($5.75), and Evernote ($7.99), a team of five consolidating into Notion saves meaningful money while getting a more integrated experience.

2. What Notion Gets Wrong

Performance degrades on large databases

Databases with 2,000+ items load slowly. Databases with 10,000+ items can become painful to use. For power users with large datasets — a multi-year client archive, a research database with thousands of entries — this is a real limitation. Filtering, sorting, and relation lookups all slow noticeably at scale. Notion has improved performance over time, but it's still not a tool for truly large data sets.

Real-time co-editing struggles with large simultaneous teams

Notion supports simultaneous editing, but it's not as smooth as Google Docs for large teams all editing the same document at once. For a 20-person team editing a quarterly plan simultaneously, you'll see conflicts and refresh delays. For most use cases (one or two people editing at a time), this is fine. But it's not a Google Docs replacement for high-concurrency real-time editing.

Offline mode is unreliable, especially on mobile

Notion's offline support is inconsistent. Content you've previously viewed is cached, but making changes offline — especially on mobile — often results in sync conflicts or data loss when you reconnect. For a tool used for critical business data, this is a meaningful reliability concern. Don't use Notion as your only place to store critical information without a backup plan.

No native time tracking

Despite being used heavily by freelancers, Notion has no built-in time tracking. You can build workarounds using formulas and date properties, but they're not intuitive and require manual management. If time tracking is central to your workflow, you'll need a separate tool (Toggl, Harvest, or Clockify) that ideally integrates with Notion via API.

Automations are limited and complex to set up

Notion's built-in automations (trigger → action) are functional but limited compared to dedicated workflow tools. Complex automations require Zapier or Make, which adds cost and complexity. If you need sophisticated conditional workflows — "when task is marked done, send a client update and create an invoice" — you're looking at a multi-tool setup rather than something Notion handles natively.

Team pricing becomes expensive above 10+ users

At $8/user/month for the Plus plan, a 25-person team pays $200/month. Not unreasonable, but it adds up. The Business plan ($15/user/month) is required for advanced permissions, SAML SSO, and audit logs — features that enterprise teams typically require. At 50 users, you're at $750/month before the Enterprise custom pricing tier.

Significant learning curve for new users

Notion's flexibility is also its biggest barrier. A new user opening a blank Notion workspace sees an empty page with no guidance on what to do next. Without a template or onboarding, many users abandon Notion within two weeks having never built anything useful. The learning curve is real — it takes 10–20 hours of genuine use to understand how to structure a Notion workspace effectively.

3. Who Notion Is Perfect For (and Who Should Look Elsewhere)

Notion works best for:

  • Freelancers and solopreneurs — managing clients, projects, proposals, and invoices in linked databases. FreelanceOS Pro was built specifically for this use case.
  • Students and researchers — organizing coursework, research notes, reading lists, and project timelines in a single workspace.
  • Content creators — managing content calendars, video pipelines, brand deal trackers, and audience analytics.
  • Knowledge workers building a second brain — capturing, connecting, and referencing information across projects and contexts.
  • Small teams (2–20 people) — who want one workspace for wikis, project management, meeting notes, and documentation without the complexity of enterprise tools.

Notion is not ideal for:

  • Large enterprise teams that need deep IT admin controls, complex role-based permission hierarchies, and enterprise security compliance (SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001 at all tiers).
  • Teams that live in spreadsheets — Notion's database formulas are limited compared to Excel or Google Sheets. Financial modeling, complex data analysis, and pivot tables all belong in a dedicated spreadsheet tool.
  • High-concurrency real-time collaboration — large teams that need 10+ people editing the same document simultaneously. Google Docs handles this better.
  • Users who need reliable offline access — if you frequently work without internet, Notion's offline mode isn't dependable enough to rely on for critical work.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the main pros of Notion?
Notion's main advantages are: extreme flexibility (works as a note app, database, project manager, and wiki simultaneously), all-in-one tool that replaces multiple apps, powerful linked databases, a generous free plan for individuals, built-in Notion AI, a large template ecosystem, and cross-platform availability. For freelancers and small teams, the combination of these features in one affordable tool is hard to match.
What are the main cons of Notion?
Notion's main limitations are: performance issues on large databases, inconsistent offline sync on mobile, no built-in time tracking, complex automation setup, real-time co-editing that struggles at high concurrency, team pricing that becomes expensive above 10+ users, and a significant learning curve for new users. None of these are dealbreakers for most use cases, but they're worth knowing before committing.
Is Notion worth it?
Yes, Notion is worth it for most individuals, freelancers, and small teams. The free plan is genuinely useful, and the Plus plan at $8/month is excellent value. Notion works best as a central hub for information, project management, and documentation. It's less ideal as a pure task manager (Todoist is better), a high-concurrency real-time editor (Google Docs is better), or a complex spreadsheet tool (Excel/Sheets are more powerful for pure data work).
Who should use Notion?
Notion is ideal for freelancers managing clients and projects, students organizing coursework and notes, knowledge workers building a second brain, content creators managing content pipelines, and small teams wanting one workspace for wikis, projects, and meeting notes. It's less ideal for large enterprise teams needing deep IT controls, teams that rely on complex spreadsheet formulas, or users who need reliable offline access.
What is Notion's pricing in 2026?
Notion's 2026 pricing: Free plan (unlimited personal pages, limited block history), Plus plan at $8/user/month billed annually, Business plan at $15/user/month (advanced permissions, SAML SSO), and Enterprise at custom pricing. The free plan is sufficient for individual use. The Plus plan is ideal for freelancers and small teams and represents exceptional value given what Notion can replace.
