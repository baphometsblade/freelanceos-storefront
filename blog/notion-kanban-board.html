Notion Kanban Board: Build Your Perfect Workflow in Under 10 Minutes

The complete setup guide: Board view, WIP limits, swimlanes, multi-project Kanban, best practices for solo freelancers, and 5 ready-to-use Kanban templates.

Kanban is one of the most effective workflow methods ever developed — and Notion's Board view makes it accessible without a dedicated tool like Trello or Jira. The visual flow of cards moving from left to right, from Backlog through Done, creates a mental model of your work that spreadsheets and to-do lists can't match.

This guide covers everything from the initial build (10 minutes) to advanced setups like swimlanes and multi-project Kanban boards. Whether you're managing client projects, personal tasks, a content calendar, or a product backlog, the same database structure applies — only the column names and views change.

What Is Kanban and Why Does It Work?

Kanban originated at Toyota in the late 1940s as a production scheduling system. The word means "visual card" in Japanese. The core insight: make work visible. When tasks are cards on a board moving through defined stages, the entire team (or just you) can see the state of all work without a status meeting.

Three principles drive Kanban's effectiveness:

  • Visualize work: Every task is a visible card. Nothing exists invisibly in someone's head or buried in an email thread. If it's not on the board, it's not being worked on.
  • Limit Work in Progress (WIP): The #1 productivity killer for knowledge workers is context switching — jumping between tasks without finishing any. WIP limits force you to finish before starting something new. The classic rule: limit In Progress to 3 items maximum.
  • Manage flow: Pay attention to where cards pile up. If ten cards are stuck in In Review because clients are slow to respond, that's a process problem you can fix. Kanban makes bottlenecks visible before they become crises.

For freelancers specifically, Kanban solves the "too many open loops" problem. When you have 12 active tasks across 4 clients, the mental overhead of tracking their status is exhausting. The board carries that cognitive load so your brain doesn't have to.

Why Notion Kanban Instead of Trello?

Trello is pure Kanban — simpler, with a better native mobile experience. Notion wins for freelancers because: your Kanban cards can link to client records, project databases, and meeting notes. One workspace, zero context switching between apps. If you already use Notion for anything, your Kanban belongs there.

Build Your Notion Kanban Board: Step-by-Step

1

Create a Database

On any Notion page, type /database and select "Table - Inline" or "Table - Full page". Name it something descriptive — "Tasks", "Client Projects", "Content Calendar". This database is the data source your Kanban board will visualize. The database can have many views simultaneously — one Board view, one Table view, one Calendar view — all showing the same underlying data.

2

Add a Status Select Property with Your Workflow Stages

Add a Select property named "Status". Add these options in order: Backlog → To Do → In Progress → In Review → Done. Assign distinct colors to each: Backlog (gray), To Do (blue), In Progress (purple), In Review (yellow), Done (green). These colors appear on cards in Board view and make the stage instantly scannable without reading text.

3

Switch to Board View and Group by Status

At the top of your database, click Add a view, select Board, and choose "Group by: Status". Your database instantly rearranges into columns — one per Status option. Cards appear in the column matching their Status value. Drag a card from Backlog to In Progress: the Status property updates automatically. That's the whole Kanban mechanic in Notion.

4

Customize Card Properties

Click the ··· menu on the Board view and select "Properties". Choose which properties appear on cards — you want 2-4 maximum. Recommended: Due Date (so deadlines are visible without opening the card), Priority (select with colors — red/yellow/blue), and either Assignee (for teams) or Project tag (for multi-project boards). Keep cards minimal — the goal is a quick visual scan, not a data table.

5

Simulate WIP Limits with a Count Formula

Notion doesn't enforce WIP limits natively. Build a manual system: create a new Table view filtered to Status = In Progress and add a "Count" aggregation at the bottom showing how many tasks are in that column. Or add a callout block above your board: "WIP Policy: Max 3 items in In Progress at any time." When you see the column filling up, your process signals: finish before starting something new.

6

Color-Code by Priority

Add a Priority Select property with High (red), Medium (yellow), Low (blue). Make Priority visible on cards (step 4). In Board view, the colored badge on each card makes priority immediately visible across the entire board. At a glance, you can see if your In Progress column is full of Low priority tasks while High priority items sit in Backlog — a clear signal to reorder.

Advanced Kanban: Multi-Project Boards

When you manage multiple simultaneous client projects, a single Kanban board across all projects is either overwhelming (too many cards) or too narrow (you miss the cross-project picture). Here are three approaches:

Approach 1: Single Board with Project Filter Tabs

Keep one Tasks database. Add a Project property (select or relation). Create multiple Board views, each filtered to a different project: "Acme Corp Board", "Beta Co Board", "Personal Board". Tab between them. This is the simplest approach and works well for up to 5-6 concurrent projects.

Approach 2: Master Board with Project Color-Coding

One unfiltered Board view showing all tasks across all projects. Use a Project select property with distinct colors (Acme = red, Beta = blue, etc.) visible on cards. The full picture is visible at once — you can see if one project is dominating your In Progress column at the expense of others. Works best for 2-3 projects where the total card count stays manageable (under 40-50 items).

Approach 3: Project-Level Boards with Task Rollup

Each project gets its own Notion page with its own Tasks database and Kanban board. A master "All Projects" view uses a linked database view showing tasks across all project databases. This is the most organized but requires more setup. Best for 6+ simultaneous projects with complex task structures.

Swimlanes: Group and Sub-Group in Notion Kanban

Swimlanes turn your board from a single-dimension flow (left to right by status) into a two-dimension grid (left to right by status, top to bottom by another category). In Notion, this is the Sub-group feature in Board view settings.

Useful swimlane configurations:

  • Group by Status, Sub-group by Assignee — team workload overview: see each person's cards across all stages. Immediately spot if one person is overloaded while another has capacity.
  • Group by Status, Sub-group by Project — project progress across stages. Each project gets its own horizontal swimlane. Good for a project portfolio overview.
  • Group by Status, Sub-group by Priority — priority visibility. High priority tasks are in one row, Medium in another. Prevents low-priority tasks from crowding out high-priority work in the In Progress column.
Swimlane Caution

Swimlanes are powerful but add visual complexity. Use them for review sessions, not as your daily working view. For daily use, keep the board simple — just Group by Status with 2-3 properties on cards. Switch to swimlane view for your weekly project review to get the full picture.

Client-Specific Kanban Boards

Some clients want to track their project's progress directly. Build a client-facing Kanban board by creating a filtered Board view in Notion and sharing it as a public read-only page.

  1. Create a filtered Board view for the specific client (filter Tasks by Client = "Acme Corp")
  2. Clean up column names if needed — replace "In Review" with "Awaiting Your Feedback" for client clarity
  3. Limit visible properties on cards to ones clients should see: Task Name, Due Date, and Status
  4. Share via Notion's "Share to web" — generates a public read-only URL
  5. Send the link to your client at project kickoff

This creates a living project tracker that clients can check any time instead of emailing you for status updates. Update cards as work progresses — clients see changes in real time. This alone significantly reduces "just checking in" emails and positions you as a transparent, professional operator.

Kanban Best Practices for Solo Freelancers

Generic Kanban advice assumes a team. Here's what works for solo operators:

Limit In Progress to 3 Items Maximum

This is the most important Kanban rule and the hardest to follow. Every new task that moves to In Progress before you finish existing ones adds cognitive load. With 3 items in In Progress, you can mentally track them all. With 7, you can't. When you're tempted to start something new while existing items are stuck, ask: what would it take to unblock one of the existing items instead?

Use Backlog as an Inbox

Every new task — from any source, any urgency — goes into Backlog first. Never move a task directly to In Progress when it arrives. This prevents reactive urgency from hijacking your planned workflow. Once a task is in Backlog, you evaluate it against your existing In Progress items before deciding when to pull it in. The Backlog is a buffer, not a to-do list.

Archive Done Items Weekly

Don't let Done pile up. Create a second view filtered to Status = Done as your archive. Move items there (or just filter them out of the main board view) weekly. A cluttered Done column obscures the work in progress. The satisfaction of clearing Done is also a real motivational reward for your weekly review ritual.

Review the Board Every Morning

Your Kanban board is only useful if it reflects reality. Spend 5 minutes each morning: are all In Progress items actually being worked on today? Are there items in Backlog that should be pulled forward? Are any items blocked that need a note explaining why? This 5-minute review keeps the board honest and sets your intention for the day.

Pull, Don't Push

True Kanban is pull-based: you pull the next task from Backlog into In Progress only when you have capacity (i.e., In Progress is below your WIP limit). Never let someone else push tasks directly into your In Progress — everything goes through Backlog first. This applies to client requests: "I'll add that to the backlog and we can discuss priority" is a complete response.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does Notion have a Kanban board?
Yes — Notion has a built-in Kanban board called Board view. Create any database, add a Select property for Status, then switch from Table view to Board view. Notion groups cards into columns based on the Select options. You can drag cards between columns, customize what appears on each card, and create multiple Board views with different filters on the same database.
How do I create a Kanban board in Notion?
Create a database (type /database on a page), add a Status select property with stages like Backlog, To Do, In Progress, In Review, Done. Click "Add a view", select Board, and choose "Group by Status". Your database immediately displays as a Kanban board with one column per status. Add properties like Due Date, Priority, and Assignee to appear on cards. The whole setup takes under 10 minutes.
Can Notion Kanban show multiple projects?
Yes — a single Notion database can show tasks from multiple projects on one Kanban board. Add a Project property to your tasks and create filtered Board views per project to see each one separately. Or use the full unfiltered view to see everything across all projects with color-coding by project. For 6+ projects, consider project-level boards with a master linked database view.
Does Notion Kanban support WIP limits?
Notion doesn't enforce WIP limits natively — it won't block you from adding cards to In Progress. Simulate WIP limits by adding a count aggregation showing items per column, posting a policy note above the board (max 3 in In Progress), and practicing the discipline of finishing before starting. The human enforcement is actually more effective than automated blocking for solo operators.
Is Notion Kanban better than Trello?
For freelancers who already use Notion, yes — your Kanban cards can link to client records, project databases, and meeting notes in one workspace. Trello is better for pure Kanban simplicity and mobile card experience. Notion wins for all-in-one workspace integration, linked database relations, and having one fewer app to manage. If you need standalone Kanban and nothing else, Trello is simpler.
What are swimlanes in Notion Kanban?
Swimlanes are horizontal groupings within a Kanban board creating a grid of columns (status) and rows (another category). In Notion, use the Sub-group feature in Board view settings. Set Group by Status and Sub-group by Assignee to see each team member's workload across stages. Or Sub-group by Project to see each project's tasks as separate horizontal rows. Best used in weekly reviews, not as a daily working view.
How do I archive done items in a Notion Kanban?
Create a second Table or Board view filtered to Status = Done as your archive. This keeps your main working board clean while preserving history. Alternatively, add an Archived checkbox — when a Done task is reviewed, check Archived, and filter your main Board view to exclude Archived items. Run this cleanup weekly during your project review ritual. Never delete completed tasks — they're valuable for reporting and reference.
