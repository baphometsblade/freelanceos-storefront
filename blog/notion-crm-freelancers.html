What Freelancers Actually Need from a CRM

Most freelancers use CRM software for managing warm inbound leads, nurturing ongoing relationships with a small number of valuable clients, and ensuring no one falls through the cracks. The feature requirements are much simpler than enterprise CRM:

  • Lead pipeline — a visual board showing where every prospect is from first contact to signed contract
  • Client records — a database of active and past clients with contact details, budget, and relationship notes
  • Last contact tracking — knowing when you last spoke to each person so relationships do not go cold
  • Deal value — estimated project value for each lead so you can prioritize which ones to chase
  • Next action — one clear field: what is the next thing you need to do for this deal?
  • Invoice history — total billed, outstanding balance, and payment history linked directly to the client record

5-Step Notion CRM Setup Guide

  1. Create your Client database. One row per contact. Add these properties: Name (title), Status (Select: Lead / Prospect / Active / Past / Lost), Email (Email type), Industry (Select), Deal Value (Number, currency format), Last Contact (Date), Next Action (Text), Source (Select: Referral / Inbound / Cold Outreach / Other).
  2. Build your pipeline board view. In your Client database, create a Board view grouped by Status. You now have a visual Kanban pipeline: Lead to Prospect to Active to Past Client. Drag and drop contacts as they progress through the stages.
  3. Set up last contact reminders. Create a "Days Since Contact" formula: dateBetween(now(), prop("Last Contact"), "days"). Create a filtered view "Needs Follow-Up" showing clients where Days Since Contact is greater than 30. Review this view weekly.
  4. Add communication notes to each record. Every database record in Notion is also a full page. Use this as a running log of every meaningful conversation. This context is invaluable when you pick up a relationship after a break.
  5. Connect your Client database to Invoice and Project databases. Add Relation properties linking to your Invoice and Project databases. Now every client record shows their complete project history and invoice history automatically.
Weekly CRM ritual (10 minutes): Every Monday, open your Pipeline board and move any deals that progressed. Open Needs Follow-Up and reach out to anyone who has gone quiet. Create next-action items for your three highest-value open leads.

The Essential Views for Your Pipeline

Pipeline Board (Group by Status)

Your default view for prospecting and deal management. Drag contacts from Lead to Prospect when they respond, from Prospect to Active when they sign a contract, from Active to Past when the engagement concludes.

Active Clients (Filtered)

Filter: Status = Active. Sort by Last Contact ascending (the client you have spoken to least recently is at the top). Aim to touch base with every active client at least once every two to three weeks.

Revenue Forecast (Gallery or Table)

Show all contacts with Status = Lead or Prospect. Display the Deal Value property. The sum of these values is your near-term revenue forecast. Compare to your current monthly revenue target to see whether you need to fill the pipeline.

Connecting Your CRM to Invoices and Projects

The biggest limitation of standalone CRM tools for freelancers is the separation between sales data and financial data. In Notion, everything lives in the same workspace and can be connected with Relation properties.

Once your Client database relates to both Invoice and Project databases, each client record becomes a complete financial and operational history: active projects, past projects, all invoices ever sent, current outstanding balance, whether a proposal is outstanding, whether a contract is pending. This connected view is what makes a Notion CRM genuinely more powerful than most paid tools for the freelance use case.
