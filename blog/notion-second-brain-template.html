The PARA System Explained

PARA stands for Projects, Areas, Resources, and Archives. It's a four-category system for organizing every piece of information in your life. The key insight is that everything you save is either actively relevant to something you're working on right now, or it's reference material for later. PARA separates those two things clearly.

Category What It Is Example

The most common mistake when building a Second Brain is treating it like a filing cabinet — dumping everything into nested folders and never looking at it again. PARA avoids this by keeping Projects front and center. Everything active is visible. Everything inactive is archived.

Setting Up PARA in Notion

The cleanest Notion Second Brain uses four linked databases, not just four folders. Here's how to build it:

Projects database. Create a full-page database called "Projects." Properties: Name, Status (Active/Paused/Complete), Client (Relation to Client database), Deadline (Date), Goal, and Notes. This is your single source of truth for everything you're working on right now. Archive completed projects into the Archives database when they're done.

Areas database. Create a database called "Areas of Responsibility." Properties: Name, Category (Work/Personal/Health/Finance), Last Reviewed (Date), Notes. Areas don't have deadlines — they're things you maintain continuously. Examples: "Client relationships," "Tax records," "Health routines."

Resources database. Create a database called "Resources." Properties: Name, Topic (multi-select tags), Source (URL or reference), Date Saved, Summary. This is your knowledge library. Tag everything so you can filter by topic when you need it. A well-tagged resource library becomes one of the most valuable tools you have.

Archives database. One simple database with a Name, Original Category (Select: Project/Area/Resource), Archive Date, and Notes. When you complete a project or sunset a client, move it here. Don't delete it — archived items are invaluable as reference for future similar work.

The Capture Habit

A Second Brain only works if you actually capture things when you encounter them. The Notion Web Clipper browser extension is the most practical tool for this. When you read an article, a case study, or a research paper that's relevant to your work, clip it directly into your Resources database. Tag it immediately with 1–3 topic tags. Don't wait to process it perfectly — capture first, organize later.

On mobile, the Notion app's share sheet integration lets you save from Safari, Chrome, or any app directly to a Notion page. Set your default inbox as a "Quick Capture" page — a single page with a simple table where everything goes when you're on the go. Process it into the right database during your weekly review.

Progressive Summarization

Tiago Forte's technique for making captured notes actually useful is called Progressive Summarization. The idea is that you process notes in layers over time, rather than trying to write perfect summaries immediately. Here's how it works in practice:

  1. Layer 1 — Capture. Save the raw content. Don't edit it. Just get it into your Resources database with a tag.
  2. Layer 2 — Bold. The next time you open the note, bold the most important sentences. These are the things that jumped out at you as genuinely useful or interesting.
  3. Layer 3 — Highlight. On a later pass, highlight (or color) only the bolded sentences that are most relevant to your current work. This should be 10–20% of the original content.
  4. Layer 4 — Executive Summary. For your most important resources, write 2–3 sentences at the top of the note summarizing the core insight. This is what you'll actually read when you come back to this note in 6 months.

Most notes never get past Layer 2. That's fine. Progressive Summarization means you only invest deeper processing time in notes that prove themselves valuable over time.
