What Is a Weekly Review?

A weekly review is a structured, recurring process where you review everything that happened in the past week and plan intentionally for the week ahead. It's not the same as glancing at your to-do list on Sunday night or checking your calendar Monday morning. A proper weekly review is a complete clearing: email, open loops, project status, financial state, and forward planning all get addressed in a single, dedicated session.

The Origins: GTD's Weekly Review

David Allen introduced the weekly review in Getting Things Done (2001) as the linchpin of his productivity system. His argument: the human mind creates anxiety not from having too much to do, but from having open loops — uncommitted, unreviewed tasks floating in the back of your mind. The weekly review closes those loops. After a proper review, you've committed to what you're doing, what you're not doing, and what you're waiting on. The result is what Allen calls "mind like water" — a clear, calm state where you can respond appropriately to what's actually in front of you rather than worrying about what you might be forgetting.

Why Freelancers Need It More Than Anyone

Employed professionals have external accountability structures — managers, meetings, performance reviews, team standup. These external checkpoints force a minimum level of weekly awareness. Freelancers have none of that. There's no one checking whether you reviewed last week's deliverables or planned next week's revenue. Without a weekly review, freelancers tend to drift: reacting to client requests, procrastinating on business development, and ending each week uncertain whether they actually moved forward or just stayed busy.

The weekly review is the structure that replaces external accountability for solo operators. It's 45 minutes per week that has more impact on your freelance business than most other practices combined.

The 10-Step Notion Weekly Review Process

1

Clear Your Email Inbox to Zero

Start with email because it's the highest-density source of open loops. Every email requires exactly one of four actions: archive or delete (no action needed), respond now (if it takes less than 2 minutes), add to task list (if it requires more than 2 minutes to handle), or add to calendar (if it requires a scheduled action). The goal is a genuinely empty inbox — not "inbox zero" by archiving everything, but inbox zero by processing every message.

Inbox zero at the end of a weekly review signals that you've seen and processed every external input of the week. You're not carrying hidden open loops into next week.

2

Clear All Capture Inboxes

Your email isn't your only capture inbox. During the week, you accumulate inputs in many places: paper notes, voice memos, browser bookmark tabs you didn't process, apps you downloaded and need to evaluate, files on your desktop you haven't organized, Slack messages you starred but never acted on. In this step, you process everything: paper notes get typed into Notion, voice memos get transcribed or deleted, browser tabs get bookmarked to a reading list or closed, desktop clutter gets organized or deleted. Nothing carries over as an unnamed open loop.

3

Review Last Week's Tasks

In your Notion Tasks database, filter to "This Week" + Status is not "Done." For each uncompleted task: was it actually completed and just not marked? Mark it done. Is it still relevant? Reschedule it to next week. Is it no longer relevant? Delete it without guilt — your priorities changed. This step is the most honest part of the review. Your uncompleted task list is a mirror of your actual decision-making and time management over the past week.

4

Review Active Projects

For each project in your Projects database with status "Active," check: What is the current status? Is anything blocking progress? Has the deadline changed? Is the scope creeping? What is the single next action required to move this forward? This last question — the "next action" — is the most important. A project without a defined next action will stall. At the end of this step, every active project should have a clear next action assigned and scheduled for next week.

5

Review Client Commitments

For each active client: what deliverables are due this week or next? Any follow-ups you said you'd send? Any invoices that should go out? Any client who hasn't heard from you in a suspiciously long time? Review your communication history (Notion client pages should log key messages) and create tasks for any follow-ups needed. Client relationships decay without consistent, proactive communication — this step prevents silent drift.

6

Review Your Finance Database

Open your Finance database and check: any invoices marked "Sent" that haven't been paid within your terms? Any expenses from this week not yet logged? Any upcoming bills to schedule? Any income received that needs to be recorded? This doesn't need to be a full accounting session — 10 minutes of checking and logging keeps your financial records current and prevents the tax-time panic of reconstructing 12 months from memory.

7

Identify Three Wins

Before you look at what didn't happen, acknowledge what did. Write down three specific wins from this week. They don't need to be large. "Finished the Chen project homepage," "Sent my first cold pitch in three weeks," and "Stuck to deep work block every morning" are all legitimate wins. This step is frequently skipped by high-achievers who take accomplishments for granted and focus only on shortfalls. Skip it, and you'll create a review habit associated with self-criticism rather than growth — and you'll quit within a month.

8

Identify One Thing to Improve

After acknowledging wins, identify exactly one specific behavior to change next week. Not a list of everything wrong — one change. "Next week, I'll respond to client emails only at 10am and 4pm, not reactively throughout the day." One change, stated precisely, with the mechanism specified. Multiple changes create overwhelm and dilute focus. One change creates a clear target and, over 52 weeks, produces 52 compounding improvements to how you work.

9

Set Next Week's Top 3 Priorities

Based on your project review, client commitments, and business development priorities — what are the three most important things to accomplish next week? Not a full task list — three headline priorities. These become your Weekly Top 3, displayed prominently on your Notion dashboard. When competing demands appear during the week (and they will), your Top 3 serves as the decision framework: does this new thing matter more than my three commitments? Usually the answer is no.

10

Time Block Next Week

Open your calendar (or Notion time blocks database) and schedule specific time for your Top 3 priorities plus recurring commitments. A priority that isn't time-blocked is an intention, not a plan. If your Top 3 is "finish the Mendoza website, send three new pitches, and record the podcast" — block 6 hours for website work, 2 hours for pitching, and 2 hours for recording. The week becomes a plan, not a prayer.

Creating Your Weekly Review Template

Inside the Weekly Review database, create a template that pre-populates the structure of each review. The template page should contain:

  • A checkboxes section with all 10 review steps listed — check them off as you complete each
  • A heading "📬 Email + Inbox" with space for notes on what you processed
  • A heading "📋 Task Review" with a linked database showing last week's incomplete tasks
  • A heading "🏗️ Projects" with a linked database showing active projects
  • A heading "👥 Client Check-In" with the active client list linked
  • A heading "💰 Finance" with outstanding invoices linked
  • A heading "🏆 Wins This Week" with a text field for 3 wins
  • A heading "🔧 One Improvement" with a text field for next week's behavior change
  • A heading "🎯 Next Week's Top 3" with numbered list
  • A heading "📅 Time Blocking" with a linked view of next week's calendar

When you click "New" in your Weekly Review database, this entire structure appears instantly. Your review session starts with a clear agenda — you're not figuring out what to review, you're just executing the process.

The Power of Looking Back at 52 Weeks of Reviews

After a year of consistent weekly reviews, you have a 52-entry database of your working life. Filter it, sort it, and analyze it. The patterns you'll find are often surprising:

  • Rating trends: Which weeks consistently rated 5 stars? What was different about those weeks — lighter client load, more deep work, better sleep? The conditions for your best weeks become reproducible.
  • Top 3 completion rate: What percentage of weeks did you actually accomplish all three priorities? Honest answer: most people complete all three in fewer than half their weeks. The gap between Top 3 stated and Top 3 completed is a measurement of how well you estimate your capacity.
  • Revenue trends: Week-by-week revenue data reveals seasonal patterns you'd never notice in a monthly or quarterly view. Some weeks are structurally low-revenue (conference weeks, holiday weeks) — knowing this lets you plan buffer income in advance.
  • Recurring blockers: If "waiting on client feedback" appears in your project review notes 20 times over the year, you have a systematic client communication problem — not a one-off issue. Your weekly reviews surface patterns invisible in day-to-day work.

When the Weekly Review Breaks Down (And How to Fix It)

Problem: The Review Takes 2+ Hours

Fix: You're over-reviewing. The weekly review is not the time for deep project planning, difficult client conversations, or strategic business decisions. It's a processing and planning session. If you find yourself spending an hour on one item, you've left the weekly review and entered a different work mode. Time-box the entire review to 60 minutes maximum. Set a timer. Stop when it ends.

Problem: The Review Feels Like Self-Punishment

Fix: You're focusing disproportionately on what didn't happen instead of what did. Require yourself to write three wins before you're allowed to look at incomplete tasks. The wins section is not optional — it's the psychological fuel that makes the review sustainable long-term. A review that feels bad every week gets skipped within a month.

Problem: You Skip the Review When Busy

Fix: Counterintuitively, busy weeks are the most critical ones to review. When you're overwhelmed, the weekly review prevents you from carrying unexamined open loops into the next week, which compounds the overwhelm. Designate a 30-minute "abbreviated review" for busy weeks: just Steps 3, 4, 9, and 10. Four steps, 30 minutes, most of the value.

Problem: Review Without Action

Fix: Make Step 10 (time blocking) mandatory, not optional. A review that doesn't end with next week's calendar at least partially blocked has left the planning abstract. Concrete time blocks are the bridge between review and execution. If you skip time blocking, you've planned beautifully and will execute haphazardly.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a Notion weekly review?
A Notion weekly review is a structured, recurring process done inside Notion to review completed tasks, clear capture inboxes, identify wins and areas to improve, and plan the following week with specific priorities and time blocks. It typically takes 30–60 minutes and is done on Friday afternoon or Sunday evening. The power comes from doing it consistently — weekly entries compound into a searchable record of your professional growth.
How long should a weekly review take?
30–60 minutes is the ideal range. The first few reviews may run up to 90 minutes while you're building the habit and setting up your databases. Set a timer for 45 minutes and work within it. If you find yourself going over regularly, you're either over-reviewing or using the session for deep project planning (which belongs in a separate session).
What is the best time to do a weekly review?
Friday afternoon is popular because the week is fresh in your mind and you can start Monday with a clear plan. Sunday evening is also effective and ensures each week starts with intentional priorities rather than inbox firefighting. The most important factor is consistency — doing the review at the same time each week, whatever time that is, builds a habit that sticks.
