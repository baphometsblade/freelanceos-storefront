2. What Makes a Great Notion Template

With thousands of Notion templates available across the marketplace, the ability to evaluate quality quickly is a genuine skill. Here are the five criteria that separate excellent templates from mediocre ones, plus the red flags that tell you a template is not worth your time.

The Five Criteria for a Great Notion Template

1. Immediately useful without setup. A great template should be usable within fifteen minutes of duplicating it. If you need to spend two hours customising properties, deleting irrelevant fields, and trying to figure out what the creator intended, the template has failed a basic usability test. The best templates ship with a small amount of realistic sample data so you can see the system working before you clear it and add your own data.

2. Sensible properties, not property overload. More properties is not better. The most common template failure mode is a database with twenty-five properties, most of which you will never fill in. Great templates include exactly the properties a typical user needs and nothing more. If you find yourself thinking you would never fill in a particular field, that is a sign the template was not designed by someone who actually used it every day.

3. Helpful views pre-configured. Notion databases are powerful precisely because you can view the same data in completely different ways. Board view for status. Calendar view for deadlines. Gallery view for visual content. Filtered list views for specific contexts. A great template ships with the most useful views already configured, named sensibly, and filtered appropriately. You should not need to build your own views from scratch on day one.

4. Clear naming conventions. Consistency in naming matters more than most people realise. If some databases use "Status" and others use "Stage" or "Phase" for the same concept, navigating across a multi-database workspace becomes cognitively taxing. Great templates use consistent terminology throughout. One convention for statuses, one for relation field names, one for date properties.

5. Works on mobile. A surprising number of Notion templates are designed exclusively with desktop in mind. In 2026, a significant portion of Notion usage happens on mobile devices. Templates should be tested on mobile, and the most frequently accessed views should be navigable on a phone screen without excessive horizontal scrolling or truncated content.

Red Flags in Notion Templates

  • Too many linked databases. Five or six cross-linked databases sounds impressive, but most users will find it overwhelming and unsustainable. The systems that people actually maintain long-term are simple enough to use without consulting a diagram of the relations every time.
  • No instructions page. If a template ships without a dedicated guide explaining what each section is for and how to use it, the creator either does not care about the user experience or built the template in one afternoon. Either way, it is a warning sign.
  • Properties no one will ever fill in. Priority fields that never get changed from the default. Owner fields in a personal workspace. Tags that only ever get one value assigned. These signal that the template was built to look comprehensive in screenshots rather than to be used in practice.
  • Gallery view with no cover images configured. Gallery view looks beautiful in marketing screenshots and terrible in reality if there is no system for adding cover images. Templates that default to gallery view without a clear image pipeline are optimising for the screenshot, not the actual user experience.

4. Free vs Paid Templates Comparison

One of the most common questions from people new to Notion templates is whether it is worth paying for a template when so many free options exist. The honest answer is: it depends on what you need and how seriously you plan to use the system.

Free templates are genuinely excellent for getting started, experimenting with a new use case, or covering a clear single-purpose need. A free habit tracker or book notes system is perfectly adequate. Where free templates consistently fall short is in complexity, instructions quality, and the cross-database linking that makes a workspace feel like a coherent integrated system rather than a collection of independent pages that happen to live in the same Notion workspace.

Paid templates, particularly those sold as suites, typically ship with significantly better documentation, video walkthroughs, and ongoing updates as Notion adds features. More importantly, they often include the inter-database relations that make a workspace genuinely powerful: your clients linking to your projects, your projects linking to your invoices, your invoices rolling up to your income tracker. Building those connections yourself from a collection of free individual templates is a significant amount of work and requires a solid understanding of Notion's relation and rollup features.

6. Building a Second Brain in 2026

The second brain concept has been around long enough to be thoroughly tested against the reality of how people actually work. Here is what is genuinely working in 2026, informed by years of community iteration on the original PARA framework and the arrival of Notion AI as a meaningful productivity layer that changes what a second brain can do.

The PARA Method: Still the Most Practical Foundation

Tiago Forte's PARA framework remains the most practical organisational structure for a digital knowledge system. Its defining insight is that information should be organised by actionability rather than by topic or type. A document lives where it is most immediately useful, not where it thematically belongs. This single distinction eliminates most of the confusion that plagues alternative approaches.

Projects

Active commitments with a defined outcome and a real end date. Current client work, ongoing goals with deadlines, specific initiatives with milestones. If it does not have an end, it is probably an Area.

Areas

Ongoing responsibilities without a defined end date. Health, finances, relationships, professional development, business operations. Things you maintain over time rather than complete and close.

Resources

Reference material organised by topic for future use. Saved articles, research notes, how-to guides, templates, inspiration collections. The library you pull from when working on Projects.

Archive

Inactive items from any other category. Completed projects, paused areas, outdated resources. Archived rather than deleted so the information is retrievable if the situation changes.

The Three Daily Actions That Make It Work

The second brain system only functions if it is used consistently. Strip away the theory and the elaborate database structures, and what makes the difference between a system that works and a sophisticated collection of pages you never look at comes down to three daily actions.

Capture. Anything potentially useful goes into the inbox immediately. Articles worth reading, ideas that surface in the shower, tasks that appear in conversations, quotes from books, insights from calls. The capture action has no quality filter and no organisational requirement. The goal is zero friction and zero mental overhead in the moment of capture. Evaluation and filing come later.

Process. On a daily or weekly cadence, review the inbox and make a deliberate decision about each item. Does this require action? It becomes a task. Is it reference material worth keeping? It goes into Resources under the relevant topic. Does it relate to an active project? It moves there. Is it not actually useful on reflection? Delete it without guilt. Processing is the discipline that keeps the system clean and functional rather than becoming a digital attic.

Create. The entire point of capturing and organising information is to produce better work from it more efficiently. When writing, designing, planning, or making decisions, pull from the second brain rather than starting research from scratch each time. The output of a consistently maintained second brain is faster, higher-quality work built on accumulated knowledge. The compounding effect becomes tangible after three to six months of consistent use.
