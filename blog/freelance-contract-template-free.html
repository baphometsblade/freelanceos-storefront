Free Freelance Contract Template: The 8 Clauses Every Contract Needs (2026)

Protect yourself from scope creep, non-payment, and disputes — with a contract that's clear, professional, and easy to use

January 2026  ·  13 min read

Freelancers who work without contracts eventually pay for it — sometimes with weeks of unpaid work, sometimes with a legal dispute that costs more to resolve than the project was worth, always with stress that a signed agreement would have prevented entirely. A written contract isn't just legal protection; it's a clarity tool that forces both sides to agree on exactly what's being done, for how much, and under what conditions, before a single hour of work begins.

This guide covers the eight clauses every freelance contract must include, the most common red flags to watch for, and how to send and sign contracts efficiently. At the bottom is a link to generate your own contract for free in minutes using the FreelanceOS Contract Wizard.

Why Every Freelancer Needs a Written Contract

Without a contract, you're operating on a handshake — and handshakes have a way of meaning different things to different people. The client who seemed perfectly aligned during the sales conversation may have a completely different understanding of what "finished" looks like, how many revision rounds are included, and what happens if they change direction halfway through.

Consider the pattern that repeats constantly: a freelancer completes a project to spec, the client requests extensive revisions they claim are covered under the project fee, the freelancer has no documentation showing how many rounds were agreed upon, and now they're working unpaid extra hours or having a tense argument that damages the relationship. A single sentence in a contract — "This project includes two rounds of revisions; additional rounds are billed at $X/hour" — eliminates the entire dispute.

The other dangerous scenario is non-payment. Freelancers lose thousands of dollars every year to clients who simply stop responding after work is delivered. Without a contract specifying payment terms, the legal path to recovery is expensive and uncertain. With a contract, you have a document that establishes what was owed, when it was due, and what the late fee structure is — the foundation for a small claims case or collections if it comes to that.

Contracts also establish you as a professional. Experienced clients expect contractors to use contracts. Presenting one signals that you operate a real business, not a side hustle, and that you've done this before.

The 8 Essential Clauses — What to Include and Why

Clause 1

Scope of Work

This is the most important clause and the one most often written too vaguely. The scope should describe exactly what you will deliver — and explicitly state what is NOT included. If you're building a website, does it include copywriting? SEO optimization? Ongoing maintenance? The scope defines the boundary of your obligation. Anything not explicitly listed in scope is out of scope, and additional work costs more.

Tip: Write scope in terms of deliverables, not hours. "A 5-page website including Home, About, Services, Blog, and Contact pages with responsive design" is clearer and more defensible than "approximately 40 hours of web development work."

Clause 2

Payment Terms

Specify: the total project fee, the payment schedule (e.g., 50% deposit on signing, 50% on delivery), the accepted payment methods, and the late fee structure. A standard late fee is 1.5% per month on overdue balances. For projects over $2,000, a net-15 or net-30 payment term is reasonable; for smaller projects, due on delivery or within 7 days is appropriate.

Never begin a project without a deposit. A 25–50% upfront payment filters out low-commitment clients, covers your initial time investment, and gives you legal standing if the engagement ends early. Clients who refuse to pay a deposit are a meaningful red flag.

Clause 3

Revision Limits

Define what constitutes a "revision" and how many rounds are included. A revision is typically defined as changes to the existing direction — not a change in direction, which is new work. "This project includes two rounds of consolidated revisions. Additional revision rounds are billed at [hourly rate]/hour." Without this clause, clients can request unlimited changes under the assumption that revisions are unlimited and free. This single clause eliminates one of the most common freelance disputes.

Clause 4

Kill Fee

A kill fee governs what happens if the client cancels the project mid-stream. Without one, you may deliver significant work and receive nothing if a client's budget gets cut or they change priorities. A standard kill fee structure: the client owes payment for all work completed to date plus a cancellation fee (typically 25–50% of the remaining project fee). The deposit is non-refundable in all circumstances. This clause is not punitive — it's protection for work you've already done in good faith.

Clause 5

Intellectual Property

IP clauses answer the question: who owns what, and when? The standard for most project work is that the client receives full ownership of deliverables upon final payment. Until payment clears, you retain ownership. This gives you meaningful leverage if a client uses your work before paying — they're technically infringing your IP. Also specify: the client grants you a license to display the work in your portfolio, unless confidentiality prevents it. This is a standard ask and most clients accept it readily.

Clause 6

Confidentiality / NDA

You will often receive access to sensitive business information during a project — internal strategy, financial data, unreleased products, customer lists. A confidentiality clause commits you to keeping that information private. It also protects you: if the client has asked you to sign their own NDA separately, having this clause in your own contract as well clarifies expectations from both directions. Keep it simple: "Both parties agree to keep confidential any non-public information shared in connection with this engagement."

Clause 7

Termination Clause

Either party should be able to end the engagement with reasonable notice — typically 7 to 14 days for project work, 30 days for ongoing retainers. The termination clause should specify the notice period, what happens to work in progress (you deliver everything completed to date, client pays for all completed work), and the kill fee if applicable. Without a termination clause, ending an engagement cleanly is legally ambiguous.

Clause 8

Governing Law and Dispute Resolution

Specify which state or country's law governs the contract and how disputes will be resolved. For most freelancers, this means: "This agreement is governed by the laws of [Your State]. Any disputes will first be attempted through good-faith negotiation; if unresolved, disputes will be settled through binding arbitration in [City, State]." Arbitration is faster and cheaper than court for small disputes. This clause also establishes that you — not the client — choose the jurisdiction, which matters if you're doing cross-state or international work.

Red Flags: Clients Who Refuse to Sign Contracts

Legitimate businesses sign contracts routinely. Contracts are normal in professional services; they protect both parties, clarify expectations, and are standard practice in every agency, consulting firm, and law office. A client who resists signing a contract is communicating something about how they plan to behave when things get difficult — and things always get at least a little difficult.

Common excuses for refusing a contract: "We work on trust," "Our usual vendors don't need contracts," "This is just a small project," "Legal will take too long." None of these are valid reasons to proceed without written agreement. The smaller the project, the simpler the contract needs to be — a one-page scope, payment terms, and signature takes 15 minutes to prepare.

Other red flags to watch for before you sign: scope described in vague terms without being willing to be specific, requests to start before contract or deposit, pressure to reduce your rate significantly from the initial ask, and reluctance to discuss what happens if things don't go as planned.

How to Send and Sign Contracts for Free

You don't need a lawyer or expensive software to get a contract signed. Several free tools make the process quick and legally binding:

  • DocuSign: The industry standard. Free plan allows 3 sends per month — plenty for most freelancers starting out.
  • HelloSign (now Dropbox Sign): Clean interface, free plan for 3 signature requests per month. Legally binding in most jurisdictions.
  • Adobe Acrobat Sign: Built into Adobe's ecosystem. Free tier available; links well if you already use Adobe tools.
  • PDF with typed signature: For low-stakes, low-value projects, a PDF with the client's typed name and the date in an email thread can be sufficient evidence of agreement. This is not as clean as a formal e-signature, but it's better than nothing.
  • Notion contract + email confirmation: Share a Notion page with the contract and request a reply email confirming agreement. Again, not ideal, but legitimate documentation.

The Contract Conversation — How to Bring It Up Naturally

Some freelancers feel awkward introducing a contract because they worry it signals distrust. It doesn't — it signals professionalism. The key is presenting the contract as a normal part of your process, not a defensive reaction to the specific client.

After a client verbally agrees to move forward, send a confirmation email: "Great — I'll put together a project agreement covering scope, timeline, payment terms, and IP. Once that's signed and the deposit is received, I can [start / schedule our kickoff call]. I'll have it over to you within 24 hours." This frames the contract as part of your standard process — because it should be.

If a client asks "do we really need a contract?", a confident answer: "I use contracts for every project — they protect us both. It clearly defines what's in scope so there are no surprises on either side. It's standard practice and it only takes a few minutes to review."

When to Hire a Lawyer vs. Use a Template

For most standard freelance projects — design, development, writing, marketing, consulting — a well-written template contract is entirely sufficient. Lawyers become valuable for: large contracts over $50,000; work involving significant IP development (software products, patents, unique creative IP); equity compensation arrangements; non-compete or exclusivity clauses; or any situation where the legal risk to either party is substantial.

If a client sends you their own contract to sign, have a lawyer review it before signing if: it contains non-compete clauses (could restrict your ability to work with other clients), it claims broad IP rights over everything you create while under contract, it includes indemnification clauses that could expose you to liability, or it significantly alters standard payment, termination, or revision terms to your disadvantage.
