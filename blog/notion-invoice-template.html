The Best Free Notion Invoice Template for Freelancers (2026)

Published May 2026  ·  Last updated: May 2026  ·  5 min read

Every freelancer has a version of the same problem: invoices scattered across three different apps, a spreadsheet that stopped getting updated in February, and a vague sense that someone out there owes you money but you're not sure exactly how much. It sounds like a minor inconvenience. It isn't.

Chasing unpaid invoices is one of the biggest sources of cash flow problems for freelancers. The average freelancer loses an estimated 13% of annual invoiced revenue to late payments — and most of that comes down to poor tracking, not deadbeat clients. When you don't have a system that makes overdue invoices impossible to miss, they slip through.

The fix isn't complicated. A well-built Notion invoice tracker solves this for most freelancers in about 15 minutes of setup. Here's exactly what it needs to include, and where to get one for free.

Why Spreadsheets Fail for Invoice Tracking

Spreadsheets are where freelancers start, and understandably so. They're free, familiar, and flexible. But invoice tracking in a spreadsheet has a fundamental problem: it's disconnected from everything else.

When your invoice list is a separate file from your client list, which is separate from your project tracker, you're constantly copying and pasting data between them. You rename a client in one place and forget to update it in three others. You want to see "all unpaid invoices for clients I'm actively working with" — but that requires combining two different spreadsheets, which you never get around to doing.

The other problem is visibility. In a spreadsheet, overdue invoices look exactly the same as paid ones unless you've remembered to color-code them or sort by date. There's no automatic flag, no filter that surfaces "these five invoices are now 30+ days past due." You have to manually hunt for what you're owed.

Notion solves both problems with its relational database system. Your invoices can link directly to your clients and projects. Filter views surface exactly what you need — outstanding invoices, overdue ones, invoices by client. And rollup fields automatically calculate how much each client owes you without any manual math.

What a Proper Notion Invoice System Includes

A solid Notion invoice tracker isn't just a database of invoice numbers and amounts. The real power comes from how it connects to the rest of your workspace. Here's what it needs:

Core Invoice Properties

  • Invoice number — sequential, easy to reference in client conversations
  • Client relation — linked to your Client database, not just a text field
  • Project relation — linked to the specific project this invoice covers
  • Amount — formatted as currency
  • Status — Draft, Sent, Paid, Overdue (a Select property with color coding)
  • Date sent — when you sent it
  • Date due — when payment is expected
  • Date paid — when you actually received payment

Calculated and Rollup Fields

  • Days outstanding — a formula: if unpaid, calculates days since sent
  • Total billed per client — a rollup on the Client database showing total invoice amounts
  • Total outstanding — filtered sum of all unpaid invoices

Filter Views That Make It Useful

  • Unpaid invoices — everything with status Draft, Sent, or Overdue
  • Overdue — date due is before today AND status is not Paid
  • This month's income — status is Paid AND date paid is within current month
  • By client — grouped view showing invoice history per client

The combination of these views means you can check your financial position in 30 seconds. Friday afternoon: open "Overdue" view, follow up on anything that's there. End of month: open "This month's income" to confirm your cash position.

Pro tip: Set the Status property to automatically sort so "Overdue" items appear at the top of your default view. In Notion, go to Sort > Status > Ascending, and set your Select options in this order: Overdue, Sent, Draft, Paid. Overdue invoices will always surface first without any manual sorting.

Building It Yourself vs. Using a Template

You can build this from scratch in Notion. It takes roughly 2 hours if you're comfortable with Notion's database and formula system, or considerably longer if you're not. The tricky parts are:

  • Setting up the bi-directional relations between Invoices, Projects, and Clients correctly
  • Writing the formula for days outstanding (it needs to handle null dates gracefully)
  • Configuring rollup fields on the Client database to aggregate invoice totals
  • Building filter views for each use case without breaking the underlying structure

For most freelancers, the better move is to start with a pre-built template, use it for a few weeks to understand what you actually need, and then customize. The logic is already correct; you just duplicate and fill in your data.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Notion send invoices to clients?
No, Notion cannot send invoices directly. It is a workspace tool, not invoicing software. What Notion does exceptionally well is tracking invoices — their status, amounts, due dates, and which client and project they belong to. For sending invoices, use a dedicated tool like Wave (free) or FreshBooks alongside your Notion tracker.
