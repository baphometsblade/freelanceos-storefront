Freelance Health Insurance in 2026: What You Need to Know (And Afford)

The health insurance cliff is one of the scariest parts of going freelance. Here's what your options are — across the US, UK, Australia, and Canada — and how to budget for it.

June 2026  ·  14 min read
Home › Blog › Freelance Health Insurance Guide
Important Disclaimer

This article provides general educational information about health insurance options for freelancers. It is not insurance or financial advice. Health insurance rules, options, and costs vary significantly by country, state/province, and individual circumstances. Always consult a licensed insurance broker or advisor for personalized recommendations. Tax-related information should be verified with a qualified tax professional.

The health insurance cliff is real. When you leave a traditional job, you often leave behind employer-sponsored health insurance — typically the most affordable coverage you'll ever have, because your employer pays a large portion of the premium. Going freelance means taking on that cost yourself, often for the first time. For many people, this is the scariest part of making the leap.

The good news: there are more options than most people realize, and costs are often more manageable than the horror stories suggest — especially if you know how to navigate the system in your country. This guide covers the major options for US, UK, Australian, and Canadian freelancers, plus how to plan and track health costs in Notion.

The Health Insurance Cliff: Why It Scares People

When you're employed, your employer typically covers 70-80% of your health insurance premium. The amount you see deducted from your paycheck is the remaining 20-30%. When you go freelance, you pay 100% of the premium. This often means the monthly cost triples or quadruples compared to what you paid as an employee — not because the insurance got more expensive, but because you're now seeing the full cost for the first time.

For context: a typical individual health insurance plan in the US costs $400–600/month before subsidies. With ACA subsidies, this can drop significantly based on income. Understanding your subsidy eligibility is the most important single step US freelancers can take to manage health insurance costs.

US Freelancers: Your Health Insurance Options

🇺🇸
United States
ACA Marketplace (Healthcare.gov)

The ACA Marketplace (Healthcare.gov for federal plans, state exchanges for states that run their own) offers income-based subsidies — called Premium Tax Credits — that can significantly reduce your monthly premium. Freelancers with income between 100% and 400% of the federal poverty level (FPL) may qualify for substantial subsidies. Importantly: the subsidy is based on your Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI) for the year, not your invoiced revenue. As a freelancer, this is your net self-employment income after deducting business expenses. Open enrollment runs from November 1 to January 15. If you lose job-based coverage (e.g., by leaving a full-time job), you qualify for a Special Enrollment Period — typically 60 days from the qualifying event.

Spouse or Partner Employer Coverage

If your partner has employer-sponsored health insurance, joining their plan as a dependent is often the most affordable option available to you. Many employer plans allow adding a spouse or domestic partner at significantly lower cost than marketplace plans. This is worth calculating carefully: compare the marginal cost of adding you to your partner's plan versus the cost of your own marketplace plan (after subsidies). The math often favors the employer plan.

COBRA (Bridge Coverage)

COBRA allows you to continue your former employer's health insurance for up to 18 months after leaving a job. The coverage is identical to what you had — the catch is price. Under COBRA, you pay the full premium (what you paid plus what your employer paid) plus a 2% administrative fee. This typically makes COBRA significantly more expensive than marketplace alternatives. However, COBRA can be useful for a short bridge period, especially if you're mid-treatment and don't want to switch plans, or if you qualify for strong subsidies starting next year but need coverage for a few months now.

Health Share Plans

Health sharing ministries (Sedera, Liberty HealthShare, Medishare) are not insurance — they're membership-based programs where members share each other's medical costs. Monthly costs are typically 30-50% lower than marketplace plans, and there's no enrollment period restriction. Important caveats: health shares are not regulated the same way insurance is, which means they can change rules or deny claims in ways that regulated insurance cannot. Pre-existing conditions may be excluded. They typically require members to share certain values or lifestyle commitments. Research carefully and understand you are not purchasing insurance. Health shares work for some freelancers; they fail others catastrophically. Consult an independent broker before choosing.

Freelancers Union

The Freelancers Union offers group health insurance access to US-based members in some states. Availability and plans vary by location. Membership in the Freelancers Union is free — check their website for plan availability in your state and compare to marketplace options.

Health Savings Accounts (HSA) — The Tax-Advantaged Tool

If you choose a qualifying High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP) — a plan with lower monthly premiums but higher out-of-pocket costs before coverage kicks in — you become eligible to open a Health Savings Account (HSA). For freelancers who are generally healthy and want to minimize monthly premium costs, an HSA-paired HDHP can be an effective strategy.

The three tax advantages of an HSA:

  1. Contributions are tax-deductible: Money you put into your HSA reduces your taxable income dollar-for-dollar. As a self-employed person, this reduces both income tax and self-employment tax.
  2. Growth is tax-free: Many HSA providers allow you to invest your HSA balance in funds — and the growth is not taxed.
  3. Withdrawals for medical expenses are tax-free: Use HSA funds for qualifying medical expenses (doctor visits, prescriptions, dental, vision, many OTC medications) with no tax owed.

2024 contribution limits: $4,150 for individual coverage, $8,300 for family coverage (both increase slightly each year). Unused funds roll over indefinitely — unlike Flexible Spending Accounts (FSAs), HSA money is never "use it or lose it." Many freelancers treat their HSA as a dedicated healthcare emergency fund, contributing maximum amounts and investing the balance for growth.

Key Decision

Choosing between a lower-premium HDHP (with HSA access) vs. a higher-premium plan with lower deductibles depends on your expected medical usage. If you're generally healthy and primarily want protection against catastrophic costs, an HDHP + HSA often wins on total cost. If you have regular prescriptions or planned medical procedures, a higher-premium plan with lower out-of-pocket costs may save money overall. Use a healthcare cost calculator and consult a licensed broker for personalized analysis.

UK Freelancers: NHS and Private Options

🇬🇧
United Kingdom

UK freelancers receive NHS coverage regardless of employment status. Your National Insurance contributions (Class 2: £3.45/week flat rate; Class 4: 9% on profits between the lower and upper profit limits) fund your access to NHS services. You do not need separate health insurance to access GP services, emergency care, or most NHS treatments.

NHS Coverage (All UK Residents)

NHS provides: GP visits, emergency care, mental health services, most specialist referrals, and the vast majority of healthcare needs — at no direct cost at point of use. For most UK freelancers, NHS coverage is completely adequate for routine healthcare needs. Prescription charges apply in England (currently £9.90 per item) unless you qualify for exemptions; free in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland.

Private Health Insurance (Optional)

Private health insurance in the UK provides: faster access to specialists without the NHS waiting list, private hospital rooms, access to treatments or procedures not available on NHS, and some mental health services with shorter waiting times. Major UK private health insurers: AXA Health, Bupa, Vitality, Aviva, Cigna. Monthly premiums for a basic individual policy typically range from £30–80 depending on age, coverage level, and chosen excess (deductible). Many freelancers purchase private health insurance for the speed benefit — reducing waiting times for specialist appointments from months to days.

Australian Freelancers: Medicare and Extras

🇦🇺
Australia

Australian freelancers are covered by Medicare regardless of employment status. The Medicare levy (2% of taxable income) funds this coverage. Medicare covers GP visits, public hospital care, and most specialist appointments on a bulk-billing or partially subsidized basis.

Medicare (All Australian Residents)

Medicare covers: GP consultations (bulk-billed or partially covered), public hospital care, specialist appointments (with referral), diagnostic tests, and most surgical procedures. Australian freelancers are in a fortunate position: substantial healthcare is available regardless of employment or income status, funded through the Medicare levy.

Private Health Insurance — Hospital Cover

Private hospital cover allows you to be treated as a private patient in a public or private hospital — giving you choice of specialist and private room. It also reduces your Medicare Levy Surcharge if your income exceeds $93,000 (individual) or $186,000 (family). Basic hospital policies start from around $80–100/month for individuals.

Extras Cover (Dental, Optical, Physio)

Extras cover pays for services Medicare doesn't: dental, optical, physiotherapy, chiropractic, psychology, and other allied health services. These are the "extras" most Australians buy private insurance for. Extras-only policies start from around $30–60/month. The value of extras cover depends entirely on how much you use these services — run the numbers based on your actual usage.

Lifetime Health Cover (LHC) Loading

Important for younger Australian freelancers: if you don't take out private hospital cover before your 31st birthday, you pay a 2% loading on top of your hospital insurance premium for every year you're over 30 when you first take out cover. This loading applies for 10 continuous years of coverage. Taking out hospital cover before 31 avoids this loading permanently.

Canadian Freelancers: Provincial Coverage and Supplemental Insurance

🇨🇦
Canada

Canadian freelancers are covered by their provincial or territorial health insurance plan, which covers most medically necessary services regardless of employment status. Coverage details vary by province.

Provincial Healthcare (All Provinces)

Provincial health plans cover: doctor visits, specialist care, hospital stays, diagnostic tests, and emergency services. Most provinces require enrollment when you become a resident; some have waiting periods for new residents. As a self-employed person, you continue to be covered — provincial healthcare is not employment-linked.

Supplemental Insurance for Extras

Provincial plans don't cover dental, vision, prescription drugs (in most provinces), physiotherapy, or mental health counseling. Canadian freelancers often purchase supplemental insurance to cover these gaps. Options: individual supplemental plans through insurers like Sun Life, Manulife, or Blue Cross, or group plans through Chamber of Commerce membership (available in most provinces — a popular option for small business owners and self-employed individuals). Group plans through professional associations may also be available in your field.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do freelancers need health insurance?
Whether freelancers need health insurance depends on your country. In the US, you're not legally required to have it, but going uninsured is a significant financial risk — a single hospitalization can cost tens of thousands of dollars without coverage. In the UK, Australia, and Canada, public healthcare systems provide baseline coverage regardless of employment status. In most countries, the question for freelancers isn't whether to have healthcare, but how much supplemental private coverage to add on top of public healthcare.
How do freelancers get health insurance in the US?
US freelancers have several options: (1) ACA Marketplace plans at Healthcare.gov — income-based subsidies can significantly reduce premiums. Open enrollment is November 1 to January 15. (2) Spouse or partner employer coverage. (3) Health Share Plans — not insurance, but lower-cost alternatives with significant risks. (4) COBRA — continuation coverage from a former employer, typically expensive but useful as a short-term bridge. (5) Freelancers Union group plans in some states. Consult a licensed insurance broker for personalized advice.
What is a Health Savings Account (HSA) for freelancers?
An HSA is a tax-advantaged savings account available to people enrolled in a qualifying High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP). Triple tax benefits: contributions are tax-deductible, growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free. 2024 limits: $4,150 individual, $8,300 family. Unused funds roll over indefinitely. Consult a tax advisor for guidance on your specific situation.
What is health insurance for freelancers in the UK?
UK freelancers receive the same NHS coverage as any other resident — employment status has no bearing on NHS access. Private health insurance is optional and provides faster specialist access, private hospital rooms, and some treatments not available on NHS. Major UK private insurers: AXA Health, Bupa, Vitality. Individual basic plans typically start around £30–80/month.
How does health insurance work for freelancers in Australia?
Medicare provides universal baseline coverage for Australian freelancers regardless of employment status. Private health insurance is optional and covers extras not included in Medicare: dental, optical, physiotherapy, and private hospital care. Important: taking out private hospital cover before age 31 avoids the Lifetime Health Cover loading. Consult a licensed insurance broker for advice specific to your situation.
What is the ACA Marketplace for freelancers?
The ACA Marketplace is where US self-employed individuals purchase health insurance plans with income-based subsidies (Premium Tax Credits). Open enrollment: November 1 to January 15. Plans are categorized as Bronze, Silver, Gold, and Platinum based on cost-sharing. Visit healthcare.gov or consult a licensed broker to estimate your subsidy eligibility.
How do I estimate income for health insurance subsidies as a freelancer?
Use your best estimate based on last 12 months of actual earnings, adjusted for expected changes. If you overestimate and get smaller subsidies than qualified for, you'll receive a refund at tax time. If you underestimate, you'll owe excess subsidies back. Many freelancers use their previous year's net self-employment income as a baseline. Track income monthly in Notion so you can update your marketplace estimate if circumstances change significantly.
Can I deduct health insurance premiums as a freelancer?
In the US, self-employed individuals can generally deduct 100% of health insurance premiums paid for themselves and their family (the Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction). This is an above-the-line deduction that reduces Adjusted Gross Income. You cannot deduct premiums for any month you were eligible for employer-sponsored coverage. Tax rules vary by situation — consult a qualified tax professional for advice specific to your circumstances and jurisdiction.
