A roadmap is one of the most valuable planning tools you can build — but most roadmaps live in slide decks that go stale the moment you update one thing, or spreadsheets that show dates but hide the reasoning behind decisions. Notion changes this entirely. A Notion roadmap is live, linked to your actual project databases, filterable by status and priority, shareable as a public link, and updateable in seconds.

This guide covers everything you need to build a Notion roadmap template from scratch — the database structure, essential views (timeline, priority matrix, Now/Next/Later board), and the three roadmap types every freelancer and founder needs to know. We'll also look at how to connect your roadmap to your project and task databases so it stays accurate automatically.

Why Use Notion for Roadmaps?

Traditional roadmap tools — Jira, Asana, dedicated tools like Roadmunk — require maintaining a separate system that's disconnected from where your actual work happens. The roadmap lives over there; the tasks live over here. Someone (usually you) has to manually sync them.

Notion solves this with linked databases. Your roadmap is a view of the same database as your projects. Update a project's status in the project board, and the roadmap reflects it automatically. Add a new initiative to the roadmap, and it appears in your project tracker. No syncing. No duplication. No staleness.

The second advantage is flexibility. You can show a Notion roadmap as a timeline (Gantt-style), a board (Now/Next/Later Kanban), a table (sortable spreadsheet), a calendar, or a gallery — all from the same database, all updated in real time. Show executives the timeline view. Show your team the Kanban board. Share a filtered public link with clients showing only their projects.

The 3 Types of Notion Roadmaps

🗺️
Product Roadmap
Features, improvements, and launches organized by quarter. Includes effort estimates, priority levels, and links to spec documents. Used by product teams to communicate what's being built and when.
📁
Project Roadmap
A timeline of milestones and deliverables for a single complex project. Shows phases (Discovery, Design, Build, Review, Launch), owners, and dependencies. Used for client work, launches, and multi-phase projects.
🎯
Personal Roadmap
Goals and major life projects organized by quarter or life area. Career milestones, financial targets, health goals, creative projects. Used for annual planning and quarterly reviews.

All three roadmap types share the same core database structure — the differences are in what properties you use, what views you configure, and what linked databases you connect. The template you build in this guide works for all three with minor adjustments.

Features of a Great Notion Roadmap Template

Timeline View (Gantt-style)
Visual bars across a time axis showing start and end dates for each item. Drag to adjust dates. Group by owner or category to see resource allocation at a glance.
Now/Next/Later Board
Kanban board grouped by timeline bucket — Now (active), Next (up next), Later (backlog). The simplest way to communicate priorities without over-committing to specific dates.
Priority Matrix
Board view grouped by Priority (P1/P2/P3) and filtered to active items. Instantly shows what matters most vs what can wait, without needing a 2x2 diagram.
Status Tracking
Clear status options (Planning, In Progress, Complete, On Hold, Cancelled) with color coding. A filtered "Active" view and a "Completed" archive view keep the main roadmap clean.
Linked Projects
A Relation to your Projects database so roadmap items connect to the tasks and deliverables inside them. A Rollup shows overall task completion percentage for each roadmap item.
Public Share Link
Share a view-only Timeline or Board view with clients, stakeholders, or teammates. Notion's share to web feature makes this a permanent, always-current link — no PDF exports needed.

How to Build a Notion Roadmap from Scratch (4 Steps)

1
Step 1: Set Up the Roadmap Database
Create a new Notion page and add an inline database (/database inline). Add these properties: Name (Title — the initiative or goal), Status (Select: Planning / In Progress / Complete / On Hold / Cancelled), Priority (Select: P1 Critical / P2 Important / P3 Nice to Have), Timeline Bucket (Select: Now / Next / Later), Start Date (Date), End Date (Date), Category (Multi-select — for filtering by team, product area, or life area), Owner (Person — if using in a team context). Color-code your Status and Priority options for visual clarity.
2
Step 2: Configure the Timeline View
Click "Add a view," select Timeline. Set the Date property to Start Date and the End date property to End Date. Set the Zoom level to Month (for quarterly roadmaps) or Week (for project roadmaps). Group by Category to create swimlanes showing each area's timeline side by side. Add a filter to hide Completed and Cancelled items from the default view. The Timeline view is now a Gantt chart — you can drag the start and end handles to adjust dates directly on the timeline.
3
Step 3: Build the Now/Next/Later Board
Add a Board view, group by Timeline Bucket (Now/Next/Later). Filter out Completed and Cancelled items. Sort within each bucket by Priority so P1 items appear at the top. This board is your most communication-friendly view — stakeholders understand it instantly. When a "Next" item gets prioritized, drag it to "Now." When completed, mark Status = Complete and it drops out of the filtered view automatically.
4
Step 4: Connect to Your Projects Database
Add a Relation property to your roadmap database linking to your Projects database. For each roadmap item, relate the 1–5 projects that deliver it. Add a Rollup property on the Projects relation: show the Count of projects with Status = Completed vs Total to calculate delivery progress. This makes your roadmap self-updating — as you complete projects, the roadmap item's completion percentage rises automatically. Add a Formula property to calculate percent complete: prop("Done Projects") / prop("Total Projects") * 100.

The Now/Next/Later Framework Explained

The Now/Next/Later framework was popularized by product teams at companies like Intercom and Basecamp as an alternative to date-heavy roadmaps. It works because it communicates priority without over-promising delivery dates.

Visual: Now/Next/Later Timeline
NOW
Actively working
NEXT
Prioritized & planned
LATER
Backlog
  • Now: What you're actively building or working toward this week/sprint/month. Should contain 3–7 items maximum. More than that and "Now" loses meaning.
  • Next: Clearly prioritized items that begin when Now items complete. Enough detail to start planning, but not yet scheduled to specific dates.
  • Later: The backlog. Important enough to capture, not yet important enough to plan. Review this quarterly and either promote items to Next, delete them, or confirm they still belong in Later.

The key insight: moving something from Next to Now is a commitment. Moving something from Later to Next is a signal of increasing priority. The friction of these moves keeps your roadmap honest.

Roadmap Maintenance Tip

Schedule a 15-minute "roadmap review" every Friday. Move completed items to Done. Promote any "Later" items that should now be "Next." Update the Progress percentage for active items. A roadmap that's updated weekly stays useful; one that's updated monthly becomes inaccurate within 3 weeks.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you make a roadmap in Notion?
Yes. Notion's Timeline view creates a visual Gantt-chart-style roadmap from any database with date properties. Add a database with Start Date and End Date properties, switch to Timeline view, and you have a visual project roadmap. Add Board view grouped by Status for a Now/Next/Later framework.
What is a Now/Next/Later roadmap?
Now/Next/Later is a lightweight roadmap framework. 'Now' contains what you're actively working on. 'Next' contains what's prioritized for after Now items complete. 'Later' is the backlog — important but not scheduled. This framework avoids over-committing to dates while still communicating priorities clearly.
What properties do I need for a Notion roadmap?
Essential roadmap properties: Name (Title), Status (Select — Now/Next/Later/Done), Priority (Select — P1/P2/P3), Start Date and End Date (Date — enables Timeline view), Category or Team (Multi-select for filtering), and Owner (Person). Optional: Effort (Select — Small/Medium/Large), and Impact (Select or Number).
What is the difference between a project plan and a roadmap?
A roadmap shows strategic direction — what you plan to build or achieve over weeks/months/quarters and why. A project plan shows the tactical execution — the specific tasks, dependencies, and day-by-day schedule for one project. Roadmaps are for stakeholders; project plans are for the team doing the work.
Does Notion have a Gantt chart view?
Notion's Timeline view functions like a Gantt chart — it shows items as horizontal bars across a time axis. You can see overlapping timelines, group by person or category, and drag items to adjust dates. It's not as feature-rich as dedicated Gantt tools (no dependency arrows), but for most roadmapping needs it's more than sufficient.
Can I share a Notion roadmap publicly?
Yes. Click Share on the roadmap page, toggle 'Share to web', and copy the link. Anyone with the link can view (but not edit) the roadmap. For client-facing roadmaps, set the specific view you want clients to see (e.g., a clean Timeline view) before sharing the page link.
How do I build a personal roadmap in Notion?
A personal roadmap tracks your goals and major life projects across quarters. Create a database with Name, Category (Career/Personal/Financial/Health/Creative), Target Quarter (Select — Q1 2026/Q2 2026 etc.), Status, and Priority. Add a Board view grouped by Target Quarter to see your goals by quarter. Add a Timeline view for date-based goals.
