How to Track Content Performance in Notion: A Creator's Dashboard Guide (Free Template)

Published April 26, 2026  ·  8 min read

You check YouTube Studio for video views. You open Instagram Insights for engagement rates. Your podcast host has its own dashboard. Your newsletter platform shows open rates in a completely different interface. Google Analytics tracks your blog traffic somewhere else entirely. And if you run paid content or sponsorships, that revenue data lives in spreadsheets, invoices, or your email inbox.

Every platform gives you analytics. None of them talk to each other.

The result is that most creators have plenty of data but no actual insight. You know Tuesday's video got 12,000 views. You know last week's newsletter had a 42% open rate. But you can't answer the question that actually matters: across everything I'm doing, what's working and what isn't?

That's the problem a Notion analytics hub solves. Not by replacing YouTube Studio or Instagram Insights — those tools are better at their specific jobs than Notion will ever be. Instead, Notion becomes your central command center: the one place where you aggregate key metrics from every platform, spot cross-channel patterns, and build a historical record that helps you make better creative decisions over time.

In this guide, we'll build that command center step by step. There's a free template at the end if you'd rather skip the setup.

What You Actually Need to Track

Before we build anything, let's talk about what not to track. The biggest analytics mistake creators make is tracking too many metrics. When you're logging 50 data points per piece of content, two things happen: the logging takes forever, and you can't see the forest for the trees. Fifty numbers per post is noise. Five numbers per post is signal.

Here are the five metrics that actually tell you whether your content is working:

1. Views / Impressions

The broadest measure of reach. How many people saw this piece? This is views on YouTube, impressions on Instagram and Twitter, downloads on your podcast, opens on your newsletter. It's not the most important metric, but it's the baseline. If reach is declining consistently, everything downstream suffers.

2. Engagement Rate

Combine likes, comments, and shares into a single number. Engagement tells you whether your content resonated enough for people to do something about it. A video with 50,000 views and 12 comments is very different from one with 10,000 views and 400 comments. The second one is doing more work for you.

3. Growth Rate

Did this piece of content bring in new followers, subscribers, or email signups? Growth is the metric that compounds. A piece of content that drives 200 new subscribers is more valuable than one that gets 200,000 views from existing followers. Track net new audience per piece, even if it's approximate.

4. Revenue Per Piece

Not every piece generates direct revenue, and that's fine. But when content does drive income — through ad revenue, sponsorship deals, affiliate links, or product sales — log it. Over time, you'll see which content types and topics generate the most revenue per unit of effort.

5. Best-Performing Content Types

This isn't a number — it's a pattern you'll notice once you've logged enough data. Tutorials outperform opinion pieces. Listicles get shared more than deep dives. Short-form content drives followers while long-form drives revenue. You can only see these patterns when your data is aggregated in one place.

Start with less. If five metrics feels like too many, start with just Views and Engagement. Add the others once the weekly logging habit is established. A system you actually use with two metrics beats a perfect system with ten metrics that you abandon after three weeks.

Building Your Analytics Hub in Notion

Here's the step-by-step setup. This creates a single database that serves as your cross-platform analytics command center.

Step 1: Create the Analytics Log Database

Create a full-page database called "Analytics Log." Each entry represents one piece of published content with its performance data. Add these properties:

  • Content Title — Title property. If you already have a content calendar database, make this a Relation property that links to it.
  • Platform — Select: YouTube, Instagram, TikTok, Twitter/X, LinkedIn, Newsletter, Blog, Podcast
  • Publish Date — Date property
  • Views — Number property (views, impressions, downloads, or opens)
  • Engagement — Number property (likes + comments + shares combined)
  • Revenue Generated — Number property, formatted as currency
  • Performance Score — Formula: Views / 1000 + Engagement * 2 + Revenue / 10
  • Notes — Rich text for observations

Step 2: Create the "Top Performers" View

Create a Table view sorted by Performance Score descending. This is your leaderboard — the pieces that resonated most, ranked by a formula that weights the metrics you care about. Check this view monthly. You'll start to see patterns: certain topics, formats, or posting times consistently appear near the top.

Step 3: Create the "Platform Breakdown" View

Create another Table view grouped by the Platform property. Now you can see all your YouTube content performance together, all your Instagram performance together, and so on. This answers: "Is my Instagram growth stalling while my newsletter is taking off?"

Step 4: Create the "Monthly Trends" View

Create a Calendar view using the Publish Date property. Now you can see your publishing cadence and performance visually across time. Are you publishing more but getting less engagement? Calendar view makes publishing patterns visible in a way that tables can't.

Pro tip: Create a filtered "This Month" Table view that only shows content published in the current month. Sort by Performance Score. This becomes your quick-reference dashboard for what's working right now.

The Experiment Tracker

This is the database most creators skip, and it's the one that accelerates growth the fastest. Create a separate database called "Content Experiments" with these properties:

  • Hypothesis — Title. Write it as testable: "Posting YouTube videos at 6am EST will get 20% more views than posting at noon."
  • What Changed — Rich text. Only change one variable at a time.
  • Result — Rich text with numbers.
  • Keep / Kill — Select: Keep, Kill, Test More.
  • Date Tested — Date property.
  • Related Content — Relation to your Analytics Log.

Without documentation, creators repeat the same failed experiments. You tried shorter thumbnails six months ago and they tanked, but you forgot. An experiment log prevents that and builds institutional knowledge about your audience.

Monthly Review Template

Data without reflection is just numbers. Create a Notion page template for your monthly content review:

  1. Top 3 Performers — What were your three best pieces? What do they have in common?
  2. Bottom 3 Performers — What underperformed? Can you identify why?
  3. Do More Of — Be specific: "More tutorial-style YouTube videos under 10 minutes" beats "make better content."
  4. Stop Doing — What's consistently underperforming? Most creators add but never subtract.
  5. Growth Snapshot — Log total followers per platform. This creates a growth trendline useful for sponsorship negotiations.

Duplicate this template monthly. It takes 15 minutes when your analytics data is already logged. Twelve monthly reviews create strategic clarity most creators never achieve.

Why This Isn't a Replacement for Native Analytics

Let's be honest about what Notion can and can't do here.

Notion requires manual data entry. There's no automatic sync between YouTube Studio and your Notion database. That takes about 15 minutes per week. Some find this annoying. Others find it valuable because it forces them to actually look at the numbers.

Native analytics tools are more granular. YouTube Studio shows audience retention, traffic sources, CTR on impressions. Instagram Insights shows reach vs. impressions and demographic breakdowns. Notion can't replicate that. Keep using those tools for platform-specific optimization.

The value is the aggregated view. No native tool will show you YouTube performance next to newsletter performance next to podcast numbers. That cross-platform, historical perspective is what Notion uniquely provides.

The real asset is the historical record. After six months of consistent logging, you'll have something no analytics tool provides: a complete history of everything you published, how it performed, what experiments you ran, and what you learned.

The weekly habit matters most. 15 minutes every Sunday evening logging the week's data. The pattern recognition from 6+ months of consistent tracking is invaluable for strategic decisions about what to create next.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Notion replace YouTube Studio or Instagram Insights?
No, and it shouldn't try. Native analytics tools provide real-time, granular data. Notion gives you a single aggregated view across all platforms for pattern recognition and historical records. Think of it as your analytics command center, not a replacement.
How long does it take to log content performance data each week?
10-15 minutes per week. Log 5-6 data points per piece at a consistent time — Sunday evenings or Monday mornings work well. The time investment pays off when you can see 3-6 months of trends in one view.
What's the best free Notion template for tracking content analytics?
CreatorHQ Lite includes an analytics log, performance tracking properties, and a top performers view. For the full dashboard with experiment tracking and monthly reviews, CreatorHQ Pro ($29) includes the complete system.
How many metrics should I track per piece of content?
5 core metrics: views/impressions, engagement, growth contribution, revenue, and a performance score. Tracking more leads to analysis paralysis.
Can I automate data entry from YouTube or Instagram into Notion?
Partially via Zapier or Make. Most creators find manual weekly logging more practical — it forces you to actually review the numbers. Automation is useful at scale (50+ pieces/month).
