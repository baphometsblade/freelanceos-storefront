Notion Database Templates — Complete Guide 2026

The 10 Best Notion Database Templates for Productivity in 2026

The exact properties, views, and formulas for the 10 Notion databases every freelancer and knowledge worker needs — from Client CRM to Annual Goals.

June 2026  ·  12 min read
Home › Blog › Best Notion Database Templates 2026

Notion's database system is the most powerful thing about it — and the most misunderstood. Most people treat databases like spreadsheets (they're not), or add so many properties they never fill them in (less is more), or set up a database for every possible thing they might want to track (consolidate first).

A great Notion database template has exactly the properties you'll fill in consistently, the views that show the data in the most useful format for the task, and ideally a formula or two that automates something you'd otherwise calculate manually. This guide gives you all three for the 10 databases that actually move the needle for productivity and business management.

What Makes a Great Notion Database Template

Before the list, the framework for evaluating any Notion database template:

  • Property discipline: Only include properties you'll fill in for 80%+ of entries. Sparse properties create visual clutter and tempt you to skip entries altogether.
  • View intentionality: Each view should answer a specific question you have regularly. "All Tasks" is a view; "What's due this week?" is a useful view.
  • Formula leverage: One good formula can save hours of manual calculation. Days overdue, streak counters, and completion percentages are worth setting up.
  • Relation architecture: The databases you connect matter more than the databases themselves. A Project database connected to Clients, Tasks, and Invoices is a business management system. The same database in isolation is just a list.

The 10 Best Notion Database Templates

1
Client CRM

The single most valuable database for freelancers. A Client CRM tracks every client relationship — not just active projects but the full lifetime of the relationship, including warm leads, past clients worth re-engaging, and referral sources.

Properties to include:

  • Name (title) — client or company name
  • Company (text) — organization name
  • Email (email) — primary contact email
  • Status (select) — Lead / Active / Completed / Paused / Lost
  • Last Contact (date) — when you last communicated
  • Value (number) — total revenue from this client (lifetime or current project)
  • Tags (multi-select) — industry, service type, referral source
  • Notes (text) — personal details, preferences, communication style
  • Projects (relation) — linked to your Projects database

Best formula — Days Since Last Contact:

dateBetween(now(), prop("Last Contact"), "days")

Use this to filter clients you haven't contacted in 60+ days — your re-engagement target list. On a linked dashboard view, filter by this formula > 60 to see who's going cold.

Views:Table (full view) + Board (by Status) Connect to:Projects, Invoices
2
Project Tracker

Manages every freelance project from kick-off to final delivery. Connected to Clients and Invoices, this becomes the command center for your business — all active work visible in one place.

Properties to include:

  • Project Name (title)
  • Status (select) — Not Started / In Progress / In Review / Completed / On Hold
  • Priority (select) — Low / Medium / High / Urgent
  • Start Date (date)
  • Deadline (date)
  • Client (relation) — linked to Client CRM
  • Revenue (number) — project value in your currency
  • Invoiced (checkbox) — has the invoice been sent?
  • Notes (text) — scope, deliverables, any important context

In the Calendar view, set the date property to Deadline so every project's due date appears on the calendar. Filter by Status != Completed so finished projects don't clutter the timeline view.

Views:Kanban (by Status) + Calendar (Deadline) Connect to:Clients, Tasks, Invoices
3
Content Calendar

For freelancers doing content marketing or managing client content, this database makes the difference between scattered publishing and a consistent, strategic content operation.

Properties to include:

  • Content Title (title)
  • Platform (select) — Blog / LinkedIn / Twitter / YouTube / Instagram / Newsletter / Podcast
  • Status (select) — Idea / Draft / Review / Scheduled / Published
  • Publish Date (date)
  • Topic / Keyword (text)
  • Word Count / Length (number)
  • Content URL (URL) — the live URL after publishing
  • Tags (multi-select) — content themes, campaigns

The Calendar view with Publish Date is the primary view — seeing a month of planned content in calendar format immediately reveals gaps and clustering. The Board view by Status shows your content pipeline from idea to published. Filter the Board by Platform to see just your LinkedIn queue or just your newsletter issues.

Views:Calendar (Publish Date) + Board (by Status) Best filter:Platform = specific channel
4
Invoice Database

Every freelancer needs a clear record of what's been invoiced, what's been paid, and what's overdue. This database makes your cash flow legible at a glance and creates an automatic follow-up list for overdue payments.

Properties to include:

  • Invoice # (title)
  • Client (relation) — linked to Client CRM
  • Project (relation) — linked to Project Tracker
  • Amount (number)
  • Status (select) — Draft / Sent / Paid / Overdue / Cancelled
  • Invoice Date (date)
  • Due Date (date)
  • Paid Date (date) — fill in when payment arrives
  • Payment Method (select) — Bank Transfer / PayPal / Stripe / Check

Key formula — Days Overdue:

if(prop("Status") != "Paid", dateBetween(now(), prop("Due Date"), "days"), 0)

Use a filtered view showing invoices where this formula is greater than 0 — your follow-up queue. The Calculate function at the bottom of the Amount column lets you sum total outstanding invoices instantly. This is your real-time accounts receivable balance.

Views:Table (all) + Filtered (unpaid only) Connect to:Clients, Projects
5
Task Manager

A task database distinct from your project tracker. Projects are deliverables; tasks are the individual actions that produce them. The best task manager setup for solo freelancers uses the Eisenhower Matrix (Urgent/Important priority system).

Properties to include:

  • Task Name (title)
  • Priority (select) — Urgent + Important / Important / Urgent / Low Priority
  • Status (select) — To Do / In Progress / Done / Blocked
  • Due Date (date)
  • Project (relation) — linked to Project Tracker
  • Estimated Hours (number)
  • Actual Hours (number)
  • Notes (text) — context or blockers

The primary view is a Board grouped by Priority — four columns representing the four Eisenhower quadrants. Drag tasks into Done to complete them (or filter Done out of your main view for a clean working board). Filter by Project to see a project's task list. Filter by Due Date = Today for your daily task list on your dashboard.

Views:Board (Priority) + List (Today) + Calendar Connect to:Projects
6
Knowledge Base

Your personal Wikipedia — a searchable repository of everything you've learned, referenced, or want to remember. This database is the heart of any Second Brain setup.

Properties to include:

  • Note Title (title)
  • Topic (multi-select) — the subjects this note covers: Marketing, Pricing, Client Management, Writing, etc.
  • Source (select) — Book / Article / Podcast / Video / My Own Thinking / Client Work
  • Source Name (text) — the specific book, article, or person
  • Date Added (created time)
  • Key Points (text) — the main insights in your own words
  • Related Projects (relation) — link insights to active work
  • Related Books (relation) — link to your Reading List database

The power is in the search and filter. Filter by Topic = "Pricing" to find every insight you've ever captured about pricing strategy. The Gallery view shows recent additions; the Table view is for searching and filtering by topic. Add everything, tag deliberately.

Views:Gallery (recent) + Table (filtered) Connect to:Projects, Books/Reading List
7
Meeting Notes

A database for meeting notes beats page-based notes because it's searchable, filterable by client or project, and creates an automatic history of all commitments made in every meeting.

Properties to include:

  • Meeting Title (title) — e.g., "Acme Corp Q3 Kickoff"
  • Date (date)
  • Attendees (text) — names of people present
  • Client (relation) — linked to Client CRM
  • Project (relation) — linked to Project Tracker
  • Meeting Type (select) — Kickoff / Check-In / Review / Sales Call / Internal
  • Action Items (text) — concrete next steps from the meeting
  • Next Steps (text) — longer-range commitments made
  • Summary (text) — 2–3 sentence meeting summary

After every client call, create a new entry, fill in Action Items while the meeting is fresh, and send a follow-up email linking to or summarizing the action items. The database creates an automatic meeting history per client — filter by Client to see every meeting you've ever had with them and what was discussed.

Views:List (by Date) + Filtered (by Client) Connect to:Clients, Projects
8
Habit Tracker

A Notion habit tracker database creates a visual streak record and makes habit completion data searchable and filterable — something apps like Habitica don't offer.

Properties to include:

  • Date (title or date) — one row per day
  • One checkbox per habit — e.g., Morning Pages ✓, Exercise ✓, Read 30min ✓, No Sugar ✓, Sleep by 11pm ✓
  • Notes (text) — brief daily reflection or blocker note
  • Completion % (formula) — percentage of habits completed today

Completion formula (for 5 habits):

round((toNumber(prop("Exercise")) + toNumber(prop("Read")) + toNumber(prop("Morning Pages")) + toNumber(prop("No Sugar")) + toNumber(prop("Sleep 11pm"))) / 5 * 100)

Sort by Date descending for your current month. Use the Gallery view with a card preview of the Date property to see the month visually. Color-code completed days (100%) using Notion's colored backgrounds on entries. This visual streak is genuinely motivating — seeing a red day breaks a green streak, which creates behavioral pull.

Views:Table (current month) + Gallery (visual) Best used on:Daily dashboard as linked view
9
Reading List

Already covered in detail in our Notion Reading List guide, this database tracks every book you read with the properties that matter most for knowledge workers.

Core properties: Title, Author, Status (Want to Read / Reading / Finished / Abandoned), Rating (1–5), Genre (multi-select), Date Finished, Key Takeaways (text), Cover URL, and a Relation to your Knowledge Base for capturing insights.

Best views: Gallery (visual bookshelf), Board by Status (reading pipeline), Table (filter/sort by rating or genre), and a filtered view for this year's finished books connected to your reading goal tracker.

The real value: when your Reading List connects to your Knowledge Base via Relation, every book becomes a node in your personal knowledge graph. Insights from five years of reading become instantly searchable and filterable by topic.

Views:Gallery + Board (Status) + Table Connect to:Knowledge Base
10
Annual Goals

A goals database (not a goals page) creates structure and measurability that text-based goals lists can't match. Organize by quarter, track status dynamically, and connect goals to the projects and tasks that advance them.

Properties to include:

  • Goal Title (title)
  • Category (select) — Business / Financial / Health / Learning / Relationships / Personal
  • Quarter (select) — Q1 / Q2 / Q3 / Q4 / Full Year
  • Status (select) — Not Started / In Progress / On Track / At Risk / Completed / Abandoned
  • Progress % (number) — manually updated 0–100 percentage
  • Key Milestone (text) — the single most important measurable outcome
  • By When (date) — target completion date
  • Related Projects (relation) — which projects advance this goal

Use the Timeline view with By When as the date property to see your goals plotted across the year. Group by Quarter in Gallery view for a quarter-by-quarter priorities overview. Update Progress % weekly during a Sunday review. Goals at 0% progress after 30 days need either a project attached or an honest evaluation of whether they're actually a priority.

Views:Gallery (by Quarter) + Timeline + Table Connect to:Projects, Tasks
Database Architecture Tip

The order in which you build these databases matters. Start with Tasks, then Projects, then Clients, then Invoices. Connect each new database to the ones you've already built. Build the Knowledge Base and Reading List independently. Build Goals last, when you have Projects to connect them to. This sequence creates a connected system rather than 10 isolated databases.

Frequently Asked Questions

What makes a great Notion database template?
A great Notion database template has five qualities: (1) The right properties — only what you'll actually fill in, not every possible field. (2) Pre-configured views that match how you actually use the data. (3) Formulas that automate calculations you'd otherwise do manually. (4) Relations that connect databases so data flows without duplication. (5) Rollups that surface aggregate information from related databases. The biggest mistake is over-engineering — adding 20 properties when 7 would serve you better.
How many databases should a Notion workspace have?
For most freelancers, 6–10 core databases is the right range. The essential set: Tasks, Projects, Clients, Invoices, Notes/Knowledge Base, and a Reading List. Additional databases for specific needs: Content Calendar, Meeting Notes, Habit Tracker. The temptation is to create a database for everything — resist it. Linked views of existing databases can often serve the purpose without adding a new database.
What is the best Notion database template for freelancers?
The single most valuable Notion database template for freelancers is a Client CRM — tracking every client relationship with status, last contact date, project count, and revenue. This gives instant visibility into your business health and ensures no client relationship goes cold. The second most valuable is a Project Tracker connected to your Client CRM and Invoice Database so you can see the full project-to-payment flow in one place.
Can I connect multiple Notion databases together?
Yes — and this is where Notion's real power lives. Use the Relation property to connect two databases (e.g., link Projects to Clients). Use the Rollup property to surface data from the related database (e.g., show the Client's Company Name on the Project page). One client can have multiple projects; one project can have multiple tasks and invoices. This relational layer is what separates Notion from a simple spreadsheet.
What Notion formulas are most useful for database templates?
The most useful Notion formulas are: (1) Days since last contact: dateBetween(now(), prop('Last Contact'), 'days'). (2) Days overdue: if(prop('Status') != 'Paid', dateBetween(now(), prop('Due Date'), 'days'), 0). (3) Project duration: dateBetween(prop('End Date'), prop('Start Date'), 'days'). (4) Completion percentage: round(prop('Completed Tasks') / prop('Total Tasks') * 100). (5) Read this year: if(year(prop('Date Finished')) == 2026, 1, 0).

Should I use table view or board view for a Notion task manager?
Use board view (Kanban) as your primary task manager view — it makes status changes visual and immediate. Use table view when you need to filter, sort, or bulk-edit tasks. Use calendar view to see task deadlines in time context. Use list view on mobile. Most productive Notion users set up 3–4 views and use different ones for planning vs. executing vs. reviewing.
What is the difference between a Notion template and a Notion database template?
A Notion template is any page structure you can duplicate — it might be a single page with text and images, or a complex multi-database system. A Notion database template specifically refers to a database with pre-configured properties, views, filters, and formulas. Database templates are more powerful because they're functional systems, not just documents. Most serious Notion users eventually move from templates (pages) to database templates (structured data systems).
