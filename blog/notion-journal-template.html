Notion Journal Template: 5 Journaling Systems to Transform Your Life

From daily reflection to dream journaling — build a complete, searchable journaling system in Notion that connects to your goals, habits, and life OS.

June 2026  ·  11 min read
Home › Blog › Notion Journal Template Guide

The best journaling app is the one you actually use. For millions of people, that's Notion — not because Notion is the most polished journaling experience, but because it's already where they live. When your journal is in Notion, every entry can link to the project you were working on, the goal you were pursuing, the habit you were building, or the person you were thinking about. That connectivity is what transforms journaling from a private diary into a thinking and growth system.

This guide covers five distinct journaling systems you can build in Notion — from a daily reflection journal to a dream journal — with exact properties, templates, and views for each. Whether you're new to journaling or migrating from Day One, Bear, or a physical notebook, you'll have a complete system running by the end of this guide.

Why Notion Is the Best Digital Journaling Tool

Before diving into templates, it's worth understanding why Notion earns the "best" label for serious journalers — and where it falls short.

Searchability: Three years of Notion journals are searchable in 2 seconds. Find every entry that mentioned "anxiety," "milestone," "client conflict," or any word you ever wrote. No other free tool matches this for unstructured text at scale.

Linkability: Every journal entry can link to a Notion page — a project, a person, a goal, a book. This context makes entries far more valuable on review. "Today I was frustrated" is less useful than "Today I was frustrated [→ Project: Brand Redesign for Acme]."

Rich media: Embed images, videos, PDFs, and audio recordings directly in entries. Your journals can hold screenshots of messages that upset you, sketches of ideas, screenshots of wins, or voice memo transcripts.

Templates: Create a journal entry template that pre-populates all your daily prompts. One click, and your entry structure is ready — you just fill in the content. This removes the blank-page friction that kills journaling habits.

Privacy: Notion pages you don't share are completely private. Keep your mental health journal, personal diary, and creative writing in a Private section that never appears in shared workspaces.

One Database, Five Views

You don't need five separate databases for five journaling styles. One Journal database with a "Type" property (select: Daily / Gratitude / Weekly Review / Mental Health / Dream) and template buttons for each type gives you five journaling formats in one searchable database. Filter by Type to see just daily entries, or search across all types at once.

The 5 Notion Journal Templates

Template 1
Daily Journal
  • Date (Date property, auto-filled with today)
  • Mood (Select: Excellent / Good / Okay / Low / Struggling)
  • Energy (Select: High / Medium / Low)
  • Gratitude 1, 2, 3 (Text — three specific things, not vague)
  • Today's Top Focus (Text — one sentence describing the most important thing)
  • What Went Well (Text block — at least 2-3 sentences)
  • What to Improve (Text block — specific and actionable)
  • Evening Reflection (Open text — stream of consciousness)
  • Tags (Multi-select: Work / Relationships / Health / Money / Creative / Personal)

Best practice: fill Gratitude and Top Focus in the morning (2 minutes), complete the rest in the evening (5-10 minutes). This two-touch system works better than trying to do everything at once.

Template 2
Weekly Review
  • Week of (Date property)
  • Week Rating (Number 1-10 — your overall assessment)
  • 3 Wins This Week (Text — specific accomplishments, not "worked hard")
  • Biggest Lesson (Text — what you learned or relearned)
  • Habits Kept (Text or rollup — which habits hit their targets)
  • Goals Progress (Text — how did your quarterly goals advance?)
  • What Was Hard (Text — obstacle acknowledgment without catastrophizing)
  • One Change for Next Week (Text — specific, behavioral, measurable)
  • Top 3 Priorities Next Week (Text — in order of importance)

Schedule this every Sunday at the same time. Even 20 minutes of weekly review compounds into extraordinary self-awareness and intentionality over a year.

Template 3
Gratitude Journal
  • Date (Date)
  • Gratitude 1 (Text — specific person, experience, or thing)
  • Gratitude 2 (Text)
  • Gratitude 3 (Text)
  • Category (Multi-select: People / Work / Health / Experiences / Home / Nature / Growth)
  • Why It Matters (Text — add one sentence of depth to deepen the practice)
  • One Person to Thank Today (Text or Relation to a People database)

Use Gallery view sorted by Date. Set the card preview to show Gratitude 1. This creates a visual wall of things you've been grateful for — one of the most mood-elevating things you can look at on a hard day.

Template 4
Therapy & Mental Health Journal
  • Date (Date)
  • Mood Score (Number 1-10, or Select with nuanced labels)
  • Anxiety Level (Select: None / Mild / Moderate / High / Overwhelming)
  • Triggers (Multi-select: Work / Finances / Relationships / Health / Social / Uncertainty)
  • Body Sensations (Text — where do you feel this in your body?)
  • Journal Entry (Long text — unfiltered processing)
  • Coping Methods Used (Multi-select: Exercise / Breathing / Talk to Someone / Nature / Rest / Journaling)
  • What I Need Right Now (Text)
  • Therapist Notes (Text — things to bring to your next session)

Keep this database in a Private section in Notion. The mood score over time creates a chart you can share with a therapist. The triggers log reveals patterns that are invisible entry-by-entry.

Template 5
Dream Journal
  • Date (Date — record immediately on waking, before the dream fades)
  • Dream Title (Text — give it a name to anchor the memory)
  • Description (Long text — full stream-of-consciousness recall)
  • Themes (Multi-select: Flying / Water / People I Know / Strangers / Chase / Falling / Work / Home / Conflict / Joy / Exploration)
  • Lucidity Level (Select: Not Lucid / Slightly Aware / Semi-Lucid / Fully Lucid)
  • Vividness (Number 1-5)
  • Emotional Tone (Select: Positive / Neutral / Anxious / Frightening / Confusing / Peaceful)
  • Analysis Notes (Text — waking interpretation, symbols, connections to current life)

Group by Themes in a Board view to see recurring dream patterns. Sort by Vividness to find your most memorable dreams. The analysis over time becomes a fascinating personal mythology.

How to Build a Searchable Journal Entry System

The real power of Notion journaling emerges when entries become interconnected. Here's how to make your journal searchable, linkable, and analytically valuable:

Tags and Multi-Select Properties

Add a Tags multi-select property to every journal database. Use consistent tags across all journal types so you can search "Anxiety" and find daily journal entries, mental health entries, and weekly reviews that all touched on the same theme. Tags create cross-temporal patterns that raw text search can't easily surface.

Link Journal Entries to Projects and People

Add Relation properties to your daily journal: one linking to your Projects database, one linking to your People/Contacts database. When you write about a client conflict, link to that client. When you process emotions about a project, link to it. Six months later, searching the project page will surface the emotional context of when you worked on it — invaluable for retrospectives.

Mood Charts with Timeline View

If you use a Mood Score number property (1-10), Notion's Chart view can plot your mood over time. Turn on Chart view in your journal database, set the X-axis to Date and Y-axis to Mood Score. This creates a visual mood history. Add a trend line and you can see whether your baseline mood has shifted over months — information that's invisible when reading individual entries.

Making Journaling a Habit: Trigger Stacking for Writers

Journaling failures aren't motivation failures — they're friction failures. The entry form takes too long to open, the blank page feels intimidating, the "perfect time" never arrives. Here are the friction-reduction tactics that work:

The Two-Minute Morning Entry

Commit to only two minutes in the morning. Set a template that contains three fields: Today's Gratitude (one item), Today's Focus (one sentence), and Today's Intention (one word). That's it. Two minutes, three fields. The full daily journal entry happens in the evening when you have more time and more to reflect on. Splitting the entry removes the "I don't have time" barrier that kills morning journaling.

The Evening Wind-Down Anchor

Attach evening journaling to a ritual you already have: making herbal tea, changing into pajamas, turning off the TV. "After I make my evening tea, I open Notion and complete my journal entry." The anchor makes the trigger automatic. Keep your Notion journal tab always open in a pinned browser tab to eliminate the open-and-navigate friction.

Keep It Private, Keep It Honest

The most common journaling-avoidance pattern is self-censorship: editing your writing as you type, avoiding uncomfortable topics, writing for an imaginary audience. Your Notion journal is private. Write exactly what you think, uncensored. The most valuable insights come from the entries where you wrote something that surprised you. Give yourself permission to be completely honest with no audience except future-you.

How to Do a Notion Year-in-Review from Journal Data

At the end of each year, your Notion journal becomes one of your most valuable assets. Here's how to turn it into a genuine retrospective:

  1. Mood chart review: Look at your mood score chart across all 52 weeks. When were the high points? What was happening then? When were the lows? What patterns do you see by month or season?
  2. Tag frequency analysis: Sort your journal by tag and count entries. Your top 5 tags represent what dominated your mental bandwidth this year.
  3. Win inventory: Filter for entries tagged "Win" or search for entries where you wrote about accomplishments. Count them — freelancers dramatically undercount their wins when living inside the day-to-day.
  4. Lesson distillation: Open every Weekly Review and pull one lesson from each. By December, you have 52 lessons — distill these to 10 that you carry into next year.
  5. Relationship map: Look at which names appear most in your journal. Who was most present in your life this year? Who do you wish had been more present?
  6. Future self letter: Write a letter to yourself dated January 1 of next year, from the you who completed this year-in-review. What do you want to remember, what do you want to carry forward, what are you letting go?

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Notion good for journaling?
Notion is excellent for digital journaling because entries are fully searchable, can be tagged and filtered, link to other pages (like projects and people), and can include rich media like images and embedded content. Unlike paper journals, Notion lets you search 3 years of entries for a name, theme, or feeling instantly. Unlike dedicated journaling apps like Day One, Notion connects your journal to your work system, goals, and habit tracker in one workspace.
How do I create a journal template in Notion?
To create a journal template in Notion: create a new database (table view), then click the dropdown arrow next to the New button and select "New template." Design the template page with your journal prompts, property blocks, and sections. Every time you click the template button, it creates a pre-filled entry. You can have multiple templates (daily journal, weekly review, monthly reflection) all in the same database.
What should a daily journal template in Notion include?
A strong daily Notion journal template should include: Date (auto-filled), Mood (select property), 3 Gratitude items (text or numbered list), Today's Top Focus (one sentence), What Went Well (text block), What to Improve (text block), and Evening Reflection (open text). Optional: energy level, one thing you're proud of, tomorrow's priority, and a quote for the day.
Can I keep a private journal in Notion?
Yes — pages and databases in Notion that you don't share are completely private. If you share your workspace with team members or clients, use a Private section in your sidebar to keep personal pages separate. For extra security, Notion's free plan supports page locks. For highly sensitive content, note that Notion is not end-to-end encrypted — for maximum privacy, a dedicated encrypted app may be more appropriate.
How do I make journaling a habit in Notion?
The most reliable approach is trigger stacking: "After I pour my morning coffee, I open my Notion daily journal." Keep the template minimal for morning entries (three prompts maximum). Reduce friction by pinning your journal database to your Notion sidebar. For evening reflections, schedule a calendar reminder and open Notion immediately when it fires. The two-minute morning entry plus a 10-minute evening entry is more sustainable than a daily 20-minute all-in-one session.
What is the difference between a daily journal and gratitude journal in Notion?
A daily journal is comprehensive — it captures mood, focus, what went well and what to improve, and open-ended reflection. A gratitude journal focuses specifically on appreciation: 3 specific things you're grateful for, categorized and visualized over time. Both can live in the same Notion database with different templates, or in separate linked databases. Many people do gratitude journaling in the morning and full daily journaling in the evening.
How do I do a year-in-review from my Notion journal?
At year end, use Notion's filter to view all journal entries from the past year. Review your mood chart trend, count entries by tag to see what dominated your thoughts, filter for entries about wins and accomplishments, pull one lesson from each weekly review, and write a letter to your future self. A thorough year-in-review takes 2-3 hours and produces insights about patterns and priorities that are invisible in individual entries.
