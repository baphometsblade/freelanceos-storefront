What a CRM Actually Is (vs What Most People Think)

People think a CRM is a contacts list. The acronym — Customer Relationship Management — says it better: it's about managing relationships, not just storing contact details. A real CRM tracks:

  • Who you know and how you met them
  • Where they are in your sales process (pipeline stage)
  • The history of every interaction (calls, emails, meetings, demos)
  • What happens next and by when (next action and next action date)
  • The potential value of the relationship (deal value, probability)
  • What projects or revenue that relationship has generated

Most freelancers manage this across a combination of email, memory, and occasional spreadsheet entries. The Notion CRM centralizes all of it in one searchable, filterable, linkable database.

Step 1: Build Your Contacts Database

Step 1 of 5
Contacts Database — Every Relationship in One Place

The Contacts database is your address book — but more powerful. Every person or company you have or want a business relationship with lives here: current clients, past clients, prospects, partners, collaborators, referral sources. Don't limit it to just clients; the best CRMs track the full relationship ecosystem.

  • Full Name — Title property. Person's full name.
  • Company — Text. What company they work for or own.
  • Email — Email property. Their primary contact email.
  • Phone — Phone number property.
  • LinkedIn URL — URL property. Link to their LinkedIn profile for quick research.
  • How We Met — Multi-select: Referral / Event / Cold Outreach / Inbound Website / LinkedIn / Social Media / Other. This is your lead source tracking — critical for understanding where your best clients come from.
  • Tags — Multi-select: Client / Prospect / Partner / Referral Source / Collaborator / Advisor. One contact can have multiple tags.
  • Status — Select: Active / Past Client / Cold / Do Not Contact.
  • Last Contact — Date. Updated every time you interact. A formula on the Deals side flags contacts untouched for 30+ days.
  • Notes — Long text. Relationship history, personal details, context that helps you personalize every interaction.
  • Linked Deals — Relation to Deals database. All deals associated with this contact.
  • Linked Projects — Relation to Projects database (if you have one). All projects that came from this relationship.

Step 2: Build Your Deals Pipeline

Step 2 of 5
Deals Pipeline — Visual Sales Tracking

The Deals database is separate from Contacts — one contact can have multiple deals over time. This separation lets you track the history of each opportunity independently while still seeing everything linked to a contact on their contact page.

  • Deal Name — Title. Descriptive: "Website Redesign — TechStartup Inc" not just "Web project."
  • Contact — Relation to Contacts database. Who is this deal with?
  • Stage — Select: Lead / Qualified / Proposal Sent / Negotiating / Won / Lost. This drives your Kanban view.
  • Value — Number. Estimated or proposed project value in dollars.
  • Expected Close Date — Date. Your best estimate of when this deal will close.
  • Probability % — Number (0-100). Your estimated likelihood of winning this deal. Weighted Value = Value × Probability gives a realistic pipeline forecast.
  • Source — Select: Referral / Cold Outreach / Inbound / Event / LinkedIn / Other. Should match the contact's "How We Met" for analysis.
  • Next Action — Text. Specific next step — not "follow up" but "send revised proposal with phased payment option."
  • Next Action Date — Date. When the next action is due. Drives your daily follow-up view.
  • Notes — Long text. Deal-specific notes, objections raised, budget constraints, key decision factors.
  • Days Stale — Formula: dateBetween(now(), prop("Last Contact"), "days"). Flag deals that haven't moved in 30+ days.

Step 3: Interaction Logging (for High-Touch Sales)

Step 3 of 5
Interactions Log — Complete Relationship History

The Interactions database is optional for simple freelance CRM use, but essential if you have longer sales cycles (2+ weeks), multiple decision-makers, or high-value deals where context matters. It logs every meaningful touchpoint with a contact — creating a searchable history that's invaluable for picking up a conversation weeks later.

  • Date — Date of the interaction.
  • Type — Select: Call / Email / Meeting / Demo / LinkedIn Message / Follow-Up / Referral Introduction
  • Contact — Relation to Contacts. Who was this interaction with?
  • Deal — Relation to Deals. Which deal does this relate to?
  • Summary — Text. What was discussed or what was the email about? 2-3 sentences max.
  • Next Step — Text. What happens next as a result of this interaction?
  • Follow-Up Date — Date. When to follow up based on this interaction.

Log interactions immediately after they happen — don't trust your memory. The detail that seems obvious today ("she mentioned they just hired a new CMO who might want a brand refresh") becomes invisible in three weeks without a note.

Step 4: Views and Dashboards

The views transform your raw databases into actionable dashboards. Here are the four most valuable views to build:

Active Pipeline View

Filter: Stage is not "Won" and not "Lost." Sort by Value descending. This shows every active deal with the highest-value opportunities at the top. This is your Monday morning view — the complete picture of what's in play and where your attention should go this week.

Today's Follow-Ups View

Filter: Next Action Date = today or earlier. This is your daily action list — every deal where something needs to happen now. If you check only one CRM view per day, this is it. Deals in this view should be acted on before the end of day.

Revenue Won This Month

Filter: Stage = "Won," Expected Close Date = this month. Sort by Value. This is your monthly revenue tally — instant visibility into what's been closed and what the month looks like financially.

Stale Deals Alert

Filter: Stage not "Won" or "Lost," Days Stale formula > 30. These are deals that have gone quiet and may need a direct follow-up or a decision about whether to continue pursuing them. Stale deals are the biggest leak in most freelance sales pipelines.

Pipeline Formula

Add a "Weighted Value" formula property to your Deals database: prop("Value") * prop("Probability %") / 100. Sum this column in your pipeline view for a realistic forecast of expected revenue — more accurate than summing full deal values.

Step 5: Email Templates by Stage

Step 5 of 5
Templates — Fast Follow-Ups Without Starting From Scratch

Create a Templates page in your CRM with quick-copy email scripts for each pipeline stage. This eliminates the mental friction of starting from scratch on repetitive emails and ensures consistency across your outreach. Each template takes 30 seconds to personalize rather than 15 minutes to write.

  • Initial Outreach — First contact email with clear value proposition and specific call-to-action.
  • Follow-Up After No Reply (7 days) — Short, direct follow-up assuming the email was missed.
  • Proposal Sent Follow-Up (3 days) — Check-in after sending a proposal with offer to answer questions.
  • Negotiation Response — How to respond to pushback on price (value-based, not discount-based).
  • Won Deal — Onboarding Kick-Off — Welcome email with next steps after contract is signed.
  • Re-Engagement (Cold Contact) — Reach out to contacts who've gone cold with a new value hook.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Notion be used as a CRM?
Yes — Notion works effectively as a CRM for freelancers and small businesses managing up to 50-100 active contacts or deals. You can build a contacts database, a pipeline with Kanban stages, deal values, interaction logging, and follow-up dashboards. The main advantage over paid CRMs: it's free, fully customizable, and lives next to your project management and notes. The limitation: no automatic email logging, no automated sequences, and less robust reporting than HubSpot or Pipedrive for large sales teams.
What is the best free CRM for freelancers?
For freelancers managing under 50 active deals, Notion CRM is the best free option because it combines client management with project tracking, notes, and your knowledge base in one workspace. For email-heavy outreach, HubSpot Free (up to 1 million contacts, basic pipeline) is an alternative. For pure CRM without integration needs, Trello with a pipeline layout is simpler but less powerful. Notion wins on flexibility and integration.
How do I build a Notion CRM?
Build a Notion CRM in 5 steps: (1) Create a Contacts database with name, company, email, status, and how you met. (2) Create a Deals database linked to Contacts with pipeline stages and deal values. (3) Set up Kanban view by Stage for visual pipeline management. (4) Add filtered views for Active Deals, This Week's Follow-Ups, and Won Deals This Month. (5) Create email templates for each pipeline stage. The whole setup takes about 2 hours and replaces $50-100/month CRM software.
Notion CRM vs HubSpot — which is better?
Notion wins on price (free vs $50-800/mo), flexibility, and integration with your project management. HubSpot wins on automatic email logging, contact activity tracking, email sequences and automation, and team-wide reporting. For solo freelancers and small businesses under 5 people, Notion CRM handles most needs for $0. When you need high-volume outreach, automated sequences, or team-wide CRM with reporting, HubSpot becomes worth the cost.
How do I track clients in Notion?
To track clients in Notion: create a Contacts database with client status (Active/Past/Cold), a Deals database for sales pipeline, and a Projects database linked to both. Set up a 'My Clients' filtered view showing Status = Active contacts. Add a Last Contact date property and a Days Since Last Contact formula — any active client over 60 days without contact gets a visual flag. Link each client to their invoices and projects for a complete relationship view.
Is Notion good for sales pipeline management?
Notion handles sales pipeline well for teams of 1-5 people. The Kanban view with pipeline stages gives clear visual management. Deal values and close dates enable basic forecasting. The main limitations: no automated reminders when deals go stale, email activity doesn't log automatically, and reporting is manual. For teams with more than 5 salespeople or high deal volume, a dedicated CRM like Pipedrive adds value that justifies the cost.
What CRM properties should I track in Notion?
Essential Notion CRM properties: Full Name, Company, Email, Pipeline Stage, Deal Value, Expected Close Date, Source (how the lead came in), Last Contact Date, Next Action (specific next step), Next Action Date (drives your daily follow-up view), and Notes (relationship history). Optional but valuable: Probability % for weighted pipeline forecasting, How We Met for referral tracking, and a formula for Days Since Last Contact.
When should I upgrade from Notion CRM to a paid CRM?
Upgrade from Notion CRM when: you have more than 100 active deals; you need automated email sequences; your sales team is more than 3-4 people; you need robust reporting for stakeholders; or you need integrations with email, phone dialers, or marketing tools. Until those thresholds, Notion CRM handles the job for $0/month versus $50-800/month for alternatives.
