The Complete Freelance Business Setup Checklist (2026) — 40 Things to Do

May 2026 · 10 min read · FreelanceOS

Starting a freelance business feels overwhelming because there's no onboarding document. Nobody hands you a checklist. You figure it out through trial and error — until you don't have to. This 40-item checklist covers every dimension of setting up a professional freelance operation, from legal basics to your first client.

Work through it in order, or jump to the sections most relevant to where you are right now. Check each item off in your own Notion workspace as you complete it.

Section 1 — Legal (5 items)

Register your business structureSole trader is simplest and quickest. LLC / Ltd gives liability protection. Research what applies in your country — most freelancers start as sole traders.
Open a dedicated business bank accountMixing personal and business finances makes tax time painful and looks unprofessional. Open a separate account from day one — many online banks have free business accounts.
Set up a basic contract templateYou need a signed contract for every project. Include scope, payment terms, IP ownership, revision limits, and termination. Use a legal template from your country's freelancer union or a lawyer.
Establish your invoicing formatYour invoice needs: business name, client name, invoice number, line items, total, due date, and payment method. Test it with your first invoice before you need it urgently.
Register for taxUnderstand your self-employment tax obligations. In most countries you need to register as self-employed and file annually. Set aside 25–30% of every payment for taxes from day one.

Section 2 — Financial (5 items)

Set up a simple accounting systemWave (free) or a spreadsheet tracks income and expenses. You need this for taxes, for knowing your profitability, and for making business decisions. Keep receipts.
Get a dedicated business card or payment methodEvery business purchase goes on the business card. This makes tax deductions obvious and keeps records clean.
Set up quarterly tax estimatesIn most countries, self-employed people pay tax quarterly or twice a year. Missing these payments leads to penalties. Set a calendar reminder and put the money aside monthly.
Build a 3-month emergency fundFreelance income is irregular. If you don't have 3 months of expenses saved, a slow month can become a crisis. Build this before you rely on freelancing full-time.
Get professional liability insuranceAlso called errors and omissions insurance. If a client claims your work caused them financial harm, this covers you. It's inexpensive and increasingly expected by corporate clients.

Section 3 — Tools & Systems (8 items)

Project management: NotionYour command centre for projects, clients, tasks, and notes. FreelanceOS Pro gets you set up in 15 minutes.
Invoicing toolWave (free), FreshBooks, or Invoice Ninja. You need automatic numbering, PDF export, and payment reminders.
Time trackingToggl (free) for logging hours by project. Essential if you bill hourly and useful even if you don't — it reveals how long work actually takes so you can price better.
Client communicationPick one: email-first with optional Slack per client. Define your communication norms and stick to them. Don't let every client pull you into a different app.
File storage and sharingGoogle Drive or Dropbox. Create a consistent folder structure for every client from day one.
Professional emailyourname@yourdomain.com. Gmail with a custom domain costs ~$6/month and instantly looks more professional than gmail.com addresses.
Calendar with booking linkGoogle Calendar or Calendly. A booking link eliminates 5-email scheduling chains. Set your availability and send the link.
Password manager1Password or Bitwarden. Every client will give you logins. Never store passwords in plain text, in spreadsheets, or in Notion.

Section 4 — Online Presence (7 items)

Portfolio website or pageA single page showing who you help, what you do, and 3–5 examples of your work. Doesn't need to be fancy. Notion, Carrd, or Webflow all work.
LinkedIn profile fully optimisedHeadline: who you help + how. About section: what you do and how to hire you. Featured section: portfolio links. Connection requests sent to 50+ relevant people.
Relevant platform profilesGitHub for developers, Behance/Dribbble for designers, Upwork if you're starting out. Go where your clients look for people like you.
Professional email addressSee tools section — done.
Email signatureName, title, phone, website, LinkedIn. Set it once and forget it. Every email is a touchpoint.
Google Business ProfileIf you serve local clients, claim and complete your Google Business Profile. It's free and improves local search visibility.
Directory listingsClutch, Bark, or industry-specific directories. One well-filled profile per relevant directory is better than ten ignored ones.

Section 5 — Client Acquisition (6 items)

Define your ideal client profileIndustry, company size, budget range, decision-maker role, common problems, where they hang out online. The more specific you are, the easier marketing becomes.
Create 3 case studies or project examplesEven if they're self-initiated or free projects, document the problem, your approach, and the result. Concrete examples close more clients than vague claims.
Set your rate card and pricing tiersHave a clear answer to "what do you charge?" — ideally with 2–3 tiers so clients can self-select. Vague pricing loses clients before the conversation starts.
Build a referral ask processAfter a successful project, ask: "Do you know anyone else who might benefit from [what you did]?" Most referrals happen when you ask directly. Build it into your offboarding.
LinkedIn outreach sequenceConnect with 10 ideal client profiles per week. Send a personalised note. Follow up once after one week. No pitching in the first message — build the relationship first.
Cold email templateA concise, personalised email: who you are, what you do, one specific observation about their business, and one low-friction next step. Test it on 10 people before iterating.

Section 6 — Operations (5 items)

Weekly review ritualFriday afternoon: review completed work, outstanding invoices, upcoming deadlines, and plan next week. 20–30 minutes. Non-negotiable.
Monthly revenue reviewTotal income, by client, by service type. Is the trend up? What's your most profitable service? Where did you lose time?
Quarterly business reviewBigger picture: pricing, client mix, services offered, marketing effectiveness. Make one strategic change per quarter based on what you learn.
Annual goal settingTarget income, target client type, skills to build, life goals. Freelancing is a vehicle — get clear on where you're driving it.
Reading and learning habit30 minutes per week on business, craft, or industry knowledge. The best freelancers invest in themselves continuously.

Section 7 — Admin Templates (4 items)

Contract templateReview it annually and update for anything you've learned the hard way.
Proposal templateProject overview, deliverables, timeline, investment, next steps. Personalise the top third for each client.
Invoice templatePre-filled with your details, ready to customise per project. Test the payment link before sending to a real client.
Welcome packetWhat to expect working with you, your process, communication norms, revision policy. Sent to every new client after signing.
