The OKR Method for Freelancers

OKR — Objectives and Key Results — is the goal-setting framework used by Google, Intel, and now thousands of individual freelancers and solopreneurs. It works because it separates the ambitious direction (the Objective) from the concrete measurements that define success (the Key Results).

How OKRs Work

An Objective is qualitative, ambitious, and inspirational: "Build a client base I'm proud of" or "Establish myself as the go-to freelance copywriter for SaaS companies." It answers: what do I want to achieve?

Key Results are 3–5 measurable outcomes that define what achieving the objective would look like: "Revenue exceeds $7,000/month for three consecutive months" or "Land 2 clients in my target niche by June 30." They answer: how will I know I've succeeded?

Example OKR — Q3 2026
🎯 Objective: Build a consistently profitable freelance business
KR1: Monthly revenue exceeds $6,500 for all three months of Q3
KR2: Effective hourly rate reaches $85+ across all projects
KR3: 80% of Q3 revenue comes from returning clients or referrals (not cold outreach)
KR4: Raise minimum project rate to $2,500 by August 1

In Notion, create one entry per Key Result in your Goals database, all linked to a parent Objective (using a Relation property that points to itself, or a separate Objectives page). Check off Key Results as you achieve them. Update a Progress % based on measurable progress.

OKR Rule

OKRs should be slightly uncomfortable — if you're 100% confident you'll hit all your Key Results, they're not ambitious enough. The sweet spot is about 70% confidence: you'll definitely hit some, probably hit most, and might hit all if things go well.

Writing SMART Goals in Notion

SMART goals work alongside OKRs — use the SMART criteria to quality-check each Key Result before committing it to your tracker. A Key Result that fails the SMART test is usually a red flag that it won't drive meaningful action.

S
Specific
Is it clear exactly what you're trying to achieve? "Earn more" is not specific. "Earn $7,500 in August" is specific. Bad: "Get better at client communication." Good: "Implement a weekly client update system by July 15."
M
Measurable
Can you produce a number that objectively shows progress? Revenue, client count, projects completed, hours, words written, streak days — whatever it is, it should resolve to a number. "Feel less stressed" is not measurable. "Maintain a 30-hour work week" is.
A
Achievable
Is this possible given your current resources, time, and context? OKRs should be stretch goals (70% confidence), but impossible goals (0% confidence) aren't motivating — they're depressing. Ground your targets in what you know about your own trajectory.
R
Relevant
Does this goal connect to your broader objectives and life priorities? Goals that don't connect to anything larger tend to get deprioritized when things get busy — and rightfully so. Every goal should trace back to a "why."
T
Time-bound
Does it have a specific deadline? Without a deadline, "someday" goals live in purgatory indefinitely. "By Q3 end" or "Before October 1" creates the urgency that moves goals from intentions to plans.

Tracking Cadence: Daily, Weekly, Quarterly

A goal tracker is only as good as your review rhythm. Here's the cadence that keeps goals alive and connected to your work:

Daily
The Connection Check
During your morning planning (5 minutes): look at your Top 3 tasks for the day. For at least one, ask: which active goal does this serve? If none of your top priorities connect to an active goal, something is wrong — either your tasks are reactive or your goals are disconnected from your real work.
Weekly
The Progress Pulse
During your Sunday planning session (15–20 minutes): scroll through active goals and note what moved in the past 7 days. Did any goals advance? Which goals have been untouched for two+ weeks? Those are your warning signals — either reprioritize or mark as Paused and remove them from your active attention.
Quarterly
The Full OKR Review
Every 90 days (60–90 minutes): score each Key Result (0–100% achieved), assess the overall objective, capture your biggest lessons, then set new OKRs for the next quarter. This is the most important planning session of the quarter — it connects your operational work to your strategic direction.

Goal Categories for Freelancers

Freelancers need goals across five categories. Focusing only on income goals is the most common mistake — it optimizes the business while neglecting the skills, systems, and personal sustainability that make the income possible long-term.

1. Income & Financial Goals

Annual revenue target, monthly revenue floor, effective hourly rate target, savings rate, emergency fund. These are the numbers most freelancers start with. They're important, but they're outputs of everything else — work on the inputs (below) and the income follows.

2. Business Development Goals

New clients in target niche, referral partnerships established, productized service launched, testimonials collected, portfolio case studies published. These are the sales and marketing activities that fill your pipeline with better work at better rates.

3. Skill & Portfolio Goals

New capability developed, certification earned, portfolio piece completed in a new format, industry knowledge deepened. Skills are the foundation of everything — raising rates without improving skills is a temporary trick. Improving skills gives you permanent leverage.

4. Operations & Systems Goals

Proposal process streamlined, client onboarding system built, invoicing automated, proposal win rate improved. Operational goals make the business more efficient and scalable — each hour you save on admin is an hour you can give to billable work or genuine rest.

5. Business Milestones

Hit first $10k month, transition from generalist to specialist, raise minimum rate to a target number, fire a problematic client type, launch a product or course. Milestones are the meaningful waypoints that mark professional growth — the moments you'll remember and reference in future annual reviews.

Building Accountability Into the System

External accountability multiplies the effectiveness of any goal system. Inside Notion, you can build accountability with structure:

  • The "Last Reviewed" date: Any goal where Last Reviewed is more than 30 days ago is at risk of becoming decoration. A filtered view showing "Active goals not reviewed in 30+ days" creates automatic accountability.
  • Public goals page: Some freelancers create a public Notion page with their quarterly goals — visible to clients, collaborators, or a small community. Public commitments are dramatically more likely to be honored.
  • Accountability partner check-in: Share your quarterly goals with one trusted peer at the start of each quarter. Brief weekly check-ins (5 minutes async via voice note or text) are more effective than elaborate accountability systems.
  • Goal retrospective log: Keep a running "Retrospective" text property on each goal. After each weekly review, add one sentence: what moved, what's stuck, what you're learning. Over a quarter, this becomes a rich log of your growth process — and a reference for the OKR scoring session.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I build a goal tracker in Notion?
Build a Notion goal tracker by creating a Goals database with these properties: Goal Title (text), Category/Life Area (select), Type (Annual/Quarterly/Project), Status (Active/Achieved/Paused/Dropped), Start Date, Target Date, Progress % (number or formula), Key Results (text), and Review Date (date). Link goals to a Projects database so projects trace back to goals. Add multiple views: a board grouped by Category, a table filtered to Active goals, and a calendar view showing target dates.
What is the OKR method for goal setting?
OKR stands for Objectives and Key Results. An Objective is an ambitious, qualitative direction: 'Build a thriving freelance business.' Key Results are 3–5 measurable outcomes that define what achieving the objective looks like: 'Revenue exceeds $8,000/month by Q4,' 'Client retention rate above 80%,' 'Launch one productized service by June.' OKRs work because they separate the WHY (objective) from the WHAT (key results), making it easy to check whether you're actually making progress or just feeling busy.
How often should I review my goals in Notion?
Review goals at three cadences: (1) Weekly — a 5-minute check: are this week's tasks connected to any active goal? (2) Monthly — a 30-minute deep review: update progress on each active goal, note what moved, assess whether the goal still makes sense. (3) Quarterly — a full goal review: assess the quarter against your OKRs, set new quarterly goals, adjust annual goals based on what you've learned. Annual reviews complete the cycle. Most people skip the monthly review and wonder why their goals don't progress.
What goal categories should freelancers track?
The five most important goal categories for freelancers are: (1) Income goals — total revenue, per-project rates, income diversification. (2) Business development — new clients, referral partnerships, service launches. (3) Skill and portfolio — new capabilities, case studies, portfolio quality. (4) Operations — systems, tools, processes that make the business more efficient or scalable. (5) Personal development — health, learning, relationships, life quality. Goals should span all five categories, not just income.
What is the difference between a goal and a project in Notion?
Goals are outcomes you want to achieve — they describe a future state. Projects are initiatives with tasks that move you toward a goal — they describe activities you'll undertake. A goal might be 'Earn $10,000/month consistently by December.' Projects that serve that goal might be: 'Raise rates with existing clients,' 'Launch productized SEO audit service,' 'Develop 3 referral partnerships.' In Notion, link Projects to Goals via a Relation property so every project traces back to the goal it serves.
How do I track progress toward goals in Notion?
Track goal progress in Notion with a Progress % property (number, 0–100). Update it manually during weekly or monthly reviews, or auto-calculate it using a rollup of linked projects or tasks. For numeric goals (revenue target), use a formula: current_value / target_value * 100. For milestone goals, use a Select property (Not Started / In Progress / Complete) for each key result. Add a 'Last Updated' date property so you can see at a glance which goals haven't been reviewed recently.
Should I use SMART goals or OKRs in Notion?
SMART goals and OKRs are complementary, not competing. Use OKR structure at the annual and quarterly level — objectives set ambitious direction, key results define what success looks like. Use SMART criteria when writing each key result — it should be specific, measurable (has a number), achievable (not fantasy), relevant (connects to the objective), and time-bound (has a deadline). In Notion, your goal entries are the Key Results, written SMART, nested under Objectives as a parent goal.
