What to Look for in a Free Notion Template

Not all free Notion templates are created equal. Before you duplicate anything, run through this quick checklist:

1. Linked Databases (Not Just Pages)

A great Notion template uses relational databases — tables that talk to each other. A client database should link to a projects database, which links to tasks and invoices. If a template is just a bunch of styled pages with no linked views, it will feel impressive for a day and then fall apart when you need to actually manage real work.

2. Multiple Views Pre-Built

Look for templates that include Board, Calendar, Gallery, and List views already configured. Building these from scratch is tedious and easy to get wrong. A well-made template includes a Board view for visual workflow management, a Calendar view for deadlines, and a Table view for data entry.

3. Clear Property Structure

Every database should have sensible properties: Status (Select), Priority (Select), Due Date, Assignee, and relevant rollup/formula fields. Templates with only a Name column aren't templates — they're empty databases with a nice icon.

4. Documentation Inside the Template

The best templates include a "Start Here" or "How to Use" page that walks you through the system. This is especially important for complex freelance or business templates where the interconnected databases need a setup order to work correctly.

5. A Creator You Can Trust

Check if the template has a real author with other templates, a website, or a community. Templates from established creators get updated when Notion changes its features. One-off templates from unknown sources often become stale.

How to Import a Notion Template (Step by Step)

Importing a Notion template is straightforward once you know the steps. Here's the exact process:

  1. Get the duplicate link. Every shared Notion template has a URL that, when opened, shows a "Duplicate" button in the top right corner. This is the share link, not a file download — Notion templates live in Notion, not as files on your computer.
  2. Click "Duplicate." You'll be prompted to select which workspace to duplicate the template into. If you're logged in, your workspaces will appear as options. Choose the right one.
  3. Wait for duplication. Large templates with many databases and pages can take 30-90 seconds to fully duplicate. Don't close the tab — let it finish.
  4. Find it in your sidebar. The duplicated template will appear in your sidebar under "Private" pages. Move it to your preferred location in your workspace structure.
  5. Customize properties and data. Delete any sample data included in the template, then update the database properties to match your specific needs. Rename, reorder, and remove properties that don't apply to you.
  6. Read the setup guide. Any good template includes a "How to Use" or "Start Here" page. Read it before you start adding real data — it will save you time and prevent structural mistakes.

Pro tip: When you first duplicate a template, rename it immediately and move it out of the default location. Templates that sit in your sidebar unorganized become clutter within a week.

Common Mistakes When Using Notion Templates

Even great templates get misused. Here are the most common mistakes to avoid:

Customizing Before Using

The most common mistake is spending two hours redesigning a template before entering a single piece of real data. Use the template as-is for one week first. You'll discover what actually needs to change versus what just looks different from your mental image of the ideal system.

Adding Too Many Properties

Every new database property feels useful when you add it. After a month, half of them are empty because they're too much friction to fill in. Start with the properties in the template, and only add new ones when you find yourself needing them repeatedly.

Not Linking Databases

If you're using a project template and a separate client template as two independent systems, you're missing Notion's superpower. Link them so your project records include a relation to the client, and client records show a rollup of total project revenue. The power of Notion is the connections, not the individual databases.

Ignoring the Mobile App

Test your template on mobile before committing to it. Some templates with many side-by-side columns or complex gallery views are unusable on a phone. If you update tasks on the go, a mobile-friendly layout matters.
