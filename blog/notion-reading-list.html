The Ultimate Notion Reading List Template (Free Setup Guide 2026)

Build a Notion reading list that beats Goodreads — with book covers, 5 smart views, knowledge base linking, and reading goal tracking. Full database setup guide.

Goodreads tracks whether you finished a book. Notion tracks what you actually learned from it. That's the fundamental difference — and it's the reason thousands of readers are migrating from Goodreads and spreadsheets to custom Notion reading databases that function as part of a broader knowledge and productivity system.

A well-built Notion reading list does everything Goodreads does (track books, manage statuses, log ratings) and then goes further: it links books to the notes you took, connects insights to the projects you're working on, and turns your reading habit from passive consumption into active knowledge building.

This guide covers the complete setup: the right database properties, five essential views, book cover automation, second brain integration, and three template ideas for different types of readers.

Why Notion Beats Goodreads and Spreadsheets

Goodreads has 150 million books in its catalog and a thriving social layer — it's an excellent discovery tool. But as a personal reading management system, it has hard limits:

  • You can't add custom properties (no "Key Takeaways" field, no "Applied To" project link)
  • There's no connection to your notes, second brain, or productivity system
  • The views are fixed — you can't build a "Top Rated Business Books" filter
  • Social features add noise if you just want a private library

Spreadsheets solve the custom property problem but lack Notion's relational features, gallery views for cover images, and integration with the rest of your workspace. A Google Sheet of books is a list. A Notion reading database is a living knowledge asset.

The sweet spot for most readers: use Goodreads for discovery and social sharing, Notion for active reading management and knowledge capture.

Building Your Reading List Database: The Core Properties

Here are the essential properties to add when creating your Notion reading list database. Create a new full-page database in Notion (type /database and select "Full page database") and add these:

PropertyTypePurpose
TitleTitleBook title — the default Notion property
AuthorTextAuthor's full name for search and filtering
GenreMulti-selectCategories: Business, Fiction, Self-Help, History, Science, etc.
StatusSelectWant to Read / Reading / Finished / Abandoned
RatingSelect or Number1–5 star rating (use ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ emojis in select options for visual appeal)
Date StartedDateWhen you began the book
Date FinishedDateWhen you completed it — used for annual reading goal tracking
Key TakeawaysText3–5 bullet points of your most important insights from the book
Buy LinkURLAmazon or Bookshop.org link for quick gifting or referencing
Cover URLURLDirect image link to book cover — used in gallery view card preview
FormatSelectPhysical / Ebook / Audiobook — useful for identifying reading pace patterns
Recommended ByTextWho suggested the book — useful for tracking whose recommendations you trust
Notes PageRelationLinks to a separate Notes database for detailed book notes (optional but powerful)
Property Tip

Don't add all properties at once and feel obligated to fill them all. Start with Title, Author, Status, and Rating. Add Key Takeaways and Date Finished once you're actually using the database. Add Cover URL and the Relation property when you want to level up. Complexity you don't use is clutter.

The 5 Views to Set Up for Your Reading List

This is where Notion's power becomes obvious. Five views, one database — each showing the same books through a different lens.

1
All Books — Gallery View
View type: Gallery. Card preview: Cover URL property (or Page Cover if you set covers on individual pages). Sort by Date Added, descending. No filters. This is your visual bookshelf — scrolling through it gives the satisfying sense of a real library. Set card size to Medium for the best balance of cover visibility and density. This is your default home view for the reading list.
2
Currently Reading — Filtered View
View type: List or Gallery. Filter: Status = Reading. Sort by Date Started, oldest first (so you see which book you've been reading longest). Show properties: Author, Date Started, Format. This is your active reading dashboard — typically 1–3 books max. If you see 6 books in this view, you're juggling too many at once and likely finishing none of them.
3
Want To Read — Prioritized Queue
View type: List. Filter: Status = Want to Read. Sort by a manual Priority property (if you add one) or Date Added, descending. Show properties: Author, Genre, Recommended By. Add this view to your Notion sidebar for quick access when you finish a book and want to pick the next one. If your "Want to Read" list exceeds 50 books, consider being more selective — not every book you think you should read is one you actually want to read.
4
By Genre — Grouped View
View type: Gallery or Board. Group by: Genre. No filter. This view shows your entire reading history organized by topic — all your Business books in one column, all Fiction in another. Useful for spotting imbalances ("I've read 30 business books and 0 science books this year") and finding books to recommend when someone asks for a read in a specific area. Works best in Board view for a visual overview of your genre distribution.
5
Top Rated — Sorted by Rating
View type: Gallery or Table. Filter: Status = Finished. Sort by Rating, descending. This is your "best of" list — the books you'd recommend without hesitation. Share this view (via a public Notion share link) instead of a Goodreads shelf when people ask what you recommend. Sort by Genre within this view to answer "what's the best business book you've read?" instantly. This view also reveals your rating tendencies: if everything is 4–5 stars, you need a stricter scale.

How to Add Book Covers Automatically

Book covers transform a reading list database from a spreadsheet into something genuinely beautiful to use. Here's the most reliable workflow:

Method 1: Amazon Cover URL

Go to Amazon and search for the book. Right-click the cover image and select "Copy image address." Paste this URL into the Cover URL property in your Notion entry. This works for the vast majority of books and gives you high-quality covers. Note: Amazon occasionally changes image URLs, which can break the link — check periodically for broken covers.

Method 2: Google Books API

For a more stable approach, use Google Books: search for a book at books.google.com, open the book's detail page, and copy the cover image URL from the page source or by right-clicking the cover. Google Books cover URLs tend to be more stable than Amazon's. Alternatively, use the Open Library (openlibrary.org) which provides direct cover image URLs in a documented format: https://covers.openlibrary.org/b/isbn/[ISBN]-L.jpg — replace [ISBN] with the book's ISBN number.

Setting Up the Gallery View to Show Covers

Once you have Cover URL populated for a few books: in your Gallery view, click the three dots at the top right, select "Properties," then set "Card preview" to "Cover URL" (or "Page cover" if you've set page covers individually). The gallery now shows book cover images instead of empty cards. Properties shown on the card: keep it to 2–3 maximum — Author, Status, and Rating is a clean set.

Time Saver

If cover hunting feels tedious, just skip it for new entries and batch-update covers once a month. Even 40% cover completion makes the gallery view dramatically more visually engaging. Perfect shouldn't be the enemy of useful.

Linking Your Reading List to a Second Brain / Knowledge Base

This is where a Notion reading list becomes genuinely more powerful than any alternative. By linking your reading list to a knowledge base, every book becomes a source node in your personal knowledge graph.

The Setup

Add a Relation property to your reading list database that points to a Notes or Knowledge Base database. If you don't have one yet, create a simple Notes database with properties: Note Title (title), Topic (multi-select), Source (relation to Books), Date (date), and Content (text).

For each book, create linked note entries in the Knowledge Base — one entry per major concept or takeaway, tagged by topic. Now your Knowledge Base becomes searchable by topic: filter for all notes tagged "Pricing Strategy" and you'll see insights from every business book, article, and podcast that touched on pricing.

The Power Move: Linking Insights to Active Projects

For maximum utility, add a second Relation property in your Notes database that links to a Projects database. When you read something directly applicable to a current project — a negotiation tactic for a client pitch, a pricing framework for your rate revision — link that insight to the relevant project page. The insight now appears in two places: your knowledge base (for future reference) and your project page (where you actually need it right now).

This turns passive reading into active capability building. Books stop being things you consumed and started being resources you're actively using.

Bonus: Podcast Episode Tracker Variant

The same database structure works for podcasts with minor modifications. Add or change these properties:

  • Show Name (text) — the podcast's name
  • Episode Number (number) — for numbered episodes
  • Duration (text) — "1h 22min" etc.
  • Platform (select) — Spotify / Apple Podcasts / YouTube
  • Status options become: In Queue / Listening / Finished

You can either create a separate Podcasts database or combine it with your reading list using a Content Type property (select: Book / Podcast / Article / Video). Separate databases keep things cleaner; combined databases let you see all your "input" — books, podcasts, articles — in one place and filter across all of it.

3 Reading List Template Ideas

📊
Business Books Only
Focus on Genre = Business/Marketing/Strategy/Entrepreneurship. Add extra properties: Business Area (select: Marketing/Sales/Finance/Operations/Leadership), Applied To (relation to Projects), and Action Items (text). The goal isn't just to read business books — it's to extract specific tactics and apply them. Your "Applied To" links make this immediately visible.
📖
Fiction Tracker
Optimized for pleasure reading. Add Series (text), Book Number in Series (number), and Mood (select: Light/Dark/Intense/Cozy). Drop Key Takeaways (fiction doesn't usually produce structured takeaways). Keep Rating prominently displayed. Add a "Would Reread" checkbox. Gallery view with covers is especially rewarding for fiction — it's a visual record of your literary life.
🎓
Learning Library
For deliberate skill building. Add Skill (select: Design/Copywriting/SEO/Finance/Coding/etc.), Difficulty (select: Beginner/Intermediate/Advanced), and Exercises Completed (checkbox). Connect strongly to a Knowledge Base. Track not just what you read but what you practiced. This template treats books as courses — the rating system shifts to "Did this make me measurably better?" rather than enjoyment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is Notion better than Goodreads for a reading list?
Notion beats Goodreads for readers who want a connected knowledge system. Goodreads is great for book discovery and social reading. Notion wins when you want custom properties, book notes linked to projects, integration with a second brain or PKM system, and views Goodreads can't offer. Many readers use both: Goodreads to discover and rate books publicly, Notion to capture insights and connect reading to their work.
What properties should a Notion reading list have?
The essential properties for a Notion reading list are: Title (title type), Author (text), Status (select: Want to Read / Reading / Finished / Abandoned), Rating (number 1-5 or select), Genre (multi-select), Date Started, Date Finished, Key Takeaways (text), Buy Link (URL), and Cover URL. Optional power properties: Recommended By, Format (Physical/Ebook/Audiobook), Pages (number), and a Relation to a Notes or Knowledge Base database.
How do I add book covers to my Notion reading list?
Add a URL property called 'Cover URL' to your database. For each book, find the cover image on Amazon (right-click the cover and copy image address) or use Open Library (covers.openlibrary.org/b/isbn/[ISBN]-L.jpg). Paste the URL in the Cover URL property. In your gallery view, go to view settings and set 'Card Preview' to the Cover URL property. The gallery displays cover images automatically.
How do I connect my Notion reading list to a second brain?
Add a Relation property in your Books database that links to your Notes or Knowledge Base database. For each book, create linked notes entries tagging insights by topic (Productivity, Marketing, Mindset). You can then filter your Knowledge Base by topic to find all insights from books on a subject. This transforms a simple tracker into a knowledge management system that makes you smarter at work.
What is the best view for a Notion reading list?
The best default view depends on how you use it. Gallery view gives a visual bookshelf experience. Board view (grouped by Status) shows your full pipeline. Table view is best for sorting — sort by Rating to find your best reads, filter by Genre for topical discovery. Most readers set up 3–4 views and switch between them.
Can I track a podcast listening list in Notion?
Yes. The same database structure works perfectly for podcasts. Add Show Name (text), Episode Number (number), Duration (text), Platform (select: Spotify/Apple/YouTube). Status options become: In Queue / Listening / Finished. You can either create a separate Podcasts database or combine it with your reading list using a Content Type property (Book / Podcast / Article / Video) to filter between types.
How do I set a reading goal in Notion?
Create a 'Reading Goals 2026' page. Add a Callout block with your target ('Goal: Read 24 books in 2026'). Below it, add a linked view of your Books database filtered to Status = Finished and Date Finished = this year. Click Calculate at the bottom of any column and select Count All — this shows how many books you've finished this year. Update manually or use a formula to calculate the progress percentage automatically.
