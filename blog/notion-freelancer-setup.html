The Core Principle: Everything Connects

The reason most Notion setups fail is that they're built as collections of separate pages and databases that don't talk to each other. You have a client list over here, a project tracker over there, and a to-do list somewhere else. When you want to know everything about Client X, you have to manually check three places.

A properly set up freelance Notion workspace is built on relations. Every database connects to the relevant others. Clients connect to Projects. Projects connect to Tasks and Invoices. This means opening a single client record shows you all their projects, all their invoices, all their tasks — without navigating away. That's the system worth building.

Phase 1: The Five Core Databases

A complete freelance Notion setup needs exactly five databases. Not more — adding databases for things you don't actually need to track creates maintenance burden without value. Not fewer — cutting any of these five creates gaps that show up at inconvenient moments.

Database 1: Clients

Your client database is the foundation of everything else. It tracks who you work for, the status of each relationship, where the client came from, and links to all their work.

Key fields: Client Name, Status (Lead / Proposal Sent / Active / Past / Paused / Nurture), Primary Contact, Email, Phone, Source (Referral / Inbound / Outbound / Platform), Last Contacted date, Notes, and Relations to Projects and Invoices.

The critical view: a Board view grouped by Status. This is your pipeline, your renewal radar, and your follow-up queue all in one place. Open it every Monday morning for a five-minute review.

Database 2: Projects

Every piece of work you do for a client is a project. Each project has a start date, deadline, status, budget, and links to the client above it and the tasks and invoices below it.

Key fields: Project Name, Client (Relation), Status (Scoping / Active / Review / Complete / On Hold), Start Date, Deadline, Budget, Type (your service categories), and a Notes section for scope details and important context.

The critical view: a Timeline or Calendar view sorted by Deadline. This is your capacity planner — it shows you at a glance whether your next four weeks are manageable or whether you've overcommitted.

Add a Progress percentage field to your Projects database. Even if you only update it weekly, seeing a project at 80% complete signals that an invoice and a renewal conversation are coming up. This field has a disproportionate effect on your ability to stay ahead of cash flow.

Database 3: Tasks

Tasks are the atomic unit of your work. They link to projects (and through projects, to clients). They have due dates, priorities, and status fields. This is your daily work queue — the place where "what should I be doing right now?" gets answered.

Key fields: Task Name, Project (Relation), Status (To Do / In Progress / Review / Done), Priority (High / Medium / Low), Due Date, and Estimated Time.

The critical view: a Table view filtered to Status is not Done, sorted by Due Date ascending. This is your daily task list — the filtered view that shows you only what needs to get done, in the order it's due. You should open this view every morning.

Database 4: Invoices

Every invoice you issue gets a record here. Invoices link to both Clients and Projects, giving you a complete financial picture at both levels.

Key fields: Invoice Number, Client (Relation), Project (Relation), Amount, Status (Draft / Sent / Paid / Overdue / Partial), Issue Date, Due Date, Paid Date.

The critical view: a Table view filtered to Status = Sent or Overdue, sorted by Due Date ascending. This is your collections dashboard — the list of outstanding invoices you need to follow up on.

Database 5: Weekly Goals (or Goals + Review)

This is the most commonly omitted database, and the most commonly regretted omission. A weekly goals database gives you a place to set 3–5 priorities at the start of each week and review them at the end. Without it, weeks blur together and you lose the sense of whether you're making progress on things that matter versus just staying busy.

Key fields: Week (a date field representing the week start), Goals (a text field listing the week's priorities), Wins (what went well), Learnings (what to improve), and a Rating (1–5 on the week overall).

This doesn't have to be a complex database. Some freelancers use a single recurring page with a template — new entry each Monday, review each Friday. The habit matters more than the structure.

Phase 2: Setting Up the Relations

Once all five databases exist, the next step is connecting them. This is where Notion beginners often get confused — and where the system either comes together or falls apart.

  1. Connect Projects to Clients: In the Projects database, add a Relation property and select the Clients database. Enable bidirectional sync so the client record also shows linked projects.
  2. Connect Tasks to Projects: In the Tasks database, add a Relation property and select the Projects database. Enable bidirectional sync.
  3. Connect Invoices to Clients: In the Invoices database, add a Relation property and select the Clients database. Enable bidirectional sync.
  4. Connect Invoices to Projects: Add a second Relation property in Invoices and select the Projects database. Enable bidirectional sync.
  5. Add Rollup fields to Clients: In the Clients database, add Rollup properties that calculate: Total Invoiced (sum of Invoice amounts), Total Collected (sum where Status = Paid), and Count of Projects.

After these five steps, every database in your workspace is connected. Opening a client record shows you their projects, their invoices, and their total revenue contribution — all automatically maintained as you add data.

Phase 3: Your Home Dashboard

With five databases built and connected, you need a Home page that surfaces the most important views without requiring you to navigate through the workspace sidebar every time.

A well-designed Home dashboard for freelancers includes:

  • Today's Tasks — a linked database view of Tasks, filtered to Due Date = Today
  • This Week's Tasks — Tasks filtered to Due Date is within the next 7 days
  • Active Projects — Projects filtered to Status = Active
  • Outstanding Invoices — Invoices filtered to Status = Sent or Overdue
  • Client Pipeline — Clients in Board view, grouped by Status
  • This Week's Goals — a linked view of the current week's entry in the Goals database

This page becomes your daily operating view — the one page you open when you start work and the one page that tells you what needs your attention right now.

Use Notion's "Synced Block" feature to keep your most important callout blocks (like "Top 3 Priorities This Week") synchronized across multiple pages. Write them once; they update everywhere. This is especially useful for goals and key metrics you want visible no matter which page you're working from.

Phase 4: Building the Weekly Rhythm

The workspace is only as good as the habits that use it. The most effective freelancers using Notion have a weekly rhythm — a predictable set of routines that keep the system up to date and the business moving forward.

Monday Morning (15 minutes)

  • Open the Client Pipeline board. Move any cards that have changed status.
  • Check the Follow-Up Queue for any leads or proposals you need to contact this week.
  • Open the Active Projects view. Are any deadlines sneaking up this week?
  • Set this week's goals in the Goals database. Write 3–5 specific priorities, not a wishlist.
  • Review your task list and add any tasks needed to hit this week's goals.

Daily (5 minutes)

  • Open the "Today's Tasks" view on your Home dashboard.
  • Mark tasks done as you complete them.
  • If a new task comes in, add it immediately with the right project and due date.

Friday Afternoon (15 minutes)

  • Update project progress percentages.
  • Check outstanding invoices — send follow-ups on anything more than 7 days late.
  • Write your weekly review: what went well, what didn't, what to carry forward.
  • Clear your task list — anything not completed either gets a new due date or gets deleted.

Common Setup Mistakes to Avoid

Building too many databases up front. It's tempting to create databases for everything: reading lists, meeting notes, idea captures, content calendars. Start with the five core databases and add others only when you have a specific, recurring need that the existing system doesn't cover.

Ignoring the relations. A Notion workspace with five disconnected databases is no better than five separate spreadsheets. The relations are the entire point — don't skip them.

Overcomplicating the views. Ten views per database sounds thorough; in practice, you'll use two or three and ignore the rest. Start with one primary view per database and add filtered views only when you catch yourself manually filtering to find the same subset repeatedly.

Not adding real data immediately. New workspaces feel abstract and clinical until there's actual data in them. Your first act after setting up should be entering all your current clients, all active projects, and all outstanding invoices. The system becomes real the moment it has real data.
