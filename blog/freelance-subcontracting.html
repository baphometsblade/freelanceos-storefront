Freelance Subcontracting: When to Outsource Work and How to Manage Subcontractors

By FreelanceOS · July 2026 · 10 min read

What Is Freelance Subcontracting?

Subcontracting means hiring another freelancer or contractor to perform some or all of the work on a client project, while you remain the primary contract holder with the client. You become the subcontractor's client — your client doesn't have a direct relationship with the person actually doing (part of) the work.

This model is more common than most freelancers realize. Web developers subcontract copywriting. Designers subcontract development. Marketing consultants subcontract content production. When a client needs a complete website package from a solo developer, subcontracting makes delivering that scope possible without turning down the project or hiring a full-time employee.

Why subcontract at all? You've taken on more work than you can handle alone. You need a skill you don't have. You want to grow past the "one person = one billable hour" revenue ceiling. A project requires multiple specializations you can't provide solo. Subcontracting solves all four problems simultaneously.

The Legal Side: What You Need to Know Before You Hire a Sub

Check Your Client Contract First

Before you hire any subcontractor, read your client contract for a "personal services" clause or language about subcontracting. Some client agreements prohibit subcontracting entirely. Others require written consent. If your contract is silent on the matter, subcontracting is generally permitted — but when in doubt, ask your client directly. It's easier to get permission upfront than to explain a "subcontractor situation" mid-project.

The Subcontractor Agreement

Never bring on a subcontractor without a written agreement, even on short projects with people you know. Your subcontractor agreement should include:

  • Scope of services — exactly what the sub is doing (not "the project" — the specific deliverables and revisions included)
  • Payment terms — rate (hourly or fixed), payment schedule, and what triggers payment (milestone completion, delivery approval)
  • IP assignment — all work product created by the sub belongs to you (and through you, to your client). This is non-negotiable. Without it, the sub may retain copyright.
  • Confidentiality / NDA — the sub will access client information. Require confidentiality obligations matching or exceeding what your own client contract requires
  • Non-solicitation clause — prevents the sub from approaching your client directly after the project. Typically 12-24 months post-project.
  • Timeline and revision process — delivery dates, number of revision rounds, and what constitutes an acceptable deliverable

Subcontractor vs. Employee Classification

This is the most serious legal risk in subcontracting. If your subcontractor works exclusively for you, follows a schedule you set, uses equipment you provide, or has no other clients — tax authorities (IRS in the US, HMRC in the UK) may reclassify them as employees. This triggers payroll tax obligations, benefits requirements, and potential back taxes.

True subcontractors: set their own hours, work for multiple clients, use their own equipment, and have meaningful control over how they deliver results. If your arrangement doesn't look like this, consult an accountant or employment lawyer before proceeding.

US tax note: Issue a Form 1099-NEC to any US-based subcontractor you pay $600 or more in a calendar year. Collect their W-9 before you pay them — getting it after is an administrative headache. Use accounting software (Wave, QuickBooks, FreshBooks) to track sub payments and generate 1099s at year-end.

Finding Quality Subcontractors

1. Your Professional Network (Best Option)

Other freelancers you've worked with, collaborated with informally, or met through professional communities are your best subcontractor candidates. You know their work quality. They know your standards. The trust foundation already exists. The biggest mistake new subcontractors make is starting with strangers from platforms — start with people you know or can get a warm introduction to.

2. Niche Freelance Communities

Every freelance specialty has active Slack groups, Discord servers, and forums where experienced practitioners gather. If you're a developer looking to subcontract copywriting, the Copyhackers community or Copy Hackers Slack is full of vetted copywriters. If you're a designer looking for a developer, a specific tech stack community (Vue.js Discord, React community) will have freelancers. The signal-to-noise ratio is much better than general platforms because members self-select into communities by specialty.

3. Freelance Platforms (With Vetting)

Upwork, Contra, Toptal, and PeoplePerHour all work — but now you are the client doing the vetting. Review their portfolio, check reviews from other clients, conduct a brief interview, and always start with a small paid trial project before handing over a full client engagement. Toptal pre-vets for technical skills (top 3% claimed), which reduces your vetting burden for development work.

How to Price Subcontracted Work

The margin model is the standard approach: you charge the client more than you pay the subcontractor, and keep the difference as your project management and coordination fee.

Example: Fixed-Price Website Project

Client price (total project)$5,000
Your strategy/design work (your time)$2,000
Subcontracted development$1,800
Project management overhead (your time)$400
Your gross margin on this project$800 (16%)

On hourly subcontracted work, a 20-50% markup is typical. If your subcontractor charges $60/hr for their work, you charge the client $80-90/hr for that resource. The markup covers: your project management time, client communication and briefing, quality review before delivery, business risk (you are liable if the sub fails to deliver), and your margin for building the business.

Price check: Don't over-inflate the subcontractor markup beyond what the market will bear. Clients do research. If you're billing a subcontractor at $120/hr when the market rate for that skill is $80/hr, and the client finds out, the trust damage to the relationship is severe. Keep markups reasonable and defensible.

Managing Subcontractors Without Losing Control

Write Detailed Briefs

The most common cause of subcontractor failure is an inadequate brief. You briefed the client once and understood the nuances through a relationship. Your subcontractor is starting cold. Write down everything: the client's goals, the target audience, the tone, the specific deliverables, what "done" looks like, and what the client has rejected on previous projects. Share the relevant parts of the client's original brief (redact anything the NDA requires you to keep confidential).

Milestone Check-Ins

Don't wait for the deadline to see the work. Set check-in points at logical project milestones — a rough draft review at 30%, a direction confirmation at 60%, final delivery at 100%. Early course corrections are cheap. Late course corrections (after the due date, when you need to deliver to the client) are expensive and stressful for everyone.

Quality Review Gate

Every deliverable from a subcontractor passes through your quality review before it reaches the client. No exceptions. You are the accountable party in the client relationship — the client doesn't know who did the work, and it doesn't matter to them. What matters is that it meets the standard they hired you to meet. Review it as if you did it yourself.

Own the Client Relationship

In the standard subcontracting model, the client communicates exclusively with you — not with your subcontractor. This protects your relationship, protects your margin (the client doesn't learn your sub's rates), and keeps quality expectations managed through one voice. If the client ever asks to "speak with the developer" or "meet the designer," you can facilitate a brief introduction call, but all project communication flows through you.

When NOT to Subcontract

  • Your client contract prohibits subcontracting or requires prior consent you haven't obtained
  • Your project margin is too thin to cover the quality review time the work requires
  • The project is too complex to brief properly — the subcontractor needs context you can't transfer efficiently
  • You don't yet have a vetted network of subcontractors (don't start a client project as your subcontractor discovery exercise)
  • You're not ready for the management overhead — subcontracting adds coordination work; if you're already at capacity, adding subs may make things worse, not better

Starting small: Subcontract one non-critical component of one project to test the model before building a full sub network. A blog post from a copywriter. One feature from a developer. One icon set from a designer. Learn the briefing, review, and payment process at low stakes before scaling.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it legal to subcontract freelance work?
Subcontracting is legal in most situations, but you must check your client contract first. Some agreements include "personal services" clauses or require written consent before subcontracting. If your contract doesn't address subcontracting, it is generally permitted. Always have your subcontractor sign an NDA and a subcontractor agreement that includes IP assignment (all work product belongs to you) and a non-solicitation clause.
How do I find good freelance subcontractors?
Your professional network is the best source — other freelancers you've worked with or know through communities in your field. Secondary sources include niche Slack groups and Discord servers for your specialty, and platforms like Upwork, Contra, and Toptal. Always start with a small paid test project before committing a new subcontractor to a client engagement. Trust and quality are built over multiple projects, not guaranteed from a portfolio.
How much should I charge when subcontracting?
A markup of 20-50% on the subcontractor's rate is standard. If your sub charges $60/hr, you charge the client $80-90/hr for that work and keep the margin. The margin covers your project management time, client communication, quality review, and business risk. On fixed-price projects, agree on a fixed payment with your sub and keep the difference as your coordination fee. Keep markups defensible — over-inflating beyond market rates for that skill can damage client trust if discovered.
Do I need a contract with my subcontractor?
Yes, always — even for short projects with people you know and trust. A written subcontractor agreement should include: the scope of services and deliverables, payment terms and schedule, IP assignment (work product belongs to you), confidentiality and NDA obligations, a non-solicitation clause preventing the sub from approaching your client directly, and timeline and revision terms. A simple one-page agreement is far better than no agreement at all.
How do I manage subcontractors without losing control of quality?
Write detailed project briefs so the sub understands exactly what the client expects — context you absorbed through your client relationship needs to be explicitly documented for someone coming in cold. Set milestone check-ins rather than waiting for the final deadline. Review all subcontractor work before it goes to the client — you are accountable for quality, not your sub. Keep all client communication flowing through you so quality standards remain consistent and your client relationship stays intact.
