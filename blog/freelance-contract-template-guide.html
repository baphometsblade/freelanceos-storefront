Freelance Contract Templates: What to Include and How to Use Them

Published May 8, 2026  ·  13 min read

A handshake is not a contract. An email thread is not a contract. A verbal agreement on a discovery call is not a contract. If you're doing client work without a written, signed agreement, you're operating on trust alone — and trust is not enforceable in a dispute.

This guide covers everything you need to know about freelance contracts: why they matter, the ten clauses that belong in every agreement, how to handle the situations that make contracts necessary, and what to do when things go wrong.

Why Every Freelancer Needs a Written Contract

The purpose of a contract is not to prepare for the worst-case scenario — it's to make the working relationship clear enough that the worst case becomes less likely. A good contract defines expectations so precisely that both parties know what "done" looks like, when money changes hands, and what happens if things go sideways. That clarity prevents misunderstandings from escalating into disputes.

"A designer completed a full brand identity system over six weeks — logo, typography, colour palette, brand guidelines, and all asset files. The client then said they weren't happy with the 'direction' and wanted to start over. No contract meant no defined revision process, no kill fee, and no legal recourse. The designer had already spent 80 hours of billable work with nothing to show for it."

This kind of story is common. Not because clients are systematically malicious, but because without a contract, everyone has a different mental model of what was agreed. The client genuinely believed they were entitled to a full redo. The designer genuinely believed revisions were limited and a restart would be a new project. Both were working from assumptions rather than a written agreement.

Important: This guide provides general information about freelance contracts and is not legal advice. For complex projects, high-value contracts, or specific legal questions, consult a qualified solicitor or attorney in your jurisdiction.

The 10 Essential Clauses Every Freelance Contract Must Have

Clause 1

Scope of Work — Be Painfully Specific

The scope of work section describes exactly what you're delivering — not in vague terms, but in specific, measurable language. "A website" is not a scope. "A five-page WordPress website including homepage, about page, services page, contact page, and blog page, built using the provided brand assets and content supplied by the client, with no custom development or e-commerce functionality" is a scope.

The test: if a stranger read this section and then saw the final deliverable, would they agree that the work matched the description? If not, it's not specific enough. Every line you leave vague is an argument waiting to happen.

Clause 2

Deliverables and Deadlines

List each deliverable separately with its expected delivery date. If the project has phases, list the phase deliverables and dates for each. This includes what you're delivering (e.g., "source files in AI and PDF format"), what format it'll be in, and when the client can expect it.

Also clarify client responsibilities that affect your deadlines — for example, "Deadlines are contingent on client providing requested content and feedback within 5 business days. Delays in client feedback may extend the project timeline accordingly."

Clause 3

Revision Policy — Number and Process

Define how many rounds of revisions are included and what constitutes a revision. A round of revisions is one consolidated set of feedback addressed in one round — not unlimited back-and-forth. Be explicit: "Two rounds of revisions are included. A revision round consists of one set of consolidated feedback provided by the client, addressed by the freelancer in one response. Additional revision rounds are billed at $[X]/hour."

Also define what a revision is versus a new request. Changing a headline is a revision. Adding a section that wasn't in the original scope is a new request and should be quoted separately.

Clause 4

Payment Terms — Amount, Schedule, Method, Late Fees

Specify the total project fee, when each payment is due, and the payment method. For most projects over $500, a deposit is standard — typically 25–50% upfront before work begins, with the remainder due on delivery or at defined project milestones.

Include late payment terms: "Invoices unpaid after [X] days are subject to a late fee of [X]% per month." This clause rarely needs to be invoked, but its presence encourages clients to pay on time. Also specify your preferred payment method and any currencies or fees that apply.

Clause 5

Kill Fee / Project Cancellation Clause

The kill fee protects you if a client cancels a project partway through. Without it, a client can pull the plug after you've done significant work and owe you nothing beyond what's already been paid. A standard kill fee structure: the deposit is non-refundable regardless of when the project is cancelled; if the project is cancelled after [X]% of work is complete, an additional fee equivalent to [X]% of the total project fee is due.

Some freelancers use a sliding scale: 25% cancelled = 25% of total fee due; 75% cancelled = 75% of total fee due. Whatever the structure, it needs to be written in the contract and agreed to before work begins.

Clause 6

Intellectual Property and Ownership Transfer

This is one of the most legally significant clauses and also one of the most commonly omitted. By default in many jurisdictions, you (the creator) own the copyright to work you create, even if you created it for a client. Ownership only transfers if it's explicitly granted in a written agreement.

Most clients assume they own everything you create for them the moment they pay. Your contract needs to clarify: does the client receive a licence to use the work (limited or unlimited), or does full ownership transfer? Ownership transfer should carry a higher fee than a usage licence. Specify what rights transfer, when (typically upon full payment), and whether you retain the right to display the work in your portfolio.

Clause 7

Confidentiality / NDA Clause

If your client shares business-sensitive information during the project — unreleased products, financial data, customer information, trade processes — they need to know you'll keep it confidential. Include a straightforward confidentiality clause: you won't disclose or use their confidential information for any purpose other than completing the project.

This clause protects the client relationship and signals that you understand professional norms. For high-stakes projects, a client may send their own NDA — review it carefully before signing, particularly around non-compete clauses that might restrict your ability to work with similar clients in the future.

Clause 8

Non-Solicitation (Optional but Worth Considering)

A non-solicitation clause prevents either party from directly recruiting the other's team members or employees for a defined period. This is more relevant for agency work or when you're working alongside a client's internal team. For standard freelance projects, it's optional. If you're working in an environment where you'll have significant access to the client's staff, it can prevent awkward situations down the line.

Clause 9

Dispute Resolution

If a dispute arises, this clause defines how it's resolved before going to court. Options include direct negotiation (attempt to resolve in good faith within X days), mediation (a neutral third party facilitates a resolution), or arbitration (a binding decision from a neutral arbitrator). Specifying dispute resolution in the contract saves both parties the cost and time of litigation over small-to-medium disputes.

At minimum, include: "In the event of a dispute, both parties agree to attempt resolution through good-faith negotiation for a period of 30 days before pursuing formal legal action."

Clause 10

Governing Law and Jurisdiction

Specify which country and state/region's laws govern the contract, and which courts have jurisdiction if legal action is required. For freelancers working internationally, this matters — you generally want your own jurisdiction specified so any dispute is heard in a location convenient to you. "This agreement is governed by the laws of [Your State/Country]" is the minimum needed.

Red Flags to Watch for Before Signing

  • Vague scope: Any deliverable description that could be interpreted multiple ways is a problem waiting to happen
  • No payment schedule: "Payment on completion" with no deposit puts all the financial risk on you
  • No kill fee: Clients who resist this clause should give you pause — it suggests they anticipate changing direction
  • Unlimited revisions: Some clients will request this expecting it means "until I'm happy," which has no logical endpoint
  • Broad IP clauses in client-provided contracts: Watch for language that assigns ownership of everything you create — including tools, templates, and processes that you brought to the project

How to Handle Contract Negotiations Without Losing the Client

Most clients don't negotiate contracts in bad faith — they negotiate because they don't understand why certain clauses are standard. When a client pushes back on your kill fee, deposit requirement, or revision policy, treat it as an education opportunity rather than a conflict.

Explain the business logic calmly: "The deposit ensures I can reserve time in my schedule for your project. Without it, I'd have to keep my calendar open indefinitely." Or: "The kill fee protects both of us — it gives you clarity on exit costs and means I can confidently commit to your timeline without holding other clients on standby."

A client who insists on removing standard protective clauses and won't accept a clear explanation is showing you something important about how they operate. That's useful information before you've committed time.

Digital Signing Options

Getting a signature is non-optional. These are the main options:

  • Dropbox Sign (formerly HelloSign): Free up to 3 requests/month. Clean interface, no client account required, legally binding, full audit trail
  • DocuSign: Industry standard, widely recognised, paid plans starting around $10/month
  • Bonsai: Purpose-built for freelancers, combines proposals, contracts, and invoicing in one platform
  • PDF via email: Technically valid in most jurisdictions if the client responds with typed acknowledgment. Keep the email thread. Less professional, no audit trail

When a client dispute arises — or even when you just need to check what revision round you're on — you have everything in one place in seconds.

What to Do When a Client Refuses to Sign

Some clients will push back on signing any contract, citing excessive formality or implied trust. This is your clearest signal about how the relationship will go. A client who won't commit to a written agreement is a client who isn't prepared to commit to clear terms.

Your options: present a stripped-down one-page agreement that covers scope, payment, and IP only (lower friction, still legally meaningful); offer to use their contract template if they have one; or decline the project. "I work with a standard contract on all projects — it protects us both" is a reasonable, professional position. A client who can't respect that is not a client you should take on without protection.
