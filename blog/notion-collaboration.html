Teams that run on too many tools are slow teams. There's a document in Google Docs, the project in Asana, meeting notes in Notion, company policies in Confluence, and no one quite knows where to look for the thing they need. Notion can replace all of this — and for most small to medium teams, it does it better and cheaper.

This guide covers everything you need to build a functioning shared Notion workspace: how to set it up, how to manage permissions, how to assign and track tasks, how to run meetings, and the five best practices that separate teams that thrive in Notion from those that abandon it after a month.

Who this guide is for: Teams of 2–50 people considering Notion as a primary collaboration tool, or currently using Notion but finding it disorganized and underutilized. Freelancers working with clients in shared Notion workspaces will also find the permissions and structure sections directly applicable.

1. Setting Up a Shared Notion Workspace

A Notion workspace is the top-level container that holds all your team's pages, databases, and content. Everyone invited to the workspace can see and interact with content based on the permissions they're assigned.

Step 1

Create or upgrade your workspace

If you're starting fresh, create a new Notion workspace (Settings & Members > Workspaces > Create). For team collaboration, you'll need at least the Notion Plus plan ($8/month per member). The free plan limits you to 10 guests and doesn't support full team features.

Step 2

Invite your team

Go to Settings & Members > Members > Add Members. Invite by email address. New members get the default workspace permission level — set this before inviting so they land in the right role. For external collaborators (clients, contractors), use the Guest role, which restricts access to only the pages you explicitly share with them.

Step 3

Set up your top-level page structure

Create 4–6 top-level sections in the sidebar to organize your workspace. A common structure for small teams: Home (dashboard and links), Projects (all active and archived projects), Wiki (company knowledge base), Meetings (notes and agendas), Resources (templates and reference), and Archive. Keep the top level simple — the sidebar becomes unwieldy fast when you create too many top-level pages.

Step 4

Create your first shared databases

Start with three core databases: a Projects database, a Tasks database, and a Meeting Notes database. These cover 80% of team collaboration needs. Avoid creating more databases than you'll actively maintain — an abandoned database clutters the workspace and erodes trust in the system.

Step 5

Write your workspace norms document

Before rolling out the workspace, write a one-page "How We Use Notion" document pinned to the top of your Home page. Define: where new projects go, how tasks are named, how meeting notes are stored, how the Archive works, and who to ask if you can't find something. Teams that skip this step end up with a disorganized workspace within 60 days.

2. Notion Permission Levels Explained

Getting permissions right is the most overlooked part of setting up a team Notion workspace. Too restrictive and the tool becomes a bottleneck. Too open and sensitive information leaks or gets accidentally deleted.

FULL ACCESS

Can edit, share, and delete the page and all subpages

Best for: workspace admins, team leads, and anyone who needs to restructure content. Use sparingly. In most teams, 1–3 people have Full Access at the workspace level.

EDIT

Can edit content, but cannot change sharing settings

Best for: most team members. This is the safe default — they can contribute and update content without accidentally sharing sensitive pages externally or restructuring the information architecture.

COMMENT

Can view content and add comments, but cannot edit

Best for: stakeholders who need visibility without editing rights (clients, executives, cross-functional reviewers). They can leave feedback without disrupting the document structure.

VIEW

Read-only access — cannot edit or comment

Best for: public-facing pages, external stakeholders who don't need to interact, or archived content you want accessible but not editable. Can also be shared as a public link without requiring a Notion account.

Permission hierarchy in practice

Permissions in Notion are inherited from parent pages but can be overridden at the page level. This means you can set the workspace default to Edit access, then lock down specific sensitive pages (HR policies, salary data, board materials) to Full Access for only the people who need them. This layered approach is how most teams manage permissions effectively without creating a complex permission matrix.

Guest vs. Member: Guests (external collaborators) can only see pages explicitly shared with them. Members can see the full workspace sidebar. When sharing with clients or contractors, always add them as Guests with Comment or View access to specific pages — never as Members, which would expose your entire workspace.

3. How to Structure Your Team Notion

The biggest mistake teams make in Notion is replicating their org chart in the sidebar. "Marketing," "Engineering," "Design," "HR" as top-level sections means cross-functional projects get fragmented across silos and no one can find things that span departments.

The recommended team structure

Organize around workflows, not departments:

  • Home — Team dashboard, quick links, current priorities, company announcements
  • Projects — All active projects in a single database, with Department and Status properties for filtering by team
  • Tasks — Cross-project task database, assignable to any team member
  • Meetings — Meeting notes database organized by date and meeting type
  • Wiki — Company knowledge base: processes, onboarding, policies, how-tos
  • Resources — Templates, brand assets, reference documents

Department pages vs. department databases

If departments genuinely need their own space, give each department a page within the Projects or Wiki section — not a top-level sidebar entry. Marketing lives inside Projects (filter: Department = Marketing) and inside Wiki (Marketing SOPs). This keeps the sidebar navigable and prevents the 20-item-sidebar problem that plagues most team workspaces.

4. Assigning Tasks and Tracking Work

Task management in Notion is built on databases. A simple Tasks database with the right properties handles the full task lifecycle for most small teams:

  • Name — Task title (required)
  • Status — Not Started / In Progress / In Review / Done / Blocked
  • Assignee — Person property (supports multiple assignees)
  • Due Date — Date property with reminder toggle
  • Priority — Select: Low / Medium / High / Critical
  • Project — Relation to the Projects database
  • Tags — Multi-select for flexible categorization

Creating personal "My Tasks" views

The power of a single shared task database comes from filtered views. Every team member should have a personal view filtered to Assignee = Me. This gives everyone a clean personal task list while keeping the data centralized. Create a Board view filtered to "In Progress" for standup discussions, a timeline view for managers overseeing delivery, and a "Due This Week" view filtered by date for weekly planning.

@mention for task notifications

Use @mention in task comments to ping team members: "Hey @Sarah, can you review the draft in this task by Thursday?" Notion sends an email notification to the mentioned person. This keeps task communication attached to the task itself rather than scattered across Slack threads.

Don't overengineer tasks: Many teams start with a simple task database and gradually add more properties until it takes 3 minutes to create a new task. The rule: add a property only when you'll actively filter or sort by it. A task database with 20 unused properties is more friction than no system at all.

5. Running Meetings in Notion

Meeting notes in a shared Notion database is one of the highest-value use cases for teams. Instead of notes scattered across personal apps and never referenced again, a shared meeting notes database becomes searchable institutional memory.

The meeting notes database structure

  • Meeting Name — Descriptive title (e.g., "Product Standup 2026-07-11")
  • Date — Date of the meeting
  • Meeting Type — Select: Standup / Planning / Review / 1:1 / All-Hands / Client Call
  • Attendees — Person property (multi-select)
  • Action Items — Relation to Tasks database
  • Recording — URL field for Loom or Zoom recording link

The meeting template (what to capture)

Each meeting page should follow a consistent template: Agenda (prepared before the meeting), Notes (live-updated during the meeting), Decisions Made (explicit list of decisions reached), Action Items (each with an owner and due date). Standardizing this structure means anyone can find what came out of a meeting 6 months later.

Before vs. during vs. after: The meeting notes page serves three phases. Before: write the agenda and share the link. During: take live notes in the same doc. After: clean up notes, create linked tasks for action items, and share the link in Slack or email. This single workflow replaces the chaotic "email meeting summary" ritual most teams use.

6. Notion for Async-First Teams

For remote and distributed teams, async collaboration is a competitive advantage. Notion is particularly well-suited to async work because everything is written, documented, and searchable by default — unlike Slack threads that disappear into history.

The async-first Notion setup

Replace standup calls with a Standup database where each team member fills in a brief daily update: What I did yesterday, What I'm doing today, Any blockers. Create a Board view grouped by Team Member for a quick scan of where everyone is. This saves 30 minutes of daily meetings and creates a searchable log of who worked on what.

Use Notion pages with comments for decisions that would otherwise require a meeting. Write the context, the options, and your recommendation in a page. Share it with relevant stakeholders. Use @mention to request input. Set a decision deadline. This "decision doc" approach works remarkably well for decisions that don't require real-time discussion.

8. Team Notion Best Practices

1
Designate a Notion admin. Assign one person to own the workspace structure — handle permission requests, enforce naming conventions, run quarterly audits, and archive stale content. Teams without a designated admin end up with a cluttered workspace within 90 days.
2
Template everything. Use Notion's built-in template functionality for recurring content — meeting notes, project briefs, onboarding checklists, weekly reports. When a team member opens a new page for the weekly standup, a pre-filled template should be waiting. This dramatically increases adoption and consistency.
3
Archive aggressively. Completed projects, old meeting notes, and obsolete wiki pages should move to an Archive section quarterly. A lean active workspace is faster to navigate and easier to maintain. Archive doesn't mean delete — everything stays searchable.
4
Keep the sidebar under 10 items. A sidebar with 25 entries signals organizational debt. Consolidate ruthlessly. Everything your team needs in a given workday should be reachable in 2–3 clicks from the home page.
5
Measure adoption, not setup. A beautifully structured Notion workspace that nobody actually uses is a failure. Track whether team members are creating tasks, writing meeting notes, and updating project statuses. Survey the team every 90 days on what's working and what's getting ignored. Evolve based on actual behavior.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you use Notion for team collaboration?
Yes, Notion is an excellent tool for team collaboration. It supports shared workspaces with granular permission controls, real-time co-editing, @mention notifications, task assignments with due dates, comment threads on any block, and team databases with filtered views per person. Small to medium teams (2–50 people) often use Notion as their primary collaboration hub, replacing Confluence, Google Docs, and Asana simultaneously.
How do I set up a shared Notion workspace?
To set up a shared Notion workspace: create a new workspace or upgrade your plan, invite teammates via email, set default member permissions, create a top-level page structure (Home, Projects, Wiki, Meetings), and build your first shared databases (Projects, Tasks, Meeting Notes). Write a "How We Use Notion" norms document before rolling out — teams that skip this step end up with a disorganized workspace within 60 days.
What are the Notion permission levels for teams?
Notion has four permission levels: Full Access (edit, share, and delete), Edit (edit content but not sharing settings), Comment (view and add comments but not edit), and View (read-only). Permissions inherit from parent pages but can be overridden at any page level. Most teams use Edit as the workspace default and restrict sensitive pages to Full Access for specific members only.
Is Notion better than Confluence for team collaboration?
Notion is generally better than Confluence for small to medium teams (under 100 people) because it's faster to set up, more flexible, has a cleaner UI, and is cheaper at scale. Confluence has advantages for large enterprise teams deeply embedded in the Atlassian ecosystem (Jira, Bitbucket). For most teams, Notion's combination of wikis, databases, and task management outperforms Confluence's pure wiki approach.
How do I assign tasks in Notion?
Create a Tasks database with a Person property for Assignee and a Date property for due dates. Assign tasks by selecting the team member in the Assignee field. Create a filtered view called "My Tasks" (filter: Assignee = Me) for each team member's personal task view. Use @mention in task comments to send notifications when you need someone's attention on a specific task.
