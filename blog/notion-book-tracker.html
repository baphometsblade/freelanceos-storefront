Why Track Your Reading

The average knowledge worker reads 12–24 books per year. Without a system, most of what they read evaporates within weeks — the specific ideas, the frameworks, the quotes that felt important in the moment. Research on the "forgetting curve" shows that without review, we lose 50% of new information within a day and 90% within a week.

A book tracker solves this in two ways: it creates a record (so you can search what you read) and it encourages active note-taking (which encodes information more durably than passive reading). Freelancers and independent workers have an additional motivation — reading strategically in your field compounds over time. Knowing which books actually moved the needle (and being able to re-reference them) is a genuine competitive advantage.

The secondary benefit is accountability. Tracking your books makes your reading goals real. A goal of "read more" is vague. A Notion tracker that shows you 7 books finished this year against a goal of 24 is honest feedback — and feedback is what changes behavior.

Key Principle

The goal of a book tracker isn't just to count books — it's to capture what you learned and make it searchable and usable later. Build your tracker for knowledge retrieval, not just logging.

The Complete Database Structure

Here is the exact database structure for a Notion book tracker that balances comprehensiveness with ease of entry. Every field listed is there for a reason:

Property Name Type Purpose
Title Title (default) Book title — the primary field. Keep just the main title, not subtitle.
Author Text Author's full name. Use last name first if you want to sort alphabetically.
Status Select To Read / Reading / Finished / Abandoned. The core organizer.
Rating Select (★ to ★★★★★) 5-point scale. Only fill in once finished. Blank for unread books.
Date Started Date When you began reading. Helps track pace and reading patterns.
Date Finished Date When you finished (or abandoned). Powers your annual reading count.
Genre Multi-select Business, Productivity, Fiction, Biography, Science, etc. Use 1–3 tags.
Format Select Physical / Ebook / Audiobook. Useful for seeing which format you prefer.
Pages Number Total page count. Enables reading pace calculations.
Key Takeaways Text 3–5 bullet points of your biggest lessons. Fill while reading is fresh.
Recommended By Text Who or what recommended this book. Helps identify your best book sources.
Would Re-Read Checkbox A single yes/no that identifies your most impactful books over time.

Optional Advanced Properties

  • Current Page (Number): Track reading progress. Pair with a formula: Current Page / Pages * 100 for a percentage readthrough.
  • Notes (Relation → Notes database): Link individual highlight or note entries to the book. Transforms the tracker into a full knowledge base.
  • Related Books (Relation → same database): Link sequels, companion books, or books on the same topic.
  • Cover (Files & Media): Upload or paste the book cover URL for gallery views.

The Four Reading Statuses Explained

The reading status field is the most important organizer in your book tracker. Four statuses cover every state a book can be in:

To Read
Books you intend to read — your reading list. This is typically the largest category. Consider a "Priority" property or sorting by date added so your best To Read options are easy to find.
Reading
Currently in progress. Most people have 1–3 books here at once (one per format: a physical book, an audiobook for commutes, an ebook for travel). If you have more than 5, some probably belong in Abandoned.
Finished
Completed books. This is your library. Over time it becomes the most valuable category — searchable, rated, noted. Filter by Date Finished = This Year to see your annual reading progress.
Abandoned
Books you started and decided not to finish. Don't feel guilty — tracking abandonment is valuable data. A high abandonment rate on a certain genre tells you something. Some abandoned books deserve a second try later.
Reading Tip

Give every book 50 pages before deciding to abandon it. If it hasn't earned your attention by then, move it to Abandoned without guilt. Life is too short for books that don't serve you. Track why you abandoned it in the Notes field — it sharpens your future reading choices.

Building a Rating System That Works

The most common mistake in book rating systems is not defining what the ratings mean. Without personal definitions, ratings drift — what was a 4 in January feels like a 3 in June — and the data becomes meaningless for comparison.

Here is a clear, defensible 5-star system for non-fiction readers (adjust for fiction as needed):

  • ★ (1 star): Didn't finish and wouldn't recommend. The core premise failed or the execution was poor. Rare — most books you'd rate this way belong in Abandoned.
  • ★★ (2 stars): Finished but struggled. A few useful ideas buried in too much filler. You got something from it but probably wouldn't recommend it.
  • ★★★ (3 stars): Solid, worth reading. Delivered what the title promised. You learned something useful and might recommend it to someone specific who needs it.
  • ★★★★ (4 stars): Excellent. Meaningfully changed how you think about something, or gave you frameworks or tools you'll actually use. Would recommend broadly.
  • ★★★★★ (5 stars): Transformative or essential. You'll re-read this. You'll push it on people. It changed a significant belief or opened up a whole domain you needed to understand.

With this system, 5-star books should be rare (5–10% of what you read). If everything is 4 or 5 stars, your ratings have lost discrimination. If most things are 2–3 stars, reconsider your book selection process — you should be reading things more likely to be excellent before you start.

Setting and Tracking Reading Goals

A reading goal without tracking is just hope. A reading goal in Notion is a living accountability system. Here's how to set one up:

Step 1: Set Your Annual Reading Target

Choose a target that stretches you slightly but stays realistic given your life. 12 books (one per month) is a good starting goal. 24 is ambitious but doable for regular readers. 52 (one per week) requires short books or significant audiobook use — achievable but demanding.

Step 2: Create a Reading Goals Page

Create a dedicated "Reading Goals 2026" page. Add a callout block with your target: "Goal: Read 24 books in 2026." Below it, add a linked view of your Books database filtered to Status = Finished and Date Finished = This Year.

Step 3: Add a Progress Counter

In the linked view, click "Calculate" at the bottom of any property column and select "Count all." This shows how many finished books match your filter. Add a formula callout: "(7 / 24) × 100 = 29% complete" — update the numerator as you finish books, or use Notion formulas to automate it.

Step 4: Track Monthly Pace

Add a second linked view of your Books database grouped by month (using the Date Finished property). This shows your reading pace by month — you can spot when you read heavily (vacation, slow business month) and when reading dropped off (crunch periods, life disruptions).

Book Notes: Turning Reading into Knowledge

The difference between readers who grow and readers who just consume is what they do during and after reading. Notes in your Notion book tracker convert passive reading into active knowledge acquisition.

The Three-Tier Notes System

Tier 1 — In the book entry (Key Takeaways field): 3–5 bullet points of your most important lessons. Write these immediately after finishing. These are your "permanent record" — the ideas that will still be useful in five years. Keep them brief and in your own words.

Tier 2 — A dedicated notes page (linked via relation): For books that deserve deeper engagement, create a dedicated notes page with your highlights, chapter summaries, and extended reflections. Link it to the book entry via a Relation property. This is where detailed synthesis happens.

Tier 3 — Connected to live projects (via relation to Projects database): The most powerful setup links book insights to projects they're relevant to. If you're working on a pricing strategy and read a book on negotiation, that book note can be linked to your Pricing Strategy project page. The insight arrives where it's needed.

Note-Taking Tip

Fill in Key Takeaways within 24 hours of finishing a book — before the forgetting curve steals the specific insights. Even 5–10 minutes of deliberate reflection immediately after finishing is worth more than an hour of reconstruction a week later.

The Best Views for Your Book Tracker

Set up these four views to get full utility from your book tracker database:

View 1: Board by Status (Default)

Group by Status field. Columns: To Read, Reading, Finished, Abandoned. This is your main operating view — at a glance you can see what you're reading, what's next, and your library. Drag cards between columns to update status.

View 2: Gallery — Finished Books

Filter: Status = Finished. Card preview: Cover image. Sort by Date Finished, descending. This creates a visual bookshelf of everything you've completed — satisfying to scroll through and good for sharing what you've read.

View 3: Table — All Books (Sortable)

No filters. Show all properties. This is your search-and-sort view — find a book by title, sort by Rating to see your top-rated reads, sort by Author for alphabetical browsing, filter by Genre for topical discovery.

View 4: List — To Read Queue

Filter: Status = To Read. Sort by a "Priority" property (if you add one) or Date Added, descending. This is your "what should I read next" view. Keep it sorted by the books you're most excited about, not just the order you added them.

Frequently Asked Questions

What fields should I include in a Notion book tracker?
The essential fields for a Notion book tracker are: Title (text), Author (text), Status (select: To Read, Reading, Finished, Abandoned), Rating (select or number 1–5), Date Started, Date Finished, Genre (multi-select), Format (Ebook/Physical/Audiobook), Pages or Hours (number), Notes (text), and Key Takeaways (text). Optional additions: Recommended By, Source (how you heard about it), and a Related Books relation property linking books in the same topic area.
How do I track reading progress in Notion?
Track reading progress in Notion by adding a 'Current Page' number property and a 'Total Pages' property, then creating a formula property: round(prop('Current Page') / prop('Total Pages') * 100) formatted as a number with % symbol. Update Current Page as you read. In the gallery view, this displays as a percentage on each book card. For audiobooks, use 'Hours Listened' and 'Total Hours' instead.
What is the best view type for a Notion book tracker?
The best default view for a Notion book tracker is a Board (kanban) view grouped by Status — columns are To Read, Reading, Finished, Abandoned. This shows your whole library at a glance and makes it easy to move books between stages by dragging. Gallery view is best for browsing by cover. Table view is best for sorting and filtering by rating, genre, or date finished. Most people set up 3–4 views and switch between them.
How do I add book covers to Notion?
To add book covers in Notion: (1) Find the book cover image on Google Images, Amazon, or Goodreads — right-click and copy image address. (2) In your book entry, click the page cover or the cover image property, paste the URL. (3) For a gallery view to show covers, set 'Card Preview' to 'Page Cover' in the view settings. You can also upload cover images directly as file attachments in a Files & Media property.
Should I use 5 stars or 10 stars for book ratings?
A 5-star system works best for most readers because it forces meaningful differentiation: 1 = didn't finish and wouldn't recommend, 2 = finished but struggled, 3 = solid read, 4 = excellent, recommended, 5 = life-changing or re-readable. A 10-point system feels more precise but in practice most readers only use 6–8 anyway, without consistent meaning for the fine gradations. Use 5 stars and define what each number means to you personally.
How do I set and track reading goals in Notion?
To track reading goals in Notion: create a 'Reading Goals' page with a target (e.g., 24 books in 2026). On that page, add a linked view of your Books database filtered to Status = Finished and Date Finished = This Year. Use the Calculate function at the bottom of the Count column to show how many you've finished. Add a formula: (finished / goal) * 100 displayed as a percentage to show progress toward your annual reading goal.
Can I link my book tracker to a knowledge base in Notion?
Yes — and this is one of the most powerful Notion book tracker setups. Create a relation property in your Books database that links to a separate Notes or Highlights database. Each book can then have multiple highlight or note entries linked to it. You can filter your Notes database by book, tag highlights by topic (marketing, mindset, writing), and build a searchable knowledge base from everything you've read. This transforms a simple tracker into a personal knowledge management system.
Is Notion better than Goodreads for tracking books?
Notion and Goodreads serve different purposes. Goodreads has a massive book catalog you can search and add from, social features (friends, reviews, reading challenges), and automatic tracking. Notion requires manual entry but gives you full customization — you can add any fields, link books to notes and projects, integrate with your personal knowledge base, and build views that Goodreads can't. Many readers use both: Goodreads for discovery and social reading, Notion for personal notes and knowledge management.
