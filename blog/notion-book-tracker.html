Notion Book Tracker: Build Your Ultimate Reading System in 2025

From gallery bookshelf to reading progress formulas, highlight capture, and Readwise sync — everything you need to build a reading system that actually works.

July 2025  ·  13 min read  ·  By FreelanceOS

Goodreads tells you what you've read. A Notion book tracker tells you what you learned, which projects those ideas connect to, and how fast you read in any given month. That gap — between cataloging books and actually using them — is why serious readers eventually leave Goodreads and build something in Notion.

This guide covers everything: the core book database with all the right properties, a gallery view that looks like an actual bookshelf, reading progress formulas, a highlight capture workflow, author relations, book club setups, and Readwise integration. By the end, you'll have a reading system that's genuinely more useful than any app built specifically for books.

Why Notion Beats Goodreads for Serious Readers

Goodreads has 150 million users and a comfortable social layer — friends' reviews, community challenges, author Q&As. If that's what you want from a reading tracker, it's fine. But if you read with intention — taking notes, connecting ideas, applying what you learn to work and projects — Goodreads hits a wall almost immediately.

The fundamental problem is that Goodreads is a catalog, not a thinking tool. You can mark a book as read, give it stars, and write a public review. You cannot link that book to the project it informed, tag the specific insight that changed how you work, or build a formula that tells you your average reading pace across genres.

Notion, by contrast, is a relational database and editor in one. Every book can be a database record and a full page of notes. That means:

  • Custom properties: Track exactly what matters to you — reading pace, pages per day, genre, format (physical/Kindle/audio), acquisition cost, re-read count
  • Relational linking: Connect books to projects, ideas, people, and goals in the same workspace
  • Full-text search: Find every note, quote, and takeaway across your entire reading history in seconds
  • Formula power: Calculate reading progress, annual stats, average rating by genre — anything you can express mathematically
  • Visual views: Switch between table, gallery (bookshelf), board (by status), and calendar (by date read) in one click

The trade-off is setup time. Notion won't auto-populate book metadata — you add covers, authors, and page counts manually (or via Readwise). That 30-minute setup cost pays dividends for years if you're a consistent reader.

Pro Tip

Use the ISBN database approach: when you start a new book, search for it on Open Library (openlibrary.org), grab the cover image URL and page count, and paste both into Notion. Adds about 90 seconds per book — negligible at 2-3 books per month.

Building Your Book Database — All Core Properties

The foundation of your Notion book tracker is a single database with the right set of properties. Don't over-engineer this upfront — start with the core set below, then add properties when you feel friction. Here are all the properties you need:

Property Type Notes
Title Name (default) Book title — the default Name property
Author Relation Relation to Authors database (see section 8 below)
Genre Select Color-coded: Business, Fiction, Non-Fiction, Science, History, Biography, Self-Help, Philosophy
Status Select Reading (blue), TBR (yellow), Read (green), DNF (red), Wishlist (gray)
Rating Number 1–5 scale. Format as number, not bar
Date Started Date When you began reading
Date Finished Date When you completed (or abandoned) the book
Pages Total Number Total page count — used in progress formula
Pages Read Number Current page — update as you read
Progress % Formula Calculated from Pages Read / Pages Total
Cover Files & Media Book cover image — shows in Gallery view
Key Takeaways Text 3–5 bullet summary of core ideas
Favorite Quotes Text Best 1–3 quotes from the book
Format Select Physical, Kindle, Audiobook, PDF
Link to Projects Relation Relation to your Projects database
Highlights Relation Rollup from Highlights database — count of captured quotes
Re-read Count Number How many times you've read this book
Recommended By Text Who suggested this book — useful for TBR curation

Open each book's page (the rich text area below the properties) for your full reading notes, chapter summaries, and extended thoughts. The properties give you database power; the page gives you unlimited writing space.

Gallery View as Your Virtual Bookshelf

The gallery view transforms your book database from a spreadsheet into something that actually looks like a bookshelf. It's one of the most satisfying Notion setups you can build — and takes about three minutes to configure.

Setting Up the Gallery View

  1. Inside your Books database, click "+ Add a view"
  2. Choose Gallery and name it "Bookshelf"
  3. Click the three dots (⋯) next to the view name → Properties
  4. Set Card preview to your Cover property (Files & Media)
  5. Set Card size to Large
  6. Enable Fit image to fill the card without cropping
  7. Show only: Title, Author, Genre, Rating (hide the rest for a clean look)

Color-Coding by Genre

When you assigned colors to your Genre select options (Business = purple, Fiction = blue, Self-Help = green, etc.), those colors appear as small dots on each gallery card. Group the gallery by Genre to see color-coded genre shelves — a visual way to see at a glance how your reading skews by category.

Creating Multiple Gallery Views

Create separate gallery views for different contexts:

  • All Books: No filter — your complete library
  • Read: Filter Status = Read, sort by Date Finished descending — your completed shelf
  • Best of: Filter Status = Read AND Rating ≥ 4 — your personal recommendations
  • By Genre: Group by Genre, no filter — browse shelves by category
Pro Tip

For your "Read" gallery, sort by Date Finished descending. When you open the view, you'll see the most recently finished books first — like pulling the newest addition off a physical shelf. This makes it easy to recommend recent reads from memory.

Reading Progress Formula

The reading progress formula is one of the most satisfying pieces of your book tracker. Here's the core formula for the Progress % property:

Formula — Progress Percentage

if(prop("Pages Total") > 0, round(prop("Pages Read") / prop("Pages Total") * 100), 0)

This returns a clean number like 67, representing 67% read. Add a % sign in the Number format settings to display it as 67%.

Visual Progress Bar Formula

For a more visual option that renders directly in your table view:

Formula — Progress Bar

if(prop("Pages Total") > 0, slice("██████████", 0, floor(prop("Pages Read") / prop("Pages Total") * 10)) + slice("░░░░░░░░░░", 0, 10 - floor(prop("Pages Read") / prop("Pages Total") * 10)) + " " + format(round(prop("Pages Read") / prop("Pages Total") * 100)) + "%", "Not started")

This renders as something like ███░░░░░░░ 30% directly inside your database row — no charts needed.

Reading Pace Formula

Add a "Days to Read" formula: dateBetween(prop("Date Finished"), prop("Date Started"), "days"). Then "Pages Per Day": if(prop("Days to Read") > 0, round(prop("Pages Total") / prop("Days to Read")), 0). Over time, this reveals your natural reading pace across different book lengths and genres.

Monthly Reading Challenge Setup

A monthly reading challenge in Notion works through a Goals database that relates to your Books database. Here's the minimal setup:

Reading Goals Database

Create a new database called "Reading Goals" with these properties:

  • Goal Name: e.g., "July 2025 — 4 Books"
  • Month: Date (first day of the month)
  • Books Target: Number (how many books you aim to finish)
  • Books Completed: Relation to Books database (add the books you finish each month)
  • Count Completed: Rollup of Books Completed → Count (auto-counts your finished books)
  • On Track?: Formula comparing Count Completed to Books Target

Each month, create a new goal entry. As you finish books, add them to the Books Completed relation. The Count Completed rollup updates automatically — you always know exactly where you stand against your challenge target.

Pro Tip

Set a realistic monthly challenge: 2–4 books per month is achievable for most working professionals. The goal isn't to maximize volume — it's to maintain a consistent habit. A completed 2-book month beats an abandoned 10-book goal every time.

Highlight and Note Capture Workflow

The most underused feature of a Notion book tracker is a dedicated Highlights database. Most readers scribble in margins or use Kindle highlights that they never revisit. A linked Highlights database makes every quote searchable and connectable for life.

The Highlights Database

Create a new database called "Book Highlights" with these properties:

Property Type Purpose
Quote Name (title) The actual highlight or quote text
Book Relation → Books Links this highlight to its source book
Page Number Page number for reference
My Commentary Text Your reaction, application, or connection to this quote
Tags Multi-select Insight, Actionable, Quote, Question, Disagree
Date Captured Date When you added this highlight
Used In Relation → Projects Track when you actually apply a quote in your work

During Reading Workflow

Keep the Notion mobile app open (or a web browser) as you read. When you hit a passage worth keeping: add a new entry to Book Highlights with the quote, page number, and a quick comment about why it matters. Don't filter — capture anything that resonates, then curate later.

After Reading Workflow

Within 48 hours of finishing a book, do a 20-minute "distillation session": go through all your highlights from that book, add commentary to the ones without it, tag each highlight, and write the Key Takeaways property on the book itself. This session converts passive reading into active learning.

How to Build a Notion Book Tracker — Step by Step

1
Create the Books Database with All Core Properties
Create a new full-page database called "Books". Add all the core properties from the table above: Status (Select), Genre (Select with colors), Rating (Number), Date Started/Finished (Date), Pages Total/Read (Number), Cover (Files & Media), Key Takeaways (Text), and Format (Select). Set Status options as: Reading (blue), TBR (yellow), Read (green), DNF (red), Wishlist (gray). The color-coding makes status scannable at a glance in any view.
2
Set Up the Gallery View as Your Virtual Bookshelf
Click "+ Add a view", choose Gallery, name it "Bookshelf". Set Card preview to your Cover (Files & Media) property. Set Card size to Large and enable Fit image. Show only Title, Author, Genre, and Rating in the card properties — hide everything else. Create a second Gallery view called "Read Shelf" filtered to Status = Read. This becomes your completed library, sorted by Date Finished descending so your newest reads appear first.
3
Build the Reading Progress Formula
Add a new Formula property called "Progress %". Enter: if(prop("Pages Total") > 0, round(prop("Pages Read") / prop("Pages Total") * 100), 0). For the visual bar version, use the block-character formula from the section above. Update Pages Read as you read — the percentage and bar update instantly. In your "Reading Now" table view, make Progress % a visible column so you can see how far along every active book is.
4
Create a Daily Highlights Database Linked to Books
Create a second Notion database called "Book Highlights". Add: Quote (Name/Title), Book (Relation to your Books database), Page (Number), My Commentary (Text), Tags (Multi-select: Insight, Actionable, Quote, Question), and Date Captured (Date). Back in your Books database, add a Highlights Relation property pointing to Book Highlights, and a Rollup showing count. Now you can see at a glance how many highlights you captured from each book.
5
Set Up Filtered Views (Reading Now, TBR, Read, DNF)
Inside your Books database, create one view per status: Reading Now (filter Status = Reading, sort Date Started descending), TBR (filter Status = TBR), Read (filter Status = Read, sort Date Finished descending — use Gallery for visual bookshelf), DNF (filter Status = DNF), and Wishlist (filter Status = Wishlist). Set "Reading Now" as your default view. This is what you see every time you open the tracker — only your active reads, with progress visible.

Linking Books to Projects and Ideas — Zettelkasten in Notion

The biggest upgrade over Goodreads isn't the gallery view or the formulas — it's the ability to link books to the rest of your life. This is where Notion becomes a genuine thinking tool rather than a catalog.

The basic setup: add a "Link to Projects" Relation property to your Books database, pointing to whatever you use for project management in Notion. When you read a book that informs a project, add that relation. Now, from inside the project, you can see exactly which books are informing your thinking.

The Zettelkasten Approach

The zettelkasten method (developed by sociologist Niklas Luhmann, who wrote 70 books and 400+ articles using it) is a note-taking system where ideas are stored as atomic notes and linked to each other. In Notion, you can replicate this with:

  • A "Notes" database where each note is a single idea (not a summary of a book)
  • A "Source" relation on each note pointing back to the book it came from
  • A "Related Notes" relation linking notes that connect across different books
  • Tags on notes for broad themes (Leadership, Creativity, Systems, etc.)

When you finish a book, you don't just add the Key Takeaways property — you write individual notes for each major insight and link them together. Over time, you build a web of connected ideas that spans your entire reading history. A note about "the power of compounding" might link to books on investing, fitness habits, and writing craft simultaneously.

Linking Strategy

Don't link every book to every project — only add a Book-to-Project relation when you actually use a specific idea from the book in that project. Meaningful links beat comprehensive links. You want to be able to open a project and see which books actually shaped it, not just which books you happened to read that quarter.

Author Database Relation

If you read multiple books by the same author, an Author database gives you a dedicated page per author with all their books linked — a mini author profile inside your reading system.

Setting Up the Authors Database

Create a database called "Authors" with these properties:

  • Name: Author name (default title property)
  • Books: Relation to Books database (all books by this author)
  • Books Count: Rollup of Books → Count
  • Avg Rating: Rollup of Books → Average of Rating
  • Genres: Rollup of Books → Show unique values of Genre
  • Notes: Text property for your thoughts on the author's style, themes, and body of work

In your Books database, change the Author property from Text to Relation (pointing to the Authors database). Now every book is linked to its author record. The Author page automatically rolls up every book you've read by them, with average rating, so you can instantly see which authors you tend to rate highly.

This setup also lets you spot author trends: if you've read five books by the same author and consistently rated them 4-5 stars, that's a signal to read everything they've written. Filter your Authors database by Avg Rating ≥ 4 and Books Count ≥ 2 to surface your most reliable authors.

Wish List and TBR View Setup

Your TBR (to-be-read) list is the intake system for your reading tracker. Without a good TBR setup, books you want to read get lost in browser bookmarks, Twitter favorites, and half-remembered recommendations. With Notion, every potential read has a home.

TBR View Configuration

In your Books database, create a filtered view called "TBR" with:

  • Filter: Status = TBR
  • Sort: by Recommended By (alphabetical) — groups recommendations by source
  • Visible properties: Title, Author, Genre, Recommended By, Date Added

Wishlist View (Not Yet Acquired)

Add a separate "Wishlist" status for books you want but don't own yet. Filter this view to Status = Wishlist. Add a "Price" (Number) and "Available At" (Select: Amazon, Library, Borrowed) property to track how you'll acquire each book. When you get a book, change status from Wishlist to TBR.

Smart TBR Curation

The danger of a TBR list is infinite growth — you add faster than you read. Add an annual curation pass: every January, archive all TBR books added more than two years ago that you still haven't started. If you haven't read something in two years, you've implicitly decided not to read it. Archiving these clears the signal from the noise and makes your TBR list genuinely useful.

Book Club Shared Database

A Notion book club database lets multiple readers track their progress on the same books, share highlights, and discuss what they're reading — all in a shared Notion workspace.

Book Club Setup

  1. Create the Books database in a Notion Team Space (requires Notion Plus or above for unlimited members)
  2. Invite book club members via the Share menu — enter their emails, set permission to "Can edit"
  3. Add a "Member" property (Person type) to the Books database — each member tags themselves on books they're reading
  4. Add a "Discussion Notes" text property for shared observations
  5. Create a "Book Club Picks" filtered view showing books tagged with all current members

Shared Highlights for Discussion

In the Highlights database, add a "Shared By" (Person) property. Each member adds their own highlights from the shared book, tagged with their name. Before your club meeting, filter Highlights by the current book — you'll see every member's top quotes side by side. This is far richer than a general discussion because you start from specific text.

Annual Reading Stats Formulas

At year-end, your Notion book tracker can generate reading stats that would make Goodreads jealous. Here are the key formulas to set up:

Books Read This Year

Create a Formula property called "Read This Year": if(prop("Status") == "Read" and year(prop("Date Finished")) == year(now()), true, false). Filter your database to Read This Year = true to see your current-year reads. A Rollup in a separate "Annual Stats" database can count them.

Pages Read This Year

Similarly: if(prop("Status") == "Read" and year(prop("Date Finished")) == year(now()), prop("Pages Total"), 0). Sum this across all books for your annual page count.

Genre Breakdown

Create a "By Genre" Gallery view of your Read database, grouped by Genre. Count the cards in each group for an instant visual breakdown. Alternatively, build a small "Reading Stats" page with Linked Database views filtered by each genre — one section per genre showing all books read in that category.

Average Rating

In your Reading Goals database, add a Rollup on the Books relation → Average of Rating. This gives your average rating per month or year, telling you whether your book selection quality is improving over time.

Integrating with Readwise and Kindle Highlights

Manual highlight capture is sustainable for physical books. For Kindle readers who highlight heavily, Readwise is the essential bridge between your Kindle and your Notion book tracker.

Readwise + Notion Integration

Readwise ($7.99/month) syncs all your Kindle, Apple Books, Instapaper, Pocket, and physical book highlights to Notion automatically. To set it up:

  1. Create a Readwise account at readwise.io
  2. Connect your Kindle (or upload your My Clippings.txt file from your Kindle)
  3. Go to Readwise Settings → Integrations → Notion
  4. Authenticate with Notion and select which database to export to
  5. Choose your export format: "Book page per book" creates a dedicated Notion page per title, with all highlights organized by location

Readwise exports to Notion create a separate "Readwise Books" database. You'll want to link these to your existing Books database via the book title — it's manual matching, but only once per book.

Readwise Reader

Readwise Reader is a read-later app (like Pocket) that handles highlights from web articles, PDFs, newsletters, and physical books (via camera). All highlights sync to your Notion database automatically. For physical book readers, the camera feature lets you photograph a page and extract highlighted text — game-changing for non-Kindle readers.

Manual Kindle Export (Free)

If you don't want to pay for Readwise, export your Kindle highlights manually: connect your Kindle to your computer, navigate to the "documents" folder, and copy "My Clippings.txt". Open this file — it contains every highlight you've ever made, organized by book. Copy-paste the highlights into your Notion Highlights database, batch by book.

Readwise vs Manual

If you highlight more than 20 passages per book or read more than 2 books per month on Kindle, Readwise pays for itself in saved time within the first month. Below that, manual export is perfectly manageable and keeps your setup cost-free.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I track books I've read in Notion?
To track books in Notion, create a database with a Status property (Select type) with options: Reading, Read, TBR (to-be-read), DNF (did not finish), and Wishlist. Add a Date Finished property (Date type) to record when you completed each book. Create filtered views for each status — your "Read" view becomes your reading history, sorted by Date Finished. Add a Rating property (Number, 1-5) to rate each book, and a Key Takeaways text property to capture the main ideas. For a visual library, switch your Read view to Gallery mode and set Cover images as the card preview. Update the Pages Read property as you progress through a book, and the Progress % formula calculates your percentage automatically.
Is Notion better than Goodreads for book tracking?
Notion beats Goodreads for serious readers who take notes and apply what they read. Goodreads gives you fixed shelves and star ratings — Notion lets you create any property: reading pace, custom genres, personal notes, book-to-project links, and a full highlights database. Notion also integrates with your entire workspace: a book on leadership can link to the leadership project it informed; a quote can link to a blog post you're drafting. Goodreads is a social cataloging app with a community layer (friends' reviews, author Q&As, community reading challenges). Notion has none of that. The trade-off: Goodreads is ready in seconds; Notion requires 30 minutes of setup. If you read 2-3 books a year casually, Goodreads is fine. If you read 20+ books a year and take notes, Notion is vastly more powerful.
How do I add a book cover image in Notion?
There are three ways to add book cover images in Notion. First, upload directly: open the book page, click the Files & Media property (your Cover property), and upload a JPG/PNG of the cover — grab it from Google Images or the book's Amazon page. Second, use an image URL: instead of uploading, paste the direct image URL from Amazon's book listing (right-click the cover on Amazon → Copy image address). Third, set the page cover: open the book's page, click "Add cover" at the top, and upload or paste an image URL — this shows in gallery view automatically when card preview is set to "Page cover." The Files & Media property approach gives more control and keeps your images inside Notion; the page cover approach is fastest for gallery views but uses Notion's hosting.
Can I calculate my reading pace in Notion?
Yes. Add a "Days to Read" formula property: dateBetween(prop("Date Finished"), prop("Date Started"), "days"). Then add a "Pages Per Day" formula: if(prop("Days to Read") > 0, round(prop("Pages Total") / prop("Days to Read")), 0). This shows how fast you read each book. For a books-per-month calculation, create a "Month Read" formula: formatDate(prop("Date Finished"), "YYYY-MM") — then filter your database by this property to see all books finished in a given month. These formulas let you spot patterns: do you read faster in certain genres? Do long books take proportionally longer? Over 20+ books, the data becomes genuinely revealing about your reading habits.
How do I capture book highlights in Notion?
The best Notion highlight capture workflow has two phases. During reading: keep a Notion page open (or use the mobile app) and add quotes directly as you read, with the page number. Create a dedicated Highlights database with a Relation property linking each highlight to its source book in your Books database. After reading: go through your physical notes or Kindle highlights and transfer them into Notion within 48 hours, adding your own commentary to each highlight. Tag highlights by type (Insight, Actionable, Quote, Question) so you can filter later. The game-changer: when you search Notion for a topic, you'll find relevant highlights from every book you've read — your own curated anthology on any subject, spanning your entire reading history.
What's the best Notion formula for reading progress?
The core reading progress formula is: if(prop("Pages Total") > 0, round(prop("Pages Read") / prop("Pages Total") * 100), 0) — this gives you a clean percentage like 67. For a visual progress bar, use: if(prop("Pages Total") > 0, slice("██████████", 0, floor(prop("Pages Read") / prop("Pages Total") * 10)) + slice("░░░░░░░░░░", 0, 10 - floor(prop("Pages Read") / prop("Pages Total") * 10)) + " " + format(round(prop("Pages Read") / prop("Pages Total") * 100)) + "%", "Not started"). This renders as a 10-block progress bar like ███░░░░░░░ 30% directly in your database view — no charts or external apps needed. Keep Pages Read updated as you read; the formula recalculates instantly.
How do I share my Notion book list?
To share your Notion book tracker publicly, open the database or the page containing it, click "Share" in the top right, and toggle "Share to web" on. Copy the public link — anyone with this link can view your reading list without a Notion account. For a curated public bookshelf, create a filtered Gallery view showing only Status = Read with Rating ≥ 4, then share that specific view's URL — visitors see only your recommended reads. For a book club shared database, add members directly via their email in the Share menu — they can add and edit books. Use a Notion Team Space for ongoing book clubs with multiple collaborators (requires Notion Plus or Business plan).
Can Notion sync with Kindle highlights?
Notion doesn't have a native Kindle sync, but Readwise is the gold-standard bridge. Readwise ($7.99/month) imports all your Kindle highlights automatically and exports them to Notion via an official integration. Set it up in Readwise Settings → Integrations → Notion. Each book gets its own Notion page with all highlights organized by location. You can also export Kindle highlights manually for free: connect your Kindle to your computer, navigate to the documents folder, and copy "My Clippings.txt" — this file contains every highlight you've ever made, organized by book. For physical books, Readwise Reader's camera feature lets you photograph pages and extract highlighted text, which then syncs to Notion. If you highlight heavily (20+ per book) or read 3+ Kindle books per month, Readwise pays for itself in saved time.
