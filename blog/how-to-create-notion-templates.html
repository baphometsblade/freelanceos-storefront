What Are Notion Templates?

In Notion, "template" has two distinct meanings that are worth separating from the start:

Page templates are complete Notion pages — with all their sub-pages, databases, linked views, and content — that can be duplicated into any workspace. When you find a template online and click "Duplicate," you're duplicating a page template. The entire structure copies into your workspace, where you can modify it freely. This is the kind of template most people sell on Gumroad or give away as lead magnets.

Database templates (also called entry templates) are pre-configured entries that get applied each time you create a new item inside a specific database. If you have a Projects database, you might have a "New Client Project" database template that pre-fills the Status to "Active," adds a checklist of standard project tasks in the content area, and prompts you to fill in the client name and deadline. Database templates are Notion's most underused productivity feature — they eliminate setup time for every single recurring workflow.

Both types are covered in this guide. Most complete Notion templates you'll build combine both: a page template (the overall workspace structure) that contains multiple databases, each with their own database templates.

Should You Build Your Own or Buy a Template?

The honest answer depends on what you're trying to accomplish:

Build your own when: you have a very specific workflow that existing templates don't match, you want to learn Notion deeply, you're building something to sell or share, or you have a unique process that requires custom formulas and property structures.

Buy or use a free template when: you need something working quickly, the use case is standard (freelancer CRM, project tracker, content calendar), or you've tried building from scratch before and abandoned it. A good template built by someone who's spent hundreds of hours on Notion gives you a starting point that would take you weeks to reach independently.

The 6-Step Process for Creating Notion Templates

1
Step 1: Define the Problem Your Template Solves
Before opening Notion, write down in one sentence what your template does. "This template helps me track freelance client projects from proposal to final delivery." A clear problem statement prevents scope creep and keeps the template focused. Then list the data points you need to capture (client name, deadline, project status, budget, deliverables) and the views you'll need (by client, by status, by deadline). Sketch this on paper or in a notes app first — it saves enormous time in Notion.
2
Step 2: Build Your Core Databases
Create a new Notion page and add your databases using the /database inline command. For most templates, you'll want one primary database and one or two related databases. Choose your properties carefully — every property is something a user has to fill in or maintain. Include only what you'd actually use on week one. You can always add more later, but you can't easily remove properties once users have data in them. Add properties in this order: the most important identifying properties first (Name, Status, Date), then relational properties, then formulas last.
3
Step 3: Configure Views and Filters
Raw databases without views are hard to use. Add the views that match how people will actually work with the data. A project tracker might need: Table view (full data), Board view grouped by Status, Calendar view by Due Date, and a Gallery view for visual project cards. For each view, set a meaningful default sort (Due Date ascending, or Priority descending). Add a default filter where relevant — for example, filter out "Completed" items from the primary Table view so the board stays clean. The views you configure become part of the template.
4
Step 4: Create Database Templates (Entry Templates)
Click the dropdown arrow next to the "New" button in your database, then select "New template." Build the entry structure — add property defaults, content blocks, checklists, headers, and instructions directly in the template page. Create a separate template for each major entry type. For a Projects database: one template for "Client Project," another for "Internal Project," a third for "Quick Task." Each template can have completely different pre-filled properties and page content. This is the feature that makes a template truly useful vs just a pretty structure.
5
Step 5: Add Formulas, Relations, and Rollups
Once your structure works with sample data, add the automation layer. Relations connect two databases (Tasks relate to Projects, so you can see all tasks for each project). Rollups let you pull aggregate data across the relation (sum all invoice amounts related to a client). Formulas calculate values automatically — days until deadline, completion percentage, revenue per hour. Test every formula with real sample data before publishing. A broken formula in a template erodes trust immediately.
6
Step 6: Polish, Document, and Share
Add a "Start Here" page with setup instructions. Include sample data in every database so users can see how it works before they delete the examples. Add icons and covers to make the template visually clear. Then click Share in the top right, enable "Share to web," toggle on "Allow duplicate as template," and copy the share link. Anyone with the link can duplicate the entire template into their Notion workspace with one click.

Notion Property Types: Choosing the Right One

Properties are the data fields in your database. Choosing the wrong property type is the most common beginner mistake — it locks you into the wrong input method and breaks formulas downstream.

Title (Text)
The primary identifier — every database has exactly one. Keep it as the first property. Names, project titles, task descriptions.
Select
Single-choice dropdown. Use for Status (Active/Paused/Complete), Priority (High/Medium/Low), Type fields. Best when options are mutually exclusive.
Multi-select
Multiple tags allowed. Use for Skills, Categories, Tags — anything where an entry might belong to multiple groups.
Date
Single date or date range. Use for Due Date, Start Date, Created Date. Enables Calendar view and date-based formulas.
Number
Numeric values with format options (dollar, percent, etc.). Use for Budget, Hours, Revenue, Quantity. Required for numeric formulas.
Relation
Links two databases together. Use to connect Tasks to Projects, Invoices to Clients, Notes to Projects. The foundation of multi-database templates.
Rollup
Aggregates data across a Relation. Requires a Relation property. Count tasks per project, sum invoice totals per client, show earliest due date.
Formula
Calculates values from other properties. Days until due date, completion percentage, conditional tags. Most powerful but most complex — add last.

Advanced Technique: Template Buttons

Template buttons (type /button in any Notion page) are different from database templates. They insert a pre-built set of content blocks anywhere on a page when clicked. This is useful for:

  • Weekly planning pages: A template button that inserts your standard weekly plan structure — Monday through Friday blocks, priorities section, review prompts
  • Meeting notes: Click a button to insert Attendees, Agenda, Discussion, Action Items blocks with a single click
  • Daily journal entries: Insert a pre-structured journal with mood tracker, gratitude section, and daily goals
  • Project kickoff checklists: Add a standardized project checklist to any project page

In the Template Button editor, you can add any block type — text, headers, toggles, checklists, callouts, code blocks, and even inline databases. Template buttons are one of the most underused features in Notion and one of the most impressive things to build into a template.

Advanced Technique: Linked Databases

A linked database (type /linked view of database) shows data from an existing database in a new location with its own view settings. This is the key to building template dashboards. Your homepage might show:

  • A linked view of your Tasks database filtered to Due Today
  • A linked view of your Projects database filtered to Status = Active
  • A linked view of your Clients database filtered to Status = Current

Each linked view can have completely different filters, sorts, and view types — all pulling from the same underlying database. When building a template with a dashboard homepage, always use linked databases rather than separate databases for dashboard views. This keeps your data in one place and your template coherent when someone duplicates it.

Critical: Test Before Sharing

Before sharing any template, duplicate it yourself into a fresh Notion workspace and go through the full setup process as a new user would. Check that all linked databases connect correctly, all formulas compute with sample data, and all views show the right results. Broken templates damage your reputation and generate support requests.

Useful Notion Formulas for Templates

Formulas add real value to templates by automating calculations users would otherwise do manually. Here are the most useful formulas to include:

Days Until Due Date
dateBetween(prop("Due Date"), now(), "days")
Overdue Flag (returns "OVERDUE" or "")
if(dateBetween(prop("Due Date"), now(), "days") < 0, "⚠️ OVERDUE", "")
Completion Percentage (requires Number "Done" and "Total" properties)
if(prop("Total") > 0, round(prop("Done") / prop("Total") * 100), 0)
Days Since Last Contact (for CRM databases)
dateBetween(now(), prop("Last Contact"), "days")
Progress Bar (visual — returns emoji bar)
slice("██████████", 0, floor(prop("Progress") / 10)) + slice("░░░░░░░░░░", 0, 10 - floor(prop("Progress") / 10))

Always add formulas after your base properties are stable. Changing a property name after formulas reference it breaks the formula — and in a shared template, that breaks it for every user who has already duplicated it.

Common Mistakes When Creating Notion Templates

✕
Too many properties. Every property is cognitive load for the user. If you wouldn't fill it in on day one, remove it. Ten focused properties beat twenty redundant ones every time.
✕
No sample data. An empty template is hard to understand. Include 3–5 sample entries in every database so users can see how the system works before they start filling in their own data.
✕
Skipping database templates. A great structure without database entry templates misses 60% of the value. Build an entry template for every recurring workflow your database supports.
✕
No documentation page. Users shouldn't need to guess how your template works. A "Start Here" page with a 2-minute setup walkthrough dramatically improves the user experience and reduces support questions.
✕
Broken formulas on share. Always test your template in a fresh workspace before publishing. Formulas that reference deleted properties silently return empty values — and users won't know why.
✕
Mixing too many use cases. The best templates solve one specific problem extremely well. A "freelancer OS" template that also tries to be a student planner and a recipe manager will do all three poorly.

How to Share and Sell Notion Templates

Once your template is polished and tested, you have several options for sharing it:

Free sharing: Enable "Share to web" and "Allow duplicate as template" on the page, then share the link anywhere — your website, Twitter, Reddit, email newsletter. Free templates build your audience and establish credibility.

Selling on Gumroad: Create a Gumroad product, upload a PDF or description page, set your price, and add the template duplicate link as a digital download. When someone purchases, they receive the link. Gumroad handles payments and delivery. Templates on Gumroad range from $5 for simple single-database templates to $149 for complete systems.

Selling on Etsy: Etsy has an active Notion template market. List your template with good screenshots showing the database views and the key features. Etsy drives organic traffic, but takes 6.5% in transaction fees.

Your own storefront: Build a simple landing page (GitHub Pages, Carrd, or Webflow) with Stripe or Lemonsqueezy for payments. More work to set up, but zero platform fees and full control over your customer relationship.

Marketing that works: Screenshots of the actual Notion interface outperform mockups. Show the before/after — how much time the template saves, or the specific problem it solves. Tutorial-style content (short videos showing how the template works) converts well on YouTube and Twitter.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are Notion templates?
Notion templates are pre-built pages or databases that can be duplicated into any Notion workspace. They come in two forms: page templates (a complete Notion page with structure already built) and database templates (pre-configured entry templates inside a Notion database). Anyone can create and share them.
Can I sell Notion templates?
Yes. Notion templates are commonly sold through Gumroad, Etsy, Lemonsqueezy, or personal websites. The seller shares a duplicate link behind a paywall. Prices range from $5 for simple templates to $100+ for complete systems. Successful template sellers make $1,000–$10,000+ per month from digital product sales.
What is a Notion database template?
A database template in Notion is a pre-configured entry that gets applied each time you create a new item in that database. You create them by clicking the dropdown arrow next to the 'New' button and selecting 'New template'. Database templates can pre-fill properties, add structured content blocks, and include prompts to guide the user.
How do I share a Notion template publicly?
Open the page, click 'Share' in the top right, toggle on 'Share to web', then enable 'Allow duplicate as template'. Copy the link — anyone who opens it will see a 'Duplicate' button that copies the entire template into their own Notion workspace.
Can I use linked databases in a Notion template?
Yes, linked databases work in templates and are one of the most powerful features. When someone duplicates your template, the linked databases duplicate as separate source databases. The relations between databases are preserved within the duplicated workspace. Test your linked database structure before sharing.
How do I make a Notion template button?
Type /button in any Notion page to insert a Template Button block. Click 'Edit' to define what blocks get created when someone clicks it. Template buttons insert pre-built blocks into a page — like a weekly planning structure or a meeting notes format — at the click of a button.
What makes a Notion template worth buying?
A premium Notion template is worth purchasing when it solves a specific, complex problem that would take many hours to build from scratch. The best paid templates have linked databases with working relations and rollups, pre-built formulas, multiple views configured for different use cases, and clear documentation or a setup guide.
What properties should I include in a Notion template?
Include only properties users will actually fill in. The most universally useful properties are: Name (Title), Status (Select), Priority (Select), Due Date (Date), Tags (Multi-select), and Notes (Text). Add Relation properties when connecting to another database, and Formula properties for automatic calculations.
