Most productivity advice tells you to find the right app and stick with it. But if you're managing multiple client projects, running a freelance business, and trying to actually think through your work — a simple to-do app starts to feel like trying to run a restaurant on a napkin. That's where a Notion task manager changes the game.

This guide is a complete walkthrough for building a Notion-based task management system from scratch. 1. Notion vs Traditional To-Do Apps

Before we build anything, let's be honest about the tradeoffs. Notion is not the right choice for everyone, and pretending otherwise would be a waste of your time.

Why Traditional To-Do Apps Fall Short for Complex Work

Apps like Todoist, Things 3, and TickTick are excellent for what they do: capturing tasks quickly, organizing them by project, and reminding you when things are due. If your work is relatively simple — a handful of recurring responsibilities, some personal errands, a few client deliverables — they're probably all you need.

The cracks start to show when your work gets more complex. Imagine you're a freelance designer with six active clients. Each client has multiple projects. Each project has tasks with different deadlines, file attachments, meeting notes, and invoices attached to them. In Todoist, your task "Deliver logo revisions" lives in a flat list. There's no way to see it next to the creative brief for that project, or the invoice you still haven't sent, or the date the client last emailed you.

In Notion, that task is a database row with relations to a Projects database, a Clients database, and a Files database. You can open the task and immediately see everything connected to it. That's not a productivity hack — it's a fundamentally different way of storing work information, and it's the core reason knowledge workers eventually migrate to Notion despite its steeper setup curve.

What Notion Adds to Task Management

The specific Notion features that make task management more powerful than dedicated apps:

  • Relations: Link a task to its parent project, client, or any other database. When you open a task, you can navigate directly to the project it belongs to.
  • Rollups: Aggregate data across linked databases. See how many tasks are open in a project, or what percentage of a project is complete, without leaving the project page.
  • Multiple views: One database, many lenses. See your tasks as a list, kanban board, calendar, gallery, or timeline — all from the same underlying data.
  • Custom properties: Add fields that match your actual workflow. Priority, estimated time, energy level, area of life, tags — you define what matters.
  • Filters and sorts: Build views that show exactly what you need. "All high-priority tasks due this week that are still in Inbox status" is a real view you can save and use daily.
  • Linked databases inside pages: Embed a filtered view of your Tasks database inside a project page, so you can see all tasks for that project without leaving the project.

The Honest Downside

Notion is overkill if you just need a simple checklist. Setting up a proper Notion task system takes real time — budget 3-5 hours minimum to build something solid from scratch, or 30 minutes if you start from a pre-built template. Mobile capture is slower than Todoist's natural language input. And Notion's mobile app, while much improved, still doesn't match the snappiness of native iOS apps like Things 3.

If your task management needs are simple, use Todoist and spend the time you save on actual work. Notion's task management power is real, but it has to be worth the investment for your specific situation.

2. Building Your Core Task Database

The foundation of your Notion task manager is a single Tasks database. Everything else — your views, your dashboard, your project pages — will pull data from this one database. Getting the properties right here is the most important decision you'll make, so let's be deliberate about it.

Create a new full-page database in Notion and name it Tasks. Then add the following properties in order:

Required Properties

Task Name — This is the default Title property. It's the text of your task. Keep it action-oriented: "Send invoice to Acme Corp" not "Invoice." The more specific your task name, the less thinking you'll have to do when you're ready to execute it.

Status — Select property with these values, in this order:

  • Inbox (gray) — Just captured, not yet processed
  • Next Action (blue) — Ready to work on, no blockers
  • In Progress (yellow) — Actively being worked on right now
  • Waiting (orange) — Blocked on someone else or an external event
  • Done (green) — Completed
  • Archived (gray) — Done and no longer needed in active views

Priority — Select property with four values:

  • Critical (red) — Must be done today or there are serious consequences
  • High (orange) — Important, should happen this week
  • Medium (yellow) — Standard priority, fits in the week's plan
  • Low (gray) — Nice to do but not urgent

Due Date — Date property with time enabled. Turn on "Include time" so you can set specific deadlines like "Tuesday at 5:00 PM" rather than just the day. This property also powers your calendar view.

Project — Relation property linked to your Projects database. If you don't have a Projects database yet, create one alongside your Tasks database. This relation is what turns Notion from a to-do app into a proper project management system.

Area — Select property for the life or business area this task belongs to:

  • Client Work
  • Business Development
  • Admin
  • Personal
  • Learning

Estimated Time — Select property to capture how long you think the task will take:

  • 15 min
  • 30 min
  • 1 hr
  • 2+ hrs

This property is critical for time-blocking. When you're building your day's schedule and have a 30-minute gap, you need to know which tasks fit that slot without having to re-read every task description.

Energy Required — Select property:

  • Deep Work (writing, designing, coding, strategic thinking)
  • Medium (emails, calls, reviewing documents)
  • Admin (filing, scheduling, data entry)

This pairs with Estimated Time to help you match tasks to your energy level throughout the day. You do Deep Work in the morning when you're sharpest. You do Admin in the afternoon when your brain is tired.

Tags — Multi-select property for context labels: @computer, @phone, @errands, @client-name, @waiting-on-reply, etc. Use tags for GTD contexts and filtering.

Pro tip: Don't add every property on day one. Start with Task Name, Status, Priority, and Due Date. Add Project relation once you've built your Projects database. Add the other properties one at a time as you actually need them. A half-used database with eight properties is worse than a fully-used database with four.

Your First Three Views

Once your properties are set up, create these three views immediately. They'll cover 80% of your daily usage:

All Tasks (Default)

No filters. Sorted by Status, then Priority. This is your master list — you'll use it mainly for setup and housekeeping, not daily work.

Active Tasks

Filtered: Status is not Done and not Archived. Sorted by Priority then Due Date. This is your working list — everything that still needs doing.

Due This Week

Filtered: Due Date is within the next 7 days, Status is not Done. Sorted by Due Date. Your weekly execution view.

3. GTD in Notion: Getting Things Done

David Allen's Getting Things Done methodology is arguably the most influential personal productivity system ever published. The core premise is deceptively simple: your brain is for having ideas, not storing them. When you keep tasks, worries, and commitments in your head, you burn cognitive energy maintaining them. When you capture everything in a trusted external system and process it regularly, you free your mind for actual thinking.

GTD has five stages: Capture, Clarify, Organize, Reflect, and Engage. Your Notion task database maps directly onto these stages.

Capture: The Inbox View

Every incoming task, idea, or commitment goes to one place: your Inbox. Create a dedicated Notion view filtered to Status = Inbox. This is your capture bucket. The rule is simple: when something comes in — an email request, a task you think of in the shower, something a client mentions on a call — it goes into Inbox immediately. You don't stop to figure out when you'll do it or where it belongs. You just capture it.

In practice, this means Inbox might contain entries that look like:

  • "Reply to Mark's email about the Q3 proposal"
  • "Renew domain for project site"
  • "Look into Notion AI pricing"
  • "Call accountant about Q2 estimated taxes"
  • "Buy new webcam for client calls"

None of these have priorities or due dates yet. They're just captured. That's enough for now.

Clarify and Organize: Daily Processing

Once or twice a day (morning and end of day works well), you process your Inbox. For each item, you ask one question: is this actionable?

If no — delete it, or convert it to a reference note in a separate Notion page. Inbox is only for actionable items.

If yes — assign the appropriate Status:

  • Next Action: You can do this now, no blockers, no prerequisites. Assign Priority and Due Date. Link to Project.
  • Waiting: You're waiting for someone else before you can move forward. Add a note in the task description: "Waiting on [name] since [date] for [what]." Set Status to Waiting.
  • Someday/Maybe: Not doing it now, might do it later. Add it to a Someday view (Status = Archived or create a dedicated Someday status value) and move on.

The goal is to get Inbox to zero. Not to complete all the tasks — just to process every item so everything has a clear next status and nothing is floating in limbo.

Next Actions View

Create a view filtered to Status = Next Action, sorted by Priority (descending) then Due Date (ascending). This is your execution list — the things you've committed to doing with no blockers. When you sit down to work, you look at this view and pick what to do next based on your available time, energy level, and priorities.

A clean Next Actions list might have 10-20 items. If it has 80 items, your system is being used as a parking lot, not a task manager. The fix is doing a weekly review (more on that below) where you ruthlessly reassign, defer, or delete anything that isn't genuinely a next action.

Waiting For View

Create a view filtered to Status = Waiting. When you look at this view, you should see every task where you're blocked on someone else. The task description should tell you exactly who you're waiting on, what you're waiting for, and since when. If something has been in Waiting for two weeks, it's time to follow up.

This view is also useful for building a follow-up habit. Review your Waiting view on Fridays and decide which items need a nudge. This prevents things from falling through the cracks simply because you forgot you were waiting on them.

Projects View

GTD distinguishes between tasks (single actions) and projects (outcomes requiring multiple actions). Your Projects database stores one row per active project. Relate each task to its parent project. This lets you create a Projects view in your Tasks database that shows how many open tasks each project has — giving you a quick health check on everything you're running.

A common mistake in GTD is having projects in your head without a captured Next Action in your system. If a project has no Next Action in your Notion task database, it's a source of anxiety every time you think about it because there's nothing you can actually do with it. Every active project needs a Next Action. Your weekly review enforces this rule.

Reference vs. Action: What Goes Where

Not everything that comes through your system is a task. Many things are reference material — information you need to keep but don't need to act on. A contract PDF, a client's style guide, meeting notes from a kickoff call — these are reference, not tasks. They belong in Notion pages, not in your Tasks database.

The discipline of separating reference from action is one of the hardest habits to build in GTD, but one of the most valuable. When your Tasks database contains only actionable items, you can trust it. When it contains a mix of tasks and reference material, you start skimming and stop trusting.

4. Time-Blocking with Calendar View

GTD tells you what to do. Time-blocking tells you when. The two methodologies are completely compatible and work best together — use GTD to maintain a clean, trusted list of Next Actions, and use time-blocking to schedule specific tasks into specific time slots on specific days.

What Time-Blocking Is and Why It Works

Time-blocking is the practice of assigning every hour of your work day to a specific task or type of work. Instead of working from a to-do list and picking tasks reactively, you create a schedule the night before or morning of, and then follow it.

The research on this is consistent: people who time-block get more done and report lower stress than those who work from unstructured task lists. The reason is straightforward — deciding what to work on takes mental energy. When you've already made those decisions in your planning session, you can spend your work hours executing instead of deliberating.

Setting Up the Calendar View in Notion

Add a Calendar view to your Tasks database. In the view settings:

  • Set the calendar property to Due Date
  • Filter out tasks where Due Date is empty (otherwise your calendar is cluttered with every undated task)
  • Color-code by Priority: Critical = red, High = orange, Medium = blue, Low = gray
  • Switch between Month view (for the big picture) and Week view (for day-by-day planning)

When you're planning tomorrow, switch to Week view, find tomorrow's column, and drag tasks into it or set their Due Date property. You can see at a glance whether you've overscheduled yourself — if every task shows as High or Critical and there are twelve of them in one day, you need to push some back.

The Ideal Daily Time-Block Structure for Freelancers

The optimal structure varies by person and work type, but here's a framework that works well for freelancers managing both creative and administrative work:

9:00 AM – 12:00 PM

Deep Work block. No meetings, no email, no social media. This is for your most cognitively demanding tasks: writing, designing, coding, strategic planning. Guard this time ferociously.

12:00 PM – 1:00 PM

Client communications. Check and respond to email, Slack, or DMs. Schedule calls for this window when possible. Your brain is starting to tire, but communication work doesn't require the same depth.

2:00 PM – 4:00 PM

Admin and medium-energy tasks. Invoicing, scheduling, research, reviewing documents, light editing. Tasks that require attention but not peak creative energy.

4:00 PM – 5:00 PM

Capture and review. Process your inbox, update task statuses, plan tomorrow, do a quick system check. End each day with a clean, up-to-date system.

The 9-12 deep work block is non-negotiable. If you start your day with email, you've handed control of your morning to whoever happened to send you a message. Deep work is where your best, highest-value output happens — protect that time first, and let everything else fit around it.

Protecting Deep Work Blocks

Structure alone doesn't create focus — you need to actively protect your deep work blocks from interruption. Practical tactics:

  • Notion reminders: Set a reminder on your "Deep Work Block" recurring task to fire at 9:00 AM. It's a simple cue to close everything else and start.
  • Phone on Do Not Disturb: Turn it on before you start your deep work block. Turn it off at noon. No exceptions.
  • Block your calendar: Add "Deep Work — Do Not Schedule" blocks in Google Calendar so clients and collaborators see you as unavailable during those hours.
  • Browser blocking: Apps like Freedom or Cold Turkey block social media and news sites during your chosen hours. Remove the willpower requirement entirely.
  • Communicate your availability: Let clients know you respond to messages during your communication window (12-1 PM). Most clients are completely fine with this once they understand the expectation.

Google Calendar Sync

Notion doesn't have native two-way sync with Google Calendar, which is one of its more frustrating limitations. The workaround depends on how important calendar visibility is to you:

Manual approach (recommended for most): Use Notion's calendar view for task scheduling and keep Google Calendar purely for meetings, appointments, and events with other people. They serve different purposes — Notion is your task execution view, Google Calendar is your shared availability layer. You check both, but you don't need them to sync.

Zapier automation: Build a Zap that triggers when a Notion database item is updated, and creates or updates a Google Calendar event with the task name and due date. This requires a Zapier account (paid plan for multi-step Zaps) and some setup time, but it's fully automatic once configured.

Make (Integromat) approach: More flexible than Zapier for complex sync scenarios, with a more generous free tier. Build a scenario that creates a Google Calendar event from new Notion tasks with Due Date set, and updates the event when the task changes.

5. Your Daily Dashboard Setup

The daily dashboard is the page you open every morning when you start work. It shows you exactly what you need to see without any searching or navigating: what's due today, what you're actively working on, and what's sitting in your inbox waiting to be processed.

Creating the Dashboard Page

Create a new Notion page called "Today Dashboard" or "Daily HQ" — something you'll recognize instantly. This page should be favorited (starred) in Notion's sidebar so it's one click away. Some people make it their browser homepage via the Notion web app URL.

The page layout is simple: three linked database views stacked vertically, followed by a journal section.

View 1: Due Today

Insert a linked view of your Tasks database. Apply these filters:

  • Due Date is on or before today
  • Status is not Done
  • Status is not Archived

Sort by Priority descending. Name this view "Due Today." This shows you every task with a deadline at or before right now — your non-negotiables.

View 2: In Progress

Insert another linked view of your Tasks database. Filter: Status = In Progress. No date filter — everything actively being worked on, regardless of due date. This is your "what am I actually doing right now" view. If there are more than three items here simultaneously, you're juggling too many things and need to focus.

View 3: Inbox for Capture

A third linked view filtered to Status = Inbox, sorted by Created Time ascending (oldest first). This reminds you every time you visit the dashboard that there are unprocessed items needing attention. Having the Inbox visible on your daily dashboard creates the processing habit naturally — you see it, you process it.

The Daily Startup Ritual

The ritual is five minutes at the start of each work day. No longer, no shorter:

  1. Open the dashboard. One click from your browser homepage or Notion sidebar.
  2. Scan Due Today. Are there surprises? Anything that appeared since yesterday that needs to be addressed immediately?
  3. Scan In Progress. Are these still actively in progress, or did some of them get stuck? Update statuses if needed.
  4. Pick your top three. Write your three most important tasks for today somewhere visible — in the journal section below, on a sticky note, anywhere. Three is the maximum. If you accomplish your top three, the day was a success.
  5. Glance at Inbox. If it's empty, great. If there are items, process them now or schedule time to process them before noon.

The Journal Section

Below your three views, add a simple text block for daily journaling. A few prompts that work well:

  • Top 3 priorities for today:
  • What would make today a win?
  • End of day reflection (what got done, what got blocked, what to carry forward):

The journal section serves two functions. First, it forces you to articulate your priorities out loud (in writing), which dramatically increases the odds that you actually pursue them. Second, it creates a daily log you can reference later — useful for client billing, performance reviews, and spotting patterns in what does and doesn't get done.

Dashboard tip: Add a full-width Notion workflow template divider between your task views and your journal section. It creates visual separation and helps your brain switch modes from "reviewing tasks" to "writing and reflecting." Small UX details like this compound over thousands of daily uses.

6. Recurring Tasks with Template Buttons

Recurring tasks are one of Notion's most notable gaps compared to dedicated task managers. Todoist has native recurrence with natural language ("every Monday at 9am"). Things 3 has an excellent repeating task implementation. Notion has neither — but it has Template Buttons, which can get you most of the way there with a bit of creative setup.

How Notion Template Buttons Work

Template Buttons are a Notion feature (found in the block menu as /button or /template) that creates one or more pre-configured database entries on click. You set up the button once with the template content — task name, status, properties, even default content — and every time you click it, a new row matching that template is added to your chosen database.

This is not automatic recurrence. You have to click the button. But for weekly or monthly routines, clicking a button at the start of each week to generate your recurring tasks takes about five seconds and keeps your task list clean the rest of the time.

Setting Up a Weekly Reset Button

Create a Template Button on your Today Dashboard or in a separate "System" page. Configure it to create multiple tasks simultaneously when clicked. A weekly reset button for a freelance content creator might generate:

  • "Monday: Review content calendar for the week" — Priority: High, Area: Business Dev, Estimated Time: 30 min, Energy: Medium
  • "Monday: Check all client deadlines and adjust calendar" — Priority: High, Area: Admin, Estimated Time: 15 min, Energy: Medium
  • "Wednesday: Send weekly progress update to active clients" — Priority: High, Area: Client Work, Estimated Time: 30 min, Energy: Medium
  • "Friday: Send outstanding invoices" — Priority: Critical, Area: Admin, Estimated Time: 15 min, Energy: Admin
  • "Friday: Weekly review" — Priority: High, Area: Admin, Estimated Time: 1 hr, Energy: Medium

Click this button every Monday morning. Your week's recurring tasks are populated instantly. The five tasks above require about three seconds to generate and replace something that would otherwise require maintaining a separate recurring task system.

The Frequency-Based System (Advanced)

For users who want more structure, build a Recurring Templates database alongside your Tasks database. Each row represents one recurring task template with these properties:

  • Task Name: The title of the recurring task
  • Frequency: Select — Daily, Weekly, Bi-Weekly, Monthly, Quarterly
  • Last Completed: Date — updated every time you complete the task
  • Default Priority: Select
  • Default Area: Select
  • Due Soon: Formula — checks if (Today - Last Completed) >= Frequency in days, returns a flag you can filter on

A filtered view of this database showing only rows where Due Soon = true becomes your "recurring tasks due" list. When something appears there, you create the actual task in your Tasks database and update Last Completed. It's more manual than native recurrence, but it's also more flexible — you can have a "monthly client follow-up sweep" that's genuinely monthly, not just 30 days after the last completion.

Recurring Task Library

Here's a complete library of recurring tasks worth having in your system:

Weekly recurring (every Monday):

  • Marketing content review — review what published last week, plan next week
  • Client project status check — are all active projects on track?
  • Financial quick check — what's outstanding, what's been paid, what's coming?

Weekly recurring (every Friday):

  • Invoice check — send any outstanding invoices before the weekend
  • Weekly review — the 45-minute ritual (see Section 9)
  • Next week prep — look at next week's calendar and block time

Monthly recurring (first Monday):

  • Client follow-up sweep — review all current and past clients for re-engagement opportunities
  • Subscription audit — are there any tools you're paying for that you're not using?
  • Goal review — check your monthly goals and assess progress
  • Income vs. target review — how did last month's income compare to your target?

Quarterly recurring:

  • Rate review — are your rates still aligned with your experience and market?
  • Client testimonial request — reach out to happy clients for testimonials or case studies
  • 90-day goal set — plan the next quarter's objectives
  • Tax prep review — gather documents and check in with your accountant

7. Project-Level Task Views

The global Tasks database is powerful, but some of the best productivity gains come from the project-level view — seeing all tasks for a single project in one place. This is where Notion's relational database capability really shines compared to any traditional task app.

Why You Need Both Global and Project Views

The global view (your Tasks database) is where you do daily prioritization and planning. You're looking across all your work to decide what to do next. The project view is where you do project-level thinking: how is this project progressing? What's the next milestone? Are there dependencies I'm not tracking?

A freelance web designer managing a client website project needs to see the global view when they're deciding what to work on Monday morning. They need the project view when they're on a client call and the client asks "what's the status of the homepage redesign?" Both views serve distinct, real needs.

Adding a Linked Database to Each Project Page

Inside each project page in your Projects database, add a linked database view of your Tasks database. Filter it to show only tasks where Project = this project. Give it a clear view name like "Project Tasks."

Now every project page in Notion shows you that project's task list inline, alongside the project brief, client information, files, and meeting notes. The project page becomes a true hub for everything related to that client engagement.

The Project Kanban Board

Switch the project-level task view to Board view with Status as the grouping column. You'll see columns: Inbox, Next Action, In Progress, Waiting, Done. Drag tasks between columns to update their status. This visual representation makes it instantly obvious whether a project is stuck (everything piling up in Waiting or Inbox) or healthy (tasks flowing through In Progress to Done).

Share a filtered public version of this board with clients using Notion's "Share to web" feature. Clients who can see their project's task status in real time ask fewer "what's the status?" questions, and feel more in control of their engagement with you. It's a simple, low-cost way to deliver a premium client experience.

Milestone Tracking

For longer projects, create a Milestones database related to both your Projects database and your Tasks database. Each milestone represents a meaningful deliverable: "Homepage mockup delivered," "Client approval on brand direction," "Site goes live." Milestones have Due Dates and a Status property (Upcoming, In Progress, Complete, At Risk).

Rollup properties in your Projects database can count open milestones, calculate percentage complete, and show the next upcoming milestone due date — all automatically, pulling from the linked Milestones database. This gives you project health metrics without any manual data entry.

The Project Cockpit Concept

The most mature implementation is what some Notion power users call the "project cockpit" — a single project page that aggregates everything relevant to that project in one view:

  • Project brief: Objectives, scope, client expectations, key constraints
  • Linked task view: All tasks for this project, board or list view
  • Meeting notes: A linked view of your Notes database filtered to this project's tag
  • Files and deliverables: Embedded files or links to Figma, Google Drive, etc.
  • Client info: Linked row from your Clients/CRM database
  • Financial summary: Linked invoice rows from your Invoices database, with rollup showing total billed vs. paid
  • Milestones: Linked milestone view showing upcoming deadlines

When a project cockpit is fully built out, you can walk into any client meeting, open that one page, and have every piece of relevant information immediately accessible. No app-switching, no file searching, no "let me check my notes." It's the kind of professional experience that justifies premium rates — and it's built in the same Notion workspace where you manage everything else. For more on this, see our guide to Notion project management.

8. Mobile Capture Workflow

The single biggest bottleneck in any task management system isn't the system design — it's capture friction. If adding a task to your system takes more than ten seconds, you'll start keeping things in your head again. And once you start keeping things in your head, your task system stops being a trusted external brain and becomes just another app you check occasionally.

Mobile capture is where this problem is most acute. You're walking out of a meeting and a client mentions a small request. You're commuting and think of something you need to do tomorrow. You're reading a newsletter and see an idea worth acting on. In all these cases, your phone is in your hand — and the speed of getting that task into your system matters enormously.

Notion Mobile App: Quick Create

The Notion mobile app has a quick-create button (the pencil icon or "+" button in the bottom bar) that lets you add a new database entry to a specific database without navigating to it. If you configure this to point directly at your Tasks database, you can go from phone in pocket to task captured in under five taps.

The key: set the default Status on new tasks to Inbox, and set your quick-create target to your Tasks database. Then when you create a new task from the mobile quick-create, it lands in Inbox automatically. You're not trying to categorize it, prioritize it, or link it to a project on the go. You're just capturing it. The rest happens during your morning or evening processing session.

Voice-to-Text Capture

For the fastest possible mobile capture, use voice-to-text. On both iOS and Android, tapping the microphone on your keyboard and speaking "pick up dry cleaning by Thursday" takes about three seconds total. It's faster than typing, it works while driving (hands-free), and it produces surprisingly accurate results with most modern speech recognition.

The workflow: open Notion mobile, tap quick-create, tap the task name field, tap the microphone, speak your task, done. Edit the transcription if needed. Set Status to Inbox. Five to ten seconds from start to captured.

Some people prefer to voice-capture directly to a notes app (Apple Notes, Google Keep) and batch-transfer to Notion during their daily processing. This trades some system purity for capture speed — and it's worth considering if you find the Notion mobile app too slow to open under real-world conditions.

Notion Web Clipper

Install the Notion Web Clipper browser extension on your desktop and mobile browsers. When you're reading something on the web — an article, a product page, a job posting — the Web Clipper lets you save it directly to Notion with one tap. You can choose which database or page to save it to, add a comment, and the page URL is preserved for reference.

For your task system, configure the Web Clipper to save to your Tasks database with Status = Inbox and a Tags value of "to-review." Now web content that needs action (read this article, look into this tool, apply for this opportunity) goes directly into your task system instead of getting lost in browser bookmarks you never revisit.

The Paper Notepad Backup

Here's the contrarian recommendation: keep a small paper notepad and pen in meetings. Not for everything, but for the specific scenario where you're in a client meeting and don't want to be seen fiddling with your phone.

Taking notes on paper during meetings signals respect and attentiveness in a way that typing on a phone doesn't. Write action items with a star next to them. At the end of your workday, transfer starred items to Notion during your end-of-day review. Your paper notepad becomes a trusted capture layer for the one context where phone-based capture doesn't work socially.

Capture rule: Every system needs exactly one capture inbox, not multiple. If some tasks go to Notion, some to your phone's reminders app, some to Slack's saved messages, and some to paper — you won't trust any of them. Pick one primary capture location (Notion with the mobile app) and use everything else only as temporary holding that flows into that one place at your end-of-day review.

9. Weekly Review Ritual

The weekly review is not optional. Every other part of the system — the database, the views, the daily dashboard, the GTD inbox — depends on the weekly review to function correctly over time. Skip the weekly review for two weeks and your system degrades. Skip it for a month and it's unusable. The review is the maintenance ritual that keeps the engine running.

David Allen calls it "the critical success factor for making GTD work." He's right. The weekly review is where you close open loops, update your trusted lists, and reconnect with your bigger picture. Without it, you're flying blind by mid-week.

Scheduling the Review

The weekly review needs a fixed appointment on your calendar, not a loose intention to "do it sometime on Friday." Block 45-60 minutes every Friday afternoon — 3:00 PM to 4:00 PM is a good choice, because it's the natural wind-down period of the week and it gives you the weekend to let any insights percolate.

Protect this appointment more aggressively than client calls. Clients can reschedule. Your weekly review cannot. If you miss it once, reschedule it for Saturday morning. Never skip it entirely for a week — the backlog grows faster than you think.

The 5-Step Weekly Review Process

Step 1: Empty the inbox to zero. Open your Inbox view in Notion. Process every single item. For each one: clarify whether it's actionable, assign a Status (Next Action, Waiting, Someday), link to the relevant project, set Priority and Due Date if applicable. The goal is zero items in Inbox by the end of Step 1. Don't skip items because they're uncomfortable or unclear — clarify them now so they don't haunt you next week.

Step 2: Review all In Progress tasks. Open your In Progress view. For each task: is it actually in progress, or has it stalled? If stalled — what's blocking it? Is it really Waiting (blocked on someone else)? Or has the deadline passed and it should be reassigned or deleted? Update every status so your In Progress view reflects reality, not aspiration.

Step 3: Review all projects — does each have a Next Action? Go to your Projects database. Scroll through every active project. For each one, ask: does this project have at least one Next Action in my Tasks database? If a project has no Next Action, it's stalled. Either add a Next Action now (the simplest choice), put the project on hold explicitly, or acknowledge it's actually complete and close it. No project should sit in your active list without a Next Action driving it forward.

Step 4: Review the coming week. Open your Tasks calendar view and look at next week. What's scheduled? Does it make sense given your energy and time? Are there any deadlines lurking that need task-level preparation this week? Block time in Google Calendar for your deep work sessions next week. Identify your top 3 outcomes for the week and write them down somewhere visible.

Step 5: Do a mind sweep. Close Notion. Set a five-minute timer. Grab a piece of paper or open a blank Notion page. Write down everything that's on your mind — worries, ideas, things you haven't done, things you're afraid to forget, half-baked plans, nagging obligations. Don't filter or evaluate. Just capture for five minutes straight. Then process what you wrote into Notion: task, reference note, project idea, or delete.

The mind sweep is the most undervalued part of the weekly review. Your brain will hold onto items it doesn't trust your system to remember. The only way to get those items out of your head is to write them down and process them into your trusted system. After a good mind sweep, you'll feel a specific mental lightness — a release of held tension. That's your brain letting go of things it no longer needs to maintain.

Monthly Additions to the Review

On the last Friday of each month, extend your weekly review by 20 minutes to cover these monthly checkpoints:

  • Review your monthly goals. Pull up whatever goal-tracking system you use. How did the month go? Which goals got traction, which got ignored? Why?
  • Check income vs. targets. Compare actual monthly income against your target. If you're behind, what actions would close the gap? Add those actions as Next Actions in your task system.
  • Review marketing activities. What content went out? What led to inquiries? What should you repeat, and what didn't work?
  • Update your someday/maybe list. Review the tasks sitting in your deferred or someday pile. Are any of them now relevant? Are any still sitting there that should be deleted?

The monthly review keeps your task system connected to your bigger business objectives. Without it, you can have a perfectly organized task system that's perfectly optimized for the wrong things — finishing every task efficiently while missing the outcomes that actually matter for your business.

Weekly review shortcut: Create a "Weekly Review" template in Notion that has the five steps listed as checkbox blocks. Duplicate it each Friday. Check off each step as you complete it. This turns the review from a vague ritual into a concrete checklist — and gives you a record of every review you've completed, which is satisfying to see accumulate over months. 10. Frequently Asked Questions

Is Notion good as a task manager?

Yes, Notion is excellent as a task manager for complex, project-based work — especially for freelancers, solopreneurs, and knowledge workers who need tasks linked to projects, clients, and documents. It shines when you need relations between databases, custom properties, and flexible views that standard to-do apps can't match. You can build views that show only your deep work tasks due this week sorted by energy level — that's just not possible in Todoist. The main caveat: if you just need a simple personal to-do list, a dedicated app like Todoist has faster mobile capture and significantly less setup friction. Notion's task management power is real, but it requires an upfront investment in building your system. If you're managing multiple client projects simultaneously or running a freelance business with CRM, invoicing, and content management needs alongside tasks, Notion is almost always the better long-term choice.

How do I create a to-do list in Notion?

The simplest way is to use the /todo block anywhere in Notion to create inline checkboxes — type your task text and check the box when done. This is great for simple lists but doesn't give you any structure for priorities, due dates, or organization. For a proper task management system, create a database (use /database or New database from the sidebar) with a Title property for the task name, a Checkbox property for completion, and a Date property for due dates. Filter the view to show only unchecked tasks and sort by Date to create a functional, self-cleaning to-do list. The database approach lets you later add properties like Priority, Area, and Project without rebuilding anything — you're investing in infrastructure that grows with your needs.

Can Notion replace Todoist?

Notion can replace Todoist for many users, particularly those with complex project structures that benefit from linked databases and rich context on each task. Todoist has clear advantages: faster mobile capture with natural language date input ("tomorrow at 3pm" works natively), better push notifications and reminders, and a simpler interface that requires no setup. Notion wins on depth: you can link tasks to clients, projects, invoices, and meeting notes in ways Todoist simply can't match. You can build a view showing only tasks for Client X sorted by deadline that's embedded directly inside Client X's profile page. For freelancers managing multiple client projects with meaningful overlap between tasks and documentation, Notion typically wins the long-term comparison. For students or individuals with primarily personal task management needs, Todoist is often the better daily driver.

How do I set up GTD in Notion?

Set up GTD in Notion by creating a Tasks database with a Status select property containing the GTD stages: Inbox, Next Action, In Progress, Waiting, Someday/Maybe, Done, and Archived. Create separate saved views for each stage — your Inbox view (Status = Inbox), Next Actions view (Status = Next Action, sorted by Priority), and Waiting view (Status = Waiting) are the three you'll use daily. Process your Inbox once or twice a day: for each item, decide if it's actionable, and if so assign the correct Status plus Priority, Due Date, and Project relation. Connect your task database to a Projects database using a Relation property — every active project should have at least one task with Status = Next Action. Run a weekly review every Friday to empty the inbox, review all projects for Next Actions, and plan the coming week. GTD in Notion works exceptionally well once the initial setup is done.

Does Notion have recurring tasks?

Notion does not have native recurring tasks in the way Todoist (natural language recurrence) or Things 3 (sophisticated repeating task engine) do. The main workaround is Template Buttons — a built-in Notion feature that creates one or more pre-configured database entries on click. You build a "Weekly Reset" button that generates your standard Monday recurring tasks when clicked at the start of each week. A more structured approach uses a Recurring Templates database with Last Completed and Frequency properties, plus a formula that flags tasks when they're due again. Neither approach is as seamless as native recurrence, but both work reliably for weekly and monthly routines. If native recurring tasks are critical to your workflow, Todoist or Things 3 remain the better choice for that specific feature — though you can run both alongside Notion, using a dedicated app for recurrence and Notion for everything else.

How do I sync Notion with Google Calendar?

Notion does not have a native two-way sync with Google Calendar, which is one of its most-requested missing features. The most reliable workaround for most users is treating them as complementary tools: Notion is your task scheduling view (using the Calendar view on your Tasks database), and Google Calendar is your meetings and appointments layer. You check both, but you don't need them to sync because they serve different purposes. If you need actual sync, the most reliable approach is using Zapier or Make (formerly Integromat) to build an automation: when a Notion task is created with a Due Date, a corresponding Google Calendar event is created automatically. This requires a paid Zapier plan for multi-step automations but works reliably once set up. A simpler manual approach: at the end of your daily planning session, add any time-blocked tasks manually to Google Calendar as time blocks. Takes 2-3 minutes and keeps both systems in sync without automation overhead.

What is the best Notion template for task management?

Pre-built options like FreelanceOS go further by including client CRM, invoice tracker, project management, and daily planning systems alongside the task manager, making it a complete business operating system rather than just a to-do list. The practical advantage of starting from a pre-built template is significant: building a solid Notion task system from scratch takes 10-20 hours of setup, testing, and iteration. A quality template compresses that to 30 minutes of customization. Look for templates that include at minimum: a Tasks database with Status, Priority, Due Date, and Project relation properties; at least three standard views (Inbox, Next Actions, Calendar); and a Daily Dashboard page with linked views. The weekly review template is a bonus that few templates include but makes an enormous difference in long-term usage.

How do I use Notion for daily planning?

Create a dedicated "Today Dashboard" page in Notion with three linked database views from your Tasks database: Due Today (filtered by today's date, excluding Done and Archived), In Progress (filtered to Status = In Progress), and Inbox (filtered to Status = Inbox for new captures). Favorite this page in Notion's sidebar so it's always one click away — some users set the direct Notion URL as their browser homepage. Start each morning with a 5-minute dashboard review: scan what's due, check what's in progress, pick your top three priorities for the day, and write them in a journal section below your task views. The journal section (a simple text block with daily prompts) serves double duty as a reflection space for end-of-day notes. Keep the review to five minutes — the value comes from doing it consistently every day, not from making it elaborate. If you find yourself spending 20 minutes on the daily review, you need to simplify your system, not extend your morning routine.
