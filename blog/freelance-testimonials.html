How to Get Freelance Testimonials: A Practical Guide for Self-Employed Professionals

By FreelanceOS · July 2026 · 9 min read

Why Testimonials Are the Most Powerful Freelance Marketing Asset

Testimonials are social proof — evidence from neutral third parties that you deliver on your promises. Unlike a portfolio (which shows your work) or your bio (which describes you), testimonials show what it's actually like to work with you.

For freelancers without a large brand or marketing budget, strong client testimonials are the primary trust-builder that converts prospects into paying clients. A prospect who has never worked with you faces uncertainty: will this freelancer deliver? Will they communicate well? Is the price worth it? A testimonial from someone in a similar situation answers all three questions more effectively than anything you could write about yourself.

The data backs this up. Nielsen research finds that 92% of people trust peer recommendations over brand advertising. BrightLocal reports that 88% of consumers trust online reviews as much as personal recommendations. Your freelance testimonials are mini-reviews — and they carry the same weight.

The asymmetry: One strong testimonial from a recognizable company or well-known client can do more for your freelance business than six months of content marketing. If you have them, use them prominently. If you don't have them yet, getting them is your highest-leverage business activity this week.

When to Ask for a Testimonial

Timing is everything. Ask too early and you've captured nothing meaningful. Ask too late and the client has moved on, the positive experience has faded, and you're asking a favor of a busy person who has already closed the mental chapter on your project.

The Golden Window: Two Weeks After Project Completion

Within two weeks of delivering a project, the client is experiencing the results — their website is live, the copy is converting, the design looks great on their Instagram — and the positive memory is fresh. This is when asking feels natural and when you'll get the most specific, enthusiastic responses.

Secondary Opportunities

  • When a client compliments you unprompted — if a client emails "I just wanted to say the deliverables were excellent," respond with gratitude and an immediate ask: "I'm so glad it landed well — would you be willing to share that as a brief testimonial?" Strike while the emotion is present.
  • When a client refers you to someone else — referral is the highest expression of client satisfaction. If someone is willing to attach their reputation to yours, they are definitely willing to write a testimonial. Ask while the momentum is high.
  • At a project milestone on long engagements — for ongoing retainer clients, don't wait until the end. Ask after a major deliverable, a campaign launch, or a strong results report.
  • During quarterly or annual reviews — if you do structured check-ins with ongoing clients, add testimonial collection as a standing agenda item.

What to avoid: Don't ask for a testimonial in the same message where you're requesting final payment or flagging a problem. The ask should land when the relationship energy is positive — not when you're in a transactional or problem-solving mode.

How to Ask Without Feeling Awkward

Most freelancers don't collect testimonials — not because clients wouldn't give them, but because asking feels uncomfortable. Here are three approaches that make the ask feel natural:

Approach 1 — Direct Email Request

Send a short, personal email within two weeks of project completion. Keep it brief, specific, and low-friction. Don't ask for a "review" — ask for a "testimonial" or a few sentences about their experience.

Email template Hi [name], I really enjoyed working on [project] together and I'm glad you're happy with how it came out. If you have 5 minutes, I'd love a brief testimonial I could share on my website — just 2-3 sentences about your experience working with me and what you got out of the project. Happy to draft something for you to edit if that's easier. Either way, thank you for the great collaboration.

Approach 2 — LinkedIn Recommendation Request

Go to the client's LinkedIn profile → click "More" → "Request a recommendation." Customize the message with context about the specific project. LinkedIn recommendations are double-sided — they appear on both your profiles, creating persistent public social proof that's visible to future clients who look you up.

Approach 3 — Video Testimonial Request

For top-tier clients, a brief video testimonial is worth 10 written ones. Frame it as quick and easy: "Would you be open to recording a 30-60 second video about our work together? I can send you a Loom link — no special setup needed, just hit record on your laptop camera." Video is far harder to fake and far more emotionally persuasive than text.

The "Draft It for Them" Technique

This is the single most effective tactic for collecting high-quality testimonials fast. Most clients are genuinely happy to write one — they just don't know what to say or how to frame it. By offering to write a draft they can edit and approve, you remove the blank-page problem and dramatically increase your response rate.

Your draft should hit four notes:

  1. Who the client is and their context
  2. The problem or goal they came to you with
  3. A specific positive outcome from working together
  4. Who they'd recommend you to
Draft testimonial example "Working with [your name] on our [project type] was an excellent experience. They delivered [specific outcome] ahead of schedule and communicated clearly throughout the process. I'd recommend them to any [client type] looking for [service type] — especially those who want someone reliable who takes the brief seriously."

The client approves it as-is, edits lightly to make it more personal, or uses it as a starting point and writes their own version. Any outcome produces a quality testimonial. The key is removing their activation energy barrier.

What Makes a Great Testimonial: The 4 Elements

01

Specific Outcome

"Our conversion rate increased 23%" beats "great work" every time. Specificity = credibility.

02

Named Attribution

Name + role + company transforms a quote into verified social proof. Anonymous testimonials carry less weight.

03

Before/After Story

"We were struggling with X, now we have Y" shows the transformation and makes the value tangible for prospects.

04

Objection Handler

"I was nervous about hiring remote — the process was seamless" directly addresses fears your next prospect probably has.

You won't always get all four in a single testimonial — but when you're drafting one for a client to approve, try to incorporate as many as possible while keeping the language natural and in their voice.

Where to Display Testimonials

On Your Website

  • Homepage, near the main CTA — 2-3 strong testimonials placed close to your "hire me" or "get a quote" button. These are the highest-converting placement spots because the prospect is actively considering reaching out.
  • Dedicated testimonials or social proof page — for freelancers with 10+ testimonials, a dedicated page gives you space to feature the full library and show volume of satisfied clients.
  • Service-specific pages — match the testimonial to the service. A testimonial from a SaaS company about your UX work belongs on your UX services page, not your copywriting page.
  • Case study pages — weave testimonials into the narrative. Show the challenge, your approach, the result, then quote the client on their experience.

In Proposals

Include 1-2 relevant testimonials in every proposal you send — matched to the client's industry or project type. A marketing director is more persuaded by a testimonial from another marketing director than by a testimonial from a startup founder, even if the work quality described is identical.

The proposal we submitted to win this piece of business was cleaner, faster, and more professional than anything I'd put together before. Working with [your name] meant we showed up to the pitch looking like a serious team.

— J. Torres, Creative Director at Brandworks Co.

On Platforms

  • Upwork / Fiverr — platform reviews are automatically collected and are the primary trust signal. On these platforms, your review count and average rating are more prominent than your portfolio. Delivering exceptional work and following up professionally drives review rates.
  • LinkedIn — recommendations appear on your profile and are highly visible to any professional who researches you before reaching out. Request recommendations strategically from clients with impressive titles or well-known companies.
  • Email signature — a brief testimonial quote in your email signature puts social proof in front of every person you email, including potential clients you're prospecting.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I ask a client for a testimonial without feeling awkward?
Frame the request as mutual value rather than a personal favor. After a successful project, say something like: "I loved working on this together — if you were happy with the outcome, a brief 2-3 sentence testimonial would mean a lot to me. I can draft something for you to edit if that makes it easier." Offering to draft the testimonial dramatically reduces friction and increases your response rate. Most clients are happy to give one — they just don't know what to write.
When is the best time to ask for a freelance testimonial?
The best time is within two weeks of project completion — when the client is experiencing the results and the positive experience is fresh. Secondary windows include: when a client compliments your work unprompted (ask immediately), when they refer you to someone new, and at quarterly check-ins with ongoing clients. Never ask for a testimonial in the same conversation where you're handling a complaint or requesting payment.
What should a great freelance testimonial include?
A great testimonial includes four elements: a specific measurable outcome (not just "great work"), clear attribution with the client's name, role, and company, a before/after narrative showing the transformation your work created, and ideally a statement that addresses a common concern prospects have about hiring a freelancer. When you draft testimonials for clients to approve, build in all four elements while keeping the language natural and in their voice.
Where should I display testimonials on my freelance website?
Display 2-3 of your strongest testimonials on the homepage near your main call-to-action — this is the highest-converting placement. Also place testimonials on each service page (matching testimonial to the service type), on a dedicated social proof page if you have ten or more, and inside project proposals. The closer a testimonial appears to a decision point, the more it influences the outcome.
Is a video testimonial better than a written one?
Video testimonials are more persuasive than written ones — they communicate emotion, are harder to fabricate, and hold attention longer. However, they are harder to collect. A practical approach: collect written testimonials as your default (easy, scalable), and invest time in requesting video testimonials from two or three of your most enthusiastic and high-profile clients to use on your homepage and in high-stakes proposals.
