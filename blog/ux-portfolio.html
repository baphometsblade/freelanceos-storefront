UX Designer Portfolio: How to Build a UX Portfolio That Gets You Hired (2026)

By FreelanceOS · July 2026 · 12 min read
Home › Blog › UX Portfolio Guide 2026

Your UX portfolio is the single most important artifact in your design career. It's how clients evaluate whether to hire you. It's how hiring managers decide if you're worth interviewing. It's how you differentiate yourself in a market that now includes tens of thousands of UX designers globally.

The good news: most UX portfolios are bad. They show pretty screenshots without explaining the thinking behind them. They list tools used without showing problems solved. They're "UI portfolios" dressed up as "UX portfolios." If yours shows your actual process and thinking, you'll stand out.

This guide covers what to include, how to structure case studies, the best platforms, common mistakes, and how to build a strong portfolio even if you're just starting out.

The fundamental thing clients and hiring managers want to see: How do you think? What's your process? Can you articulate design decisions in business terms? Screenshots are secondary — your thinking is primary.

What Makes a Great UX Portfolio

Strong UX portfolios share four characteristics:

  1. They show process, not just outcomes. Anyone can show a beautiful final screen. A strong portfolio shows the messy research, the rejected ideas, the pivots based on user feedback, and the iteration that led to the final design. Hiring managers want to see how you think, not just what you produce.
  2. They connect design to business impact. "I redesigned the onboarding flow" is weak. "I redesigned the onboarding flow, which reduced drop-off during signup by 34%." The ability to quantify design outcomes separates senior UX designers from junior ones — even if your numbers are modest.
  3. They're appropriately selective. 3–5 deep case studies outperform 15 shallow project thumbnails every time. Curating well signals maturity and judgment. Showing everything signals you can't prioritize.
  4. They have a clear narrative voice. Your portfolio should sound like a thoughtful person, not a design résumé. The best case studies read like good writing — they have a problem, a journey, and a resolution.

What to Include in a UX Portfolio

1. Homepage / Landing View

Your portfolio homepage needs to immediately answer: Who are you? What kind of UX work do you do? Who do you do it for? A short positioning statement (two sentences maximum) does more work than a wall of text. Example: "I'm a UX designer specializing in B2B SaaS products. I help companies reduce user frustration at key conversion and activation moments." Then show thumbnails of 3–5 case studies below.

2. Case Studies (3–5, minimum 2)

Case studies are the core of your UX portfolio. Each one should tell the story of a design project from problem to solution. Structure them using the framework below.

The UX Case Study Framework

Project overview (2–3 sentences): what the product is, what problem you were solving, your role on the project, and the timeline.

The problem: what was the user struggling with? What business metric was suffering? Include data if available — screenshots of bad reviews, drop-off rate stats, support ticket volume.

Research: how did you validate the problem? User interviews, usability tests, heuristic evaluation, analytics review, survey data? Show the artifacts — interview notes, affinity maps, user journey maps. This is where UX portfolios diverge from UI portfolios.

Ideation: how did you explore solutions? Show sketches, low-fidelity wireframes, alternatives you considered and rejected (and why). The rejected alternatives are often the most interesting part.

Design and iteration: show the progression from wireframes to prototypes to final UI. Include feedback you received and how it changed your design. Before/after comparisons are powerful.

Outcome: what happened after launch? Improved conversion, better usability test scores, positive NPS change, reduced support volume? If you don't have post-launch metrics, describe the stakeholder reception and your personal learning.

Reflection: one brief paragraph on what you'd do differently and what you learned. This signals growth mindset.

3. About Page

A brief About page (200–400 words) explaining your background, design philosophy, and approach. Not a résumé — a narrative. How did you get into UX? What kinds of problems excite you? What's your process at a high level? Include a professional photo. A casual, honest tone beats corporate-speak every time.

4. Resume / CV (downloadable)

Offer a downloadable PDF resume. Keep it to one page for designers with less than 10 years of experience. UX-focused résumés should list measurable achievements, not just job descriptions.

5. Contact

Make it trivially easy to reach you. Email, LinkedIn, and a contact form. If you're freelancing, make this section explicit about what you're available for and your rough timeline to start.

How Many Projects Should a UX Portfolio Have?

Three to five case studies is the right number for most UX portfolios. Here's the reasoning:

  • Fewer than 3: not enough depth to demonstrate range
  • 3–5: the sweet spot — enough to show breadth, selective enough to show judgment
  • More than 5: the marginal projects dilute the strong ones; hiring managers rarely look past the third case study

If you're early in your career and don't have 3 real projects yet, start with what you have — even 2 deep case studies is better than 8 shallow ones. Build the third through a self-initiated project (see "Building a UX Portfolio With No Experience" below).

Best Platforms for a UX Portfolio

Notion

Free – $16/mo

Fast setup, excellent for text-heavy case studies, easy to update. Notion portfolios look clean and professional. Best for designers who want to launch quickly or who already use Notion.

Webflow

$14–23/mo

Most design control — build a fully custom portfolio site without code. The best option for designers who want their portfolio itself to demonstrate design capability. Steeper learning curve.

Cargo

$13/mo

Minimalist, beautiful templates popular with visual designers and creative UX designers. Less flexible than Webflow but faster to set up with a distinctive aesthetic.

Framer

Free – $20/mo

Design tool with web publishing built in. Good for designers who want to prototype and present work in the same tool. Clean output, growing portfolio site use cases.

Behance

Free

Adobe's creative platform with built-in discovery. Useful for visibility but not ideal as your primary portfolio — link out to more detailed case study pages. Works well as a supplementary presence alongside your main site.

Squarespace / Wix

$16–25/mo

Easiest setup for non-technical designers. Good templates, hosting included. Less design flexibility than Webflow but far faster to launch. Acceptable choice for most UX freelancers.

Freelance clients and hiring managers evaluate portfolios differently — understanding this helps you optimize for your situation.

If you're primarily targeting freelance clients, emphasize results and business outcomes prominently. If targeting full-time roles, put more weight on your research methods and collaboration process.

Common UX Portfolio Mistakes

Do This

  • Show your research artifacts (interview notes, journey maps, affinity diagrams)
  • Explain why you made specific design decisions
  • Include metrics and measurable outcomes
  • Show before/after comparisons
  • Write in first person with a clear, confident voice
  • Include failed ideas and what you learned from them

Avoid This

  • Showing only final polished screens with no context
  • Listing tools without explaining what problems they solved
  • Including every project you've ever done
  • Vague outcome statements ("improved user experience")
  • Password protecting your portfolio with no way to get in
  • Using portfolio templates that look identical to 1,000 other designers

Building a UX Portfolio With No Experience

The most common question from people new to UX: "How do I build a portfolio if I haven't had a UX job?" The answer: you don't need paid client work to build a compelling portfolio. Here's how:

Option 1: Redesign an Existing Product

Pick an app or website you use regularly and find genuinely frustrating. Document your experience: what's confusing? What do you expect to happen that doesn't? Then conduct 3–5 user interviews with others who use the same product. Build an improved version based on what you learn. This follows the exact same process as real UX work — and hiring managers can evaluate your thinking regardless of whether it was a paid engagement.

Option 2: Solve a Problem You've Experienced

Think of a process in your life that has no good digital solution. Design that solution: define the problem, research potential users (friends, family, people on Reddit who share the frustration), ideate, wireframe, prototype, test. A self-initiated project with good process documentation is often more impressive than a shallow paid project.

Option 3: Volunteer for a Nonprofit

Many nonprofits have poor digital experiences and can't afford to pay for UX help. Offer to conduct a UX audit or redesign a user flow for free. You get a real project with a real organization and can name them in your portfolio (with permission).

Option 4: UX Challenges and Hackathons

Sites like Designlab, ADP List, and various UX communities run design challenges with briefs. Complete these with full process documentation. The brief-based constraint can actually produce more focused case studies than open-ended self-initiated projects.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many projects should a UX portfolio have?

3 to 5 well-documented case studies is the ideal number. Each case study should be deep — showing research, process, design decisions, and outcomes — rather than a shallow screenshot gallery. More than 5 projects dilutes the quality of the overall portfolio. If you only have 2 strong projects, that's fine — 2 excellent case studies beat 8 mediocre ones.

What should a UX portfolio include?

A strong UX portfolio includes: 3–5 case studies showing your design process from research to outcome; a clear homepage that explains who you are and what you do; an About page with your background and approach; a downloadable resume; and a contact page. Each case study should cover the problem, your research method, design decisions and alternatives considered, and measurable outcomes.

What is the best platform for a UX portfolio?

Notion and Webflow are the two best options for most UX designers in 2026. Notion is fastest to set up and excellent for case study text with embedded images — ideal if you want to launch quickly. Webflow gives maximum design control for designers who want their portfolio site itself to demonstrate their design skills. Both can look professional and are widely accepted by clients and hiring managers.

Can I include NDA work in my UX portfolio?

Yes, with modifications. Blur identifiable brand elements, anonymize the company name if required ("a fintech startup"), and describe your process without sharing proprietary research data or unreleased product screens. Most reviewers understand NDA constraints and are evaluating your thinking, not your ability to name-drop companies. Always check with your client before including their work, even anonymized.

How do I build a UX portfolio with no experience?

Create 2–3 self-initiated or volunteer projects with full process documentation. Redesign a frustrating app with your own user research, design a concept product for a problem you've experienced, or volunteer for a nonprofit that needs UX help. The case study format is identical — document your research, ideation, decisions, and final design. Many UX designers' first portfolio pieces were unpaid concept projects, and they're fully accepted by the industry as valid portfolio work.
