Why Every Freelancer Needs a Contract

Most freelancers start without contracts. They trust the handshake, the warm intro, the LinkedIn message that seemed professional. Then a client disputes the scope. Or refuses to pay. Or uses your work without crediting you. Or cancels the project after you've cleared your calendar for three weeks.

A freelance contract doesn't mean you don't trust your client. It means you're running a real business. It protects both parties by making expectations explicit — before any work begins. And when something goes wrong (and in a long freelance career, something always goes wrong), you'll want the signed agreement.

The Three Biggest Risks a Contract Prevents

  • Payment disputes. "I thought this was included" and "I didn't approve that revision" are the most common excuses for delayed or reduced payment. A signed SOW eliminates the ambiguity.
  • Scope creep. Without defined deliverables, clients will naturally expand the project. A contract lets you point to the original agreement and charge for additions.
  • IP ownership conflicts. If your contract doesn't specify who owns the final work, a client could resell or redistribute it without additional compensation. Or worse — a dispute could arise years later.
Real stat: According to Freelancers Union surveys, 71% of freelancers have dealt with a non-paying client at some point. Of those who had a signed contract, 83% recovered full or partial payment. Without a contract, that number drops dramatically.

Beyond protection, a contract makes you look more professional. It signals that you've done this before, that you take your work seriously, and that you run a business — not a hobby. Clients who intend to pay you fairly almost never object to signing a clear, reasonable agreement.

10 Essential Clauses Your Freelance Contract Must Include

1. Parties and Project Identification

Name the client's legal entity (not just their first name), your legal name or business name, and a brief project title. This makes the contract legally enforceable and ensures it's tied to the right engagement.

2. Scope of Work

The most important clause. Be specific: list exactly what you will deliver, what format it will be in, and what is explicitly out of scope. "Website design" is not enough. "5 responsive page designs (homepage, about, services, contact, blog index) delivered as Figma files" is a scope.

3. Timeline and Milestones

Include a project start date, milestone dates (if applicable), and a final delivery date. If your delivery depends on client feedback, include a clause: "Timeline assumes client feedback within 3 business days of each milestone."

4. Payment Terms

Specify the total project fee (or hourly rate), payment schedule (e.g., 50% upfront, 50% on delivery), accepted payment methods, and the due date for each invoice. Include your late payment policy — typically 1.5% per month on overdue invoices.

5. Kill Fee (Cancellation Clause)

If the client cancels mid-project, the kill fee compensates you for blocked time and work completed. A standard kill fee is 25%–50% of the remaining project value. Without this, a client can cancel with no financial consequence — after you've cleared your calendar.

6. Revision Policy

Define how many rounds of revisions are included and what constitutes a revision vs. a new request. Example: "Two rounds of revisions included per deliverable. Additional revisions billed at $X/hour." This single clause eliminates 80% of scope creep disputes.

7. Intellectual Property Ownership

Specify when IP transfers to the client. Best practice: "All intellectual property rights in the final deliverables transfer to Client upon receipt of full payment." This means if they don't pay, you retain the IP. You can also include a clause retaining rights to use the work in your portfolio.

8. Confidentiality

If you'll access proprietary information, business strategy, or unreleased products, include a mutual NDA clause. Keep it simple: both parties agree not to disclose the other's confidential information to third parties without written consent.

9. Termination

Both parties should have the right to terminate. Define the notice period (typically 14 days), what happens to work in progress (you retain it until payment is made), and which obligations survive termination (confidentiality, IP, payment for work completed).

10. Governing Law

Specify which state or country's laws govern the contract and where disputes will be resolved. Use your location, not the client's. This ensures any legal dispute happens in a jurisdiction you can access.

Full Sample Freelance Contract

Below is a complete freelance contract template you can adapt for your projects. Replace all bracketed items with your specific information.

Freelance Services Agreement

This Freelance Services Agreement ("Agreement") is entered into as of [DATE] by and between [YOUR NAME/BUSINESS], ("Contractor") and [CLIENT LEGAL ENTITY NAME], ("Client").

1. Services and Scope of Work

Contractor agrees to provide the following services ("Services"): [DETAILED DESCRIPTION OF DELIVERABLES]. Any work not explicitly described above is outside the scope of this Agreement and may be subject to a separate proposal and fee.

2. Timeline

Work will commence on [START DATE] and final deliverables will be delivered no later than [END DATE], provided that Client supplies any required materials, feedback, or approvals within 3 business days of each milestone. Contractor is not responsible for delays caused by late Client responses.

3. Payment Terms

Total project fee: $[AMOUNT]. Payment schedule: [50%] due upon signing; [50%] due upon final delivery. Invoices are due within [14] days of receipt. Overdue invoices accrue interest at 1.5% per month. Contractor reserves the right to pause work on overdue accounts.

4. Kill Fee

If Client terminates this Agreement after work has begun, Client agrees to pay: (a) all fees for work completed to date, plus (b) a kill fee equal to [25%] of the remaining project value. This compensates Contractor for reserved capacity and work in progress.

5. Revisions

This Agreement includes [TWO] rounds of revisions per deliverable. A "revision" is a minor change to existing approved work. Major changes to scope, direction, or deliverables constitute new work and will be quoted separately.

6. Intellectual Property

Upon receipt of full payment, Contractor assigns all rights, title, and interest in the final deliverables to Client. Prior to full payment, all work remains the property of Contractor. Contractor retains the right to display completed work in their portfolio unless Client requests otherwise in writing.

7. Confidentiality

Both parties agree to keep confidential all proprietary information shared during this engagement and not to disclose it to third parties without prior written consent. This obligation survives termination of this Agreement.

8. Termination

Either party may terminate this Agreement with [14] days written notice. Upon termination, Client shall pay for all work completed and approved. Kill fee provisions apply per Section 4. Contractor retains ownership of incomplete work until all outstanding amounts are paid.

9. Independent Contractor

Contractor is an independent contractor, not an employee. Contractor is responsible for their own taxes, insurance, and benefits. Nothing in this Agreement creates a partnership, joint venture, or employment relationship.

10. Governing Law

This Agreement shall be governed by the laws of [YOUR STATE/COUNTRY]. Any disputes shall be resolved in the courts of [YOUR CITY/JURISDICTION]. In the event of a dispute, the prevailing party shall be entitled to recover reasonable legal fees.

Signatures

By signing below, both parties agree to the terms of this Agreement.

Contractor: _________________________ Date: ___________
[YOUR NAME]

Client: _________________________ Date: ___________
[CLIENT NAME, TITLE, COMPANY]

Red Flags in Client Contracts

Sometimes clients will send you their contract instead. Before you sign anything, watch for these clauses that are unfair to freelancers:

Work-for-hire with no ownership transfer payment.

Work-for-hire provisions transfer copyright to the client automatically. That's fine — but if the contract says work-for-hire without a corresponding fee that reflects the full value of the work (not just your time), you're undercharging.

"Client may modify deliverables without notice."

This lets the client alter your work in ways that could damage your reputation. Negotiate for the right to have your name removed if the work is significantly changed, or remove this clause entirely.

Non-compete clauses that are too broad.

A clause preventing you from working for direct competitors in the same city for 90 days is reasonable. A clause preventing you from working in your industry for 2 years nationwide is not. Push back on any non-compete that restricts your livelihood unreasonably.

Payment "upon client's satisfaction."

Never accept this phrasing. Payment should be tied to deliverable milestones, not subjective client satisfaction. Replace with specific, objective acceptance criteria.

Unlimited revisions or "until you get it right."

This is a scope nightmare. Insist on a defined number of revision rounds and a change order process for anything beyond that.

How to Get Clients to Sign

The best contracts in the world don't help if they sit in a client's inbox unsigned. Here's how to make signing frictionless and inevitable:

Use e-signature tools

DocuSign, HelloSign (now Dropbox Sign), and PandaDoc all let clients sign in under 2 minutes from any device. No printing, scanning, or PDF confusion. Free tiers handle most freelancers' volume. PandaDoc has the best built-in proposal + contract workflow.

Frame it as standard process

Don't apologize for sending a contract. Say: "Before we get started, I'll send over the project agreement — it protects both of us and just takes a minute to sign." Clients who understand business won't blink. Clients who push back hard on signing a contract are telling you something important.

Send with the project kickoff, not after

Send the contract at the same time as your first invoice for the deposit. Tie the project start to both the signed contract AND the deposit. This eliminates the "we'll sign it later" delay while you've already started work.

Keep it readable

Clients are more likely to sign a clear, concise contract than one filled with dense legalese. Use plain language, short paragraphs, and clear headings. A 2-page agreement is better than a 12-page one that nobody reads.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a contract for small freelance projects?

Yes. Even for a $200 project, a simple contract protects you from scope creep, non-payment, and disputes over IP ownership. Small projects can escalate quickly; a signed agreement ensures both parties have aligned expectations before any work begins.

Can I use a freelance contract template without a lawyer?

Yes, for most standard freelance work. A well-drafted template covers the fundamentals. For complex projects over $10,000, international clients, or work involving sensitive IP, having a lawyer review the contract is worth the cost.

What is a kill fee in a freelance contract?

A kill fee is a cancellation fee the client pays if they terminate the project after work has begun but before completion. It typically ranges from 25%–50% of the remaining project value and compensates you for blocked calendar time and work already invested.

How do I get clients to sign my contract?

Present signing as a standard professional step — "Before we kick off, I'll send over the project agreement via DocuSign, which takes about 2 minutes to sign." Most professional clients expect a contract. If a client refuses to sign any contract, that is a significant red flag.

Who owns the IP in a freelance project?

By default in most jurisdictions, the freelancer owns copyright until explicitly transferred to the client. Your contract should specify exactly when and how IP transfers — typically upon receipt of full payment. Without this clause, ownership is legally ambiguous.
