How to Price Your Freelance Work: The Complete 2026 Guide

Published May 8, 2026  ·  14 min read

Pricing is the most uncomfortable conversation in freelancing. Most freelancers — even experienced ones — are charging less than they should. Not a little less. Often 30–50% less than the market would support and their work deserves.

This guide covers the full landscape: how to calculate rates that actually make sense, when to use hourly vs project pricing, what value-based pricing really means in practice, and how to raise your rates without losing clients you want to keep.

Why Most Freelancers Undercharge (and How to Fix It)

Undercharging comes from a handful of predictable mistakes. Understanding them is the first step to pricing with confidence.

Mistake 1: Charging what feels comfortable, not what the market supports. Most beginners pick a number that "feels reasonable" without researching what the market actually pays. Feelings about money are unreliable — they're shaped by your personal financial situation, not by what clients actually pay for the skills you offer.

Mistake 2: Confusing employee salary with freelance rate. If you were making $50,000 a year as an employee, you might think $25/hour is decent freelance money. It isn't. As a freelancer, you pay both sides of payroll taxes, your own benefits, your own software, your own equipment, and you have unpaid gaps between clients. The freelance multiplier — the rate adjustment to account for all of this — is typically 1.5x to 2x your equivalent employee hourly rate.

Mistake 3: Pricing based on time rather than value. If a 4-hour project generates $50,000 in revenue for a client, charging $200 for four hours of work is not a reasonable reflection of the value delivered. We'll get to value-based pricing shortly.

Mistake 4: Being afraid of rejection. Many freelancers underprice because they're afraid a higher rate will cause the client to say no. In practice, raising rates often has the opposite effect — higher rates signal higher quality and attract better clients who don't negotiate on every invoice.

Hourly Pricing: Pros, Cons, and How to Calculate Yours

Hourly pricing is the simplest model to understand and the easiest to start with. You charge a rate per hour, track your time, and invoice based on actual hours worked.

Pros of hourly pricing:

  • Simple to explain to clients
  • You get paid for scope changes automatically — more work means more hours means more money
  • Good for ongoing or undefined-scope work
  • Transparent — clients know exactly what they're paying for

Cons of hourly pricing:

  • Penalises efficiency — the faster you get, the less you earn for the same output
  • Creates an adversarial dynamic where clients watch the clock
  • Caps your earnings at the number of hours you can work
  • Doesn't capture the value of experience — an expert who solves in 1 hour what a junior takes 10 hours to solve should earn more, not less

How to calculate your minimum viable hourly rate:

Minimum Viable Rate Formula
Monthly target income + monthly business expenses
÷ billable hours per month
= Minimum viable hourly rate

Example: You want to earn $5,000/month. Business expenses (software, equipment, taxes set aside) are approximately $1,200/month. You can realistically bill 80 hours per month (not 160 — non-billable time for admin, marketing, and breaks is real). That's: ($5,000 + $1,200) ÷ 80 = $77.50/hour minimum.

Most beginners get this calculation wrong by assuming they'll bill 40 hours a week. They won't. 20–25 billable hours per week is a realistic target for a busy solo freelancer. Use our freelance rate calculator to run your own numbers precisely.

Project-Based Pricing: When and How

Project pricing means quoting a fixed price for a defined deliverable — "I'll build your landing page for $1,500" rather than "I charge $75/hour." Once the client approves the quote, the price doesn't change regardless of how long it takes you.

Project pricing works well when:

  • The scope is clearly defined upfront
  • You've done similar work before and can estimate accurately
  • The client needs budget certainty
  • You're confident you can complete it efficiently

The key risk with project pricing is scope creep — the project grows beyond what was quoted without the price adjusting. This is why a detailed contract with a clear scope of work and revision policy is essential whenever you quote project prices.

To build a project quote, estimate hours (honest estimate, not optimistic), multiply by your hourly rate, then add a 20–30% buffer for unknowns and admin time. Then review the total against market rates for the deliverable to sense-check it. If your rate calculation says $800 but the market charges $2,000 for the same thing, price at the market — not your hours.

Value-Based Pricing: What It Actually Means

Value-based pricing charges based on the outcome the client receives rather than the time you invest or the market rate for the task. It's the most profitable pricing model when applied correctly, and the most misunderstood.

Value-based pricing is not:

  • Charging whatever you feel like
  • Making up numbers and hoping the client accepts
  • Relevant for all projects

Value-based pricing is:

  • Quantifying the business impact of your work in the client's terms
  • Pricing as a fraction of that impact
  • Having a genuine conversation with the client about what success looks like and what it's worth

Example: You're a copywriter being asked to rewrite a SaaS product's homepage. The client's current homepage converts at 1.5%. They get 10,000 visitors a month. Their product costs $99/month. If your copy improves conversion to 3% — a realistic outcome — that's an additional 150 customers/month worth $178,200/year in annual recurring revenue. Charging $3,000 for the project isn't expensive — it's 1.7% of the first year's additional revenue your work generates.

Value-based pricing requires you to ask the right questions during client discovery: What is this project worth to your business? What does success look like, and can you measure it? What would it cost you not to do this? These questions position you as a strategic partner, not a task executor.

The 3-Question Pricing Formula

Before quoting any project, answer these three questions:

The 3-Question Formula
1. What is this worth to the client?
2. What is my time worth (minimum viable rate)?
3. What does the market charge for this?

Your price = the highest defensible number across all three.

If the value to the client is $50,000, your time cost is $2,000, and the market charges $3,500 — quote $3,500 to $5,000 and have a value conversation if needed. Never quote at your time cost when the market and value support a higher number.

How to Research Competitor Rates

Before you can use market rates as an anchor, you need to know them. Here's how to find real numbers:

  • Glassdoor and LinkedIn Salary Insights — search for freelance or contractor roles in your field and location to get baseline benchmarks
  • Upwork and Fiverr public profiles — search for your service category and review what established freelancers (4.8+ star ratings, 50+ reviews) charge
  • Community surveys — the annual Freelancers Union survey, Bonsai's Freelance Market Report, and niche community surveys (Dribbble for designers, GitHub for developers) publish real rate data
  • Ask peers directly — in professional communities and Slack groups, other freelancers will often share their rates in confidence. The conversation is almost always more productive than searching online
  • Recruitment agency rates — agencies publishing contractor rate cards publicly are a reliable market signal

Raising Your Rates: When and How to Tell Clients

Raising your rates is uncomfortable the first time. It's less uncomfortable every time after that. Here's when to do it and how.

When to raise your rates:

  • You're fully booked and turning down work — demand exceeds supply
  • You've acquired new skills, certifications, or specialisation
  • Your current clients are getting significantly more value than your rate reflects
  • It's been more than 12 months since your last increase
  • You're consistently winning projects at your current rate with little resistance

How to communicate a rate increase:

Give existing clients 30–60 days' notice. Frame it as a business update, not an apology. A simple, professional message: "I'm updating my rates for new projects starting [date]. Your new rate will be [X]. I wanted to give you advance notice so you can plan accordingly. I value our working relationship and look forward to continuing to support [their business]."

Most good clients will accept a reasonable increase (10–25%) with adequate notice. Clients who push back significantly or threaten to leave over a reasonable rate increase were already undervaluing your work — and the rate increase clarified that for you.

New clients always get your current rate. Never quote a new client at an old rate and update existing clients — this creates two-tier confusion.

How to Present Pricing in Proposals Without Losing Clients

Anchor high. Present your investment section after establishing the value of the outcome, not at the beginning of the proposal. Clients who see the price before understanding the value will always react to it as a cost rather than an investment.

Structure proposals like this: (1) Problem you understand, (2) Solution and approach, (3) What success looks like for them, (4) Your deliverables and timeline, (5) Investment. By the time they reach the number, they've already agreed conceptually with the approach.

Offering options also reduces sticker shock. A single price feels like a take-it-or-leave-it. Three options give the client a sense of control and often result in them choosing the middle option — which is the one you designed for them anyway.

Red Flags: How Clients Try to Lowball You

  • "We have a limited budget but this will be great for your portfolio" — your portfolio is built from work you chose, not work you were guilt-tripped into discounting
  • "It's a simple job" — simple to them, not necessarily to you, and your experience is what makes it look simple
  • "The last person did it for X" — the last person was either undercharging, a different skill level, or no longer works with them for that very reason
  • "We'll have a lot of work for you in the future" — future work discounts rarely materialise, and they shouldn't affect your present rate
  • Requesting a quote before sharing any project details — professional clients understand that a proper scope is required before accurate pricing

Building a Pricing Tier System

Packaging your services into tiers — Good, Better, Best — is one of the most effective ways to increase average project value. Each tier should solve a progressively more complete version of the client's problem.

Good
$X
  • Core deliverable only
  • Defined revision rounds
  • Standard turnaround
Better
$XX
  • Core + supporting deliverables
  • More revisions included
  • Strategy session
Best
$XXX
  • Full scope solution
  • Unlimited revisions
  • 30-day support included

The Good tier is designed to be slightly uncomfortable — close to your minimum viable rate, not a bargain. The Better tier is where you want most clients to land. The Best tier is for clients who want certainty and are willing to pay a premium for it. Having three options almost always performs better than one, because the framing shifts from "should I hire this person?" to "which package do I choose?"
