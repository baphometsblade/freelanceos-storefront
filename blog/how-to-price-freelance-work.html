How to Price Your Freelance Work in 2026 (Without Undercharging)

A five-step system for setting rates, handling objections, and raising your prices by 15-20% every year without losing good clients.

June 2026  ·  13 min read
Home › Blog › How to Price Freelance Work 2026

The single most common mistake freelancers make is not getting their first client, not choosing a niche, not building a portfolio — it is undercharging. Smart, talented people routinely charge half what the market will pay, work twice as many hours as they should, and then burn out wondering why freelancing is so hard.

This guide gives you a five-step process for setting freelance rates that actually work — rates that cover your real costs, reflect the value you deliver, and allow you to run a sustainable business rather than a glorified part-time job. We will also cover how to handle price objections and how to build in annual rate increases from day one.

The Undercharging Trap

Undercharging is not random. It follows a predictable pattern that affects even experienced freelancers:

  • You start low because you need work. Early on, almost any rate feels fine because you need experience, testimonials, and income. This is understandable.
  • You forget to raise rates. Months turn into years. Your skills improve dramatically, but your rates drift up only slightly — or not at all — because raising rates requires an uncomfortable conversation.
  • Your client base anchors your rates. The clients you get at low rates refer you to similar clients. You end up with a book of business full of price-sensitive clients who would leave at a 20% increase.
  • You mistake busyness for success. A full calendar at $50/hr feels successful until you calculate what you are earning after taxes, expenses, and unpaid hours. The math usually reveals an effective hourly rate far below what you thought.

The exit from this trap is a systematic approach to pricing — not a sudden doubling of rates, but a clear process that gets you to market rate and keeps you there.

Step 1: Know Your Minimum Viable Rate

1
Calculate Your Floor

Your minimum viable rate is the rate below which you are losing money. Calculate it with this formula:

(Annual income goal + taxes + business expenses + savings target) ÷ annual billable hours = minimum hourly rate

The most important corrections most freelancers forget:

  • Taxes are 25-35% of gross income for self-employed freelancers in the US. If your goal is $70,000 take-home, you need to bill approximately $100,000+ gross.
  • Billable hours are not total hours. A 40-hour week realistically yields 18-22 billable hours after accounting for sales, admin, invoicing, professional development, and marketing.
  • Expenses add up. Software tools, hardware, health insurance, accounting, and professional development typically run $6,000-$15,000 per year.

This number is your floor — the number you never quote below. It is not your target rate.

Step 2: Research Market Rates

2
Find What the Market Pays

Market rate research tells you the range of what clients actually pay for your type of work. Where to look:

  • Freelance communities. Reddit communities like r/freelance regularly discuss rates by skill and experience. Ask directly or search past threads.
  • Professional associations. Graphic Artists Guild, Copywriters Guild, and similar organizations publish annual pricing guides with percentiles by specialty and experience.
  • Glassdoor and LinkedIn Salary. Filter for contract and freelance roles to see what companies expect to pay.
  • Ask peers directly. The clearest data comes from freelancers in your niche at a similar experience level. Most are willing to share in private conversations.

Interpret market rates carefully: job board rates are often the floor of the market. Premium clients working with referral-sourced freelancers pay 30-50% above job board rates as a baseline.

Step 3: Add Your Value Premium

3
Price for the Value You Add

Your rate should not just cover your costs plus a commodity markup. It should reflect the specific value you deliver beyond a generic freelancer in your category. Value premiums come from:

  • Specialization. A copywriter who specifically writes B2B SaaS onboarding emails commands a premium over a generalist copywriter. The more specific your niche, the higher your floor.
  • Speed and reliability. Consistently meeting deadlines and being easy to work with is genuinely rare. Clients pay for certainty, not just skill.
  • Industry expertise. Deep knowledge of a specific industry (fintech, healthcare, real estate) adds immediate value to your work. Price it accordingly.
  • Track record. Case studies showing measurable results — conversion improvements, revenue generated, time saved — justify rates that pure portfolio pieces cannot.

Add a 20-50% premium above midpoint market rates based on your actual differentiators. Most freelancers with 3+ years in a niche are worth the upper half of market ranges, not the midpoint.

Step 4: Package and Present Your Price

4
The Good / Better / Best Proposal Model

Never send a single number in a proposal. Use a three-tier structure:

  • Good: Minimum viable scope at your base project rate. Enough to solve the core problem.
  • Better: Full recommended scope. This should be your target — price it to be clearly the best value of the three.
  • Best: Comprehensive scope with full support, revisions, and deliverables. Price 40-60% above Better.

Most clients choose the middle option. The top option makes the middle feel reasonable. The bottom option shows you respect their budget constraints without racing to the bottom.

Present prices directly and confidently. No hedge language. State what each tier includes and what it costs. Let silence do the work after you deliver the number — do not fill it with apologies or justifications.

Step 5: Handle Price Objections

Price objections are a normal part of freelance sales. They do not mean "no" — they usually mean "help me understand the value" or "I need to adjust scope." Here are the five most common objections and how to handle them:

"That's out of our budget."
What is your budget for this? I can show you what we can accomplish at that level — or we can look at which parts of the scope are most important to you and start there.
"Another freelancer quoted me half that price."
That is a meaningful difference and worth exploring. Quotes vary that much usually because of scope, experience level, or revision terms. I would love to walk through what my scope includes so you can compare apples to apples.
"Can you do it for less if we give you more work later?"
If you can commit to a retainer arrangement now, I can factor volume into a monthly rate. For a single project, I price each engagement on its own merits rather than on future promises.
"We're a startup — we don't have a big budget."
I understand startup budget constraints. My base package at [lower tier price] covers the essentials. If that works, we can build from there as your budget grows.
"We were thinking more around [50% of your rate]."
At that rate I could deliver [specific reduced scope]. If you want the full project, my minimum is [your rate]. I want to make sure whatever we do together, I can give it the attention it deserves.

The Annual Rate Increase Plan

Year 3 (+18%)$104/hr
Year 4 (+18%)$123/hr
Year 5 (+18%)$145/hr
5-year income increase (same billable hours)+93%

The math is compelling. A freelancer who raises rates 18% per year nearly doubles their income in 5 years without adding a single extra billable hour. A freelancer who never raises rates earns roughly 10% less in real terms over that same period due to inflation.

The mechanism: raise rates for all new clients immediately. For existing clients, give 30-60 days notice once per year. Announce it as a standard annual review, not as a request for permission. Most good clients will stay — the ones who leave were your most price-sensitive clients and were one difficult project away from leaving anyway.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I figure out what to charge for freelance work?
Start with your minimum viable rate: (desired income + taxes + expenses) divided by billable hours per year. This tells you your floor. Then research market rates for your skill and experience level, add a value premium for your specialization and reliability, and present your price confidently. Never quote below your floor.
Should I give discounts on freelance work?
Discounts are appropriate for genuine strategic reasons: a long-term retainer at volume, a non-profit with documented budget constraints, or a trade arrangement with clear value on both sides. Never discount for vague future promises or portfolio exposure. Discounting trains clients to expect lower prices and attracts the wrong type of client.
How do I handle a client who says my rates are too high?
Do not immediately lower your price. Ask what their budget is and what they can deprioritize. Offer a reduced scope at your same rate rather than the same scope at a reduced rate. If they cannot meet a reasonable minimum, it is often better to decline gracefully than to deliver a full-price project at half-price rates.
How often should I raise my freelance rates?
Raise rates at minimum once per year to keep pace with inflation and reflect your growing expertise. A 15-20% annual increase is standard for freelancers actively growing their skill set. After major skill upgrades, portfolio wins, or shifts to a premium niche, larger increases of 25-40% are justified.
