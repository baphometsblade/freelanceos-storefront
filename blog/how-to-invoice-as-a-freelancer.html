How to Invoice as a Freelancer in 2026 (Step-by-Step Guide)

Create professional invoices, set payment terms, send follow-up emails, and get paid on time — everything you need in one guide.

June 2026  ·  13 min read

Most freelancers undercharge. But many of those who charge correctly still have a cash flow problem — not because clients refuse to pay, but because their invoicing process is inconsistent, unprofessional, or nonexistent. Sending a random PDF with "please pay soon" as the instructions is not an invoicing system. This guide will fix that.

By the end of this article you will have: a clear list of everything every invoice needs, a step-by-step process for creating and sending invoices, payment term templates, three email follow-up scripts for late payments, and a late fee policy you can use immediately.

Why Invoicing Properly Equals Getting Paid on Time

Professional invoices communicate that you are a serious business, not a hobbyist who might forget about the money. They set a clear due date, explain what the payment is for, and make it frictionless for the client to pay. Unprofessional invoices — vague amounts, no due date, no payment instructions — give clients permission to delay without consequence.

Research consistently shows that invoices sent within 24 hours of completing work get paid 30–40% faster than invoices sent days later. The momentum of completing the project carries over into the payment conversation. Send the invoice while you are still top of mind.

What to Include on Every Freelance Invoice: 9 Elements

1

Your Name, Business Name, and Contact Information

Full legal name (or business name if registered), email address, phone number, and website. This is the sender of the invoice — make it unambiguous.

2

Client Name and Billing Contact

The company name plus the specific person who handles accounts payable. Sending an invoice to the person who hired you when their finance team processes payments is a common delay trap.

3

Unique Invoice Number

A sequential number like INV-001, INV-002. This makes it easy for both sides to reference the document in communication and for you to track in your records. Never reuse numbers.

4

Invoice Date

The date the invoice was issued. This is the anchor for calculating the due date. Always include it even if your due date is stated explicitly.

5

Due Date

State this explicitly as a calendar date — "Due: July 15, 2026" — not just "Net 30." Most clients process invoices by due date, not by manually calculating from the issue date.

6

Itemized Services with Descriptions

List each deliverable or service separately with a brief description, quantity or hours, rate, and line total. Never send a single lump sum — clients who cannot see what they are paying for will question it.

7

Total Amount Due

Make the total prominent and visually distinct. If you are applying a deposit credit or discount, show both the gross amount and the adjusted total.

8

Accepted Payment Methods and Instructions

Include every method you accept — bank transfer details, PayPal email, Stripe link — with full instructions. Clients should not have to ask how to pay you.

9

Late Fee Policy

A single line: "Invoices unpaid after the due date are subject to a 1.5% monthly late fee." This alone will accelerate payment without ever having to enforce it.

Step-by-Step: How to Create Your First Invoice

Step 1 — Choose Your Invoice Tool

Use the FreelanceOS free invoice generator for a quick, professional PDF. Other solid options: Wave (free, accounting integration), FreshBooks (paid, best for ongoing billing), or a well-formatted Google Doc template if you are just starting out.

Step 2 — Fill In All 9 Elements

Work through the list above. Take your time on the itemized section — vague line items like "consulting services" will slow down approvals at larger companies that require descriptive billing.

Step 3 — Set the Due Date Before You Send

Decide on your payment term (see below), calculate the calendar date, and enter it explicitly on the invoice. Do not rely on clients to do the math.

Step 4 — Export as PDF

Always send invoices as PDFs — not Word docs, not Google Doc links. PDFs cannot be accidentally edited and render consistently across all devices.

Step 5 — Send with a Professional Email

Use the email template below. Do not just attach the invoice and hit send with no message — that looks like spam and often gets delayed.

Step 6 — Log the Invoice in Your Tracker

Record it immediately in your invoice tracker with the amount, due date, and client. You cannot follow up on what you have not tracked.

Payment Terms Explained

Due on Receipt

Payment expected immediately upon receiving the invoice. Best for small, quick projects and clients you know well.

Net 15

Due within 15 calendar days. A good middle ground — professional but not slow. Standard for projects under $2,000.

Net 30

Due within 30 calendar days. Standard for larger corporate clients with formal AP departments. Common for projects over $5,000.

50/50 Deposit

50% upfront before work begins, 50% upon delivery. Best practice for all projects over $1,000. Protects you from non-payment and shows client commitment.

Recommended default: Net 15 for most freelancers, with a 50% deposit for any project over $1,000. Net 30 only when required by enterprise clients with formal procurement processes.

How to Send an Invoice Professionally

The email accompanying your invoice matters as much as the invoice itself. Here is a template that works:

Invoice Email Template
Subject: Invoice #[NUMBER] — [Your Name] — Due [DATE]

Hi [Client name],

Please find attached Invoice #[NUMBER] for [brief description of work], totalling [amount].

Payment is due by [due date]. You can pay via [method 1] or [method 2] — details are included on the invoice.

Let me know if you have any questions. It was great working on [project name] with you.

Thanks,
[Your name]

How to Follow Up on Unpaid Invoices: 3-Email Sequence

The uncomfortable truth: about 30% of freelance invoices are paid late. Having a scripted follow-up sequence removes the awkwardness and creates a professional, systematic approach.

Email 1 — Day 1 After Due Date (Gentle Reminder)
Subject: Invoice #[NUMBER] — Payment Reminder

Hi [Name],

I wanted to follow up on Invoice #[NUMBER] for [amount], which was due on [date]. It may have slipped through with everything going on — please let me know if you need me to resend it or if there is anything I can help with to get this processed.

Thanks,
[Your name]
Email 2 — Day 7 After Due Date (Firmer Follow-Up)
Subject: Invoice #[NUMBER] — 7 Days Overdue

Hi [Name],

Invoice #[NUMBER] for [amount] is now 7 days past its due date of [date]. I have not received payment or a response to my previous email. Please arrange payment by [new date — 3 days from now] or let me know if there is an issue I should be aware of.

Per my payment terms, a late fee of 1.5% per month applies to overdue balances.

Best,
[Your name]
Email 3 — Day 14 After Due Date (Formal Notice)
Subject: Formal Notice — Invoice #[NUMBER] — 14 Days Overdue

Hi [Name],

This is a formal notice that Invoice #[NUMBER] for [amount] is now 14 days past due. This invoice has now accrued [calculated late fee amount] in late fees.

If payment is not received by [date — 5 days from now], I will need to escalate this matter, which may include involving a collections agency or pursuing this through small claims court.

I would strongly prefer to resolve this directly. Please contact me to arrange payment or discuss any disputes.

[Your name]

Late Fee Policies: How to Set and Enforce Them

A standard late fee is 1.5–2% per month on the outstanding balance. To enforce a late fee legally, you must disclose it in writing before the work begins — either in your contract or on your invoice template. You cannot retroactively add fees that were not disclosed.

Practical advice: state the late fee policy on every invoice ("Invoices unpaid after the due date are subject to a 1.5% monthly late fee"), but exercise judgment about when to actually charge it. For a good long-term client who paid two days late once, waiving the fee is a goodwill gesture. For a chronic slow-payer, enforce it.

Frequently Asked Questions

What should a freelance invoice include?

A professional freelance invoice should include your name and contact info, your client's name and contact info, a unique invoice number, the invoice date, due date, an itemized list of services, the total amount due, accepted payment methods, and your payment terms.

How do I send an invoice as a freelancer?

Send your invoice as a PDF by email immediately after completing work or at a scheduled billing date. Use a professional subject line like "Invoice #001 — [Your Name] — Due [Date]" and attach the PDF. Always include payment instructions in the email body.

What are standard freelance payment terms?

The most common freelance payment terms are Net 15 (due in 15 days), Net 30 (due in 30 days), and Due on Receipt. For larger projects, a 50% deposit upfront with the balance due on delivery is standard practice.

What do I do if a client does not pay my invoice?

Send a polite reminder on day 1 after the due date, a firmer follow-up on day 7, and a formal notice on day 14 mentioning late fees. If payment remains outstanding after 30 days, consider involving a collections service or small claims court depending on the invoice amount.

Should freelancers charge late fees?

Yes. Including a late fee policy (typically 1.5–2% per month on overdue balances) on your invoices protects you and encourages on-time payment. Always state this in your contract and on every invoice — you cannot retroactively add late fees that were not disclosed upfront.
