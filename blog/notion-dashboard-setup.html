How to Set Up a Notion Dashboard for Your Freelance Business (Step by Step)

May 8, 2026  ·  9 min read  ·  FreelanceOS

A Notion dashboard is the single page you open every morning to know exactly what needs your attention. Done right, it replaces the mental overhead of maintaining a to-do list in your head, guessing at your financial status, and hunting through project pages to remember what's due. Done wrong, it becomes a pretty but useless page you never actually use. This guide walks you through building one that genuinely works — step by step.

Prerequisites

Before building your dashboard, you need the underlying databases: a Tasks database, a Projects database, a Clients database, and an Invoices database. Your dashboard won't create data — it displays and filters data that lives in these databases. If you don't have them yet, start with the databases and build the dashboard last. The dashboard is the lens; the databases are the source of truth.

Building the Dashboard

Step 1 — Home page with greeting and date formula

Create a new page called "Home" or "Dashboard" and set it as your default page (click the three dots on the page in the sidebar, select "Set as default"). At the top, add a cover image and an icon that represents your business. Use a callout block with the formula property now() to display today's date dynamically. This sounds cosmetic but it matters — a page that feels alive and personal gets opened more than a generic blank one.

Step 2 — Today's Focus filtered view

Add a linked database block pointing to your Tasks database. Filter it to show only tasks where Due Date equals today and Status is not "Done." Set the view to List (minimal clutter). Name this section "Today's Focus" and put it near the top — it should be the first substantive thing you see. This filtered view means you open Notion and your work is already curated. No scrolling through projects, no mental assembly required.

Step 3 — Revenue at a glance (invoice database rollup)

Add another linked database view from your Invoices database. Filter to show invoices from the current month. Use a Gallery or Table view and include properties: Client, Amount, Status (Paid/Unpaid/Overdue), and Due Date. If your Invoices database has a rollup from your Clients database, you can also show a sum of all amounts due. This panel tells you your current month's earned and outstanding revenue in under five seconds — exactly the pulse check you need daily.

Step 4 — Active clients panel

Add a linked view of your Clients database filtered to Status = "Active." Display it as a Table view with columns: Client Name, Health Score, Current Project, and Next Deadline. Keep it to six or fewer rows for at-a-glance readability — if you have more active clients than that, use a filter to show only those with upcoming deadlines in the next 14 days. This panel catches the relationship that's about to need attention before it becomes a problem.

Step 5 — Weekly schedule calendar view

Add a linked view of your Tasks or Projects database in Calendar view, filtered to the current week. Set the date property as the Due Date. This gives you a visual week-at-a-glance without leaving the dashboard. Calendar view is particularly useful for spotting deadline collisions — days where you have three things due that you haven't started. Seeing it visually on Monday morning gives you time to negotiate or reprioritize before you're in crisis mode.

Step 6 — Quick-add template buttons

Below your views, add a row of Template Button blocks for the things you create most often: New Task, New Client, New Invoice, New Project, Weekly Review. Each button press creates a pre-structured page or database entry with fields already populated where possible. This reduces the friction of capturing new information — instead of navigating to the right database and manually setting properties, one button click and you're writing content.

Step 7 — Pipeline kanban snapshot

Add a linked Board view of your Projects database, grouped by Status, filtered to active and in-progress projects only. Set a small limit (6–8 cards visible) to keep it compact. The Kanban snapshot on your dashboard gives you an instant workload read — you can see at a glance whether you're about to enter a busy period, whether anything is stuck in review waiting on client feedback, and whether you have capacity for a new project inquiry. This is the view that prevents the feast-or-famine cycle.

Step 8 — Content queue (if applicable)

If you use content marketing to generate leads, add a linked view of your Content Calendar database filtered to Status = "Drafting" or "Scheduled." A simple list showing the post title, platform, and publish date keeps your content pipeline visible without requiring you to navigate away. Even a 3-item view is enough to keep you honest about whether you're behind on the content that drives your inbound traffic.

Styling Tips

A dashboard you enjoy looking at gets used more than one that's purely functional. Use a consistent color scheme for your database views — toggle "Colorize" in Kanban columns or use Select property colors thoughtfully. Add divider blocks between sections with descriptive text labels (bold, slightly larger font) so sections are instantly scannable. Use the Columns feature (type /columns) to put two smaller views side by side and reduce vertical scrolling. Set a cover image that reflects your brand or work aesthetic.

Mobile Considerations

Notion's mobile app renders columns as stacked blocks, so your desktop two-column layout becomes a very long single column on mobile. Design with this in mind: put your most critical view (Today's Focus) at the very top so it's the first thing visible on mobile without scrolling. Avoid wide table views on mobile — list views and board views render much better in a narrow viewport. The Notion mobile app is excellent for quick task capture; the dashboard is best consumed on desktop.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Too many views. A dashboard with 12 linked database blocks becomes overwhelming. Start with five and add only if you genuinely miss the information.
  • No filters on the views. Showing your entire Tasks database unfiltered on the dashboard is useless — you want curated slices of data, not a full dump.
  • Dashboard as a substitute for the actual databases. You should still navigate to your full Projects or Clients database when you need to do real work. The dashboard is for awareness, not editing.
  • Rebuilding it constantly. The urge to tweak your dashboard instead of doing work is real. Set it up once, use it for two weeks, then reassess. Most first-version dashboards are 80% correct.
