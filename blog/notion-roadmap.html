Notion Roadmap Guide: How to Plan, Track, and Share Your Roadmap in Notion

How to build a product roadmap or project roadmap in Notion using timeline view, board view, and linked databases — with a step-by-step setup guide for 2026.

July 2026  ·  14 min read

A roadmap is the single most important planning document for any product, project, or business. It answers the question every stakeholder eventually asks: "What are we building, when, and why?" Most roadmaps live in slides or spreadsheets where they go stale the moment the first date slips. A Notion roadmap is different — it is a live database, linked to your tasks and documentation, updatable in real time, and shareable as a public URL with anyone who needs to see it.

This guide covers everything about building a Notion roadmap from scratch: the right database properties, how to configure timeline and board views, how to link your roadmap to a task database, how to structure roadmap phases, and how to share it with clients or stakeholders. Whether you are a solo founder building your first product, a freelancer managing a long-term client project, or a small team planning quarterly work, this setup will work for you.

What Is a Notion Roadmap?

A Notion roadmap is a database-powered planning document that tracks features, initiatives, or milestones over time. Unlike a static roadmap in PowerPoint or Google Sheets, a Notion roadmap is a live document built on a real database — which means it can be filtered, sorted, grouped, updated in real time, and linked to other databases in your workspace.

At its core, a Notion roadmap is a database with date properties and at least two views: a Timeline view (which renders items as horizontal Gantt-style bars over time) and a Board view (which organizes items by status or phase). Most roadmaps also include a Table view for data entry and a filtered "Public" view for sharing with stakeholders.

What makes Notion roadmaps different from static slides: A Notion roadmap does not go stale. When a feature slips from Q2 to Q3, you update the date in the database — every view, every linked document, and every public share link updates automatically. Compare this to updating a PowerPoint roadmap, which requires manually editing every slide where that feature appeared.

Notion roadmaps are popular with solo founders, product managers, freelancers managing long-term client projects, and small startup teams. They are less suited to large enterprise environments where dedicated roadmap tools (Jira, Productboard, Linear) offer more purpose-built stakeholder management features.

Roadmap vs. Backlog vs. Sprint Plan: What Goes Where

Before building your Notion roadmap, it helps to clarify what belongs in a roadmap versus related planning documents. Confusing these leads to a bloated, unfocused roadmap that no one trusts.

DocumentWhat It ContainsTime HorizonAudience
Roadmap Features, initiatives, and milestones at the epic/theme level. What you will build and when (in quarters or months). Strategic intent. 3–18 months out Stakeholders, clients, investors, team leads
Backlog All requested and planned features, bugs, and improvements — unscheduled and unprioritized. The "someday maybe" list. Ongoing (no dates) Product/dev team
Sprint / Weekly Plan The specific tasks being worked on this week or sprint. Granular. Day-level dates. Individual assignees. 1–2 weeks Dev team, project team

Your Notion workspace should have all three levels. The roadmap links to the backlog (features on the roadmap come from the backlog). The sprint plan links to the roadmap (each sprint implements part of a roadmap item). This three-level hierarchy keeps your roadmap clean and strategic while your backlog and sprints handle granular execution.

How to Build a Notion Roadmap from Scratch

Follow these steps to create a working Notion roadmap in about 30 minutes.

1
Create a new full-page database Type /database on a new page and select "Database — Full Page." Name it "Roadmap" or "[Product Name] Roadmap." Start with the default Table view — you will add Timeline and Board views in later steps. A full-page database gives your roadmap dedicated navigation space rather than embedding it inline on a document.
2
Add your core properties Delete the default "Tags" property. Add: Status (Status type — use Notion's built-in "Not Started / In Progress / Done" groups), Priority (Select — P1 Critical / P2 High / P3 Medium / P4 Low), Phase (Select — Discovery / Design / Development / Launch / Post-Launch or Q1 2026 / Q2 2026 / Q3 2026), Owner (Person), Start Date (Date), End Date (Date), and Category (Select — Feature / Bug Fix / Improvement / Infrastructure). You can also add a Description (Text) for a one-line summary.
3
Add your first roadmap items In Table view, add 5–10 real features or initiatives you are planning. Set Start Date and End Date for each. Assign a Phase and Priority. Do not overthink properties at this stage — you can always add more later. The goal is to have real data in your database so you can see how the views look.
4
Add Timeline view (your Gantt chart) Click "Add a view" at the top of the database. Select "Timeline." Notion will ask you to select date properties — set Date From to "Start Date" and Date To to "End Date." Your roadmap items now appear as horizontal bars on a time axis. Use the zoom controls (top right of timeline) to switch between Day / Week / Month / Quarter / Year views. Quarterly view is best for a standard product roadmap.
5
Add Board view (kanban by Phase) Click "Add a view," select "Board." In the board settings, set "Group by" to Phase. Your roadmap items now appear in columns by phase or quarter — a classic roadmap layout. In this view you can drag items between phases, which automatically updates their Phase property in the database.
6
Create a Public Share view Add one more view — a filtered Table or Timeline view. Add a filter: Priority does not equal P4 Low, or Status is not "Backlog." This creates a clean stakeholder-facing view that hides internal lower-priority items. Use this view's URL when sharing with clients, investors, or the public.

Choosing Your Roadmap View: Timeline vs. Board vs. Table

A well-built Notion roadmap uses at least two views — and often three. Each view serves a different audience and answers a different question.

▬
Timeline View
Gantt Chart
Shows items as horizontal bars over time. Best for seeing how work overlaps, identifying scheduling conflicts, and presenting to executives who want a time-based view. Answer: "When does each thing happen?"
⋮
Board View
Kanban / Phase
Shows items in columns grouped by Phase, Status, or Quarter. Best for seeing what is in each phase and moving items between phases. Answer: "What is in Q1 vs. Q2 vs. Q3?"
☰
Table View
Data Entry
The fastest view for adding, editing, and reviewing all properties across all items. Best for roadmap maintenance and bulk updates. Answer: "Let me see all the details across every item at once."

Timeline View: Getting the Most Out of Notion's Gantt

Notion's Timeline view is a capable Gantt chart with several useful configuration options:

  • Show table on left: Enable this in Timeline settings to see a properties table alongside the timeline bars. This gives you a combined Gantt + spreadsheet layout — extremely useful for roadmap reviews.
  • Group by property: Group your timeline by Phase or Owner to create swim lanes. All Q1 items appear in one row, all Q2 items in another — a classic executive roadmap layout.
  • Zoom level: Day zoom for sprint planning, Week for detailed project plans, Month or Quarter for product roadmaps, Year for strategic planning. Use Quarter zoom for most product roadmaps.
  • Drag to reschedule: Drag any timeline bar left or right to change its start and end dates. Drag the edges to extend or shrink the duration. Changes update instantly in your database.
Pro Tip

Enable "Show table" in Timeline view settings and set column width to Medium. You get a split-screen view with your roadmap items listed on the left and their timeline bars on the right — the classic project management dashboard look, entirely in Notion.

Essential Roadmap Properties

These are the properties that belong in every Notion roadmap database, with explanations of why each matters:

Title
Feature / Initiative Name
The main database title. Keep it to 5–8 words — "User authentication flow" not "Allow users to log in with email and password including SSO options." The detail lives inside the card page.
Status
Status
Use Notion's built-in Status property type (not Select) — it automatically groups into Not Started, In Progress, and Done with color coding. Add "Backlog" and "Cancelled" to the Not Started group for a more complete workflow.
Select
Priority
P1 Critical / P2 High / P3 Medium / P4 Low. Use this to filter your public share view (hide P4 from stakeholders), sort your backlog, and decide what to pull into the next quarter when capacity is limited.
Select
Phase / Quarter
The primary grouping property for your Board view. Use either phase names (Discovery / Design / Development / Launch) or quarters (Q1 2026 / Q2 2026 / Q3 2026 / Q4 2026 / Backlog / Someday). For product roadmaps, quarters are clearer for stakeholders. For project roadmaps, phases are clearer for the team.
Date
Start Date
When work on this item begins. Used as the left edge of the timeline bar. For quarterly planning, set all Q1 items to start January 1, all Q2 items to start April 1, etc. — or use actual planned start dates when you have them.
Date
End Date / Target Date
When this item should be completed. Used as the right edge of the timeline bar. This is the single property stakeholders focus on most. Keep it honest — an overly optimistic roadmap erodes trust faster than a conservative one.
Person
Owner / DRI
The person ultimately responsible for delivering this roadmap item. DRI = Directly Responsible Individual. Having a named owner is the single most reliable predictor of roadmap items actually getting done. Anonymous roadmap items drift.
Select
Category / Type
Feature / Bug Fix / Technical Debt / Infrastructure / Research. Lets you filter your roadmap to show only new features (for investor updates) or only technical work (for engineering planning). Most roadmaps are too feature-heavy — adding a type property makes the technical and maintenance work visible.
Select
Impact / Business Value
High / Medium / Low. Used with Priority for better triage — a P2 item with High Impact should jump the queue over a P2 item with Low Impact. This property is especially useful for solo founders making build-vs-buy-vs-defer decisions on limited time budgets.
Number
Effort (Story Points or Days)
Estimate of effort required. Use story points (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13) or actual day estimates. Create a Rollup or formula to sum total effort per quarter — immediately tells you if you have over-committed a quarter's capacity. This is the most under-used roadmap property that has the highest impact on planning accuracy.

Structuring Your Notion Roadmap by Phase

There are two common ways to structure a Notion roadmap: by time (quarters) and by phase (development stages). Both work — the right choice depends on your audience.

Time-Based Roadmap (Quarterly)

Best for stakeholder communication, investor updates, and external sharing. Stakeholders think in calendar quarters — Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4. Use Phase = "Q1 2026," "Q2 2026," etc. Board view grouped by Phase shows a left-to-right visual of the year's plan. Easy to understand at a glance.

Quarter 1 — Now
Core Product Buildout
Features that must exist for the product to be usable. Authentication, core workflows, basic reporting. This is your MVP scope.
Quarter 2 — Next
Growth & Retention Features
Features that improve retention and drive word-of-mouth. Integrations, automation, better UX. Scheduled after core buildout is stable.
Quarter 3 — Later
Monetization & Scale
Premium features, billing, team plans, enterprise features. Scheduled far enough out that priorities may shift before you get here.
Backlog — Someday
Ideas & Unscheduled Requests
All requested features that are not committed to a quarter. Reviewed monthly to promote high-priority items into the active quarters.

Phase-Based Roadmap (Development Stages)

Best for project management with defined delivery phases. Use Phase = "Discovery," "Design," "Development," "Testing," "Launch," "Post-Launch." Board view shows each feature moving across the delivery pipeline — more useful for project teams than for external stakeholders.

Common Mistake

Mixing both time-based and phase-based structuring in the same roadmap. Pick one. Use quarterly phases when your primary audience is external (clients, investors, board). Use development phases when your primary audience is internal (engineers, designers, QA). You can always create a second Board view grouped by the other property — but make one of them the "primary" view.

Linking Your Notion Roadmap to a Task Database

A Notion roadmap without task-level detail is a promise without a plan. The most powerful Notion roadmap setups link the roadmap database to a task database using a Relation property. Each roadmap item contains the specific tasks required to deliver it, and Rollup properties surface task completion data back up to the roadmap level.

Roadmap Database ├── "User authentication" [In Progress | Q1 2026] │ └── Tasks (Relation) → │ ├── Design login screens [Done] │ ├── Build email/password auth [In Progress] │ └── Add Google OAuth [Not Started] └── "Dashboard v2" [Not Started | Q2 2026] └── Tasks (Relation) → ├── User research sessions [Not Started] └── Design new dashboard layout [Not Started]

To set this up in Notion:

  1. Create a separate Tasks database (if you do not already have one) with Name, Status, Assignee, Due Date, and Priority properties.
  2. In your Roadmap database, add a Relation property. Choose your Tasks database as the target. This links the two databases.
  3. In the Tasks database, Notion automatically adds a corresponding Relation property pointing back to the Roadmap database — so you can tag each task with which roadmap item it belongs to.
  4. Back in the Roadmap database, add a Rollup property. Set it to roll up your Task Status property, with calculation "Count per group" or "Count values." This shows you how many tasks are Done, In Progress, and Not Started for each roadmap item.
  5. Optionally add a Formula property: prop("Tasks Done") / prop("Total Tasks") — this gives you a percentage completion for each roadmap item, calculated automatically from real task data.
Result

Your roadmap now updates automatically as tasks are completed. No one needs to manually update the roadmap status — when the last task for "User authentication" gets checked Done, the rollup shows 100% complete and the status can be updated in one click. This is the highest-value Notion roadmap configuration for teams doing real work.

Sharing a Public Notion Roadmap

One of Notion's most useful features for founders and freelancers is the ability to share any page (including a roadmap) as a public web link — no Notion account required to view. This turns your internal roadmap into a stakeholder-facing product page.

How to Share Your Notion Roadmap Publicly

  1. Navigate to your roadmap database page in Notion.
  2. Click "Share" in the top right corner.
  3. Toggle on "Share to Web."
  4. Set permissions to "Can view" (never share to web with "Can edit" for external stakeholders).
  5. Copy the public link. Anyone with the link can now view your roadmap in read-only mode without logging into Notion.

Before sharing, make sure you are on the right view. If your Timeline view is active when you share, stakeholders who click the link will land on the Timeline view — which is usually the most impressive view for a roadmap. If your Table view is active, they will see a spreadsheet.

Filtering Your Public View

Create a dedicated "Public Roadmap" view before sharing. Add a filter to hide internal or sensitive items: for example, filter to Priority does not equal "P4 Low" and Category does not equal "Technical Debt." This gives stakeholders a clean, strategic view without exposing your internal backlog churn.

Roadmap Best Practices for Freelancers and Founders

  • Review and update your roadmap weekly. A roadmap you only update monthly becomes a wish list. Block 15 minutes every Friday to move completed items to Done, check if anything needs to be rescheduled, and pull new items from Backlog into the active quarter.
  • Keep your roadmap strategic — not task-level. Roadmap items should be features or milestones, not individual tasks. "Implement payment processing" belongs on the roadmap. "Write Stripe webhook handler" belongs in your task database. If your roadmap has 50+ items, you have tasks masquerading as roadmap items.
  • Have a "Backlog" phase for everything unscheduled. Never leave items without a phase. If you do not know when you will build something, add it to Backlog rather than leaving it dateless. A roadmap without a Backlog phase fills up with half-committed items that clutter the active quarters.
  • Date your roadmap in quarters, not months. Month-level dates in a roadmap invite conflict with reality. A feature promised for "March" that slips to "late April" looks like a failure. A feature in "Q1" that delivers in mid-April is on time. For client-facing roadmaps, quarter-level dates are almost always the right granularity.
  • Add a "Why" section inside every roadmap item page. When you open a roadmap item, the page inside should have a brief rationale: why this feature matters, what problem it solves, what success looks like. This context is invaluable three months later when you are about to start building it and can no longer remember why you committed to it.
  • Never promise roadmap items to clients before they are in your committed quarter. Backlog and "Q3/Later" items are aspirations. Only items in your current quarter should be treated as commitments. Sharing your full roadmap with clients is fine; just be explicit about which phases are committed and which are planned but not confirmed.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I create a roadmap in Notion?
Create a Notion database with properties for Feature name, Status, Priority, Phase (or Quarter), Owner, Start Date, and End Date. Add a Timeline view (select your Start Date and End Date as the date range) and a Board view grouped by Phase. Enter your features and initiatives as database rows with dates. Your Notion roadmap is now functional — a Gantt-style timeline for stakeholders and a phase-based kanban board for your team, all powered by the same data.
What is a Notion roadmap?
A Notion roadmap is a database-powered planning document that tracks features, initiatives, or milestones over time. It uses Notion's Timeline view to display items as Gantt-style bars spanning their planned dates, a Board view to organize items by phase or status, and a Table view for detailed data entry. Unlike static roadmaps in slides or spreadsheets, a Notion roadmap is live — updated in real time, filterable, linkable to task databases, and shareable as a public URL.
Does Notion have a roadmap template?
Yes. Notion has built-in roadmap templates accessible via the template gallery when you create a new page. Notion's official product roadmap template includes a pre-configured database with timeline view, board view, and the core properties. Third-party Notion template packs like FreelanceOS Pro include more opinionated roadmap setups designed for freelancers and solo founders who need a roadmap linked to their project and task management systems.
Can you make a Gantt chart in Notion?
Yes. Notion's Timeline view functions as a Gantt chart. Create a database with Start Date and End Date properties, switch to Timeline view, and Notion renders each entry as a horizontal bar spanning its date range on a time axis. You can group by phase or owner to create swim lanes, zoom between days and years, and drag bars to reschedule dates. It is not as feature-rich as dedicated Gantt tools like Asana or GanttPRO, but for solo founders and small teams it is a fully capable free Gantt alternative.
Should I use a Notion roadmap or a dedicated roadmap tool?
Notion roadmaps work best for solo founders, freelancers, and small teams who already use Notion as their primary workspace. The advantage is that your roadmap lives next to your tasks, notes, and documentation — all interconnected. Dedicated tools like Linear, Productboard, or Jira Roadmaps are better for larger teams that need purpose-built features like automated progress tracking, stakeholder portals, or feature voting. If you are starting out or running a lean operation, a well-built Notion roadmap beats paying for another tool.
How do I share a Notion roadmap publicly?
Open the roadmap database page in Notion, click "Share" in the top right, and enable "Share to Web." Set permissions to "Can view" and copy the public link. Anyone with the link can view your roadmap in read-only mode without a Notion account. For a branded public URL (like roadmap.yourproduct.com), use a tool like Super.so or Potion.so to publish your Notion roadmap on a custom domain for $10–16/month.
