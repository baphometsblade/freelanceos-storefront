Australian Freelance Context: The Key Obligations

ABN: The Foundation of Australian Freelancing

An Australian Business Number (ABN) is an 11-digit identifier issued by the ATO. Every freelancer operating a business in Australia should have one. Clients can legally withhold 47% of your payment (the top marginal rate) if you invoice without an ABN — so getting one is your very first step.

The application takes around 15 minutes at abr.business.gov.au and is free. Once you have your ABN, include it on every invoice. FreelanceOS Pro's invoice tracker includes an ABN/tax details field so this information is always accessible when creating new invoices.

GST: The $75,000 Threshold

You must register for Goods and Services Tax (GST) once your GST turnover reaches $75,000 AUD in a 12-month period (or if you expect it to). Once registered, you add 10% GST to your invoices, collect it from clients, and remit it to the ATO via your Business Activity Statement (BAS).

Many Australian freelancers track their rolling 12-month revenue manually and miss the threshold until they are already over it, resulting in a backdated GST liability. Keeping a live monthly income log makes monitoring this straightforward.

BAS: Business Activity Statements

If you are registered for GST, you must lodge a Business Activity Statement (BAS) — usually quarterly. The BAS reports your GST collected, GST credits (on business purchases), and PAYG instalments. BAS agents and accountants can lodge on your behalf, but you need to provide accurate records.

EOFY: End of Financial Year Income Tracking

Australia's financial year runs from 1 July to 30 June — the opposite of the calendar year, which catches many new freelancers off guard. EOFY (End of Financial Year) is the critical time for income reconciliation, superannuation contributions, and maximising deductible expenses before 30 June.

Tracking income and expenses in real time throughout the year — rather than scrambling in June — is the single biggest quality-of-life improvement available to Australian freelancers. FreelanceOS Pro lets you filter all income records by financial year to get your gross income figure in seconds.

Australian Tax for Freelancers: A 2026 Overview

Australia taxes freelance income as personal income. Key points for sole traders operating in 2026:

  • Tax-free threshold: $18,200 AUD. Income below this is tax-free (assuming this is your only income).
  • Income tax brackets: 19% from $18,201–$45,000; 32.5% from $45,001–$120,000; 37% from $120,001–$180,000; 45% above $180,000.
  • Medicare Levy: 2% of taxable income applies to most freelancers.
  • Super contributions: Sole traders are not required to pay themselves superannuation, but voluntary contributions are tax-deductible up to the concessional cap ($30,000 AUD in 2025/26). Making regular super contributions is one of the most effective tax strategies available to Australian freelancers.
  • Tax return deadline: 31 October for self-lodgement. If using a registered tax agent, you may have until May of the following year.
  • Deductible expenses: Home office, equipment, software subscriptions (including Notion), professional development, internet, phone, and professional memberships.

This is general information only and does not constitute tax advice. Consult a registered Australian tax agent or accountant for advice specific to your circumstances.
