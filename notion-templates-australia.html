The Australian Freelancer's Tax and Compliance Guide

Australian freelancers face a distinct tax framework compared to freelancers in the US or UK. Here is a complete overview of your obligations as an Australian sole trader or self-employed contractor.

Income Tax for Self-Employed Australians

Self-employment income is included in your individual income tax return as "business income." Australian income tax rates for 2024/25 after Stage 3 tax cuts: 0% on the first $18,200 (tax-free threshold); 19% on $18,201–$45,000; 32.5% on $45,001–$135,000; 37% on $135,001–$190,000; and 45% on income above $190,000. The Low Income Tax Offset (LITO) and Low and Middle Income Tax Offset (LMITO) may further reduce your liability at lower income levels. Your taxable income is gross business income minus allowable deductions.

PAYG Instalments: Paying Tax as You Go

Once your income tax liability reaches a certain threshold, the ATO will enrol you in the PAYG (Pay As You Go) instalment system. This means you make quarterly tax payments throughout the year, rather than a single lump sum at EOFY. The ATO calculates an instalment amount based on your previous year's income — you can vary it down if your income has dropped. FreelanceOS's financial year tracker includes PAYG instalment due dates and a variation log so you have a record if you vary your instalments.

The Medicare Levy

All Australian residents for tax purposes pay the Medicare Levy on top of income tax — currently 2% of taxable income. The Medicare Levy Surcharge (an additional 1–1.5%) applies if you earn above $93,000 and don't hold an appropriate level of private hospital cover. As a self-employed freelancer, your Medicare Levy is calculated in your annual tax return — it's not withheld throughout the year, so you'll need to set aside funds for it alongside your income tax estimate.

ABN vs ACN: What Australian Freelancers Need

An ABN (Australian Business Number) is issued to sole traders, partnerships, trusts, and companies. It's the identifier you put on invoices and is used for GST registration. An ACN (Australian Company Number) is only issued to companies registered with ASIC — if you operate as a sole trader, you only need an ABN. If you've incorporated as a Pty Ltd company (which offers limited liability protection), you have both an ACN and an ABN. FreelanceOS's business registration tracker handles both, plus business name registrations, domain renewals, and state-based licence renewals.

You must register for GST if your annual turnover reaches or exceeds $75,000 AUD in any rolling 12-month period (the 2024/25 threshold). Below this, registration is optional — but many freelancers register voluntarily so clients can claim GST credits. Once registered, you add 10% GST to invoices, lodge quarterly BAS with the ATO, and remit the net GST collected. An ABN (Australian Business Number) is an 11-digit identifier issued by the ATO to businesses and sole traders. While not legally mandatory for all freelancers, you practically need one: without an ABN on your invoice, clients are legally required to withhold 47% of your payment (called no-ABN withholding) and send it to the ATO. A BAS (Business Activity Statement) is the form you lodge with the ATO to report and pay GST collected, GST paid on business purchases, and PAYG instalments. Quarterly BAS due dates for the standard reporting cycle: Q1 (Jul–Sep) due 28 October; Q2 (Oct–Dec) due 28 February; Q3 (Jan–Mar) due 28 April; Q4 (Apr–Jun) due 28 July.
