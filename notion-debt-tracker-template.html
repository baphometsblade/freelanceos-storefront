Sound Familiar?

Why Most People Stay
Stuck in Debt

Debt payoff doesn't fail because of willpower. It fails because of a lack of visibility, strategy, and motivation. Here's what's getting in the way.

😵

You don't know your real total

Most people have a rough sense of their debt but have never added it all up in one place. Student loans in one login, credit cards in another, a car payment somewhere else. Without seeing the full picture — balances, interest rates, minimum payments — you can't build a real plan. You just make minimum payments and hope for the best.

🤷

No strategy for which debt to tackle first

Should you pay off the smallest balance to get a quick win? Or the highest interest rate to save money long-term? Most people don't know which approach suits them, so they pay minimum payments on everything and make almost no progress. Without a deliberate payoff strategy, you could be adding years to your debt-free date — and thousands in unnecessary interest.

📉

Losing motivation without visible progress

Paying off debt is a long game. Without a way to visualize progress — to see balances declining, percentage bars moving, milestones getting closer — it's easy to lose steam in month three and give up. Debt payoff requires sustained discipline over months or years, and you need a system that makes every payment feel like a win. Spreadsheets don't do that. Progress trackers do.

💸

Interest costs are invisible until they're massive

If you're carrying a $5,000 credit card balance at 22% APR and only making minimum payments, you'll pay nearly $3,000 in interest and take over 11 years to pay it off. Most people don't know this. Without seeing what each debt is actually costing you per month — and how much you'll save by accelerating payments — you can't make informed decisions about where to put every extra dollar.

Debt Snowball Tracker

The psychologically proven debt payoff method. List your debts from smallest balance to largest, regardless of interest rate. Pay minimums on everything, then throw every extra dollar at the smallest debt. When it's gone, roll that payment to the next one. The snowball method creates momentum — and seeing debts disappear gives you the motivation to keep going. This module sorts and tracks your snowball automatically.

Debt Avalanche Calculator

The mathematically optimal method for paying the least total interest. Sort your debts by interest rate from highest to lowest, and attack the most expensive debt first while paying minimums everywhere else. The avalanche method saves significantly more money than the snowball — often thousands of dollars. This module shows you both your payoff date and your total interest cost, so you can see exactly what you're saving.

Frequently Asked Questions

Everything You Need to Know
About the Notion Debt Tracker

Debt snowball vs. debt avalanche — which strategy is better for paying off debt? +
Both strategies work — and the best one is whichever strategy you'll actually stick with. The debt snowball method pays off the smallest balance first, regardless of interest rate. It's psychologically powerful because you get quick wins early — eliminating your first debt in a few months creates momentum that carries you through the longer journey. Research from Harvard Business Review confirms that snowball users pay off debt more consistently.

The debt avalanche method targets the highest interest rate first, which saves the most money in total interest over time. If you have a 24.99% APR credit card alongside a 6% student loan, attacking the credit card first could save you thousands of dollars.

Our Notion debt tracker includes both trackers side by side so you can see your projected payoff date and total interest cost for each method, then decide. Many users run both simultaneously to stay informed and switch strategies as their situation changes.
Can I track student loans, car loans, and medical bills alongside credit card debt? +
Yes — the Notion debt tracker is built to handle every type of debt in one unified system. You can track federal student loans, private student loans, credit cards (as many as you have), auto loans, personal loans, medical bills, home equity loans, buy-now-pay-later balances, and informal personal debts.

Each debt entry has fields for debt type, lender, account reference, current balance, original balance, APR, minimum payment, due date, promotional rate details, and notes. The dashboard aggregates all accounts automatically — you always see your grand total, total paid off, and overall payoff trajectory regardless of how many different debt types you're juggling.

Users with student loan portfolios especially appreciate the ability to break out individual loans (most people have multiple federal loans under one servicer, each with a different interest rate) rather than treating them as a single lump sum.
Does this Notion debt tracker template include a monthly budget section? +
Yes. The Monthly Budget Allocator module is built into the template and helps you see income versus expenses versus debt payments in one view. You enter your take-home pay (salary, side income, freelance income) and your fixed and variable expenses, and the module calculates exactly how much is left over for debt payments beyond minimums.

This is where most users find "hidden money" they didn't know they had. It's common to discover $100–$300/month in recurring expenses that can be redirected to debt. The template also includes a "debt payment ratio" tracker — the percentage of your income going to debt — which you want to maximize on the path to debt freedom.

The budget module also includes a "what if" calculator: what happens to your payoff date if you put an extra $100/month toward debt? $250/month? The answer is often startling — even small increases can shave a year or more off your timeline.
How do I handle variable interest rates or promotional 0% APR periods? +
The Interest Rate Log module handles both fixed and variable rate situations with dedicated fields. For each debt you can log your current APR, any promotional rate (like a 0% balance transfer period), and the date that promotional rate expires.

The template flags expiring promotional rates so you're never caught off guard when a 0% period ends and your rate jumps to 22%. You can set notes like "0% promo ends March 2026 — pay off or transfer" directly on that debt entry.

For adjustable-rate loans (like some private student loans or HELOCs), you simply update the APR field when your rate changes and the payoff projections recalculate automatically. Many users check their loan statements quarterly and update rates at that time. The Interest Rate Log also keeps a history column so you can see how your rates have changed over time.
Can I track debt in multiple currencies or adapt this for use outside the US? +
Yes. The template is set up with USD by default, but since it's a Notion template, you can adapt it to any currency by updating the property labels and simply entering your amounts in your local currency. The calculations (totals, interest estimates, budget ratios) all work mathematically the same regardless of currency.

Thousands of users outside the US use this template with GBP, EUR, CAD, AUD, INR, and other currencies. If you have debt in multiple currencies (for example, a USD credit card and a CAD student loan), we recommend converting to your primary currency using the current exchange rate and updating it monthly — you can add an exchange rate note to each debt entry as a reminder.

The debt categories and debt types work globally — student loans, credit cards, car loans, mortgages, and medical debt exist in every country. The template structure, payoff strategies, and motivation tools all translate universally regardless of where you live.
What if I have more than 10 debts? Can the tracker handle a large number of accounts? +
Absolutely. The Notion debt tracker has no limit on the number of debts you can add — it's built on Notion's database system, which scales to hundreds of entries without any performance issues. Whether you have 3 debts or 35, the system handles it the same way.

The Debt Overview Dashboard aggregates all accounts into totals automatically, so the more debts you add, the more comprehensive and accurate your totals become. The snowball and avalanche views re-sort automatically no matter how many entries exist — the priority order recalculates instantly when you add a new debt or update a balance.

If you have a large number of debts and feel overwhelmed, the template's "Focus Mode" view filters to show only your current snowball or avalanche target — just the one or two accounts you should be focused on right now. This keeps the system motivating rather than overwhelming. Many users with large student loan portfolios (10+ individual loans) use this template and find it especially valuable for finally seeing the full picture in one organized place.
