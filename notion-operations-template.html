SOP Builder

How to Write a Great SOP in Notion

A well-written SOP takes 30 minutes to create and saves hours every time it's used. Here's the structure that makes SOPs actually get read and followed.

The SOP Anatomy

H
Header Block

Title, owner, department, last-updated date, and version number. Never ambiguous about who maintains it or whether it's current. One glance tells you everything about the SOP's status.

P
Prerequisites

What access, tools, or prior knowledge does the person need before starting? List everything. Prevents the "I tried to do step 3 but don't have access to X" support ticket halfway through.

S
Step-by-Step Instructions

Numbered steps. One action per step. Add screenshots for UI-heavy processes. Use sub-steps for complex decisions within a step. Write as if explaining to someone who just joined the company this morning.

E
Exceptions & Edge Cases

What happens when step 3 fails? What if the system returns an error? What if the value is above a threshold? Document every exception you've encountered. These are where new hires get stuck.

L
Related SOPs & Resources

Link to every upstream and downstream process. Invoice processing links to vendor-registry and payment-approval SOP. A linked SOP network means you never have to hunt for the next step.

The network effect: The first 10 SOPs save you time. By 50, you have a self-referential operations manual where every edge case points to another procedure. New hires become productive in days, not weeks.

Company Wiki

Structuring a Team Wiki That Actually Gets Used

Most company wikis fail because they have no structure — a flat list of pages nobody finds. Here's the architecture that makes wikis work.

Wiki Architecture Layers

1. Departments

Top-level sections for Engineering, Sales, Marketing, Finance, HR, and Operations. Each department owns its section. Content lives where the team expects it.

2. Roles & Career Ladder

Every role documented: responsibilities, success metrics, tools, and escalation paths. A career ladder so team members know exactly what senior looks like — fewer "how do I grow here?" conversations.

3. Tools & Access

Every tool documented with who has access and how to request it. IT access tickets drop to near zero when people can self-serve the request process.

4. Communication Norms & Decision Frameworks

Slack vs. email vs. Notion? Consensus vs. RACI? Where decisions get logged? Getting this documented removes the majority of recurring miscommunication issues.

Process Improvement

Turn "This Is Broken" Into a Managed System

Most companies know what's broken. The ones that scale are the ones with a system for actually fixing it — and measuring whether the fix worked.

The Process Improvement Cycle

1

Log What's Broken

Any team member adds an entry: process name, what went wrong, how often it breaks, and the business impact. No friction — just a quick database entry.

2

Root Cause Analysis

The process owner investigates: people problem, process gap, or tool failure? Five Whys documented in the entry. Root cause determines whether the fix is a new SOP, tool change, or training.

3

Propose & Approve Fix

Proposed solution documented with effort estimate and expected impact. Owner assigned. Status moved to Approved after ops review. No fix happens without a clear owner and timeline.

4

Implement & Update SOPs

Fix implemented and the relevant SOP updated. The improvement entry links to the SOP it changed. Version number bumped. Stakeholders find the new process immediately.

5

Measure Improvement

30 days after implementation: did the problem recur? Frequency, time saved, error rate documented. Builds institutional evidence of what works and accountability for fixes that don't stick.
