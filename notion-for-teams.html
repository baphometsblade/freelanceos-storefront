Step by Step

Setting Up Notion for Your Team: 6-Step Guide

Follow these six steps to build a Notion workspace your team will actually use — not just a folder full of half-filled pages.

1

Create Your Team Home Page

Build a single hub page linked to every major section of your team workspace — Projects, Wiki, People, OKRs, Meeting Notes. This is where every team member starts their day. Use callout blocks to highlight top priorities and quick links to frequently used databases.

2

Set Up Your Projects & Tasks Database

Build a database with properties for Task Name, Owner (person), Status (Not started, In progress, Done), Priority, Deadline, and linked Project. Add a Board view grouped by Status and a Timeline view for deadline visibility. This single database becomes your team's task layer.

3

Build Your Team Wiki

Create a Wiki section with sub-pages for: How We Work (communication norms, meeting cadence, decision-making), Tools (logins, access, how-tos), Processes (repeatable workflows written step by step), and Decisions (a log of major past decisions with context and rationale). Start small and grow it through real usage.

4

Create a Meeting Notes Database

Build a database with properties for Meeting Type, Date, Attendees (person multi-select), Decisions Made, and Action Items. Create filtered views for each meeting type — weekly standups, client calls, retrospectives. Link action items to your Tasks database so follow-ups get tracked automatically.

5

Set Up Your OKRs Database

Create an OKRs database with properties for Objective, Owner, Quarter, Key Results (sub-items or linked database), and Status. Build a gallery view grouped by Quarter for executive visibility. Link each Key Result to the projects and tasks that move the needle on it — this closes the loop between strategy and execution.

6

Build Your Team Onboarding Guide

Create an Onboarding section with a Week 1 Checklist, role-specific sub-pages, a Tools & Access page, and a FAQ page. New hires should be able to complete their first week without asking anyone a question that is not already answered. Update it every time a new hire asks something you had not covered.

Building Your Team Wiki in Notion

The team wiki is the highest-leverage thing you can build in Notion. Done well, it means your team never asks the same question twice, new hires onboard themselves, and institutional knowledge does not walk out the door when people leave.

What Belongs in a Team Wiki

The best team wikis cover six core areas. How We Work documents your communication norms — when to use Slack versus email versus Notion comments, what meetings you hold and how often, and how decisions get made. Tools & Access lists every tool your team uses with how to request access, a brief description of what it is for, and who to ask if something breaks. Processes documents repeatable workflows in step-by-step format — how to onboard a new client, how to run a sprint, how to publish a blog post. Decisions logs major past decisions with context, options considered, and the rationale so future team members understand the why behind how things work. Glossary defines your internal acronyms and jargon. Templates gives your team starting points for common documents so everyone works from the same baseline.

Organizing Your Wiki

Keep the top level simple. Three to five sections maximum. Resist the temptation to create deeply nested hierarchies — nobody navigates more than two levels deep voluntarily. Use Notion's linking features to cross-reference pages rather than duplicating content. Tag pages with an owner so every section has someone responsible for keeping it current.

Getting the Team to Actually Use It

The biggest failure mode for team wikis is that nobody reads or updates them. The solution is behavioral: make the wiki the canonical source of answers, and enforce it socially. When someone asks a question that has an answer in the wiki, respond with the link rather than re-answering. When someone asks a question whose answer is not in the wiki, add it immediately after answering. Treat the wiki as a living system, not a one-time writing project.

The most important wiki rule: every time someone asks you a question in Slack or email that should have an answer in the wiki, add it immediately. The best team wikis grow through real usage and real questions — not planned writing sessions that never get completed.

When Notion is the Right Team Tool

Notion is strongest for teams of 2 to 20 people who need both project management and documentation in one place. Marketing teams, creative agencies, operations teams, and early-stage startups consistently get the most value from Notion because their work spans both execution and knowledge management — and keeping those in separate tools creates coordination overhead that Notion eliminates.

When You Might Need a Dedicated PM Tool Alongside Notion

Once your team hits 20 to 30 people with complex sprint workflows, dependency tracking across many concurrent projects, or a need for sophisticated resource management and time tracking, a dedicated project management tool starts making more sense for the task layer. Many teams at this size keep Notion for documentation, wikis, and meeting notes while using a dedicated PM tool like Linear, Asana, or Jira for sprint execution. The two systems complement each other well because Notion handles the knowledge layer that dedicated PM tools ignore.

Practical rule: For teams of 2–10, Notion often replaces 3 or more tools: project management, documentation, and meeting notes. Once you hit 20+ people with complex sprint workflows and reporting needs, consider a dedicated PM tool alongside Notion for wikis and docs. Do not migrate your entire team to a new tool just because Notion has limits — understand exactly which limits you are hitting first.

Notion Team Permissions: How to Set Up Access Correctly

Getting permissions wrong is the fastest way to create a confusing, messy team workspace. Notion has several levels of access — and using them intentionally from the start saves headaches as your team grows.

The Three Access Levels That Matter

Full members have access to the entire workspace and can create, edit, and delete pages. They also appear in person properties and can be assigned to tasks. This is the right level for core team members who work in Notion daily. Members is the same as full members in most Notion Plus plans — the distinction matters more in Business and Enterprise plans with team spaces. Guests can only access specific pages or databases you share with them directly. They cannot see the rest of your workspace. This is the correct level for clients, contractors, and external collaborators who need limited access to specific projects without seeing internal company information.

Workspace vs. Page Sharing

Workspace-level access means someone can browse and search the entire workspace. Page-level sharing means someone only sees the specific page or database you link them to. For client and contractor access, always use page-level sharing rather than adding them as workspace members — this gives you precise control over what external parties can see and prevents accidental exposure of internal information.

When to Use Public Pages

Notion pages can be made public on the web — accessible to anyone with the link, with no Notion account required. This is useful for public-facing documentation, knowledge bases, or onboarding portals you want to share without requiring every reader to create a Notion account. Avoid making internal pages or databases public accidentally — double-check the sharing settings on any page that contains sensitive client or financial information.

Managing Contractor and Client Access

Create dedicated project pages or shared client portals for contractor and client access. Share only those pages as guest access. Consider creating a dedicated Shared with Clients section at the top level of your workspace where all client-shared pages live, making it easy to audit what external parties can see. Review and revoke guest access when a project or contract ends — Notion guest slots are limited on the Plus plan, and inactive guests clutter your member list.

Frequently Asked Questions: Notion for Teams

How do I share Notion with my team?
Go to Settings in your Notion workspace, select Members, and invite teammates by email. On the Notion Plus plan, each member gets full workspace access. You can also share individual pages with guests — external collaborators or clients — without giving them access to the rest of your workspace. Guests can be invited from any page's Share menu.
What Notion plan do I need for a team?
Notion Plus at $10 per member per month (billed annually) is the standard team plan — it includes unlimited blocks, 30-day version history, and up to 100 guests. Notion Business at $15 per member per month adds SAML SSO, advanced permissions by team space, audit logs, and 90-day version history. Most teams of 2-30 people run well on the Plus plan before needing Business features.
Can Notion replace Asana or Trello for my team?
For most teams of 2-20 people, yes — Notion can fully replace task management tools like Asana or Trello, and it also handles documentation those tools cannot. The main limitation is that Notion does not have native task dependencies or workload management views that dedicated PM tools offer. Teams with complex sprint workflows or resource management needs at 20+ people often pair Notion with a dedicated PM tool rather than replacing one with the other.
How do I manage permissions in a team Notion workspace?
In Notion Plus, all workspace members get access to the full workspace by default. Use the Notion Business plan if you need team spaces with isolated access per department. For external access, always use guest sharing on specific pages rather than adding external people as workspace members. Review your guest list quarterly and revoke access for completed projects or ended contracts.
What is the best way to onboard new team members to Notion?
Build a dedicated Notion Onboarding page that explains how your team's workspace is organized, where key sections are, how to find things using search, and the conventions your team uses for naming, tagging, and organizing pages. Include a short video walkthrough if possible. New hires who understand the structure early adopt it much faster than those who figure it out by trial and error.
Can clients or contractors access our Notion workspace?
Yes. Share individual pages or databases with clients and contractors as guests — they see only what you share and nothing else in your workspace. The Notion Plus plan includes up to 100 guests per workspace. For clients, consider creating a dedicated client portal page with only the deliverables, timelines, and feedback sections relevant to their project. Always revoke guest access when an engagement ends.
How do I keep our team wiki up to date?
The most effective method is the question-trigger rule: every time someone asks a question that should have been in the wiki, add it immediately after answering. Designate an owner for each major wiki section who is responsible for reviewing their section quarterly. Make wiki updates part of your project close-out process so processes get documented when they are fresh. A quarterly wiki review as a team where you identify stale pages goes a long way toward keeping it current.
