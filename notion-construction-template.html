The Problem

The Four Ways Contractors Lose Money Without a System

Most small contractors have the skills to do excellent work. What kills their margins isn't the work — it's the paperwork, the coordination, and the jobs they thought were profitable until the final invoice came in.

📖

Job Details Scattered Across Texts, Emails & Paper

The client's spec changes are in a text thread. The material list is on a napkin in your truck. The subcontractor's schedule is in a voicemail. When something goes wrong or a client asks a question, you're piecing it together on the fly — every time.

📈

No Clear View of Profit Margin Until the Job Is Done

You bid the kitchen remodel at $42,000 and felt good about it. Six weeks later, after the extra dumpster run, the subcontractor overrun, and the materials price that came in higher than quoted, you made $3,200. You won't know that until the last invoice is paid.

👤

Subcontractor Schedules Constantly Shifting

Your electrician pushed back two days. That means the drywall sub can't come in. That means the painter's slot you booked three weeks ago is now wrong. You're managing all of this in your head and over the phone, and one miscommunication causes a cascade.

📝

Change Order Chaos With No Paper Trail

The client asked you to add a half-bath. You said sure, you'd work it in. You did the work. At final invoice they dispute the $4,800 addition because they "don't remember agreeing to that price." You have no written record. The argument costs you the relationship and probably a chunk of the money.

Job Costing System

The Most Important Number in Your Business: Actual vs. Bid

Most contractors don't know whether they made money on a job until it's over. By then, it's too late to do anything about it. The Job Costing Sheet shows you where you stand mid-job — so you can catch problems before they compound.

1

Enter Your Bid Breakdown

Separate your original bid into cost categories: estimated labor hours and rate, estimated materials, subcontractor costs, and overhead allocation. This is your baseline — what you expected to spend to hit your target margin.

2

Log Actual Costs as They Happen

As purchase orders come in and subcontractor invoices arrive, log actual costs in each category. Material costs pull from your Purchase Order Tracker. Labor hours get entered weekly. Actual vs. estimated variance updates in real time.

3

Watch Your Running Margin

The costing sheet calculates your running gross profit as you enter costs. If you're trending over on labor in week three, you know to tighten up before you've burned through your margin. Catching a $4,000 overrun at week three is a conversation. Catching it at invoicing is a loss.

4

Add Approved Change Orders

Every approved change order adds to your adjusted contract value. The costing sheet recalculates your target margin based on the current contract, not just the original bid. Scope grows, your margin baseline grows with it — no more change orders that add revenue but don't update your cost targets.

5

Review Final Performance

At job close, the costing sheet shows your final gross profit, margin percentage, and variance from bid. Over time, these records show you which job types are most profitable, where you consistently underestimate, and which clients require scope management attention.

Change Order Process

Why Change Orders Kill Profitability — and How to Stop It

The single biggest source of margin erosion for small contractors isn't bad bids or slow crews. It's scope creep that never gets formalized. A client asks for "just one more thing" on ten different occasions and you accommodate each request informally. By the end of the job, you've added $9,000 in labor and materials and invoiced for $1,200.

  • Every scope change gets a change order entry — date, description, reason, and whether it was client-requested or site-discovered
  • Cost impact calculated before approval — labor estimate, materials, subcontractor cost, and markup documented before the client says yes
  • Client approval tracking — pending, verbally approved, or written sign-off with date recorded so there's no ambiguity at invoicing
  • Schedule impact field — does this change add days to the timeline? Document it. If the job runs long, you have the reason in writing.
  • Running change order total — your adjusted contract value reflects every approved change so your job costing margin targets update automatically
  • Change order log links to the job — click any job and see the full revision history: original scope, every change, and how the contract evolved
  • Dispute protection — when a client pushes back on your final invoice, you open the change order log and show them date, description, cost, and their approval date. The conversation is usually short.
  • Rejected change order tracking — if a client declines a change, that's documented too. No one can later claim you agreed to include it in the base price.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I use Notion for construction project management?
The Job Pipeline is your command center — one row per project with status, client, address, bid amount, start and end dates, and links to all associated costs. Jobs move through six stages from Estimate to Paid. Every job links to its subcontractor assignments, material purchase orders, change orders, and schedule so your entire project file is one click away. Views let you filter by status, phase, or week so you always know what needs attention right now.
How does the job costing system work in Notion?
The Job Costing Sheet tracks every cost category against your original bid: labor hours times rate, materials cost from purchase orders, subcontractor invoices, and overhead allocation. As you log actual costs, the sheet shows your running variance from bid — so you catch scope creep in real time. The final view shows net profit and margin percentage per job. This is an organizational tracking tool; it does not constitute financial or tax advice — consult a qualified professional for your specific situation.
How does the change order log work?
The Change Order Log has one entry per scope change: date, description, cost impact, schedule impact, client approval status, and sign-off date. Every change order links to its parent job so you see the full revision history for any project. Client approval tracking with a signed-off field and date means you have a clear paper trail for every change — protecting you if there's a billing dispute at closeout.
Can I manage subcontractors and their schedules in Notion?
Yes — the Subcontractor Roster stores every trade partner's contact information, trade, rate, insurance expiration dates, license numbers, and performance notes. An availability field shows when each sub is booked versus available. When scheduling a new job, filter by trade and check availability before committing to a start date. Insurance expiry flags let you identify subs whose certificates are approaching expiration so you never use an uninsured sub on a job.
Is this template good for specialty trades like plumbing, electrical, or HVAC?
Yes — the template works for any trade-based contractor.
