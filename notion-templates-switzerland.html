Swiss Tax Self-Assessment Tracker

Switzerland has three tax layers: federal income tax (Direkte Bundessteuer), plus cantonal and municipal tax. Rates vary significantly by canton — Zug and Schwyz have the lowest rates; Geneva and Vaud are higher. File your annual Steuererklärung (Déclaration d'impôt) in your canton. Track quarterly provisional tax installments (Provisorium) based on prior year income and update estimates as your income changes.

🏠

AHV/IV/EO Social Insurance Tracker

Switzerland's mandatory social insurance for self-employed: AHV (pension), IV (disability), and EO (loss of earnings) together equal approximately 10.1% of net income (2024 rate) — paid entirely by the self-employed, unlike employees who split this with their employer. Register with your cantonal Ausgleichskasse within 90 days. Track quarterly AHV installment payment dates and note your annual settlement amount.

📋

MWST/TVA/IVA (Swiss VAT) Tracker

Swiss VAT (MWST) is 8.1% standard rate (2024). Mandatory registration threshold: CHF 100,000 annual revenue. Switzerland has its own VAT system — NOT EU VAT — so special rules apply for EU clients. Services to foreign business clients may be zero-rated (B2B export). Track your registration status, quarterly MWST return dates, and whether you use the effective method or simplified net tax rate method.

Cantonal Business Registration Notes

Self-employed work in Switzerland is regulated at cantonal level. Notify your cantonal AHV compensation office within 90 days of starting. Some professions require cantonal business licenses (e.g., accounting, legal, medical). Track Handelsregister (commercial register) requirements if your revenue exceeds CHF 100,000 and note your canton's specific requirements for home office deductions and professional expense rules.

Freelancing in Switzerland: Europe's Premium Market

Switzerland is one of the world's most expensive countries and also one with the highest freelance rates. Swiss freelancers and independent contractors (Selbständigerwerbende / Indépendants) operate in one of Europe's most sophisticated economies, serving domestic multinationals, international organizations, and EU clients. The Swiss freelance market is particularly strong in financial services (banking and asset management), pharmaceutical and biotech, technology (especially in the Zurich-Zug-Lucerne corridor), engineering and precision manufacturing consulting, architecture and design, translation and localization (Swiss German, French, Italian, English), and education and training.

Switzerland's tax system is notably decentralized: while there is a federal income tax, cantonal and municipal taxes vary widely — choosing to live in Zug vs. Geneva can mean a 15%+ difference in total tax burden on the same income. Freelancers must register with the AHV compensation office in their canton within 90 days of starting self-employment, as AHV/IV/EO contributions are mandatory and calculated on net profit. Switzerland is NOT an EU member, though it maintains bilateral agreements with the EU. This means EU VAT rules do not apply — Switzerland has its own MWST system with its own rates and rules.

The high cost of living and strong social safety net make AHV contributions particularly important — they form the foundation of retirement income (Pillar 1). Many Swiss freelancers also contribute to Pillar 3a (private pension) for additional tax deductions up to CHF 7,056 per year (2024 limit for those without Pillar 2 / BVG coverage). The strong CHF has been beneficial for Swiss freelancers billing in CHF to international clients. Common platforms for Swiss freelancers: XING (strong in German-speaking markets), LinkedIn, local job boards (jobs.ch, indeed.ch), direct referrals from former employer networks, and European agency relationships in Zurich, Geneva, and Basel.

DE: Wenn Sie in der Schweiz selbständig erwerbstätig sind, müssen Sie sich innerhalb von 90 Tagen bei der zuständigen Ausgleichskasse anmelden und AHV/IV/EO-Beiträge (~10,1%) auf Ihrem Nettoeinkommen entrichten.

FR: Si vous êtes indépendant en Suisse, vous devez vous annoncer auprès de la caisse de compensation compétente dans les 90 jours et payer des cotisations AVS/AI/APG (~10,1%) sur votre revenu net.

Pillar 3a tip: Swiss freelancers without a Pillar 2 (BVG/LPP) occupational pension — which is common when self-employed — can deduct up to CHF 35,280 per year (2024 limit; 20% of net earned income) into a Pillar 3a account. This reduces your cantonal and federal taxable income significantly. Banks like PostFinance, Raiffeisen, and fintech options like Frankly or VIAC offer Pillar 3a accounts.

Switzerland's Key Freelance Hubs by City

  • Zurich (Zürich): Switzerland's largest city and financial center. Home to Google CH, UBS tech hub, Zurich Insurance tech teams, and a large fintech community (Crypto Valley spillover, numerous B2B SaaS). Highest client density for tech, finance, and consulting freelancers.
  • Geneva (Genève): International city with UN, WHO, WTO, Red Cross, and hundreds of NGO headquarters. Strong demand for consultants in policy, communications, data analysis, translation, and international development. Also home to pharma (Ferring, Lonza) and trading companies.
  • Basel (Bale/Basel): Pharma and life sciences capital — Novartis, Roche, and hundreds of biotech firms create demand for regulatory affairs consultants, medical writers, clinical data analysts, and pharmaceutical project managers.
  • Lausanne & EPFL Corridor: EPFL (ranked among Europe's top technical universities) drives a deep tech ecosystem. Strong demand for ML/AI researchers, hardware engineers, robotics consultants, and health tech developers.
  • Bern: Switzerland's administrative capital — government consulting, legal services, and public affairs work. Also a growing creative and startup scene.
  • Zug: Low-tax canton with a strong tech and crypto community. Many international companies headquartered here for tax efficiency. Growing demand for legal, compliance, and tech consulting.

Frequently Asked Questions — Freelancing in Switzerland

How do I register as a freelancer in Switzerland?
You must notify your cantonal AHV compensation office (Ausgleichskasse / Caisse de compensation) within 90 days of starting self-employed work. They will assess your AHV/IV/EO contributions. Depending on your profession and revenue, you may also need a cantonal business permit. If revenue will exceed CHF 100,000, register for MWST with the Federal Tax Administration. A Handelsregister (commercial register) entry is required if revenue exceeds CHF 100,000 for sole proprietorships in most cantons.
What social insurance do Swiss freelancers pay?
Swiss self-employed pay AHV (pension), IV (disability), and EO (loss of earnings) — combined at approximately 10.1% of net income (2024 rate). Unlike employees who split this with their employer, self-employed individuals pay the full 10.1% themselves. Contributions are calculated annually on your net profit and paid in quarterly installments to your cantonal Ausgleichskasse. There is also a minimum annual contribution even at very low income levels.
What are AHV contributions for the self-employed?
AHV (Alters- und Hinterlassenenversicherung) contributions for self-employed are part of the combined AHV/IV/EO rate of approximately 10.1% of net income. This forms the foundation of your Pillar 1 retirement income. Freelancers without a Pillar 2 (BVG) occupational pension — which is common when self-employed — can also make Pillar 3a contributions up to CHF 35,280/year (20% of net earned income, 2024 limit), deductible from cantonal and federal income tax.
How does Swiss VAT (MWST) work for freelancers?
Swiss MWST (Mehrwertsteuer / TVA) is 8.1% standard rate. Registration is mandatory once annual revenue exceeds CHF 100,000. Switzerland has its own VAT system separate from the EU — EU VAT rules don't apply. B2B services to foreign clients (exports) may be zero-rated. File quarterly MWST returns with the Federal Tax Administration. Two accounting methods: effective method (track actual VAT in/out) or net tax rate method (simplified flat rate — easier for smaller operations).
How does Switzerland's cantonal tax system affect freelancers?
Switzerland's tax system has three layers: federal, cantonal, and municipal. Cantons set their own rates, leading to large differences — Zug and Schwyz have the lowest combined rates (effective total rates well below 15% for many income levels), while Geneva, Vaud, and Bern are significantly higher (25-35%+ combined). Filing is done in your canton of residence. Provisional quarterly installments (Provisorium) are based on the prior year; the annual Steuererklärung reconciles the actual tax owed.
What are the best cantons for freelancers in Switzerland?
For tax efficiency: Zug (lowest rates, strong crypto/tech community), Schwyz, Nidwalden, and Obwalden offer the lowest combined tax rates. For client access: Zurich (largest tech and finance ecosystem), Geneva (international organizations, pharma), and Basel (pharma and biotech). Many Swiss freelancers live in lower-tax cantons like Zug while working with clients in Zurich — easily accessible by train in 30–40 minutes.
