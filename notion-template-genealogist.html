The Problem

Running a Professional Genealogy Practice Means Managing Research, Sources, Clients, and Business Simultaneously

Professional genealogists balance multiple active research projects, hundreds of source citations, complex ancestor databases, archive relationships, and business finances. Here is what the unmanaged version costs you.

📜

Source citations tracked across project files and notebooks

You've consulted 200 sources across four active research projects. Each project has citations in a separate Word document or research log. When a client asks whether you've checked a specific record set, you open three files to confirm. When you're writing the final report, you reconstruct citations from memory and files rather than querying a database. A structured source citation database that is searchable by record type, repository, and client is the infrastructure that separates professional practice from hobbyist research.

🔄

Negative search results not systematically recorded

You searched the 1880 federal census for a client's great-grandmother last year. You're now three months into a related research project and can't remember if you already searched that record set with alternate name spellings. Without a research log that documents negative results as explicitly as positive finds, you repeat searches you've already conducted — paying the professional cost of redundant work and presenting clients with hours that covered ground you'd already covered.

🧬

DNA analysis findings tracked separately from documentary research

A client has AncestryDNA results showing a close match at 412 cM — a predicted first cousin or half-sibling. You've been tracking this alongside documentary research but in separate notes. When you write the final research report, synthesizing the genetic and paper trail evidence requires manually cross-referencing two separate systems. Integrated DNA and documentary tracking in one workspace means your analysis and report synthesis happen in the same place.

💼

Research project scope and hours tracked informally

A client hired you for a 15-hour research project. You've been tracking hours in a spreadsheet. The project is approaching hour 14 but you're not close to a reportable conclusion. You need to discuss scope extension with the client — but you realize you don't have a clear record of what you've searched, what you've found, and what remains to be investigated. A project tracker with hours logged, scope documentation, and research progress prevents this conversation from happening without preparation.

📚 Source Citation Database

Per-source records following professional citation standards: source type (vital record, census, church record, military, newspaper, probate, land record, immigration, naturalization, DNA match, oral history, photograph, published genealogy, manuscript), repository or database (FamilySearch, Ancestry, FindMyPast, National Archives, state archives, county courthouse, local library special collections), citation elements, date accessed, quality assessment (original source vs. derivative vs. authored narrative; direct vs. indirect vs. negative evidence), relevance to research question, and report inclusion status. Searchable by record type, repository, and client — surfaces coverage gaps before you finalize a report.

🔍 Genealogical Research Log

Per-session research documentation: client or project, session date, repository or database searched, specific record set or collection, search parameters and terms used, time period searched, results found (with citation) or negative result documented, analysis notes, and next steps identified. Documenting negative search results — records consulted that yielded no relevant information — is as professionally important as documenting positive finds. It prevents repeating searches, demonstrates thoroughness in client reports, and meets the research documentation standards of the Association of Professional Genealogists and Board for Certification of Genealogists.

🌳 Ancestor & Lineage Database

Per-individual records for ancestors identified in client research: individual name (including maiden name and name variants), birth date and place, death date and place, marriage information, known parents, known children, emigration details if applicable, religious affiliation, occupations, residence history, sources documenting this individual (linked to source citation database), research notes and unresolved questions, and DNA match connections if applicable. The ancestor database functions as your working genealogical record — distinct from formal genealogy software but integrated with your research log and source citations so every finding is connected to its evidentiary basis.

🧬 DNA Analysis Tracker

Per-client DNA records: test taken (AncestryDNA, 23andMe, MyHeritage, FamilyTreeDNA, Living DNA), test kit number, test date, ethnicity estimate overview, total centimorgans in database, close matches identified (cM value and predicted relationship), endogamy factors if applicable, chromosome browser analysis if conducted, triangulation groups identified, and correlation with documentary research findings. DNA analysis is now standard in professional genealogical practice — tracking genetic and paper trail evidence in the same workspace means your report synthesis draws on both simultaneously rather than requiring cross-system navigation.
