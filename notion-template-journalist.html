The Problem

You Report the World. But Who's Running the Business of Your Journalism Career?

Journalism training teaches you how to report, write, verify, and publish. It rarely teaches you how to manage a multi-story workload simultaneously — tracking five active pieces in different stages, keeping source relationships warm across months, remembering which editors have seen which pitches, and making sure your invoices actually get submitted before the payment window closes. Great journalists often run the business side of their career on memory and chaos, and it costs them stories, sources, and income.

📋

Stories in five different places

Your active assignments live in email. Your ideas are in a notebook. Your research is in browser bookmarks, a Google Doc, and three open tabs you are afraid to close. Your drafts are in a folder with names like "final_FINAL_v3." When a new angle breaks on an existing story, you are scrambling to find notes you made three weeks ago during a phone call you cannot quite remember. There is no single view of your entire story pipeline, and there should be.

📞

Source relationships going cold

You cultivated a brilliant source at a regulatory agency eighteen months ago and they were invaluable for a story. You have not called them since. You cannot remember if their number is in your phone under their first name or their last, and you are not certain they are still in the same role. You have dozens of contacts like this — people who would pick up the phone for you if only you had maintained the relationship — but no system for tracking who you know, what they cover, and when you last spoke.

💌

Pitches lost in the void

You sent a pitch to a magazine editor six weeks ago and you have no record of whether you followed up, what angle you proposed, what rate you asked for, or whether you already tried a variation of this idea with a different outlet last year. Your pitching is reactive and scattered rather than strategic, which means you are missing opportunities to learn what works, which editors respond, and which ideas have the most traction across your target publications.

💸

Invoices delayed or forgotten

You published a piece in March, the invoice was due within thirty days, and it is now July. You missed the submission window because the invoicing reminder got buried under a breaking news cycle and you were already deep in the next story. Freelance journalists lose hundreds — sometimes thousands — of pounds or dollars a year not because publications do not pay, but because the administrative side of a freelance career gets perpetually deprioritised when reporting demands attention. A system that tracks every invoice automatically would cost you nothing and earn you everything outstanding.

The Solution

A Complete Journalist OS in Notion — Story Pipeline, Sources, Pitches & Press CRM

FreelanceOS Pro for Journalists connects your story pipeline kanban, source contact database, interview prep templates, research library, story idea backlog, deadline dashboard, freelance rate and invoice tracker, and press contact CRM — all in one relational Notion workspace built for the full reality of working journalism.

Story Pipeline Kanban — Pitch → Assigned → Reporting → Draft → Editing → Published

A full kanban board tracking every active story through every stage of the editorial lifecycle. Each story card carries the headline or working title, the publication, the commissioning editor, the agreed word count, the deadline, the rate, and any linked source records or research materials from your connected databases. The kanban view gives you an immediate visual picture of your entire workload at a glance — you can see in seconds how many pieces are simultaneously in the Reporting stage, which ones are approaching deadline with the copy still in Draft, and whether your Published column is filling while your Pitch column is running dry, which is a signal to generate and send new ideas before your revenue pipeline stalls. Switching to a Calendar view on the same database shows you every deadline spread across the month so you can plan your reporting days with full awareness of what is due when. This is the operational backbone of the entire Journalist OS and the single page most working journalists open first every morning.

Source Contact Database — Name, Beat, Phone, Reliability Rating & Last Contact

A structured, searchable database of every source, expert, and contact in your professional network, organised for the way journalists actually need to use source information. Each record captures name, title, organisation, beat or area of expertise, preferred contact method, phone number, email, how you first connected, a reliability rating on a simple scale from background-only through to confirmed on-the-record regular, the date of last contact, and a freeform notes field for sensitivities, preferences, or contextual details you would otherwise have to remember. You can link each source record to every story they have contributed to, so you can see across your coverage which sources you rely on most and identify gaps in your network for beats you cover regularly. Filtering by beat or expertise makes it fast to find who to call when a breaking story lands in a particular area. Sorting by last-contact date surfaces relationships that are going cold — valuable contacts who would pick up the phone if you maintained the relationship — so you can reach out proactively rather than only when you urgently need something, which is how source relationships stay genuinely warm over a long career.

Interview Prep Templates — Questions Framework, Recording Notes & Key Quote Tracker

A structured interview preparation template for every significant interview, keeping your pre-interview research and your post-interview notes in the same place and linked to the relevant story. Before the interview, the template prompts you to document the interviewee's background, the key areas you need to cover, your must-ask questions ranked by priority, the facts you need them to confirm or deny, and any sensitive areas that require careful framing. During or immediately after the interview, you capture the recording reference or timestamp, the key quotes with context, the factual claims that need follow-up verification, and the new leads or sources the interview surfaced. A Key Quote Tracker property lets you flag the strongest quotes for easy retrieval when you are in the drafting stage and need to pull the best lines without re-reading hours of notes. All interview records link back to the story in your pipeline kanban, creating a seamless chain from assignment through research through draft that means nothing important gets lost between the notebook and the published piece.

Research Library — Tagged Links, Documents & Background Materials by Story

A centralised research library for every piece of source material you gather while reporting — web links, PDFs, court filings, company documents, government reports, academic papers, data sets, and background reading — organised with tags so you can filter and surface the right material exactly when you need it rather than hunting through browser bookmarks or email archives. Each research item is tagged by story, by topic or theme, by source type (primary document, secondary source, background reading, data), and by relevance so you can quickly distinguish between materials that are directly quotable and materials that provided useful context. The library is relational, meaning you can link each research item to the source contact who provided it, to the story it belongs to, and to any related items in the same database — so following a thread of related documents is a filter operation rather than a memory exercise. For investigative work spanning weeks or months, the research library becomes the single most valuable part of the entire workspace, because it transforms an unmanageable volume of material into a searchable, structured archive you can navigate with confidence even when returning to a story after a period away.

Story Idea Backlog — Pitch Success Tracker & Editor Preference Log

A dedicated database for story ideas that are not yet active pitches — the concepts you want to develop, the angles you noticed but could not pursue immediately, the follow-up pieces suggested by stories you have already written, and the ideas that were pitched and rejected but still have potential with a different angle or a different outlet. Each idea record captures the concept, the newsworthiness, the potential publications it suits, the ideal timing, and your current thinking on the reporting requirements. The Pitch Success Tracker layer links your idea records to actual pitches sent, so you can see over time which types of stories land most consistently with which publications, building a data-informed understanding of your pitching performance rather than relying on gut feel. The Editor Preference Log captures what you learn about each editor's tastes, response times, and commissioning priorities from the pitches you send them, so each subsequent pitch to the same editor reflects accumulated knowledge about what they actually want — which is one of the most underrated advantages a freelance journalist can have.

Deadline Dashboard — Due Dates, Editors, Word Counts & Publication Tracker

A dedicated deadline view across your entire active story pipeline, giving you a real-time picture of every piece due in the next seven, fourteen, and thirty days with its current status, the commissioning editor, the agreed word count, and the publication. The dashboard surfaces deadline conflicts before they become crises — if you have three pieces due in the same forty-eight-hour window, you need to know that three weeks in advance so you can start one piece early, negotiate an extension on another, or decline a new commission you genuinely cannot deliver at the standard your byline requires. A formula property calculating days remaining and flagging pieces within seventy-two hours makes urgency visible at a glance without manual calculation. For freelance journalists managing commissions across multiple publications simultaneously, the Deadline Dashboard is the difference between a workload you control and a workload that controls you — and it takes less than two minutes each morning to review and adjust your day accordingly.

Freelance Rate & Invoice Tracker — Per-Publication Rates, Kill Fees & Payment Status

A complete invoice and payment tracking system that closes the gap between published pieces and paid invoices — the gap where freelance journalists lose money not because publications refuse to pay but because the administrative follow-through gets buried under the next story cycle. Each invoice record captures the story title, the publication, the commissioning editor, the agreed rate, the kill fee if applicable, the invoice number, the date the invoice was submitted, the payment terms (net thirty, net sixty, on publication), the payment due date, and the current status (Invoice Not Yet Submitted, Invoice Sent, Payment Pending, Paid, Overdue, Disputed). The Per-Publication Rate Log captures what each publication pays for different piece types — features, news, reviews, analysis, essays — so you always know before you pitch whether a commission at a given outlet is worth your time relative to competing opportunities. Filtering by status surfaces every overdue payment in seconds, removing the guesswork and embarrassment from chasing money you are legitimately owed. This tracker alone typically recovers its cost many times over in the first month for any active freelance journalist with outstanding unpaid invoices they have not yet chased.

Press Contact CRM — PR Contacts, Embargoes & Media List Management

A dedicated press contact and media relations database that captures every PR professional, communications director, press office, and spokesperson you interact with across your beats. Each contact record holds the person's name, organisation, role, phone, email, beats they handle, typical response time, embargo reliability rating, and notes on their preferences and communication style — because the best PR contacts are treated like sources, with the same attention to relationship maintenance. The Embargo Tracker lets you log every embargoed briefing you receive with the embargo lift date, the story angle, and your notes from the briefing, so you are never at risk of accidentally breaking an embargo through disorganisation rather than intent. The Media List feature lets you build and maintain targeted lists of contacts for specific beats or story types — your regulars for technology briefings, your contacts for defence and security, your entertainment and culture PRs — so when you need to reach out to confirm a story or request comment, you have the right contacts already organised rather than hunting through email or searching your phone. Maintaining a structured press contact CRM is one of the habits that separates journalists who get exclusive briefings and early access from those who find out when everyone else does.

Frequently Asked Questions

Questions From Journalists & Reporters

How do journalists use Notion?

Journalists use Notion as a central command centre for the business and organisational side of their reporting work. In practice this means building a story pipeline kanban to track every active piece from initial pitch through to published, maintaining a source contact database so relationships never go cold between stories, keeping a research library of links, documents, and background materials organised by story or beat, and managing a pitch tracker that records which editors have seen which ideas and at what rates. Notion's relational databases let you connect a source to every story they have contributed to, link a pitch to its eventual assignment, and see your full workload deadline view in a single calendar. For freelance journalists in particular, Notion also serves as an invoice and rate tracker, capturing what each publication pays, when invoices are due, and which payments are outstanding. The result is a workspace where the journalism and the business of journalism live together rather than scattered across email, notebooks, Trello, and memory.

Can Notion replace a CMS for journalists?

Notion is not a publishing CMS and should not be used as one — it cannot push content to a website, manage SEO metadata the way a WordPress or Ghost installation does, or handle reader-facing publication workflows. What Notion can replace is the chaotic collection of productivity tools, notebooks, and mental lists that journalists use to manage their work before it reaches a CMS. Think of Notion as the pre-publication layer: story ideation, pitch drafting, research organisation, source management, interview prep, and deadline tracking all happen in Notion, and then finished copy moves into whatever CMS the publication uses. For freelancers who publish on their own platforms, Notion can draft and organise content, but final publishing still requires a proper CMS. Used in this way, Notion dramatically reduces the friction between having an idea and delivering a polished piece, because everything you need — your research, your sources, your notes, your draft — is in one connected workspace.

How do I organise sources in Notion?

The most effective approach is a dedicated Source Contact Database as a Notion database where each record represents one source or contact. Each entry should capture the person's name, title, organisation, beat or area of expertise, phone number, email, how you originally connected, a reliability rating on a simple scale from background-only to on-the-record regular, the date of last contact, and notes on any sensitivities or preferences about how they like to be approached. You can then link each source record to the story records they contributed to, so you can see at a glance which sources you have used across your recent coverage and which valuable contacts you have not spoken to in months. Filtering your source database by beat or expertise makes it fast to identify who to call when a breaking story falls in a particular area. Sorting by last-contact date surfaces relationships that are going cold so you can reach out proactively rather than only when you need something, which is the approach that keeps source relationships genuinely warm over a career.

What's the best Notion database for story tracking?

A kanban board with clearly defined stages is the most effective Notion structure for story tracking. The stages that mirror a real editorial workflow are: Idea, Pitched, Assigned, Reporting, Drafting, Editing, and Published. Each story card should carry the story title, the publication, the commissioning editor, the deadline, the agreed word count, the rate, and any linked source records from your source database. The kanban view gives you an immediate visual picture of how loaded your pipeline is at each stage — you can see at a glance if you have too many pieces simultaneously in the Reporting stage, which is typically the most time-intensive, or if your Published column is filling up while your Pitched column is thin, signalling you need to generate and send new pitches. Many journalists switch between the kanban for workload awareness and a table sorted by deadline for day-to-day task management.

Can I use Notion offline as a journalist?

Notion has an offline mode on its desktop and mobile apps that caches recently viewed pages for access without an internet connection. For journalists this matters most in the field — attending a press briefing, conducting interviews in a location with unreliable connectivity, or travelling to cover a story. Pages you have opened recently will load offline, and edits you make sync automatically when connectivity returns. For critical field work, the practical advice is to open your relevant story page, source contact records, and interview prep templates before leaving a reliable connection so they are definitely cached. Notion is not designed as a primary offline-first tool in the way some dedicated note-taking apps are, so for deep reporting trips in remote locations you may want to export key documents to PDF or keep a backup in a dedicated offline note app alongside your Notion workspace. For normal freelance use — working from home, coffee shops, or newsrooms — the offline behaviour is reliable enough for day-to-day workflow.

How do I track pitches in Notion?

Build a dedicated Pitch Tracker database in Notion where each record represents one pitch to one editor. Each pitch record should capture the story idea title, a short summary of the angle, the target publication, the specific editor it was sent to, the date pitched, the rate you requested or the publication's standard rate, the current status (Sent, Under Review, Accepted, Rejected, No Response), any follow-up date you have set, and notes on the editor's response or feedback. Filtering by status lets you see every pitch currently awaiting a response so you know when to follow up. Filtering by publication lets you see your hit rate with specific outlets over time, which helps you understand where your pitching energy is best invested. Linking accepted pitches to your Story Pipeline kanban creates a seamless flow from commission to publication without re-entering data, and linking rejected ideas to your Story Idea Backlog means a good idea that was wrong for one publication can be re-pitched elsewhere with the editor feedback already captured.

What's the best Notion view for deadlines?

A Notion Calendar view filtering your Story Pipeline database by the deadline date property gives you the clearest picture of upcoming due dates spread across the month. Set the calendar to show story title, publication, and status on each date block so you can see at a glance what is due when and whether each piece is at a stage that matches its proximity to deadline. A piece that is still in Reporting with a deadline two days away needs immediate attention in a way that a Drafting piece with a week remaining does not. For a tighter week-by-week view, a Table view sorted ascending by deadline and filtered to exclude Published stories gives you a prioritised task list. Adding a formula property that calculates days remaining and highlights pieces within seventy-two hours in a distinct colour makes deadline urgency visible without having to calculate manually every morning.

Can Notion help with investigative research?

Notion is well-suited to the research organisation phase of investigative journalism, where the challenge is managing large volumes of documents, sources, leads, and threads across weeks or months of reporting. A Research Library database lets you save links to public documents, court filings, company records, and source materials with tags by topic, source type, and relevance, so you can filter and surface the right background when you need it rather than scrolling through browser bookmarks or email archives. A Source Database connected to story and research records lets you track which sources have provided which pieces of information and their reliability on specific claims. A Leads and Threads board lets you track investigative threads by status so nothing gets dropped as the investigation develops over time. Notion is not a document storage system for sensitive or legally sensitive materials — for that, use an encrypted solution appropriate to the sensitivity of your investigation — but as an organisational layer for managing the complexity of a long-running investigation, Notion provides the relational structure and flexible views that generic note-taking tools simply cannot match.
