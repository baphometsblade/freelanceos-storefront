The Problem

Journalism Is Research, Relationships, and Deadlines — All At Once

Journalism school teaches reporting, interviewing, and writing. It rarely teaches journalists how to manage a professional operation spanning multiple simultaneous stories, dozens of source relationships, competing publication deadlines, and the administrative realities of freelance invoicing — all without a structural system to keep it organized.

📋

Story pipeline living in your email inbox

You're working on three features, two news pieces, and a pitch you haven't followed up on. Where each story stands — who you've called, what you're waiting on, what the editor last said — exists across email threads, phone notes, and memory. One missed follow-up or forgotten source costs you the story.

🗂️

Sources scattered across notebooks and contacts

Your best sources are in a phone contact labeled "City Hall guy," a card from a conference two years ago, and a notebook from an investigation you finished in 2023. When a breaking story hits the beat you've covered for years, you're searching frantically for contact information that should be one click away.

📅

Multiple publication deadlines with no single view

You're filing for three different publications this week. Each editor has a different deadline, format expectation, and word count. Tracking all of it in calendar reminders and mental notes means one dropped ball creates a professional reputation problem that takes months to repair.

💸

Freelance invoicing is an afterthought

You finished the piece, it published, and now three months later you haven't sent the invoice because you can't find the rate sheet email, aren't sure of the kill fee policy, and have been too busy chasing the next story to chase the payment for the last one.

A Complete Journalist OS in Notion

FreelanceOS Pro for Journalists connects your story pipeline, source database, interview notes, research archive, deadline calendar, fact-checking workflow, FOI tracker, and freelance billing — all in one relational Notion workspace built for the professional realities of modern journalism.

Story Pipeline Kanban — Pitch → Research → Draft → Edit → Published

A visual kanban board tracking every story through its lifecycle from pitch to publication. Each card holds the story slug, publication target, editor contact, current stage, deadline date, word count target, kill fee terms if freelance, and notes from the last editor conversation. Drag cards through stages as work progresses. Filter by publication to see all your assignments for a single outlet, or filter by deadline to see what needs to ship this week. Never lose track of where any story stands across the simultaneous features, news pieces, investigations, and pitches that make up a working journalist's pipeline.

Source Database — Contact Info, Beat Relevance, Last Contact Date & Relationship Notes

A structured database of every source in your professional network — name, title, organization, beat relevance tags (education, city hall, criminal justice, healthcare, finance, technology), primary contact method, last contact date, how the relationship was established, and relationship notes from past interactions. When a breaking story hits your beat, search by relevance tag to instantly surface every source who might have insight, comment, or documentation. Track which sources you've quoted recently so you diversify attribution rather than over-relying on the same three voices. A well-maintained source database is one of a journalist's most valuable professional assets — this keeps yours organized and searchable for the duration of your career.

Interview Notes Template — Pre-Interview Prep, Recording Links & Key Quotes

A structured interview template for every conversation — pre-interview research notes, the list of questions prepared, space for answers captured during the interview, key quotes flagged for potential use, follow-up questions that emerged during the conversation, and a link to the recording or transcript file stored elsewhere. Having a consistent note-taking structure means you can return to an interview months later and instantly reconstruct what was said, what was usable, and what follow-up was promised. Attach the interview to the relevant story record in the pipeline so all your reporting on a given piece stays connected in one relational workspace.

Deadline Tracker — Publication, Editor, Format & Submission Status

A deadline management system tracking every active assignment with the publication name, editor contact, assigned word count, format requirements, submission deadline, whether a draft has been submitted, editor feedback status, and final publication date when it goes live. Filter by week to see exactly what's due and when. Sort by publication to see your full workload with any single outlet. Flag assignments where editor communication has gone quiet so you follow up before a deadline becomes a problem rather than after it's been missed. Journalists who track their deadlines systematically rather than relying on calendar reminders alone make fewer errors, communicate more proactively with editors, and build the reliability reputation that generates repeat assignments and better rates over time.

Research Archive — Notes, Documents, Links & Tags by Topic and Story

A tagged research archive connecting background documents, web links, PDF clips, data files, and research notes to specific stories and topics. Tag every item by beat area so research from a 2023 investigation on housing policy is searchable the next time the beat intersects with a new story. Attach primary documents to story records. Flag sources that need to be cited, verified, or followed up. Journalism produces enormous amounts of research that gets used once and then lost — a structured archive means the work you did on past stories compounds in value rather than disappearing when the assignment ends.

Fact-Checking Checklist — Claims, Sources, Verification Status & Editor Sign-Off

A fact-checking workflow tracking every significant claim in a story — the specific claim, the source who made it, the corroborating source or document, verification status (Verified / Needs Second Source / Disputed / Removed), and whether the copy editor or editor has signed off on the verification. Flag any claim that is still pending a second source before submission. Track corrections from past published pieces and what caused them to prevent similar errors in future work. Systematic fact-checking workflows distinguish journalists who build long-term institutional trust from those who face corrections and retractions that compound into reputation damage over a career.

Freedom of Information Request Tracker — Agency, Filing Date, Status & Response Log

A tracker for every FOIA, FOIL, or public records request — the agency or department, the specific documents requested, the filing date, legal response deadline under applicable law, current status (Submitted / Acknowledged / In Review / Partially Fulfilled / Appealed / Closed), response log with dates of every communication, and whether you've followed up on delayed responses. FOI requests are slow by design and require systematic follow-up over months or years — a tracker ensures you never let a delayed response go unchallenged past legal deadlines and gives you a complete paper trail if you ever need to litigate access.

Freelance Invoice & Publication Tracker — Rates, Kill Fees & Payment Status

A freelance billing system tracking every assignment with the publication, agreed rate, kill fee terms, invoice date, payment due date, payment status (Invoiced / Paid / Overdue / Kill Fee Applied), and notes on any billing disputes. Filter by status to see every unpaid invoice outstanding. Sort by publication to see your total billing history with any single outlet. Flag overdue invoices for follow-up before the relationship becomes adversarial. Freelance journalists are notoriously bad at following up on outstanding invoices because the next story always feels more urgent — this tracker makes your billing as systematic as your reporting so money you've earned actually lands in your account.

Frequently Asked Questions

Questions From Journalists & Freelance Reporters

What is the Journalist OS Notion template used for?

The Journalist OS is a business and workflow management template for reporters and freelance journalists. It tracks your story pipeline, source relationships, interview notes, research archives, publication deadlines, fact-checking workflow, freedom of information requests, and freelance invoicing — all in one organized Notion workspace. It is designed for the professional and administrative side of journalism: managing multiple simultaneous assignments, building a searchable source network, staying on top of editor communication, and making sure you get paid for work you complete. It is explicitly not designed for storing protected source identities, confidential documents, or any information that could harm a source if exposed. Always use end-to-end encrypted tools for sensitive source communications.

Can I store sensitive source information in Notion?

This question deserves a careful answer. Notion is a cloud-based productivity tool, not a secure source-protection platform — and you should treat it accordingly. For routine professional contacts — PR representatives, public officials, academics, industry experts, and anyone who speaks on the record — the source database works well. Their contact information, beat relevance, and last-contact notes are appropriate for a professional tool. For protected sources — whistleblowers, confidential informants, or anyone whose identity could cause them serious harm if exposed — do not store identifying information in any cloud tool including Notion. Use end-to-end encrypted communications tools such as Signal for ongoing contact, SecureDrop for initial outreach in sensitive investigations, and your news organization's secure file management systems for any related documentation. This is not legal advice. Journalists should consult their news organization's security protocols, their editors, and if necessary a media law attorney when handling sensitive source relationships.

How does the story pipeline kanban work?

The story pipeline kanban tracks every story through five stages: Pitch, Research, Draft, Edit, and Published. Each story card holds the slug or working title, publication target, assigned editor and their contact information, the current pipeline stage, filing deadline, agreed word count, kill fee terms if you're filing on a freelance basis, and running notes from editor conversations as the assignment progresses. Move cards between stages as your work progresses. Filter the entire board by publication to see only your assignments for a single outlet — useful when you're juggling three editors with different deadline rhythms. Filter by deadline date to see exactly what needs to ship in the next seven days. The pipeline gives you a real-time aerial view of your entire workload so nothing slips through the cracks of a busy week.

How do I track freelance invoices for multiple publications in Notion?

The freelance invoice tracker logs every assignment with the publication name, your agreed rate per word or flat fee, kill fee terms if applicable, the date you sent the invoice, the contractual payment due date, and payment status: Invoiced, Paid, Overdue, or Kill Fee Applied. Notes fields capture any billing dispute details or payment plan arrangements. Filter by payment status to generate an instant view of every unpaid or overdue invoice — so you know exactly which editors to follow up with this week. Sort by publication to see your complete billing history with any single outlet, which is useful for rate negotiation conversations and tax preparation. Flag invoices as they cross the due date so you follow up while the relationship is still warm rather than six months after publication when the editor has moved on.

Can I organize my research archive and notes by topic?

Yes. The research archive uses beat area tags so every item — background documents, web links, PDF clips, data files, court filings, research notes — is tagged by topic and attached to the relevant story record in your pipeline. Tag by beat (housing, education, criminal justice, technology, finance, health) so research from a past investigation is instantly searchable the next time the topic intersects with a new story. The relational structure means clicking any story record shows you all attached research directly, and searching a beat tag surfaces all the background work you've done on that topic across years of coverage. Research you did on past stories compounds in value rather than disappearing into a folder structure you can't navigate under deadline pressure.

How does the fact-checking checklist work in Notion?

The fact-checking workflow tracks every significant factual claim in a story — the specific claim as written, the primary source who made or documented it, any corroborating source or document, verification status (Verified, Needs Second Source, Disputed, or Removed from copy), and whether the copy editor or editor-in-chief has signed off on the verification before publication. As you move through the pre-publication process, every claim is either verified with two independent sources or explicitly removed from the draft. Flag any claim still pending a second source when you file — so your editor knows what remains outstanding. You can also log corrections from past published pieces with the error type and cause, creating a personal corrections history that helps you identify patterns and prevent the same category of error from appearing in future work.

Can I track freedom of information requests in Notion?

Yes. The FOI tracker logs every FOIA, FOIL, or public records request you've filed — the agency or department, the specific documents requested, the filing date, the legal response deadline under applicable law (typically 20 business days federally, varying by state), current status (Submitted / Acknowledged / In Review / Partially Fulfilled / Appealed / Closed), and a running response log with the date and substance of every communication you receive. The tracker ensures you never let a delayed response go unchallenged past the legal response deadline, and gives you a complete documented paper trail if you need to file an administrative appeal or pursue litigation to compel disclosure. FOI requests are slow by design — systematic tracking over months or years is the only way to pursue them without letting them quietly disappear.
