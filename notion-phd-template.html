THE PROBLEM

PhD research is a five-year project.
Most doctoral students have no system for it.

The intelligence and capability is there. The structure isn't. And without structure, even the best research drowns in disorganization — leaving you anxious, behind, and dreading your next advisor meeting.

📊
Dissertation progress is invisible
You know you've been working. But you can't answer "how far along are you?" with any confidence because there's no single place that shows the state of your chapters. Word count, draft version, advisor feedback pending — it all lives in your head, which means it might as well not exist.
📚
Literature notes are everywhere except where you need them
You read Sadato 2002, wrote brilliant notes in a margin, highlighted five sentences in a PDF, and typed two paragraphs into a Word doc. Three months later you need that methodology insight and you cannot find it. Your literature isn't lost — your connection to it is.
😰
Advisor meetings feel like you have nothing to show
You spent 40 hours on analysis this month. But when your advisor asks "what have you been working on?" — the panic sets in because you haven't logged any of it. The work happened; you just can't demonstrate it. That's not an intelligence problem. It's a documentation problem.
📤
The publication pipeline is completely unmanaged
You have a paper at revisions-requested, another in preparation, and a third you promised your co-author you'd submit by September. The journal deadlines, reviewer comments, and revision to-do lists live in three different places. One of those papers is about to slip through the cracks.

Dissertation Chapter Tracker
The command center for your entire dissertation. Track every chapter from outline to final submission with word count goals, draft version history, and a direct link to advisor feedback — so you always know exactly where you stand.

Literature Database
Your complete reading intelligence layer. Every paper you read gets a structured entry capturing not just what it says, but what it means for your argument — turning a scattered reading habit into a searchable research asset.
  • Full citation info: authors, year, journal, DOI, download link
  • Reading status: On List, Reading, Summarized, Integrated into thesis
  • Key quotes and page numbers extracted and tagged by theme
  • Methodology notes: what methods did this paper use and how
  • How this paper relates to your argument (supports, challenges, contextualizes)
  • Thematic tags for cross-paper searches across topics
  • Gaps and critiques: what's missing that your work addresses

Weekly Research Log
Your academic journal and accountability system in one. Log every work session, experiment, writing block, and decision so you always have a defensible record of your progress — especially valuable when imposter syndrome says you "haven't done anything."
  • Date-stamped entries for every research session with hours logged
  • Activity categories: Experiments, Writing, Literature, Meetings, Analysis, Admin
  • What you accomplished vs. what you planned (builds self-awareness over time)
  • Decisions made and reasoning logged — essential for your methods chapter
  • Problems encountered and how you resolved them
  • Ideas and hypotheses captured in the moment before they disappear
  • Weekly summary view for advisor meeting preparation

  • Reviewer feedback log with specific critiques organized by section
  • Revision tracker: what changes each reviewer requested and your responses
  • Co-author task assignments and status tracking

HOW IT WORKS

Three Habits That
Transform Your Research Life

You don't need to overhaul how you work. You need three simple habits that take 15 minutes a day — and a system that makes those habits effortless.

1
Set up your dissertation structure
In your first 30 minutes with the template, add your chapters, set word count targets, and build out your literature database with papers you've already read. The system becomes a mirror of where your dissertation actually stands — not a wishful to-do list, but an accurate map. Most users finish the initial setup in under an hour and immediately feel calmer.
2
Log everything weekly (15 minutes)
Every Friday, or every day if you prefer, spend 15 minutes logging what you worked on, what you read, what decisions you made, and any problems you encountered. Add papers to the literature database as you read them. Update your chapter word counts. Flag what needs advisor attention. This 15-minute habit compounds into an irreplaceable research record over your entire doctoral journey.
3
Arrive at every advisor meeting with clear progress
The night before your advisor meeting, open the Advisor Meeting Prep module. Your weekly log already contains everything you've done. Pull the highlights into your agenda, add your questions, review the action items from last time. Walk in prepared — not performing confidence, but genuinely having a complete picture of your research. Watch how the dynamic of your advising relationship shifts when you consistently show up this way.

FAQ

Frequently Asked
Questions

Everything doctoral students ask before purchasing the Notion PhD template.

Does this Notion PhD template work for all academic disciplines? +
Yes. The template is built around universal doctoral research workflows — structuring chapters, logging experiments or fieldwork, tracking literature, preparing for advisor meetings, and managing publication pipelines. Whether you're in molecular biology, comparative literature, sociology, engineering, or the humanities, the core system adapts to your workflow. Properties and database views can be renamed and reconfigured without any Notion expertise. We have users in STEM, social sciences, arts, and professional doctorates (EdD, DBA, DPsych) all using the same core system with minor customizations.
Can I use this template for a master's thesis, not a PhD? +
Absolutely. Master's thesis students are one of our most common user groups. The dissertation chapter tracker works identically for a thesis, and modules like the literature database, weekly research log, and advisor meeting prep are just as valuable for a 2-year master's program as they are for a 5-year doctorate. Many users purchase it at the start of their master's and continue using it throughout their early career. The academic job market tracker is also useful for master's students applying for PhD programs.
Does this Notion template replace Zotero or Mendeley for citation management? +
No — and it's not designed to. Zotero and Mendeley are best-in-class for generating formatted citations and syncing with Word or LaTeX. This template's Literature Database is your reading and thinking layer: you log papers you've read, capture key quotes, note methodology insights, and track how each paper connects to your argument. Many researchers run both in parallel — Zotero for citations, this template for understanding and synthesis. They solve different problems and work better together than either does alone.
Is this template good for both quantitative and qualitative researchers? +
Yes. The Data and Methods Notes module works equally well for documenting experimental protocols and dataset versions (quantitative) or interview codebooks, positionality statements, and field note organization (qualitative). The chapter tracker, literature database, and advisor meeting prep modules are discipline-agnostic by design. We've had qualitative researchers in anthropology, education, and sociology tell us the literature database is particularly transformative because their work is so literature-dense.
Can I share this workspace with lab-mates or my research group? +
Yes. Notion's sharing settings allow you to invite collaborators to a workspace with view, comment, or full edit access. Many research groups use this template as a shared lab knowledge base — a place where protocols, shared literature notes, and lab meeting agendas live together. The free Notion plan supports up to 10 guests, and Notion Plus unlocks unlimited guest access. For individual use, the free Notion plan is entirely sufficient for everything in this template.
Will this template still be useful after I graduate, for a postdoc or faculty role? +
Yes, and many users report this is when the system pays its biggest dividends. Postdocs and early-career faculty use the publication pipeline to track multiple papers simultaneously, the conference tracker for an ever-expanding list of talks and fellowship applications, and adapt the chapter tracker for book manuscripts. The academic job market tracker module is specifically designed for the postdoc-to-faculty transition. You'll find the system grows with your career rather than becoming obsolete at graduation.
