How to Build a Content Calendar in Notion (Step by Step)

A Notion content calendar isn't just a list of dates — it's a full production pipeline. Here's how to build one that actually gets used.

  1. Create a Content database with fields for: Title, Platform (YouTube/Instagram/TikTok/LinkedIn), Status (Idea / Scripting / Filming / Editing / Scheduled / Published), Publish Date, Format, and Tags.
  2. Add a Board view grouped by Status so you can see your Kanban pipeline at a glance — drag cards from Idea to Published as work progresses.
  3. Add a Calendar view filtered to "Scheduled" and "Published" so you get a bird's-eye view of your publishing cadence and can spot gaps before they happen.
  4. Create a Gallery view filtered by Platform to plan platform-specific content without the noise of other channels cluttering the view.
  5. Add a linked database for Scripts so each content item can have a full script document linked directly — no more hunting through Google Docs.
  6. Set up a filtered view called "This Week" with a date filter for the current week — this becomes your daily working view and keeps you focused on what matters right now.
  7. Add a Rollup or Formula field that counts your published posts per platform per month, giving you an automatic consistency score.

YouTube-Specific Additions

If YouTube is your main platform, add these fields to your content database: Thumbnail Status, Video Length, Target Keywords, Views (30-day), and Revenue (from AdSense). This turns your content calendar into a performance tracker — you can see at a glance which video formats are working and double down on them.

Tracking Brand Deals and Sponsorships with a Notion CRM

Brand deals are where most full-time creators make the majority of their income — and yet most creators are managing them in a combination of email threads, a random spreadsheet, and memory. That's how deals fall through the cracks, follow-ups get missed, and you end up undercharging because you forgot what you quoted last time.

A Notion sponsorship CRM solves this permanently. Here's what yours should track:

  • Brand name and contact — company, contact person, email, LinkedIn
  • Deal status — Prospecting / Pitching / Negotiating / Contracted / Delivered / Paid / Rejected
  • Deal value — quoted rate, negotiated rate, final paid amount
  • Deliverables — what's included (integration, dedicated video, story, post)
  • Deadlines — content due date, live date, invoice due date
  • Payment status — invoiced date, payment received date, outstanding balance
  • Notes — brand brief, talking points, restrictions, things to remember
  • Relationship history — past deals with this brand, notes on the relationship

Pro tip: Add a "Renewal" property with a date field set 60 days after a deal closes. Filter for renewals due this month and you'll always have a warm pipeline of brands who already love working with you — without having to remember to follow up.

With a Notion CRM set up this way, your entire sponsorship pipeline is visible on one screen. You can filter by status to see what's in negotiation, sort by deal value to prioritize high-value follow-ups, and filter by payment status to see who still owes you money.
