The Scaling Problem

When Freelancers Outgrow Solo Work

Most freelancers hit a ceiling. Your schedule is full, your rates are strong, but bigger opportunities keep arriving that a single person can't deliver alone. You have two options: refer the work out and lose the upside, or build a small subcontracting operation and keep it. The second path is how independent freelancers quietly become micro-agencies — without the overhead of formal employment.

Subcontracting is not about scaling for scale's sake. It's a deliberate trade: you take on coordination and management overhead in exchange for higher project values and the ability to serve clients beyond your own capacity. Done right, it's one of the most capital-efficient ways to grow a service business. Done haphazardly — with subcontractors tracked in email threads, payments in your head, and no paper trail — it becomes a liability.

This freelance subcontractor template gives you a proper operating system for that transition. Below are the clearest signals that you're ready to start using subcontractors rather than turning down work.

  • You're at full capacity and turning down qualified leads every month
  • A project requires a skill you don't have (dev, video, copy, illustration)
  • Your hourly rate justifies delegating lower-value production work
  • A client needs a faster turnaround than you can deliver alone
  • You want to offer a broader service package without hiring staff
  • Project values are growing into the $10K+ range
  • You have recurring work that benefits from a consistent specialist
  • You're testing whether a niche or service is worth building out

How It Works

The Subcontractor Workflow: Hire to Pay

Managing subcontractors well comes down to a repeatable process. Each stage of the engagement needs its own checklist and documentation. The FreelanceOS template structures this five-stage workflow so nothing falls through the cracks — from the first outreach to the final payment log.

1

Hire

Vet candidates, check portfolios, run a paid test. Log them in your contractor database.

2

Brief

Document deliverables, deadlines, scope, and tone. A clear brief prevents costly revisions.

3

Assign

Link the subcontractor to a project, log their rate, and set milestone dates in Notion.

4

Review

Track revision rounds. Mark deliverables approved before releasing payment. Keep feedback documented.

5

Pay

Log payment amount and date. Track yearly totals per contractor for 1099 prep.

The briefing stage is where most freelancer-to-subcontractor relationships break down. Vague direction produces vague work — and since you're the one accountable to the end client, the quality risk lands on you. The FreelanceOS subcontractor brief template prompts you to define the deliverable format, word count or dimensions, reference examples, target audience, tone guidelines, and revision limit before any work begins. That single document saves more time than any other part of the system.

The Paper Trail

Legal and Financial Considerations

The operational side of subcontracting — tracking deliverables and payments — is what this template handles. But there's a parallel layer of legal and financial hygiene that every freelancer running subcontractors needs to get right. Get these basics sorted before your first engagement, not after a dispute.

Contracts matter more than you think. When you're the prime contractor, you're on the hook to the client regardless of what your subcontractor does. A simple subcontractor agreement that covers scope, payment terms, revision limits, confidentiality, and work-for-hire IP assignment protects you from the most common disputes. The FreelanceOS template includes a checklist of what your subcontractor agreement should cover — but the actual contract should be reviewed by a qualified attorney, especially for high-value engagements.

The 1099 threshold is $600. In the US, you must issue a 1099-NEC to any contractor you pay $600 or more in a calendar year. The payment log in this template tracks cumulative annual payments per contractor, so you're never scrambling in January. Collect a W-9 from every subcontractor before their first payment — it's far harder to get this after the fact.

IP ownership must be explicit. By default, a contractor owns the work they create unless you have a written assignment. Your subcontractor agreement needs a clear work-for-hire or IP assignment clause. This is especially critical if you're using a subcontractor's output as a deliverable to your client — your client almost certainly expects to own what you're billing them for.

Managing subcontractors well is mostly an operational problem, not a legal one. But the legal foundation has to be there before the operational systems can do their job. This template helps with the former and prompts you toward the latter.

Quick Legal Checklist

  • Subcontractor Agreement Covers scope, payment, IP, confidentiality, and revision terms. Use a lawyer for high-value work.
  • W-9 on File Collect before first payment. Required to issue a 1099-NEC.
  • 1099-NEC Threshold $600+ paid in a calendar year triggers a filing requirement in the US.
  • IP Assignment Clause Ensure work product transfers to you (and then to your client) in writing.
  • NDA if Needed If subcontractors will access client materials or business info, add a non-disclosure clause.
  • Classification Review If the engagement looks like employment, consult an attorney before proceeding.
This template is an operational tool, not a legal document. Consult a qualified attorney for contracts and an accountant for tax compliance.

The Bigger Picture

Going From Solo Freelancer to Micro-Agency

The language shift matters: when you're managing a network of subcontractors, you're no longer selling your time — you're selling capacity and outcomes. Clients don't care whether you personally wrote every line of code or every sentence of copy. They care that the deliverable is on time, on brief, and on budget. That shift in identity is what separates freelancers who stay capped at their personal bandwidth from those who scale.

The micro-agency model doesn't require incorporation, a fancy website, or a team page. It requires three things: a reliable bench of contractors you've vetted and paid successfully, a repeatable briefing and review process, and accurate tracking of what you're paying versus what you're billing. All three are operational problems. This is the system that solves them.

When to refer out versus subcontract is a judgment call that changes as you grow. Early on, refer out work that's too far outside your lane — the reputational risk isn't worth it. As your bench of trusted specialists deepens and your project management systems mature, the calculus flips: subcontracting becomes the default because you have the infrastructure to manage the quality risk. This template is that infrastructure.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

When should I start using subcontractors?
Consider bringing in subcontractors when you're consistently turning down work due to capacity, when a project requires a skill outside your expertise, or when your hourly rate has grown to the point where delegating lower-value tasks is profitable. A good rule of thumb: if a project is large enough that you could comfortably pay a subcontractor and still clear your target margin, it's worth exploring. Most freelancers start subcontracting in a meaningful way around the $5,000–$10,000 per-project level.
How do I find reliable freelance subcontractors?
The most reliable subcontractors come from your professional network — fellow freelancers you've met in communities, courses, or past client work. Platforms like Contra, Toptal, and LinkedIn are also strong sources. Always request a portfolio, check references, and run a small paid test project before trusting someone with client-facing deliverables. Your FreelanceOS subcontractor database lets you track candidates and past contractors so you always have a warm bench ready when a project lands.
How much should I mark up subcontractor rates?
A standard markup is 20–40% above what you pay the subcontractor. This covers your time managing the relationship, reviewing deliverables, handling client communication, bearing the risk if something goes wrong, and the overhead of running a small operation. Some agency owners target a 50% margin on subcontracted work. The key is to build your markup into the client proposal upfront — never disclose your subcontractor's rate to the client. The FreelanceOS margin calculator shows your net margin before you commit to a project.
How do I track subcontractor payments and 1099s?
In the US, if you pay a subcontractor $600 or more in a calendar year, you're required to issue them a 1099-NEC. Collect a W-9 from every subcontractor before their first payment. The FreelanceOS subcontractor template includes a payment log where you can track amounts paid per contractor per year, making 1099 preparation straightforward. For actual tax filing, use accounting software like QuickBooks or work with a bookkeeper — this template organizes the data, but always consult a tax professional for compliance.
What's the difference between a subcontractor and an employee?
The IRS and most labor boards look at behavioral control, financial control, and the nature of the relationship. A subcontractor sets their own hours, uses their own tools, works for multiple clients, and is paid per project or deliverable. An employee works set hours you define, uses equipment you provide, and is integrated into your business operations. Misclassifying employees as contractors can result in significant tax penalties and back payments. If you're unsure about someone's classification — especially for long-term or full-time engagements — consult an employment attorney or accountant before proceeding.
